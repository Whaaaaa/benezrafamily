Shimon HaTzaddik was from the remnants of the Great Assembly. He used to say:

On three things the world stands.

On Torah,

On service,

And on acts of human kindness.

This week's Parsha is our first formal introduction to one of the foundational pillars of Judaism, ritual sacrifice. Parsha Vayikrah details the laws and procedures for the Olah (ascending offering), Mincha (meal offering), Shelamim (peace offering), Chatat (sin offering) and the Asham (guilt offering). From slaughter to consumption, each of these offerings has a set of unique procedures and laws that dictate every step of the ritual. These instructions feel incredibly foreign from a modern perspective. Animal sacrifice is an aspect of Judaism that has not been practiced in almost 2000 years. Our relationship with cattle and livestock has been revolutionized since then and it is very hard to understand the significance of these laws. Furthermore, after the destruction of the Temple and the end of the sacrificial era, prayer was instituted in its place. Understanding why God would want us to sacrifice animals to him is a hard enough task, but equating this bloody and messy ritual with our serene, calm and peaceful prayers is nearly impossible.

To understand the purpose of the sacrifices and how prayer can help achieve the same purpose we need only look to the second verse of this week's parsha.

Speak to the children of Israel and say to them: when one of you offers a sacrifice to the Lord, the sacrifice must be taken from the animal (behemah), cattle (bakar) and flock (tzon). (Lev. 1:2)

The First Chabad Rebbe took issue with the grammatical structure of the beginning of this verse. In Hebrew the word order of the sentence is strange and unexpected. We would expect to read: adam mikem ki yakriv, "when one of you offers a sacrifice". Instead what it says is adam ki yakriv mikem, "when one offers a sacrifice of you." From hear the Rebbe teaches that the true sacrifice that is requested from God is not that of the animal, but rather the real sacrifice is "mikem" or "of you." God is asking us to offer a part of ourselves to him, to fully give over a facet of our existence.

But what can we dedicate to God through prayer that is the equivalent of slaughtering an animal on the altar? How can it be that prayer and animal sacrifice can both fulfill the same level of self sacrifice? Rabbi Sacks explains that the answer to this question is alluded to at the end of the verse: "the sacrifice must be taken from the cattle, sheep or goats." Each of these permitted sacrifices parallel the internal sacrifice that is at their heart.

Behemah or the class of domesticated animals represents our animal instincts themselves. As these animals are domesticated, this cannot not refer to the predatory instincts of animals. Rather this refers to the constant struggle for basic survival. These animals lives are solely dedicated to the quest for food and shelter. There is no time to appreciate, ponder, or search for meaning. To sacrifice the Behema to God is to elevate these pursuits to a higher plane. This enriches the fulfillment of our basic needs and allows us to harness our animalistic desires to achieve spiritual connection.

The word Bakar or cattle shares a root with the word Boker or dawn. Just as the first rays of sunlight break through the darkness of night, stampeding cattle break through barriers. Cattle do not respect boundaries. To sacrifice the bakar within ourselves to God, is to learn to recognize and respect boundaries. Whether they be between holy and profane, pure and impure, permitted and forbidden these boundaries are what give definition to our lives and keep us on the right track.

Finally tzon, flocks, represents the herd instinct, groupthink, peer pressure, mob mentality, and the sheeple everywhere! Tzon represent our overwhelming desire to follow the crowd. What sets the Jewish people apart has been our refusal to desert our customs and faith. This commitment to our identity even in the face of extreme persecution has kept our religion alive and strong and is clearly a pillar on which our existence depends.

With this in mind, we are able to understand the correlation between animal sacrifice and prayer. While we might not walk away from Shul with our shirts covered in blood without the company of our beloved goat we still have the opportunity to reach inside ourselves and give over our precious animalism to the service of God. We reach out to God with even our most mundane and basic requests for sustenance and health. Three times daily we have the opportunity to stand before our creator and exclaim, "Holy! Holy! Holy!" a proclamation of our belief that God and his service are elevated and seperate. And while organized religion has become a four letter word, there are Minyanim everywhere of Jews eager to embrace the unique identity and continue serving God with the commitment and conviction of their ancestors. Prayer is our method of developing a relationship with God and as is true with any relationship, it takes sacrifice.

Shabbat Shalom
