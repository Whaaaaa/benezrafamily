Shimon HaTzaddik used to say: on three things the world stands -- Torah, service, and acts of kindness. This week's Parsha is our first formal introduction to ritual sacrifice: the Olah, Mincha, Shelamim, Chatat, and Asham offerings.

The First Chabad Rebbe notices an odd grammatical inversion in the Parsha's second verse -- literally, "when one offers a sacrifice of you," rather than "when one of you offers a sacrifice." The true sacrifice requested is not the animal, but a part of ourselves -- "mikem," of you.

Rabbi Sacks explains that the three permitted categories of sacrifice -- behemah, bakar, and tzon -- each parallel an internal sacrifice. Behemah, domesticated animals, represent our basic drive for survival; sacrificing this is elevating our basic needs into spiritual pursuit. Bakar, cattle, shares a root with boker, "dawn" -- cattle break through barriers, so sacrificing the bakar within us means learning to respect boundaries between holy and profane. Tzon, flocks, represents herd instinct and peer pressure -- our commitment to Jewish identity even under persecution has kept our people alive.

While we no longer walk away from shul with blood on our shirts, we still have the opportunity to give over our animal instincts to the service of G-d, three times daily, in prayer. Prayer is our method of developing a relationship with G-d, and like any relationship, it takes sacrifice.

Shabbat Shalom
