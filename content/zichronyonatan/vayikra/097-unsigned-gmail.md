Sacrifices are often seen as an outdated and meaningless practice by many. However, the Torah teaches us that offering animals and burning them on an altar is actually a beautiful and profound way to connect with God. Let's set aside our preconceived notions and try to understand this important concept that has been a central part of our ancestors' heritage.

Imagine a close relationship that is very important to you, whether it's with a parent, spouse, or child. Such a relationship requires that you constantly keep the other person in mind to some degree. The things you do for them should not be done as a burden or obligation, but rather as acts of service that come from your own desire to connect with them and strengthen the relationship.

In Judaism, our most important relationship is supposed to be with God. This means that we should dedicate our minds, emotions, excitement, and life's work to serving Him. When we offer a sacrifice, we use our hard-earned money to connect with God. We tell Him, "I would sacrifice myself to you, but I know that you want me to live and that's how I can serve you best, so instead, I offer you my sheep." When the animal is slaughtered, we imagine that we are being slaughtered as well, but happily so, because we are deeply in love with God. Even though the animal may not be able to express it, its inner soul is delighted to be used as an instrument to help us get closer to God. This is just a small glimpse of the meaning behind sacrifices, which the book of Leviticus, which we begin reading this week, explores.
