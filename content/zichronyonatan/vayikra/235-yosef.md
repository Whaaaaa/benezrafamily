This week, we begin the Book of Vayikra, which begins with Hashem calling out to Moshe from the tabernacle. One who looks inside the Sefer Torah, however, will find something peculiar about the word Vayikra, or ויקרא. Here the first four letters are written normally, but the Alef at the end of the word is written in a much smaller font. The Baal Haturim famously states that this was a result of Moshe's profound humility. He explains that to be called by Hashem is something reserved for people of tremendous levels – that Hashem himself should call a human to a meeting is something incredible. Therefore, Moshe did not want to write such an accolade about himself and wished to instead write Vayikar (ויקר) which would sound like Hashem, so to speak, just 'happened' upon Moshe, without a predesignated meeting. This is a much lower level of prophecy which even the wicked Bilaam was able to attain. Hashem insisted that the word be written in its entirety, and Moshe obeyed, but he requested the right to leave it in small letters so as not to call attention to his greatness.

This is the famous explanation of the Baal Haturim. However, when I looked up the Baal Haturim's actual words, I was perplexed to find a very strange inconsistency. His words read: 'Because Moshe was great and humble he only wanted to write Vayikar etc.'

The question that begs to be asked is why he stuck in a mention of Moshe's greatness. Yes, of course Moshe was great, but what does it have to do with the small Alef, which seems to be exclusively an expression of humility?

We can answer with an anecdote from Rav Avrohom Yeshaya Karelitz, the Chazon Ish. When he first moved to the city of Bnei Brak in 1933, the local community was still in it's fledgling state. In a (very successful) effort to expand the Jewish neighborhood, the Chazon Ish chose a house some way away from the other houses, hoping that people would eventually move to bridge the gap. Many people flocked to the sage's house at all hours of the day and night, and soon someone decided that a single street light had to be erected at the halfway point to allow for safety.

When the Chazon Ish found out about the light, he commented that we can learn a great lesson from it. When one is standing far away from the light, his shadow looks very large, while the closer he gets the smaller the shadow. When he stands directly under the light, his shadow disappears entirely. So too, the further one is from Hashem, the greater he is in his own eyes, but as he draws closer and closer to Hashem, he realizes how small man really is.

This, said the Chazon Ish is the reason that Moshe was both the greatest man that ever lived as well as the most humble. Moshe was the greatest prophet ever, and the person who achieved greater proximity to Hashem than anyone else. Because he stood right under the light, he was the epitome of humility.

Now we can return to the words of the Baal Haturim and understand that Moshe's greatness and his humility were not two disparate qualities, but two sides of the same coin. His humility was born of his greatness and his closeness to Hashem.
