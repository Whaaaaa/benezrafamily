Parsha Vayikra is all about sacrifice -- Moshe gathers the people and teaches the laws of ritual offering. Different sacrifices accomplish different things: some are brought daily out of gratitude, others by those who have sinned by accident or on purpose. What unites them is that each brings the person closer to G-d.

Sacrifice is largely lost to us in our place in time -- we are far from the Temple service that once bound us as a people, and rarely need to part with something valuable to reach our goals. Sacrifice was well understood by our nomadic ancestors, who had to decide what to carry, and by the farmer who chose to yield one crop rather than forage for himself. The sacrifice of industrialization has been the loss of the earth's natural habitats.

As humans we have sacrificed our individuality for the benefit of the group, time and again -- and living in a globalized world has made our lives more remarkable than our ancestors could imagine. There is a fine line between sacrifice and destruction; G-d does not accept burnt meat as an offering. By bringing an animal to the altar, we kill the animal within ourselves, reaffirming our humanity.
