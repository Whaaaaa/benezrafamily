There is a lesson that we learn in the Torah: Greater is one who is commanded and does then one who is not commanded but does anyway".

This is referring to doing good deeds; something that we are explicitly told we must do is a greater accomplishment than an even harder thing that we do voluntarily.

I used to think this statement was contradictory. Isn't it much greater to put ourselves out there and extend ourselves to something that we don't even have to do?

However, I found a couple of answers that clarified the matter for me.

Firstly, when we are commanded to do something, it suddenly creates the resistance in us to not want to do it, otherwise known as the evil inclination. Thus, doing the thing we are commanded to do becomes that much harder.

Secondly, when we do something voluntarily, we get a good feeling, an impression of righteousness about ourselves that detracts from the purity of the good deed.

Thirdly, when we get caught up in voluntary acts of heroism, we may neglect the core commandment that was actually necessary for us to focus on.

I find that these principles are eerily applicable in our times.

Firstly, many people were happy to stay home all day until they were told that they had to.

Secondly, some people want so badly to be the person that saves the day that they are going from hospital to nursing home trying to cheer people up and thereby spreading the gems.

And finally, (most ironic of all), some people are so busy preparing for self-isolation that they keep running to the store and buying toilet paper and hand sanitizer, but they never actually get to the part where they just stay home and don't leave the house.

Let's all try to be like the person who is given a commandment and then does what he is told. We are being implored to stay in our homes, so let's just do that.

Together (by ourselves) we can stop the spread!
