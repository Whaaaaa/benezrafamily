This week's Parsha contains the verse,

"ונקדשתי בתוך בני ישראל"

"and I will be sanctified in the midst of the Children of Israel"

From here the Sefer HaChinuch explains, we derive the mitzvah of kiddush Hashem, the imperative to sanctify the name of God. This sanctification is achieved through martyrdom, giving up our physical lives to stay consistent with our religious values. Despite this running contrary to the axiom to "live through the commandments," our sages identified three cardinal sins that are so foundational to our faith we must be willing to sacrifice our lives rather than violate them.

In the explanation of the cardinal sin of murder, the Sefer HaChinuch brings a Gemara in Sanhedrin that offers a logical metaphor as the source for the prohibition of killing another, even at the expense of one's own life. "Do you see that your blood is redder? Perhaps the blood of that man is redder." The Sefer HaChinuch explains this to mean,

The Sefer HaChinuch seems to be claiming that because of the infinite potential of a human we don't know what one individual is capable of achieving. Therefore, we cannot morally choose our own future over theirs. The next conclusion that he brings regarding an entire town (originally from a Gemara in Yerushalmi Terumot) seems to take this ideal and stretch it to its limits.

In a position that seems to run contrary to the Sefer HaChinuch's conflation of these two moral issues, the Ramach notes that the rationale of "whose blood is redder?" does not apply in the case in which townspeople must decide between handing over one person and being killed. He insists that under these circumstances, we can indeed determine which misfortune is graver, as whomever the people choose to hand over to the enemy would otherwise be killed along with the rest of them. As this decision does not require us to be selective, we can limit the damage being done.

What all of the opinions seem to reject, albeit to differing degrees is classical utilitarianism. The underlying assumption, "Nature has placed mankind under the governance of two sovereign masters, pain and pleasure" that forms the basis for utilitarian thinking reduces a human to their capacity to feel. There is much more to a human being, the infinite potential for closeness to god. Furthermore, this premise contains a duality that is alarming for Jewish thinkers. After all, we are the people that,

" מברך על הרעה מעין על הטובה ועל הטובה מעין על הרעה"

"bless for the bad just as for the good."

We don't see the good and evil as separate self-determined forces. They are both emanations from a single omnipotent god. Our commitment to unity extends even further as explained in Tanya, all Jews are one entity sharing a single spiritual presence. Connecting to this truth through our precepts and customs is how we achieve in this world. To view life as a zero-sum game of pain versus pleasure is to ignore the true point of existence, connection.

We have one sovereign master, and when we acknowledge and connect to the infinite potential in those around us, we sanctify his name.

Shabbat Shalom
