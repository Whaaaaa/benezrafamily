There is a common Jewish phobia today known as Benchaphobia, or in English Graceaftermealsaphobia, the fear of eating bread and being required to recite the Birkas Hamazon.

It is the second most common fear amongst observant Jewry today, second only to Fleishophobia, the fear of being "fleishig" and unable to consume dairy for 6ish hours.

As much as I understand these fears, I'd like to explore an idea relevant to this time of year and hopefully help treat the first of these all-too-common phobias.

Today, (Friday still) is Pesach Sheni, the second Passover. It's a minor holiday on the Jewish calendar and today consists mostly of having a bit of matzah in commemoration of the Holiday practiced in temple times.

When the temple still stood, there was a law that one who was Tamei, (ritually "impure" for lack of a better English translation) as a result of contact with the dead was not able to bring a sacrifice. On Pesach, every Jew was required to take part in a Korban Pesach, or Passover sacrifice. Naturally, since some people would have died in the week preceding each year's Holiday and others would be required to bury them or attend the funeral, those attending would not be able to participate in the Korban due to their Tamei status.

Not to worry though, the Torah had these Jews' backs; there's a second shot at this one on Pesach Sheni one month later.

Interestingly, however, this mitzvah was introduced to the Jewish people only after the first Pesach when a group of Tamei Jews came to complain to Moshe.

"Lama Nigarah, why should we lose out?" they asked.

Moshe took the question back to Hashem and returned to reveal the law of Pesach Sheni, the make-up Holiday.

At a time when many of us might have thought, "Hey, I'm Tamei, I guess I can skip this Mitzvah this year," these giants came to lay their claims.

"What have we done wrong, that we should miss out on this mitzvah opportunity?"

I think there is a lot we can learn from this outlook on Mitzvos. Korban stems from the word Karav, to draw close. These Jews understood that they were indeed missing out. They saw every Mitzvah as an opportunity to draw closer to their creator and understood that the inability to do a mitzvah, even one that they were absolved from, was truly a loss.

"Why should we lose out?!"

My oldest, Yonason Ephraim, at six years old will frequently ask," Can I have bread at this meal? I want to bench."

I thought it was a ploy to have carbs but when I offered "mezonos bread", a sweetened kind of bread that doesn't require Birkas Hamazon, he turned it down.

"I want to Bench though!"

I suppose no-one has introduced him to Benchophobia yet. And I hope he never learns.

My plea for all of us is that we take this opportunity to exchange our glasses regarding Mitzvos.

May we always view them as an opportunity to connect. May we merit to return to the Holy Land, and to bring the Korbanos, drawing ever closer to the one above and to each other!

Good Shabbos!
