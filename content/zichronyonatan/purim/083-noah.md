The Malbim opens his commentary on Megilat Esther by describing two archetypes of leader: the democratically elected leader, chosen by and answerable to the people, and the autocrat who conquers and imposes his reign. A democratic leader works in service of his country, sees its treasury as belonging to the public rather than himself, makes decisions only after consulting advisors, and is bound by precedent -- he cannot even move the capital, since his authority flows from the office itself, not from him personally.

The Malbim explains that Achashverosh, easily dismissed as a bumbling fool, was in fact undermining the very fabric of democratic norms that had kept the Jews safe in diaspora -- and that by consolidating power around himself, he directly enabled a plot to exterminate an entire people within his kingdom.

The Gemara asks why Esther invited Haman to her private party at all. Reb Nechemia answers: so the Jews wouldn't say, "we have a sister in the palace," and forget to pray. We cannot let complacency set in simply because some of our own sit in high seats of power. We had a Queen, and still avoided extinction by only a hair's breadth.
