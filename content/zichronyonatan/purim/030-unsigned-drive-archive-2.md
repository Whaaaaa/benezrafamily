The Malbim begins his commentary on Megilas Esther by describing two different archetypes of leaders. He introduces us to the democratically elected leader that is chosen in a process that reflects the will of the people. On the other hand, he presents the totalitarian and autocratic ruler, who conquers other lands and imposes his reign. The Malbim then describes some key attributes that define these types of rulers.

The democratic leader is chosen by the people, and has well defined and known powers. These rulers are required to publicly affirm their commitment to protecting and upholding the values and ideals of the country.

A democratic leader works in service of his country. He is responsible for the safety of his constituency, both physically in his capacity as a military general and economically through fair taxes. He is a first and foremost a public servant.

The democratic leader views the treasury as belonging to the country itself. They don't see the countries wealth as being connected to them at all, rather it belongs to the public. Democratic leaders do not use a countries resources for private gain.

A democratic leader is only able to make decisions after consulting with and acquiring the approval of his advisors. He is not an autocrat.

The democratic leader is bound by the customs of his country. He must respect precedent.

A democratic leader cannot change the capital of the country. Because he derives his authority from the office itself, he must sit in the same throne as all of his predecessors.

The Malbim then goes on to explain that Achashverosh, the king that can easily be dismissed as a bumbling fool, really was undermining the fabric of democracy that had been keeping the Jews safe in diaspora. By dismissing democratic norms and consolidating power around himself he was directly assisting in a plot to exterminate an entire creed within his own kingdom.

The Malbim's word take on an eerie tone in today's political environment. We are experiencing a degree of international turmoil unparalleled since WW2. Across the globe we are seeing budding democratic societies wither into kleptocracies and oligarchies. And amidst these crises, countries that were dependable pillars of democracy are starting to crumble.

In America we have seen an astounding departure from democratic norms and practices. We have a president that for the first time has no experience in the military or as a public servant. For the first time in history, the leader of the united states has refused to divest from his business interests and receives profits from visiting dignitaries, effectively leveraging his political position for personal wealth. Additionally, he has spent 12 times the taxpayer money on personal travel and business than the previous administration. Raw, unedited, unvetted, and often misspelled proclamations on Twitter have become government policy with no oversight or approval from advisors and staff. This president defended defunding the state department on national T.V. by saying, "The one that matters is me, I'm the only one that matters, because when it comes to it, that is what the policy is going to be." The current administration has intentionally defied all precedent, leaving long standing international agreements and failing to reaffirm commitments to allies. And finally, in what may be the most visible representation of the digression from tradition, Mar-a-lago has become the "Winter White House."

Meanwhile in Israel things don't look much better. The Prime Minister has been recommended for indictment on charges of bribery by the police. Along with this recommendation, has been accused of illegally undermining the free press, a cornerstone of any democratic society. While arrests relating to this case are being made, the Prime Minister denies any wrongdoing and works to discredit the efforts of his accusers. The political situation looks grim.

But the Jews aren't being persecuted now! We enjoy religious liberty in our communities. We have many of our own in high seats of power and authority. Sure, anti-semitism is far from extinct but we don't seem to be the immediate target. This is especially true in Israel where we are in control of our destiny to an even larger extent. Why should we worry?

The Gemara asks, "Why did Esther invite Haman to the party?" Reb Nechamya answered, "So that the Jews wouldn't say, 'We have a sister in the palace' and forget to pray." We cannot let complacency set in. We had a Queen and only avoided extinction by a hair's breadth, can we really rely on the Kushners and Netanyahus to save us?

No! We absolutely cannot. We must cry bitter tears, don sackcloth and weep for our democratic institutions. We must direct our cries toward both inward in the pursuit of Teshuva and to God, demanding salvation. And throughout this whole exercise of dedicating ourselves to our values and convictions, we must trust that this is all a part of the divine plan.

Shabbat Shalom
