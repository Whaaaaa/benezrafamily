One day, on his way home from school, Sammy sees the coolest looking bike he's ever seen. One look is all it takes for him to know that he's just got to have this bike. He runs home, bursts into his father's study and says, "Dad, I just saw the coolest, fastest, latest model bike in the store on my way home from school; can you buy it for me?"

His father turns to him and says, "Sammy, you already have a bike, you don't need a new one. And, as we both know, you do not deserve anything new right now. You have been slacking off both at school and at home. I'm sorry, but no. You simply do not deserve a new bike."

A couple of hours later, Sammy leaves the house to go visit a friend. As he steps into the street, a car comes flying at him and knocks him to the ground. Paramedics rush him to the emergency room and hook him up to many machines. After a few minutes, the grim-faced doctor goes out to the waiting room to tell Sammy's parents that it's over; there's nothing more than they can do. Sammy's heart-broken parents walk towards their son's room, where they see a different doctor frantically standing over him and shouting, "We have a pulse!" Sure enough, the doctors bring him back to life and slowly nurse him back to complete health.

Weeks later, when Sammy finally returns home from the hospital, what's waiting for him?

Not just the brand new bike that he had asked for, but every single toy and gadget that a little boy can dream of.

What changed from before, that now his parents bought him the bike that they had previously not agreed to? Did he suddenly deserve that which he did not deserve hours before?

We can all understand how on the day when Sammy's parents thought that they had lost their child, all of the appreciation and love for him came to the fore.

Rabbi Shimshon Pinkus explains that this is exactly what happened on Purim.

On Purim, there was a decree that all of the Jews would be killed and destroyed forever. For the only time in history, Hashem had signed and sealed the decree to destroy the entire Jewish nation. It was as if G-d was about to lose His precious only child forever.

And then, the Jews repented and the decree was miraculously changed – it was as if we came back to life! This near-death experience of G-d's precious nation brought about an incredible abundance of love for His people.

Imagine for a moment, if exactly one year after Sammy's accident, Sammy would walk up to his father and say, "Dad, you know my bike is already a year old, can you get me a new one?" Sammy's father would probably say something along the lines of, "Son, I love you very much, but nice try."

Yet, as we know, the love that Hashem has for us, His children, is exponentially greater than the love that any human father can possibly possess. In his infinite love and kindness, Hashem demonstrates the same love to us each year, as He did on the original Purim. This shower of love miraculously opens up every single year in the same quality, quantity, and depth as it did on that very first Purim.

Throughout the year, we ask Hashem for many things, most of which we do not deserve. But on Purim, when Hashem is so overjoyed that His People are alive, He will grant us whatever we ask for, even if we do not deserve it.
On Purim, we are told to give charity to whoever stretches out a hand. Whereas throughout the year, we are supposed to check to see if those who request money from us are legitimate, on Purim we give without any considerations. We are taught that Hashem also behaves this way on Purim. He gives freely, without calculating whether or not we deserve what we are asking for.

Let us take advantage of this unbelievable, once-a-year opportunity! There is no limit to what we can accomplish with our prayers on Purim. If we would all spend the whole day of Purim praying, we could change the world! We could bring the redemption! We could accomplish anything and everything! LET US! Let us tap into the incredible power that we are given on the day of Purim, when G-d's love for us is at its peak, to pray for ourselves and for the entire Jewish nation.

Good Shabbos and Happy Purim!

May all of our prayers be answered.

Tali
