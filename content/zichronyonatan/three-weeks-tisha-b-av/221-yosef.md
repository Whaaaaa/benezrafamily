This week was the first of the 'Three Weeks', during which we mourn the destruction of the Beit Hamikdash and the exile which has now stretched on for thousands of years. Each year, I grapple with the task of properly feeling the sense of pain, loss, and longing which ought to be felt by every Jew. And every year it evades me...

Unfortunately, I sense that I am not alone with this shortcoming – it seems to be a universal problem, albeit a problem that is only a generation or two old.

Our sages tell us (Taanit 30b) that anyone who mourns Jerusalem will merit to see her joy (with the redemption), and anyone who does not mourn Jerusalem will not merit to see her joy. So when we wonder why it's taking so very long for Mashiach to come, we really must direct the question inwards and ask ourselves why it's taking us so long to mourn for our loss. Our lack of feeling is not a problem which we can simply make peace with; it is something we must rectify if we ever want to dig ourselves out of the pit into which we have fallen. We must do everything in our power to feel, to mourn, to long, and to cry.

The tabernacle was called the *Mishkan*, and the Midrash explains that this is a form of the Hebrew word *mashkon*, which refers to a collateral taken in place of a debt. When the Jewish people strayed from the path of Hashem, we racked up a heavy debt – one that could only have been paid with a destruction too great to speak of. Instead of destroying the people, Hashem destroyed the tabernacle, and the *mishkan *thus served as the collateral for our people, as Hashem took it in our stead.

The question is that such a collateral seems to run contrary to Torah law, which delineates exactly what a debtor is and isn't permitted to take as collateral. The Torah states that one may not take anything which the borrower relies on for his livelihood. If he is a farmer you can't take his cow, if he's a miller you can't take his mill, and if he's an Uber driver you can't take his car.

When the Beit Hamikdash stood, the life of every Jew centered around it. Three times a year, our entire people made a pilgrimage to the site to reconnect and draw spiritual sustenance for the rest of the year. On a daily basis, the priests were busy all day performing their service on behalf of the entire people. If anyone accidentally sinned, he would immediately go to the Beit Hamikdash to repent, bring an offering, and walk away cleansed.

With the destruction of the Beit Hamikdash, we lost our spiritual livelihood. Since that time, we have so much trouble connecting to Hashem, performing mitzvos, avoiding sin, praying, learning, and everything in between. How could Hashem take such a central source of life as collateral – wasn't it at least as essential as the farmer's cow?

The answer again places the question squarely on our shoulders. It is true that a debtor may not take one's source of livelihood from him, but if the person himself doesn't consider it essential that is a different story. The debtor doesn't need to convince him of its necessity.

So too, if we would have perceived the Beit Hamikdash as something we could not live without, Hashem would never have taken it from us. But if we felt that it was nothing more than extra credit, if we felt like we could get on fine without it, then it was within our power to declare it unnecessary to us and to offer it for collection.

But it isn't too late. If we would sit down today and make a serious reckoning of our situation and what we are missing, we could again declare it essential and demand it back. So let's think just how short we fall in our service of Hashem, just how hampered we are in our Torah learning, and just how hard it is for us to overcome our evil inclination. Let us realize that almost all of our hardships are direct ramifications of the exile, and how vital the Beit Hamikdash really is to our lives. And when we truly feel that such a life is not a life and that we just cannot go on like this, let us turn to Hashem and ask for our collateral back.

We just cannot live without it.

*Good Shabbos,*

*Yosef*
