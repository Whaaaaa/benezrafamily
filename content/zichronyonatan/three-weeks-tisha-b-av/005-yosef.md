When the Israeli Defense Force regained the Old City of Jerusalem and the Western Wall, it was an incredibly emotional moment. Many soldiers who had fought in the liberation laid their heads on the Wall and wept. One soldier, Dani, very far removed from Jewish observance, watched in silence at first -- then broke down sobbing louder than anyone. When a friend asked why he was crying, Dani answered, "I am crying because I don't know what there is to cry for."

These are the "three weeks" leading to the fast of Tisha B'Av, the saddest day of the year, when we mourn the destruction of the Beit Hamikdash, the exile of our people, and the distance between us and the Divine Presence. Once, this mourning pierced every heart; today, we fast but count down the hours to a glass of orange juice, sit on the floor but complain of discomfort, recite the Kinot but rarely feel a genuine tear.

One reason for this estrangement is that we simply never knew any other existence -- we never felt the ecstasy of intimacy with G-d in the Beit Hamikdash, never witnessed its open miracles, so we lack any frame of reference for what was lost, much like a person born blind cannot truly grasp what color is, however vividly it's described to him.

So we must try mightily to glean whatever sliver of understanding we can -- and when, inevitably, we fail to fully comprehend the scale of the loss, let us at least recognize the desperate situation that failure reveals. And let us cry because we don't know what there is to cry for.

Good Shabbos, and may we merit celebrating this coming Tisha B'Av with the rebuilding of the Beit Hamikdash,
Yosef
