This week we start the final book of the torah, Devarim. Originally named "Mishneh Torah" or the repetition of the law it contains a seemingly odd mix of both new and repeated laws, historical recollection and anticipation for entering the land of Israel. Despite this varied mix of subject matter, Devarim has a form that is uniquely concrete as proven in the general dispute about the chronological order of the Torah. While from Bereshit through Bamidbar there are ongoing debates over the significance of juxtaposions of events as they may not have occurred in the sequence described, everyone agrees that in Devarim "Homiletic interpretations from juxtaposed verses" are valid. This unanimity suggest the structure of Devarim is of great significance.

Rabbi Saks explains that recent archeological discoveries from before the time of Avraham have uncovered covenants between rival nations in the region. These treaties adhere to a consistent template comprised of 6 parts. Rabbi Saks explains the structure of these covenants,

[1] They began with a preamble, establishing the identity of the person or power initiating the covenant. This was followed by [2] a historical prologue, reviewing the history of the relationship between the two parties to the covenant. Then came [3] the provisions of the covenant itself, the stipulations, which were often stated in two forms, [a] general principles, and [b] detailed provisions. There then followed [4] a provision for the covenant to be deposited in a sacred place, and read on a regular basis. Next came [5] the sanctions associated with the covenant, namely the blessings that would follow if it was adhered to, and the curses that would occur if it is broken. Lastly, there is [6] a statement of the witnesses to the agreement -- usually the gods of the nations involved. The entire book of Devarim is structured as an extended covenant, on precisely these lines.

Rabbi Saks then continues to map each of the section of Devarim into this ancient arrangement:

1. Preamble

Dev. 1:1-1:5

Announces place, time and person initiating the covenant that follows: Moses on behalf of God.

2. Historical prologue

Dev.1:6-4:49

Moses recapitulates the history that has brought them to where they are, mostly recalling the events described in the book of Bamidbar

3. Stipulations

[a] chs. 5-11 [b] chs. 12-26

[a] general provisions: Ten Commandments, Shema, etc. Recapitulation of events surrounding the making of the covenant at Sinai. [b] specific provisions: the details of the law, with special reference to how they are to be carried out by the people as a whole in the land of Israel.

4. Deposition and regular reading

27, 31

The law to be inscribed on stone (stele) at Mount Ebal; the Torah written by Moses and placed in the ark; to be read in public at a national assembly by the king every seven years.

5. Sanctions: the blessings and the curses

28

Ch. 28 states the blessings and curses; 29-30 the actual covenant renewal, together with a statement that even if the people break the covenant and the curses come to pass, return, teshuvah, is still possible.

6. Witnesses

Dev. 30:19 -- 32:1

"Heaven and earth" (Dev. 4:26, Dev. 30:19, Dev. 31:28, 32:1), "This song" (Dev. 31:19)

What emerges from this analysis is an understanding of Devarim as an expertly crafted legal document with a couple monumental changes that define the covenant we have with God. The first such modification is that it is a treaty between a nation and God, the first time this political formula was used for a religious purpose.

The next signficant departure from the covenants before it is that all of Israel is included in the agreement. Similar contactual relationships were traditional negotiated and agreed to by the ruler alone. In Devarim we are reminded that all of Israel is included in this story and party to this arrangement.

As Rabbi Saks explains, "This is covenant politics, a unique form of political structure based not on a hierarchy of power but on a shared sense of history and destiny. It is a moral politics, dedicated to creating a just and gracious society that honours the dignity of all, especially the downtrodden, the poor, the powerless and the marginal: the widow, the orphan and the stranger."

As we read these parshiot and explore this covenant this structure can help guide our understanding and deepen our appreciation for the final book of the Torah.
