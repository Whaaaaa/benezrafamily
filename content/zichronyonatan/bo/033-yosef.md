Each Passover we eat only matzoh in commemoration of the unleavened bread that the Jews ate as they left Egypt. But did you ever wonder why it had to be that way? Couldn't they have waited another few minutes for the bread to rise? Why did G-d deem it necessary to take them out in such a mad rush?

Rabbi Simcha Z. Ziv answers that this was to teach the Jewish nation an invaluable lesson. Now, as they left the Egyptian bondage en route to a life of freedom, they were prone to a grave mistake in what the true meaning of freedom is. One might think that freedom means living it up, capitalizing on every pleasure available to man, and fulfilling every desire which tickles your fancy. This is the driving force behind all of Western society. Everyone is chasing after money, comfort, and pleasure, in the belief that through having more stuff they'll somehow fill the void which eats away at their hearts, day and night. "If only I had that house, that car, or that gadget, *then* I'd finally be happy."

Here, at the outset of their free lives, G-d taught the Jews that this is not what it's all about. The only way to achieve true happiness is by breaking our wants and desires, by learning to find joy in the important things in life and not being dependent on the fulfillment of our whims. This was the message of the unleavened bread, to show for all time that happiness and freedom is something attained not by succumbing to our impulses but by rising above them and learning to be happy with our lot, or in this case our matzoh. As our sages tell us, "Who is truly wealthy? One who is happy with his share."

To illustrate, Rabbi Yeruchom Levovitz explains that true happiness comes from *menuchat hanefesh,* peace of mind. When we are in a state of inner contentment and tranquility it gives us a sense of great joy. The mistake we all make is in the belief that the way to achieve such inner peace is through *menuchat haguf,* or physical comfort. Most people assume that if we will be physically content, sleeping on the finest linen money can buy, eating the most luscious meats, and drinking the finest wines, that will give us the inner peace and happiness which we truly crave.

In truth, however, the opposite is true. Rabbi Levovitz turns our attention to the training of a soldier. The objective of his training is to teach absolute equanimity and inner peace, to the point that when in the midst of battle, as all hell breaks loose around him, he will be able to keep his composure and make calm calculated decisions. According to the mistaken formula mentioned above, one might expect training for such an event to include the fulfillment of every wish the soldier has. He should be wined and dined, treated to plenty of hours of restful sleep, the occasional sauna, and perhaps some Beethoven playing softly in the background. We know of course that the opposite is the case - boot camp is tough as nails. The soldiers are awakened roughly in the wee hours of the morning after just a few hours of sleep on a wooden plank. They are fed tasteless mush and forced to work tirelessly all day in poor conditions. They are denied all the pleasures they are used to at home and taught to make do with nothing but the bare minimum. And this all in order to teach them peace of mind?

Indeed. As long as one is wrapped up and dependent upon the materialistic things in life he can never achieve inner peace. If he needs his beauty rest, then inevitably when his baby keeps him up at night he will be off-kilter for the day ahead. If he craves wealth or honor then he can rest assured that he'll never have enough. When our happiness depends on physical crutches, then we will always remain a cripple. We will be subject to all the winds blowing around us which can easily knock us off our precarious perch atop all of our stuff.

Through internalizing this timeless message of the Torah, we can learn to be happier, calmer, and more fulfilled people. We can achieve true freedom of the spirit, free from the unattainable pursuit of stuff that has decimated our society. We can live a life of unadulterated and uninterrupted joy that needs no outside fuel and is completely self-sufficient.
