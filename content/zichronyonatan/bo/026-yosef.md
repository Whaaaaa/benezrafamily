Each Passover we eat only matzoh, in commemoration of the unleavened bread the Jews ate leaving Egypt. But why the mad rush -- why couldn't they have waited a few minutes for the bread to rise? Rabbi Simcha Z. Ziv answers that this was to teach a lesson about freedom itself.

One might think freedom means indulging every desire -- chasing money, comfort, pleasure, believing that more possessions will finally fill the void inside. This is the driving force of Western society. At the outset of their free lives, G-d taught the Jews that this is not what freedom means. True happiness comes not from the fulfillment of our impulses but from rising above them, learning to find joy in what we already have. "Who is truly wealthy? One who is happy with his share."

Rabbi Yeruchom Levovitz explains that true happiness comes from menuchat hanefesh, peace of mind -- not menuchat haguf, physical comfort. Consider a soldier's training: if the goal were physical comfort, boot camp would be luxurious. Instead it strips away every comfort, teaching the soldier to find equanimity that does not depend on external circumstances. As long as our happiness depends on physical crutches, we remain crippled by them.

Through internalizing this message, we can become happier, calmer, more fulfilled people -- free from the unattainable pursuit of "stuff" that has decimated our society.
