(Based on the works of Rashi, The Rambam, and the Ramchal)

Last week’s Parsha concluded with the Plague of Hail, which demolished almost every field left in the Land of Egypt. I say almost because there was one crop left unharmed. The wheat crop, which bent instead of breaking at the impact of the hail, was left intact.

The Egyptians were strangely triumphant about the survival of their amber waves of grains. True, Egypt was largely in ruins, but the wheat not having succumbed to the previous plague gave them a sliver of hope.

Perhaps the G-d of the Jews was not quite so powerful after all? If He had intended on wiping out their last form of nourishment, He failed. (Remember, this is after every fish was drowned by blood, all the livestock had perished by plague, and all other forms of produce had been wiped out through fiery hail.)

To any sane person, the question seems ludacris. How could one possibly doubt the power of G-d, who was clearly intent on reducing the Egyption dynasty to ruins and leading His Chosen People to redemption?

But such is the power of disbelief and dismissal.

Every miracle performed by G-d, either directly or through a messenger, is only to communicate something. Once it has been witnessed it imposes obligation upon those that acknowledge it. In this case G-d was demonstrating His existence and power over and over again.

The Egyptians were avid idol worshipers, as well as scientists, astrologers, and even magicians. With the arrival of each plague, they attempted to explain it away.

When all the water in the Nile had turned to blood, Pharoah laughed at Moshe and said, “So you’re a magician! Anyone can turn water to blood!”

He then announced that he could prove it. Pharaoh summoned his cabinet of magicians and instructed them to turn water into blood. There was just one problem. All the water the Egyptians possessed was already blood. Slightly embarrassed, but left with no other option, he asked Moshe’s brother Aaron if he could BUY a cup of water from him.

That’s right. Pharaoh had to pay a Jew for a cup of water to turn into blood, only to prove that turning the Nile River red was not a miracle but an elaborate magic trick.

The irony was completely lost on him.

During the following plague, the infestation of frogs, Pharaoh demands to talk to Moshe. He pleads that the plague commence, as the frogs (and alligators, according to many sources) were ravaging Egypt.

(It’s interesting to note that Pharaoh once again demanded that his sorcerers duplicate this feat. They delivered, filling the Royal Chambers with, you guessed it, MORE FROGS!)

But once Moshe is in his chambers, Pharaoh can’t help himself not to test the authenticity of the plague’s source. Moshe offers that he can end the plague at any time; Pharaoh could choose the exact minute. Instead of the obvious answer: “This second!”, Pharaoh speculates a moment and says “Tomorrow.” The king of a country that was becoming the laughingstock of the inhabited world was willing to endure the suffering for another day, just to test if Moshe could actually do it.

The night before the invasion of the locust, the Torah mentions that an Eastern Wind blew all night. The Egyptians had an explanation too easily handed to them. Why even mention it?

It’s quite simple actually: to not rob them of their free will. Yes, it was highly unlikely that the wind caused the (prewarned) plague, but it did give the obstinate a way to explain the occurrence.

Let’s skip to the last Plague now, The Death of the Firstborn. G-d tells Moshe to warn Pharaoh that at midnight, a loud scream would rise from every home as the Oldest Child would fall dead.

When Moshe transmits the message he changes the wording slightly. Instead of announcing the event would take place AT midnight, he tells Pharaoh that it would happen “Around midnight.”

Why the change in verbiage?

Moshe knew that the Egyptians were very advanced in astronomy, and they would be waiting, calculating. If the timing didn’t match up perfectly, Pharaoh would proclaim that the Plague was not caused by G-d.

The wail was heard at EXACTLY midnight.

There were no loopholes left. No way to explain this one. So indeed, Pharaoh ran around the streets in his night robes, searching for Moshe, insisting that the Jews leave Egypt at once.

But here’s the thing about the impact of miracles: it doesn’t last very long. If Pharaoh had just allowed the Jews to freedom, he might have had a chance to rebuild and restore what was left of a once flourishing country.

As we know, he just couldn’t let it go. Three days later he was strapping his own horse to his wagon to chase down the Jews, and we all know how that ended. Pharaoh followed them directly into the Sea of Reeds where he met his gruesome end as the walls of water came crashing down, drowning him and his army, sealing their fate as a footnote in history.

What can we learn from Pharaoh’s behavior, (aside from not murdering, enslaving, and torturing people, obviously)?

There were so many miracles, so many chances to just BELIEVE that there is a G-d, Something greater than himself. He chose to ignore, explain, and defy. It was right in front of him, but he could not bring himself to admit it.

We see miracles every day. Some are as simple and expected as opening our eyes in the morning, and some are dramatic and life-altering, like missing a plane that crashes into a skyscraper.

How do we react? Do we, in fact, alter our lives? Or do we say “That’s amazing, that’s incredible, unbelievable!” and then go back to routine completely unchanged.

Only forty days after leaving Egypt, the Jews were presented with the Torah. Why so fast? What was the rush?

G-d knows how fast the effects of a miracle wear off. He knew that if we didn’t act on it, we didn’t commit to keeping the commandments immediately, our awe would also fade.

When something happens that gives us a wake-up call, we need to take something upon ourselves, resolve to change our lives in a tangible way. Otherwise, the jubilation dies down and we are the same as we were before.

This Sunday, Adam returned from a trip to Israel, where he took college students to explore their Heritage. At the end of the trip, the students were truly moved and inspired. But inspiration fades, so what did Adam do? He asked each person to make a commitment, something tangible so they would truly gain from the experience.

This is a trait I’ve witnessed in our Dear Uncle Yonatan Z’L (Of Blessed Memory). Many people see things that have the potential to change them, but they remain complacent, satisfied with who they are.

Uncle Yonatan was not one of those people. He was always working on himself, wanting to change and better himself. He saw signs and used them as directions. He brought an entire family back to their roots, simply by ploughing ahead, no matter what obstacles he faced.

The measure of a man is not what he is, but how hard and how far he worked to get there. Uncle Yonatan was constantly examining himself, reaching for greatness, aiming for higher and higher moral ground.

I can safely say that when he left this world, he was the closest thing we knew to an Angel. We feel him looking down at us, and he inspires us to look upward, to become as great as he always knew we could be.
