Egypt lays in ruins still smoldering. The Israelites are teetering on the brink of freedom and nationhood. Moshe has already warned Pharaoh about the impending plague. Right before the Israelites receive their exodus instructions there is an unexpected addition.

הַחֹ֧דֶשׁ הַזֶּ֛ה לָכֶ֖ם רֹ֣אשׁ חֳדָשִׁ֑ים רִאשׁ֥וֹן הוּא֙ לָכֶ֔ם לְחָדְשֵׁ֖י הַשָּׁנָֽה׃

This month shall mark for you the beginning of the months; it shall be the first of the months of the year for you.

This unassuming verse receives the distinction of containing the first mitzvah in the Torah, to sanctify the new moon. In the midst of our fundamental national drama, why is our first commandment as a nation so tangential to the story we are telling? Instead, let us start with the korban Pesach, or the instruction to memorialize this saga.

The Seforno explains that slaves in Egypt had no control over their time and, consequently, over their lives. They were worked to exhaustion, and always subject to having still more demands made on their time. The central benefit of achieving freedom and independence would be the control they could exercise over time. Only once they had achieved this measure of autonomy could they then decide what to devote their time to.

The Netziv takes a slightly different approach. He explains that Rosh Chodesh came first because it differentiated the Israelites from the rest of the world. While all life had been oriented around a solar calendar, the first commandment would be to break that mold. To have a new national calendar that could contain unique holidays and symbolism, it had to be governed by a different celestial body.

This whole crisis has made time act funny. Since the very first lockdown people have been mourning the loss of circadian rhythm. Confusing a Tuesday with a Thursday has gone from a concerning sign of detachment to a common occurrence. This daily struggle has been compounded by the loss of large familial celebrations. Days have blurred into weeks and weeks into seasons without our unique temporal landmarks guiding the way around the year.

That's why this first, fundamental mitzvah is more relevant for me today than ever. To come close to God, to better myself, to achieve anything, I need to reclaim autonomy over my time. I can set better boundaries in the "working from home" arrangement that has morphed into "living at work." I can keep to a healthy routine despite having nowhere to go and no one to see. And, I can instill celebratory occasions with extra meaning despite the distance. As we see from this week's parsha, these changes are the foundation on which our national identity is built.
