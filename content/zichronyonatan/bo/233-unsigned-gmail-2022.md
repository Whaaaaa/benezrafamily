In this week's parsha we have the dramatic final meeting between Moshe and Pharaoh. Amidst the plague of darkness, Pharaoh calls Moshe and Aaron seemingly to acquiesce to their demand for a spiritual retreat. Pharaoh finally agrees to allow women and children to attend, only demanding that the livestock remain. Moshe presses Pharaoh further telling him that not only would the Jews be taking their cattle but that, "[Pharaoh] You yourself must provide us with sacrifices and burnt offerings to offer up to the LORD our God." This is too much for Pharaoh to relent. "Pharaoh said to him, "Be gone from me! Take care not to see me again, for the moment you look upon my face you shall die."" Moshe confirms that this will be their last meeting and warns Pharaoh about the ultimate plague still to come and the devastation that will be wrought on Egypt. Finally, Moshe reveals how the exodus will finally begin; "Then all these courtiers of yours [Pharaoh] shall come down to me and bow low to me, saying, 'Depart, you and all the people who follow you!' After that I will depart." And he left Pharaoh's presence in hot anger."

This is one of the most contentious interactions in the entire Torah. Here are two leaders of opposing factions who are fiercely committed to achieving opposite ends. Moshe grew up in Pharaoh's home and sacrificed everything to become the leader of a ragtag nation of slaves. Pharaoh has already sacrificed his nation's infrastructure, livestock, and sanity through his continued obstinance despite horrific plagues. Each has put everything on the line and only now at this apex, do we see that this elicits an emotional reaction from Moshe; hot anger.

In this moment of confrontation, Moshe is setting an eternal example of how we deal with evil authority. We are a people passionate about pursuing justice and this means having respect for authority while also being able to confront it when it runs counter to our sense of morality. Moshe's actions form the archetype for all of Jewish resistance and as such, it is no surprise it is the subject of much debate.

Rashi comments on Moshe's final words to Pharaoh based on a Gemara in Menachos as follows:

He showed respect to the king, for as a matter of fact ultimately Pharaoh himself came down to him at night, (Exodus 12:31) "And he said, Arise, go out from the midst of my people"; but Moses did not at the outset say to him, "And thou shalt come down to me and shalt prostrate thyself", out of respect for the king, (Exodus Rabbah 7:3)

Rashi points out that even in this time of great emotion, Moshe kept a level head and continued to respect the authority of the king. He deliberately chose less provocative language to accord respect to the throne.

This interpretation is significantly different than that of Reish Lakish as expressed in Zevachim:

And is it true that for every burning anger that is stated in the Torah, its effect is also stated? But isn't it written with regard to Moses: "And he went out from Pharaoh in hot anger" (Exodus 11:8)? And Moses did not say anything to Pharaoh. Reish Lakish said: Moses slapped him and left.

The Gemara is trying to defend the assertion that whenever the Torah describes an emotional state of "hot anger" there are explicitly mentioned consequences. Our episode seems to contradict this rule as there is no explicit consequence to Moshe's anger. However, according to Reish Lakish Moshe did not simply walk away from the interaction outwardly unchanged by his emotional state. He slapped Pharaoh!

This is a very different picture than what is painted by the Gemara Rashi quotes. Ben Yehoyada finds a third route, a middle ground between these extremes.

He explains that Reish Lakish could not mean a literal slap in the face as this would contradict the principles of respect for authority we have already established. Rather, Moshe would regularly leave his meeting with Pharaoh in a deferential way, without ever turning his head away from the thone. At the end of this meeting though, Moshe slightly turned away from Pharaoh while exiting, never fully facing his back to him but turning enough that it was a noticeable slight.

These three opinions of Moshe's resistance represent a wide range of reactions that are justifiable in the face of bad authority. Sometimes like the Gemara quoted by Rashi, we need to remain humble and respectfully communicate our demands. Other times, we are forced to take it a step further and violate a social norm to make clear that we cannot fully recognize the authority of a despot. And sometimes, given the opportunity, we have to slap a tyrant.
