I once heard a story of a seemingly regular older woman who was walking with her grandchild when suddenly a car spun out of control and trapped the child underneath. With her precious grandchild's life on the line, she acted out of desperation and attempted the seemingly futile task of lifting the car off of the child. And, as if by a miracle, the impossible happened and she succeeded in saving the child.

Perhaps most remarkable, though, was the postscript of the story. She was shaken to the core by the latent power which she had witnessed in herself and had never known about. After realizing how much she could accomplish, she decided to achieve something else which she thought she never could. She went back to college, and finally got the masters she had always dreamed of.

From where did she draw such super-human strength during the car accident? How could it be that someone who just yesterday struggled to take out her garbage was now able to move thousands of pounds? And what did she learn which finally empowered her to chase and capture her dreams?

And I think we all know that this is not an isolated story, but one that has repeated itself, in different ways, too many times to count. People acting out of desperation find within themselves capacities, whether physical, intellectual, or emotional, that are all but endless. Where does it come from?

When man was created, in this week's Parshah, the Torah writes, "And He blew into man a living soul." The Zohar comments on this that "one who blows, blows from within himself", meaning that every soul is a piece of G-d Himself.

The words of Rabbi Zevi Trenk ring in my ears as he described the incredible import of this Zohar. "If you say you can't, you're saying He can't!" Because our soul is no less than a piece of infinity, nothing short of godliness, we are nearly limitless in our true capacities. We need only to believe in our greatness to unlock all the limitless potential that lies within.

To further illustrate, there is a prayer which we say every morning which begins with the declaration, "The soul which You placed within me is completely pure." Rabbi Shimon Schwab points out that even if one were to commit the worst sin, he would still wake up the next morning and be expected to recite this prayer. The soul which You placed within me is totally pure. What does that mean and how can it be recited across the board by every living person? Has he not tainted his soul through his misdeeds? The answer, says Rabbi Schwab, is the same. Our soul is hewn from the loftiest heavens, from a place so holy and otherworldly that it is untaintable from the physical realm. Its loftiness doesn't allow it to ever lose that purity, no matter how far one strays.

If we would only recognize the greatness of the human spirit, a greatness so immensely powerful and godly, we could achieve anything. So let us believe in ourselves, believe in our godly spark, and believe in the unconditional and pristine purity of our souls. Let us never sell ourselves short or let past failure stunt our forward growth. Let us achieve the impossible.

Good Shabbos,
Yosef
