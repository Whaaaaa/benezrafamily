We stand at an impossibly dark hour for our people. An hour of boundless pain and suffering, of tremendous fear, of unspeakable atrocities. The only thing that flows more copiously than the rivers of Jewish blood, is the oceans of Jewish tears.

I heard the question asked, how could it be that God granted such uncanny success to a party of such depravity and evil? One would have to be blind and deaf to think that such a bloodbath was the product of Hamas planning and IDF error. Not one part of the story makes any sense by normal human terms. So how could the Kind and Compassionate One grant these savages the divine assistance necessary to carry out their horrible intentions?

And the answer is that Hashem didn't help them. He sent them.

And if Hashem sent them, then we know and believe that behind all the darkness lies a light at the end of the tunnel. A light that is not in spite of the darkness, but which will be born of the darkness itself.

Yes, it is a dark, painful, and terrifying hour -- but it is not an hour of despair. The darkest part of night is the moment preceding dawn, and the shadows bring with them the hope for daybreak.

So why the darkness? Why must we endure such unbearable pain?

We don't know the ways of Hashem, and we can't profess to pinpoint the answer to that difficult question. But, in a general sense, we can look towards history for a frame of reference.

In Egypt, the Jews suffered terribly for generations, but somehow they held out and would have been there for the entire 400-year sentence foretold to Avraham. Until Pharaoh contracted leprosy and began to bathe daily in the blood of our children! This tragedy was too much to bear, and the Jewish people let out an unprecedented cry -- a wail that rent the heavens. And, as that darkest moment brought us to such a cry, Hashem began the process of redemption.

Then, as Moshe reappeared on the scene bearing the message of salvation, the people were relieved. But, as he approached Pharaoh, the situation only grew worse, with Pharaoh refusing to supply the people with straw for their bricks. It was an impossible task, and again we screamed out, this time with the prayer that heralded in the beginning of the ten makos.

And this, says Reb Tzadok (Tzidkas Hatzaddik ch. 151), is the process by which Hashem brings the blessing and bounty of rain. Rain is the magnificent, life-giving phenomenon that sustains the entire planet, yet it is always prefaced and produced by dark and threatening clouds. Why? Reb Tzadok explains that when we need the rain and we don't quite have the merits by which to deserve it, Hashem prepares the antidote with the darkening storm clouds. As the darkness thickens and the thunder begins to roar, we turn our eyes heavenward and are compelled to cry out to Hashem and to hope for His mercy, and thereby we merit the rain.

Now too, as we grope in these darkest of times, we can find solace in the knowledge that it is brought upon us by none other than our Loving Father. We can easily see past all the ridiculous political rhetoric that tries to view these unnatural events through an atheistic lens. We can choose to see the dawn about to break, and we can indeed merit that salvation if we truly feel the pain of our brothers and seize the moment to cry our hearts out to Hashem. And let's not think small, about returning to life as usual, but rather about the ultimate salvation with the coming of Moshiach speedily in our days.

Good Shabbos and may we hear only good news,

Yosef
