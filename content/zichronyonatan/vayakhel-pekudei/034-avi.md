In this week's double Parsha, Moshe instructs Bnai Yisrael to donate materials for the Mishkan. Enthused after their sin with the calf, the people give generously -- jewelry, dyed wool, fine tapestries. When women wish to donate their bronze mirrors, Moshe initially refuses, seeing them as vain and immodest for a holy sanctuary. G-d corrects him: those very mirrors had been used by exhausted women in Egyptian slavery to remain attractive to their equally exhausted husbands, sustaining Jewish family life under the harshest conditions -- and this sanctified them as holier than nearly any other gift.

Separately, Pekudei ("amounts of") records a full public accounting of every metal donated to the project -- not just to give credit where due, but as a mark of transparency, so that no Jew could suspect fraud or corruption in the handling of communal resources. In Judaism we are obligated to act beyond reproach -- the Kohen Gadol's garments have no pockets, and he must speak continuously in the Holy of Holies so no one could suspect him of hiding coins in his mouth. Our commandment to live beyond reproach touches not just our division of resources, but our spirituality: it is best to avoid anything that may even look forbidden. Moshe's transparent accounting exemplifies the standard of transparency a Jew should strive for in all things.
