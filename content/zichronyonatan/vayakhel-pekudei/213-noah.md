Vayakhel dives into the donations made to the mishkan. Each segment of Israelite society is represented, contributing materials and labor to the house for Hashem. The Torah goes into great detail about this process, with each nuance carrying messages about the attitudes and procedures that make for a great fundraising campaign.

Moshe first gathers, “the entire assembly of Israel.” The Or Hachaim points out that this should be obvious. Every time Moshe relayed a commandment from Hashem he would gather the people, why is this assemblage specifically mentioned here? He answers that these, “words included orphans, minors, women, etc. Moses actually used two expressions of amplification, i.e. את כל. The reason Moses used only two amplifying statements here, and not a third one representing the wealthy is simple. There was no reason why the wealthy person would not come even if not especially invited seeing he would be happy to be part of the community.”

The invitation to the first fundraising dinner wasn't sent to the big machers in the community, as any wealthy and involved community member would come out of a sense of communal responsibility. Explicitly opening up the event to the segments of society that were dispossessed of material wealth set a tone of inclusion.

Next Moshe begins his entreaty, ‘Take from among you gifts to the LORD; everyone whose heart so moves him shall bring them—gifts of the LORD: gold, silver, and copper”

קְח֨וּ מֵֽאִתְּכֶ֤ם תְּרוּמָה֙ לַֽיהוָ֔ה כֹּ֚ל נְדִ֣יב לִבּ֔וֹ יְבִיאֶ֕הָ אֵ֖ת תְּרוּמַ֣ת יְהוָ֑ה זָהָ֥ב וָכֶ֖סֶף וּנְחֹֽשֶׁת׃

The Chasam Sofer points out a difference in the phrases “gifts to the Lord” and “gifts of the Lord.” He explains the slight discrepancy is meant to point out what the true gift is. The first gift is not referring to any tangible object but rather the willingness and generosity of spirit. This is the only gift we can give to Hashem. The gold and silver were created and given to man by god, these are already gifts of Hashem. This bifurcated message highlighting the value of generosity while also reminding people that their wealth is not solely a product of their achievement, fosters a sense of responsibility without diminishing the greatness of giving.

Furthermore, the choice of words, “take from among you” are famously expounded to be taken literally, as one who gives from their heart is considered as though they sacrifice themselves on the altar.

וי"א מאתכם ממש כי הנותן מנדבת לבו דומה כאילו הקריב את גופו (כלי יקר)

Moshe here is recognizing the sacrifice that comes with giving. This further tempers the implication mentioned above that our material wealth is not our true gift to God. Sure, I didn’t earn everything through sweat and determination alone but that doesn’t make giving it up any easier. Moshe’s recognition of this sentiment makes his appeal even more sincere and resonant.

Moshe now goes on to list the different recognition levels. Gold donor status, Silver donor status etc.. No surprises to anyone who has seen the wall of plaques adorning every Jewish institution. The Or Hachaim notices a detail in the list of membership levels that drastically changes this tiered system we are so familiar with.

“The paragraph begins by listing contributions for the construction of the Tabernacle, listing thirteen different materials and joining each one to the other with the conjunctive letter ו to show that each of these materials was of equal importance in the construction of the Tabernacle and was equally indispensable. All of these materials could be subsumed under the heading תרומת השם. The Torah considered someone who had contributed copper as on the same level as someone who had contributed gold, for instance. According to Menachot 110 the Torah describes the person who offered a bird, as a total offering as having offered a ריח ניחוח, no less so than a person who offered a four-legged mammal as an עולה, a total offering. The evaluation is based on the principle אחד המרבה ואחד ההמעיט, that the quantity of a contribution does not determine how G'd evaluates it but the intent does. As long as the intent is to please Heaven that is all that matters.”

While we might look at the top row of big plaques and be impressed by a surely sizable donation, the Torah doesn’t measure value this way. Moshe again acknowledges mortal perspective by listing the items in level of material value but he counters this with a clear message that the true measure of a contribution is the intent.

The work of fundraising is often the trickiest part of community building. Drastically different perspectives on and access to wealth make it a fraught subject. In our parsha, Moshe lays the groundwork for a successful campaign. His invitation encourages inclusivity. His messaging stresses the responsibility to give without detracting from the goodness of giving or the sacrifice that comes with it. Moshe even acknowledges value from a material perspective while reinforcing that intent is the true gift. These principles are a roadmap for financing any dwelling place for hashem and form the basis for our perspective on giving.
