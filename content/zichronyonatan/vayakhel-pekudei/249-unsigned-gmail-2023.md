In light of recent events that have transpired in our hometown of Los Angeles, I feel compelled to break from our usual forum and discuss something not necessarily related to the Parsha. Over the past few weeks, we have been struck by blow after devastating blow. First a young mother in her forties, Mrs. Sharon Shenker a"h, succumbed to cancer. Then last week, Mr. Chaim Elefant a"h, a father in his thirties, was tragically killed by a heart attack while aboard an airplane. And, just this past Shabbos, Dr. Julian Mirman a"h, was hit and killed by a car while walking home on Friday night. And there are yet others among us still battling fiercely for their lives (may they have a speedy and complete recovery).

We are reeling – shocked, battered, and bruised. Hashem is shaking us, begging us to wake up, but often we grope in the darkness, unsure where to begin. What is wanted from us? How can we ensure that these tragedies should not go to waste, but should leave us changed for the better?

Of course, every individual must search to find his own personal message; the tailor-made call for self-improvement that is unique to oneself. But there is a general message which must hit each and every one of us sharply between the eyes:

Life is short. Life is precious. And we dare not let life pass us by.

Every morning, a Jew awakes with the declaration of Modeh Ani on his lips: "I give thanks before you, Living and Eternal King, for mercifully returning my soul to me. How great is your trustfulness."

With this, we acknowledge that our soul was entrusted to Hashem as we slept, and that it is He who has returned it to us. Furthermore, we state that we did not deserve another day – it was granted to us just because of His abundant mercy. This means that although we did not live the previous day correctly, He has still given us another chance to achieve and become meritorious.

In light of this statement, that we did not deserve to have our souls returned but rather received them as a charity, the final sentence is perplexing. "How great is Your trustfulness." Simply understood, this is a reference to the the fact that Hashem has returned what is ours and is thereby described as a trustworthy watchman, as it were. But what does this have to do with trust? We didn't deserve it back anyway, so isn't this more a display of His kindliness rather than His trustfulness?

I believe the answer is that we are not referring to the degree that we can trust Him, but to the degree that He places trust in us. We realize that yesterday, and the day before, and the day before that, we failed in our mission. We don't deserve to wake up in the morning and yet everyone reading this did wake up. He gives us second chance after second chance for years. Long after we have given up hope in ourselves, He keeps on trusting. He trusts that today we'll be different. Today we'll turn our lives around. Today we'll finally begin to place proper ideals before our short-term wants and desires. Today will finally be a day deserving of another.

As soon as we wake up, before we even get out of bed, we take note of Hashem's great trust in us, and we are empowered to regain trust in ourselves as well. With the Almighty's own vote of confidence, it dawns on us that perhaps we do have it in us. And, hopefully, we resolve not to let Him down, but to really attain quantum change - today. After all, Hashem believes in us.

My friends, we have lost so much. The best among us have been taken away. But – today – we are alive. We have been trusted with one more day.

So let us realize the limitless potential in each day of life. Let us remember the trust Hashem places in us. And let us make this the day that we start living up to His trust.

We have a long way to go, but we must start today. Life is just too short to wait for tomorrow.

Good Shabbos and may we only hear good news.
