Through Vaykira I feel it can be difficult to connect with the parsha. The stories disappear and are replaced with minutiae about the mishkan. It can be difficult to understand the deeper meaning behind these details and to even connect to the broader themes. Why does the Torah go on at such length to describe these processes and repeat them many times?

The Ramban answers this question in this week's parsha by referencing the Midrash's conversation about the story of Eliezer, "Rabbi Acha said: The ordinary conversation of the patriarchs' servants is more pleasing to God than even the Torah of their children, for the chapter of Eliezer is repeated in the Torah whereas many important principles of the Law are derived only from slight indications given in the Text."

Using this same principle, the Ramban points out how valuable to Hashem our work must be, as the work of the Mishkan is repeated five times. Additionally, he extols the wisdom and intuition of the craftsmen who were not given exact blueprints by Moshe but relied upon to make aesthetic and structural decisions based on their own acumen.

The Ramban's commendation praising the virtue of labor is not limited to the physical. He explains that the verses are repeated due to Hashem's deep appreciation for those who toil intellectually. They provide more opportunities for them to earn merit for their study.

This lesson is closely mirrored in pirkei avot, where the value of our physical efforts and torah study are spelled out explicitly:

"Rabban Gamaliel the son of Rabbi Judah Hanasi said: excellent is the study of the Torah when combined with a worldly occupation, for toil in them both keeps sin out of one's mind; But [study of the] Torah which is not combined with a worldly occupation, in the end comes to be neglected and becomes the cause of sin. And all who labor with the community, should labor with them for the sake Heaven, for the merit of their forefathers sustains them (the community), and their (the forefather's) righteousness endures for ever; And as for you, [God in such case says] I credit you with a rich reward, as if you [yourselves] had [actually] accomplished [it all]."

Here we can see the unique union between these two concepts. Having ambition in our labor and Torah study keeps us occupied and gives us ways of giving back to the community. It is the bedrock of productive life and comes with the ultimate reward of knowing that we helped accomplish it all.

This lesson from the Mishkan and its repetitive details seems far from trivial in this perspective. It is an exhaustive reminder of the definition of a productive and accomplished life. The emphasis on labor is a reminder to occupy ourselves with learning new skills and abilities and to use them to give back. And the repetition is a reminder that Hashem values our Torah study immensely and gives us ample opportunities to engage with it. These lessons can help imbue a once difficult set of parshiot with a new perspective and remind us to use these weeks to calibrate our sense of accomplishment.
