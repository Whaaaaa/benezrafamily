This Shabbos, the Shabbos immediately preceding Pesach, is called Shabbos HaGadol, or the great Shabbos, because of the miracle that took place on it. On the tenth of the month of Nissan, which happened to fall out on Shabbos, the Jews all tied sheep to their bedposts with the express intent of slaughtering them for the pascal offering four days later. They were commanded to take the sheep openly before the eyes of the Egyptians, and this despite the fact that the Egyptians worshiped sheep. When the Egyptians would ask what the Jews were doing with their precious deity, the Jews unabashedly and proudly responded that they intended to slaughter them for Hashem. In any natural setting, the response for such a blasphemous and audacious statement would have been immediate death, especially when perpetrated by a lowly slave whose life was valued at only a few dollars. Even so, the Jewish people courageously heeded the command of Hashem, and, with perfect trust, put their lives in the hands of
G-d. Miraculously, the Egyptians were completely tongue-tied and hand-strung, unable even to tell the Jews off and certainly unable to raise a finger against them.

Much has been said to explain why this miracle, more than all the myriad miracles of the exodus was singled out to be commemorated [see Zichron Yonatan #85], but we'd like to focus on another point. All the holidays of the Jewish calendar are set by the day of the month, independent of the day of the week. Why then was Shabbos Hagadol alone decided to be commemorated on the day of Shabbos, instead of on the tenth of Nissan?

The Meshech Chochmah (Devarim 10:20) answers with another question about Shabbos in general. Shabbos commemorates the creation of the world in its entirety. This, then, is something that should be uniform to all the nations of the world, and not specifically to the Jewish people. Yet we find that, while a non-Jew is welcome to perform any of the mitzvot, he is absolutely forbidden to keep Shabbos. This needs a serious explanation – what is the Jewish monopoly on Shabbos, which celebrates not a national event, but a global one?

He explains that Shabbos is not just about refraining from the thirty-nine categories of forbidden work – it is about resting and rejoicing in abstinence. Here in America, we are all used to the concept of a weekend, and generally speaking, even if one isn't keeping Shabbos, they probably still aren't going to work. But, back in the day, people worked every day of the week, often bringing home just enough for the day's expenses. Under such circumstances, it was quite the challenge to put away one's hammer one out of seven days. Even if one would have the fortitude to take the plunge, it would be very hard to enjoy and rejoice in the holy day, instead of biting one's nails with worry about his livelihood.

What then is the key to achieving the true aura of Shabbos, and the happiness that it is meant to contain? Trust in Hashem. The only one who truly believes that G-d is the One who provides sustenance is able to put his worries in the hands of his Creator and rejoice as his store is shuttered.

The Meshech Chochmah likens one who trusts in Hashem to a servant of the king. He lives in the palace and eats from the royal kitchen – with all his needs met, he has nothing to worry about and no personal interest in a day's work. Such a servant is the happiest man when he gets the day off because it costs him nothing and he knows he'll eat just the same either way. Not so your average working man for whom vacation carries the price tag of a day's wages.

The one who trusts in Hashem is like the servant. He knows that all his needs are met by the Almighty and he only works because Hashem decreed that he must. When Hashem gives him 'the day off' with the onset of Shabbos, he knows that the vacation carries no price tag and is able to rest with a light heart in the knowledge that he will lack nothing thereby. Only such a person is truly able to connect to the power of Shabbos.

In this vein, we can understand why Shabbos HaGadol is specifically set for Shabbos and not merely for the calendar date of the event in history. When the Jewish people dragged their masters deity through the streets to the slaughter, they achieved a paramount level of trust in Hashem, and from here they continued to capitalize and build on their achievement. They soon plunged headfirst into the sea even before it split and followed Hashem into the desert with no source of sustenance, but it all began with the act of Shabbos HaGadol.

It was this level of trust which ultimately primed the Jewish people and made them capable of receiving the gift of Shabbos. The trust which they attained on Shabbos HaGadol set them apart from all the nations of the world, making them and them alone the proper recipients of the Shabbos. Because this Shabbos miracle was the event that earned them the reward of Shabbos for all time, it was befitting for the commemoration of the event to be reserved for Shabbos every single year.

This Shabbos HaGadol, let us proudly and unabashedly denounce all of the fickle things in which we trust, realizing that Hashem alone is the One who provides. And as we rest on Shabbos, let us realize that it's a paid vacation, since our needs are met from the treasury of the King.

Good Shabbos,

Yosef
