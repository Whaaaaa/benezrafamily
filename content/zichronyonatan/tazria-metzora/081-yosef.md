One of the many pleasures of life as an orthodox Jew is the bounty of chessed, or loving-kindness, which goes on amongst our ranks. If you take out the local phone book in Lakewood, New Jersey, or any other large Jewish community, you will find *hundreds* (no exaggeration) of 'Gemachs'. These are mostly small, often private, organizations looking to meet some need or other for zero profit and even at a loss. There are stroller gemachs and gown gemachs, GPS gemachs and chair gemachs, and some you would never think of like the chicken soup gemach. Not to mention the large scale chessed organizations, such as RCCS holding every cancer patient's hand, Bonei Olam helping with infertility, and Tomchei Shabbos providing food and other necessities to anyone that needs, to list just a few. The chessed of the Jewish people is unbelievable and unrivaled anywhere in the world. If you ever need a boost of Jewish pride, I invite you to open up such a phone book and be wowed by the greatness of all the 'ordinary' members of our people.

Another facet of this nation-wide dedication to chessed is the mitzvah of 'hachnossos orchim', or welcoming guests. When visiting any orthodox Jewish community across the globe, you need just to make a phone call or two and you will find plenty of hosts who are overjoyed to welcome complete strangers in their homes. Many have set aside special guest suites just for such an opportunity, while others will happily rearrange their own family's sleeping quarters in order to make room for a guest. In the chassidic neighoborhood of New Square, New York, there is actually a community-wide rule that one must have a guest room in their house. This means that large families with only a few bedrooms have made the mitzvah of hachnossos orchim such a priority as to put their guests comfort before their own.

In this weeks Parshah, we learn about the miraculous plague of 'tzaraas', which is loosely translated as leprosy but in reality was a divine sickness unrelated to the medical phenomenon of leprosy. Such ailments were not only exhibited by people, but actually came upon clothes and houses as well.

When introducing us to tzaraas of the house, the Torah uses peculiar wording. *'When you will come to the land of Canaan which* **I** *am giving you as a stronghold, and I will place an affliction of tzaraas in the house of* **your** *stronghold. And the one whose house* **is his** *will come to the priest etc.'*

The Kli Yakar asks why the Torah first states that is is a stronghold which Hashem has given, and then immediately afterwards refers to it as none other than the man's own possession. Also, the wording in the last verse, which refers to him as the 'one whose house is his' is quite strange.

He explains according to the Talmud (Erchin 16a) which learns from this last verse that the cause of tzaraas in a house can be linked to stinginess. 'The one whose house is his' means that he has set aside his house for himself alone; he doesn't benefit others with his home. With this, the Kli Yakar explains the previous verse which is coming to highlight the folly of one who is stingy. It says that when Hashem will take you into the land, and He will provide you with a house to live in. Make no mistake, says the Torah, your house and all of the bounty which you enjoy is not thanks to you. Don't think these are your just deserts, which might entitle you to hoard things for yourself -- this is the blessing of G-d.

But if one doesn't learn this message, but rather views his home and his possessions as his alone, he's in for a surprise. Hashem says that He will bring tzaraas upon 'your stronghold', meaning the home which he views as his own. If he really thinks this is his, and he uses such a thought process to justify his stinginess, then he'll have to learn the lesson the hard way. If he doesn't shape up, he stands to lose the house entirely in face of the tzaraas.

Today, we are not privy to such an overt show of divine intervention, but the lesson of tzaraas is a timeless as ever. The people of the Torah do not own things for their personal purposes alone, and they do not view such things as their personal lot. The Torah nation lives for others and any blessing which they enjoy is viewed as an opportunity to share it with others. May we truly live up to the gold standard of our people, find pride and pleasure in sharing our homes and our hearts with others, and merit the continued benevolence of He who has given us everything.
