In olden times, when the Jewish people as a whole were on an exceedingly high level and G-d's presence was not so concealed, there was a miraculous illness called tzara'as [loosely translated as leprosy]. This illness was not a product of medical stimuli but rather was brought directly by the hand of Hashem upon one who spoke ill of his fellowman. Likewise, the healing of such an ailment could not be achieved through medical means, but only through repentance and a Torah-prescribed purification process. The focal point of the leper's purification process was his exile outside of the camp -- during the entire period of leprosy, he was not allowed contact with any other people. Quarantined.

In explanation of this punishment, the sages explain that since he separated people through his venomous words, by sowing hate and bad feelings, he is therefore forced to remain separate himself. Only once he feels the pain of being alone, of being shunned and unwanted, is he able to see the folly of his ways, correct his path, and return to society.

The correlation between tzara'as and coronavirus is uncanny in two regards. The first is the degree to which the symptoms of corona seem to be arbitrary from a medical standpoint. Yes, the virus has a medical term and can be viewed under a microscope, but the way it affects different people is so varied and unexpected that it's hard to deny the hand of Hashem. Why is it that when two people with similar statistics both contract the disease one is on the brink of death and the other isn't even sure he's sick?

The second similarity is the prescribed way to combat the disease -- quarantine. Just as the leper was forced to isolate himself, so must we all be isolated to keep the virus at bay.

In light of these speculations, Rabbi Chaim Kanievsky, largely regarded as the greatest Torah sage of our generation, has suggested that we all take a lesson from the metzora to our own situation. We must redouble our efforts to bring people together and not to break them apart. To use our words to build and not to destroy. And in the merit of our social caring and connection, may we quickly see an end to social distancing.

And if I may add one more thought along these lines. It strikes me as a funny that the need for social distancing and confinement to our houses has in some ways made us closer. On Tuesday, I effortlessly attended a wedding in Cincinnati from my living room in Los Angeles. Also in attendance were scores of people from New York, New Jersey, Michigan, Pennsylvania, Maryland, Illinois, Georgia, and even Israel. Separated by thousands of miles and each locked in our houses -- yet together through the power of the internet.

We all know the destructive power of the internet to defame, damage, or slander people in front of millions with the touch of a button. But when used properly, look how the same technology has the ability to bridge gaps and span countries. Let us learn from the situation to harness all that power only to draw close and never to set apart, and may we all be truly reunited, in person and in the Holy Land with the coming of Mashiach speedily in our days.

Good Shabbos,

Yosef
