This week's Parsha Tazria focuses on spiritual purity and impurity. The laws of conceiving and giving birth, the laws of Brit Milah and the laws of Tzarrat- a visible affliction of one's skin or belongings. A splotch on one's shell. When we have been deemed impure by a Kohen we must spend time in quarantine, we are allowed back into the community once the splotches have faded. This week's Parsha is heavy on Halacha and there is not much narrative, but back in Bereshit in Parsha Noach, we see the importance of clean speech personified by the Torahs narrative. When God tells Noach to gather animals He differentiates between the kosher "pure" animals and non-kosher animals. Instead of using the terminology found in this week's Parsha "tahor - pure" and "tamei -defiled" the torah records the words tahor twice. Referring to animals that are pure "tahorah" and those that are "einei tahorah - not pure" the Torah spends eight extra letters to teach us the importance of what passes through our lips.

However in this week's Parsha as we deal with the laws of our own spirituality we are stringent and allow the shameful word tamei to be read aloud. This is because as the Mishnah teaches us "all afflictions one sees, except his own." This means that we can't smell our own breath, but are quick to offer mints to those breathing on us. This is not a negative attribute of our existence but rather a great credit to our discernibility because after all we "see all afflictions" many pride themselves on providing sound judgment to others. The Rabbis and life coaches among us have an enormous responsibility.

When a man helps another man with his life and relationships it is like asking a horse to fix a merry go round, they think they can help, but mostly they will get freaked out. This is why when observing things as foreign as a pig we must be careful not to objectify it as disgusting but rather recognize our lack of understanding. When observing yourself, this is the time to be stringent. Our innermost faculties are between us and God and if we have our harsh judgment pointed outward we lose the opportunity to use our innate human discernibility for the ultimate good.

As Jews, it is our constant practice to catch a whiff of our own deepest breath, through constant refinement and spiritual practice it becomes possible for us to help others see the woods from the tree. It is part of our ethical code to hold ourselves to the highest standard and others to the baseline drawn out for us in the Torah and mitzvot we practice. In this manner, we use our highest discernibility for ourselves and when judging others we take a removed stance. When judgment must be made our ethics must be unwavering and not based on our highest personal standards
