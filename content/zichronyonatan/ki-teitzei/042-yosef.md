In this month of *Elul*, the period of preparation before the Day of Judgment on *Rosh Hashanah*, we are tasked with the lofty work of *teshuvah* (repentance). It is in these days that the entire Jewish nation sets out to better our ways and truly return to our Creator with all our hearts. Shaarei Teshuvah, the monumental guide on repentance, explains the incredible potency of this great gift called teshuvah. In essence, once one has sinned, he has caused tremendous damage in all of the upper spheres; damage which should not be rectifiable. In the natural world there is no delete button -- a deed cannot be taken back and its consequences cannot just be forgotten. Yet the Almighty, in His infinite kindness, gave us a way out. He changed the very material of existence and created a back door, as it were. A device through which He considers "that our change of heart and annulment of will for our actions are like a retroactive nullification of the action itself" (Shaarei Teshuvah). This means that we have the incredible power to truly rectify our misdeeds and all of the negative consequences resulting from them, in totality, as if they never occurred. [As an aside, it must be noted that this is only effective regarding sins between man and G-d; sins against our fellow man cannot be forgiven until we attain the forgiveness of those whom we have harmed.]

Now that we understand the great power of teshuvah, it behooves us to learn how exactly the teshuvah process is realized, and how we can get there. Here too, the Shaarei Teshuvah, enlightens us with a three-step regimen for the most basic level of teshuvah. The first step is *viduy*, which means admitting our sins. Before we can do anything else, the first step must be to take an honest and thorough accounting of our lives and realize where we fall short. This is no easy task; we are all blind to our own shortcomings and it takes hard work and brutal honesty to see through our own facades. However, comprehending this great power and understanding the benefits of teshuvah can give us the much-needed motivation to complete this task. The second step is a firm resolve to better our ways. This is where we begin the "annulment of will" from our previous misdeeds. Only if we can accept upon ourselves to discontinue our actions can we truly say that we're sorry. Lastly, once we are already on the road to bettering our ways, we must look back at the damage we have caused and truly feel remorse and regret. This is the last step in the "annulment of will" which is the essence of teshuvah. Not only have we changed our ways, but we actively wish that we had never committed the sin.

For most of us, the second step of resolve to better ourselves is the hardest part of teshuvah. In our human frailty, we find it very difficult to make any iron-clad changes in our lives. To make matters worse, we are unsure where to begin. We have so much that needs improvement and we can't change it all at once, so we are inclined to just throw in the towel and give up before we even start.

Where should we begin and how can we possibly get to where we need to be by the time Rosh Hashanah will be upon us?

The Alter of Kelm had a very enlightening thought based on this week's Parsha, about the *ben sorer u'moreh*, the wayward son. This young boy has set off on the wrong path. He steals money from his parents in order to buy and consume huge amounts of meat and wine. Despite being reprimanded and even lashed for his misdeeds, he continues in his ways. The Torah's sentence is astounding; it says that in such a case the boy should be put to death! For what? Thievery and gluttony? Since when do such things merit a death sentence? The Talmud (Sanhedrin 71b) answers that he is killed not for his actions now, but because of where he will inevitably end up. Such a boy is on the path to become a true bandit and even a murderer. Therefore, the Torah says that he should better die innocent now than die guilty later. This is very revealing of the Torah's perspective on life -- that it is not an end unto itself but rather just a way to earn reward in the Next World. Hence, it is merciful that the boy's life on this world be shortened, in order that he not lose out in the World to Come.

From this, the Alter of Kelm drew an analogy to each and every one of us on Rosh Hashanah. He pointed out that usually we would assume that the divine attribute of mercy would call for one to receive positive decrees whereas the divine attribute of justice might call for a negative one. In the case of the wayward son, however, even the attribute of mercy agrees that he should be killed -- after all, it is in his best interest to not be allowed the chance to sink deeper into sin. Unfortunately, this is not a predicament exclusive to the wayward son. Anyone who is on the way down and descending ever deeper into the quagmire of sin, would have a similar judgment. It would be better that he die innocent than guilty. This is a terrifying and very dangerous situation to be in, because if the trait of mercy calls for one's demise, to whom can he turn?

On the other hand, we can find a great deal of comfort from this very Parsha. If one is not yet perfect but he's climbing the mountain, albeit at his own pace, then he has the opposite judgment. For him, G-d wants to grant him life in order that he have time to reach the top. In order that he should be able to become a truly meritorious individual and he not perish now in his still sullied state.

This, then, must be our plan of action. Yes, we may be very far from perfect, and yes, we may have too many shortcomings to possibly tackle at once. But perfection is not what is required of us. All we need is to do our very best and to be on our way up. If we can take upon ourselves one or two small things that we will do better -- small but absolutely rock solid -- then we will be on our way and, with G-d's help, we will merit the time we need to keep climbing.
