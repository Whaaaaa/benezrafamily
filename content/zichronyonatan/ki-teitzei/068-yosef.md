In this month of Elul, the period of preparation before Rosh Hashanah, we undertake the work of teshuvah, repentance. Shaarei Teshuvah explains the incredible power of this gift: once one has sinned, the damage caused in the upper spheres should be irreversible -- yet G-d, in His kindness, built a "back door" into existence itself, so that genuine remorse becomes "a retroactive nullification of the action itself."

The most basic teshuvah has three steps: viduy, honest admission of our sins; a firm resolve to change; and, once change is underway, real remorse for the damage caused. For most of us, the second step -- firm resolve -- is hardest, because we have so much to improve that we're tempted to give up before starting at all.

The Alter of Kelm draws a striking lesson from this week's ben sorer u'moreh, the wayward son, executed not for his current crimes but because of where his trajectory will inevitably lead -- better, the Talmud says, that he die innocent now than guilty later. Normally we assume G-d's mercy calls for leniency and His justice for severity; here, remarkably, even mercy agrees the boy should die, because it is in his own best interest not to sink further into sin.

This is a frightening thought -- but this same Parsha offers comfort. If a person is not yet perfect, but is climbing the mountain at his own pace, the judgment reverses: G-d wants to grant him life precisely so he can keep climbing and become the person he is capable of becoming.

This must be our plan: not perfection, but movement. If we can take on one or two small, solid changes, we will be on our way -- and with G-d's help, we will merit the time we need to keep climbing.

Good Shabbos, Yosef
