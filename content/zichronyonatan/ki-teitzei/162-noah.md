This week's parsha introduces us to the Ben Sorer u Moreh, the rebellious son. The Torah tells us:

(18) If a man has a wayward and defiant son, who does not heed his father or mother and does not obey them even after they discipline him,

(19) his father and mother shall take hold of him and bring him out to the elders of his town at the public place of his community. (20) They shall say to the elders of his town, "This son of ours is disloyal and defiant; he does not heed us. He is a glutton and a drunkard."

(21) Thereupon the men of his town shall stone him to death. Thus you will sweep out evil from your midst: all Israel will hear and be afraid.

What is the concept of the Ben Sorer o Moreh adding to our Elul? What are we supposed to take from this before Yomim Noraim? Now is the time for us to start our Teshuva process and Ben Sorer U Moreh seems to teach us that there is a point of no return. On the surface, the Torah appears to say that a minor can become so immersed in sin that he cannot fix his ways.

There is an oft-quoted opinion in the Gemara that there never was a ben sorer u moreh and that this mitzvah exists solely for educational purposes. But this makes even less sense. Why would the Torah bother with a mitzva that seems to run counter to its ethical code if it was also unrealistic? What lesson are we meant to learn?

To better understand how this works we need to define exactly what is happening.

According to the Ibn Ezra the primary sins of the ben sorer u moreh are being a:

(2) drunkard; someone who drinks excessively, to the extent that he gets drunk. Such a person resembles a follower of Epicuros, for he seeks from the life of this world only pleasure, through all kinds of food and drink.

(1) glutton; is a general term, denoting someone who permits himself everything that he desires.

The Ibn Ezra's comments make it clear that there is an underlying attitude of a ben sorer u moreh. An obsession with pleasure and the lack of any boundaries seem to define this mindset.

Next, we must understand the justification for the killing:

The Mishna explains that the community kills the child for his own sake. "Better he dies innocent than live to be guilty. He will take his stand on the crossroads and rob people, and in some way or other make, himself liable to the death penalty. Says the Torah, "Let him die innocent of such crimes, and let him not die guilty of them."

The Mishnah is explaining that in the Ben Sorer u Moreh's current state he is blameless for the spiritual situation he is in. His family and community were responsible for him until now and their failings contributed to his deviation from the path. Like an infant, he does not bear the responsibility for his actions.

We are born with limitless potential. Our choices throughout life limit our ability to make further choices. While this is true in concrete matters like marriage and children, it is also true about our bechira (free choice) muscle. As we achieve higher levels in our service of God we strive to make our previous errors unthinkable to the improved us. Like a baal teshuva who, after 10 years of keeping kosher, finds no urge to enter the Mcdonalds he passes on the way to work, the same is true to the opposite extreme. When we choose to make the wrong decisions over and over again, our ability to make the right decision is further and further constrained.

The Ben Sorer u Moreh was allowed to reach a point in his obsession with physicality that even if he had a hirhur teshuva and wanted to change, his bechira muscle wouldn't be up to the task. While he could still connect to the arouse the deepest part of his soul, this connection would not be enough to raise his nekudas habechira to a point where he could live a productive life in service of God.

This point is further illustrated by a Gemara:

They said about Elazar ben Durdayya that he was so promiscuous that he did not leave one harlot in the world unvisited. Once, he heard that there was one in a city overseas who was the most expensive. He took a purse full of money and went and crossed seven rivers to reach her. When he arrived, she let out a breath and said: Just as this passed breath will not return to its place, so too Elazar ben Durdayya will not be accepted in repentance, even if he were to try to repent. This statement deeply shocked Elazar ben Durdayya, and he went and sat between two mountains and hills and said: Mountains and hills, pray for mercy on my behalf, so that my repentance will be accepted. They said to him: Before we pray for mercy on your behalf, we must pray for mercy on our own behalf, He said: Heaven and earth, pray for mercy on my behalf. They said to him: Before we pray for mercy on your behalf, we must pray for mercy on our own behalf. He asked the Sun and moon, Stars and constellations and they gave him the same answer. Elazar ben Durdayya said: Clearly the matter depends on nothing other than myself. He placed his head between his knees and cried loudly until his soul left his body. A Divine Voice emerged and said: Rabbi Elazar ben Durdayya is destined for life in the World-to-Come.

The Gemara explains that since he was attached so strongly to the sin, to an extent that transcended the physical temptation, he couldn't live even after doing teshuva.

I know I have a bit of ben sorer u moreh inside of myself. I engage in excess and lack the boundaries I should. I have been warned by my parents and my community and I have failed to change. What this week's parsha is telling me is inspiring but difficult. Like with R' Elazar ben Durdayya teshuva is always possible! But teshuva doesn't make things easy. It doesn't automatically turn me into a tzaddik and make all of my previous challenges vanish. As long as my life and values are rooted in excessive physicality the journey back to Hashem gets longer and harder and, left unchecked, can prevent me from ever reaching my potential. But while this is true, this Yom Kippur as soon as we realize the matter only in our hands, we can crawl into the fetal position and cry out "I want to be a child again! I want to return to my limitless potential untarnished by my mistakes!" And god willing a divine voice will answer.
