A poor man once approached a well-known philanthropist in shul to solicit his help. The man begged and pleaded, tears dripping from his eyes as he told of his terrible plight. His children were starving, his heat had been shut off, and his landlord was threatening to evict them. The wealthy man, kind-hearted and generous, was moved by the tale and reassured the beggar that he would take care of everything. He promised to give everything the man needed to get back on his feet – everything. There was just one caveat. The philanthropist’s policy was to only give sizable donations from the security of his own home, so he asked that the poor man meet him the following morning when a generous check would be waiting for him.

The following morning, the rich man waited in his study for his expected visitor, fully prepared to make all of the beggar’s dreams come true. But the clock ticked on and on until eventually the rich man could wait no longer, and he went on with his day’s affairs.

That night, the scene repeated itself, with the poor man pouring out his heart and pleading for mercy. The philanthropist was a little confused, but again assured the man that he would take care of everything if he would only visit in the morning. But alas, in the morning the wealthy man again waited as long as he could, but eventually had to go.

Over and over the same scene unfolded, until it reached the point that the philanthropist had no time to listen to the pleas. He knew already that the beggar could not be expected even to pick up the check of his salvation.

The Chofetz Chaim explains that this is a frightening depiction of our very own actions time after time.
Not once but three times a day, we stand and pray to Hashem to help us improve. Especially in these days of Elul, we ask him to help us repent, to help us learn Torah, and to help us conquer our yetzer hora (evil inclination). Sometimes, in a moment of truth, we are even moved to tears, overwhelmed by our predicament and at a loss as to how we can continue.

At those times, Hashem, our most kind and generous benefactor, is moved and decides to give us all the help we desire. He ‘writes out the check’ and has all the divine assistance we could possibly want waiting for us. There’s just one caveat – we are expected to come and pick up the check. We have to wake up the next morning and apply ourselves to our learning, to our repentance, and to our spiritual battles. If we would just show up and do our part, we would find all the doors wide open for us, but we unfortunately neglect our post. Like the poor man in our story, we just can’t get ourselves to do what we have to do. And so, again, we cry and we plead, and again Hashem preps everything for us. But if we won’t show up, we cannot collect the handout and we are left in the same predicament as always.

So this Elul, let’s buck the trend. Let’s continue to pour out our hearts in the quest for spiritual success, but let us follow the request by completing our end of the deal. Let us make sure to do our part by redoubling our own efforts in the service of Hashem, and we can rest assured that, with tenacity, we will receive all the help that we need to succeed.
