This week's parsha includes the mitzvah of Shiluach Haken, the commandment to scare a mother bird away from the nest before taking its eggs.

Should a bird's nest appear before you on the way, on any tree or on the earth, chicks or eggs, and the mother resting on the chicks or the eggs: You shall not take the mother with the offspring. You shall send away the mother, and take the offspring for yourself so that it be good for you, and your days be long.

This is one of the few mitzvot in the Torah that carries an explicit reward with it. This distinction makes it a central point for exploring the Torah's take on theodicy, or how God allows evil to exist in the world. While we are instructed to do many commandments without any defined reward and are encouraged to "be not like servants who serve the master in the expectation of receiving a reward but be like servants who serve the master without the expectation of receiving a reward," here we are given instruction with clear compensation.

Our standard approach to reward and merit is easier to integrate into the world we see around us. There are many beautiful benefits that come with following the Torah that is obvious and evident. It is clear that there are many rewards to be earned in this world. But at the same time, the abstractness of this promise makes the impoverished Torah scholar more understandable, God never promised him riches for following the Torah and he still enjoys the manifold other natural benefits that accompany his pious lifestyle. But with the mitzvah of Shiluach Haken, where there is a defined and explicit promise attached to the commandment, this can get much more complicated.

The gemara in Kiddushin recounts the event that drove Elisha ben Abuya to heresy:
It occurred that there was one whose father said to him: Climb to the top of the building and fetch me chicks. And he climbed to the top of the building and dispatched the mother bird and took the young, thereby simultaneously fulfilling the mitzva to dispatch the mother bird from the nest and the mitzva to honor one's parents, but upon his return, he fell and died.
Elisha ben Abuya was among the greatest sages of his generation and his faith was shattered by this event. What is the solution offered by his contemporaries?
Rav Yosef said: Had Elisha ben Avuya, interpreted [the promise of long life] homiletically, as referring to the World-to-Come, he would not have sinned.

The only way for us to confront the randomness and lack of justice that we see all around us is to have faith that it is part of something bigger. While there are positive natural consequences of living an upright and faithful life, it is never a guarantee for prosperity or physical longevity. We must abandon our notion that moral accomplishment and material success are linked otherwise we risk being driven to heresy.
