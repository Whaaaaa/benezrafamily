"Hinei mah tov u'mah na'im shevet achim gam yachad" -- "Behold, how good and how pleasant it is for brethren to dwell together in unity!" This week's parsha concludes the book of Bereishit. Yaakov, preparing for death, blesses his children and grandchildren, revealing their roles in the future nation.

Yaakov blesses his grandchildren Efraim and Menashe -- Yosef's sons -- before his own children, elevating them to full tribal status and effectively doubling Yosef's inheritance. He crosses his hands, placing his right on the younger Efraim's head -- a deliberate breach of birth order, despite Yaakov's own painful history with sibling favoritism.

Why would Yaakov, who suffered so much from favoritism himself, risk repeating that pattern? The answer reveals a deep insight into how a family rises above multigenerational struggle. Efraim and Menashe represent the perpetuation of Yaakov's heritage beyond a single generation -- raised in Egypt, away from the Land, at real risk of losing their identity to a foreign, corrupting culture. Peace between them was the sustaining force that would carry the family through coming exile.

Yaakov did not avoid the problem -- he confronted it directly, by re-creating the very situation of the original sin (a father favoring a younger son) so his adult children could face the test again, this time successfully. Efraim and Menashe's own peaceful acceptance of their blessings proved that the family's old wound had finally healed -- immunity achieved not by avoidance, but by facing the same test twice and choosing differently the second time.

Through hardship and heartbreak, slavery and servitude, may peace among brothers continue to bind us.

Shabbat Shalom, Noah
