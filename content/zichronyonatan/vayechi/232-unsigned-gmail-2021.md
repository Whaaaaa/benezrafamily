In this week's Parsha Veyechi, two of our most beloved patriarchs die in Egypt. Vayechi means and he lived, the Parsha teaches us the Halacha of proper Jewish burial and the laws regarding one's personal obligations before passing away.

As Yakov laid on his death bed he blessed his children and grandchildren making arrangements for all his progeny. He assigned each Tribe a different livelihood and role in settling the holy land. Although Yakov wished to share the details of the final redemption he was prevented from doing so.

After his death, a funeral procession including Egyptian elites and politicians accompanied Yakov's family to Hebron where Yakov was buried in the cave of doubles among his family.

Yosef also died in this Parsha at the age of 110, Yosef instructed his children not to bury him in Egypt but to bury him in the holy land. Before he died Yosef reminded his children of God's covenant and told them to have hope through their impending descent into slavery. Despite his wishes, Yosef's bones were only carried out of Egypt once Israel was freed from their bondage.

We learn from Yaakov's death that it is better to be buried in Israel, and proper to be buried next to one's family on land personally owned. We learn that making arrangements for burial is itself an omen for long life. The preparation will lead us to time better spent.

Yosef's burial teaches us that one's bones can be moved with the help of an expert Rabbi. It is important for Jews to be buried in Jewish plots near their family and for the wicked to be buried away from the group.

Burial practices are deeply primal, and even ants bury their dead. Ants inside of an enclosure will carry dead ants and parts of dead ants to the farthest corner of the enclosure and bury them together in a mass grave, science suggests this is to prevent diseases carried in the bodies of dead ants. Another interesting ceremony I heard about this week is practiced by the indigenous people in remote Papa New Guinea. In this tribe eating the bodies of dead loved ones is commonplace. A western bird researcher was astounded when on an expedition, his indigenous trail guide had to leave after receiving the bad news that his young nephew had died. It was time for the family to eat him. When asked about the practice the bird researcher said just as you wouldn't miss out on your loved one's funeral you wouldn't miss out on eating your loved one if you were from this tribe in Papa New Guinea. The researcher notes that there is a lack of big game in that region and that in a place where people starve for protein there is a good chance this practice serves a practical purpose as well.

Across the board, the ceremonies and traditions before and after death are not for the dead they are for the living. They are to facilitate the safety and well-being of those near and affected by the death.
