Parsha Vayechi focuses on the preparations Yaakov makes for his death after living in Egypt for 17 years. He famously asks Yosef to bury him in the Holy Land even though he will die in Egypt. Yosef’s sons Ephraim and Menashe are elevated to the level of their uncles by performing this task for their grandfather. As Yaakov is on his deathbed he blesses his children and divvies out roles for their future as the nation of Israel. Yaakov also tries to relate secrets about the end of time but cannot because of divine intervention. His funeral procession includes all his family along with pharaohs ministers and other high ranking citizens. He is buried with his predecessors in the cave of doubles. Yosef dies in Egypt also leaving instructions for his bones to be carried to the Holy Land. His bones are not brought to Israel until the exodus. Yosef's dying words fill us with the hope of redemption “G‑d will surely remember you, and bring you up out of this land to the land of which He swore to
Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob.”

The Baal Haturim comments that Yakkovs best years were his final 17 in Egypt. 17 has the numerical value of the Hebrew word tov, or good. These good years were made possible by Yehudah because before Yaakov made the journey to Egypt he sent Yehudah ahead to begin establishing a yeshiva in Egypt. Both Yehuda and Yosef are kingly, each in their own way. Yehuda means to give thanks- from the root hodaah while Yosef means to increase. Yosef was the king of Egypt, but Yehudah was the king of his brothers. Even as a boy Yosef, learned everything from Yehudah. Yehuda”s strength was in the primacy of action, acting consistently through hard times. Yosef's strength is in self-growth and attaining more and more knowledge as he got older. Both the attributes of Yehudah and Yosef are integral parts of Jewish life the difference between them is the emphasis. Yehudah would be the first to jump into action and Yosef would amass more skill before extending himself.

Yaakov chose Yehudah to establish the yeshivah because, through exile, turmoil and hardship Yehuda's deep devotion to Torah would not sway. Through Years of hardship and centuries of suffering a Jew's heart will sway. His seated submission to God will still exist. This is why the best approach to Jewish education is Yehuda’s route, first, you nurture survival and then you encourage growth.
