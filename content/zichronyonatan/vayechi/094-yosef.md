The enormity of the kindness of Hashem experienced with the birth of a healthy baby, is in itself quite overwhelming. But when that kindness comes against the backdrop of so many other gifts -- the gifts of health, a new home, the opportunity to be teaching Torah, and above all the privilege to still be learning in Kollel, to name but a few -- it is simply too much to bear. It leaves me feeling grossly inadequate, humbled, and undeserving. Perhaps more than anything, however, is the question and concern of what will be left in the next world. After receiving so much in this world, will any reward still await us in the next? Rav Yisroel Salanter said that, by enjoying a small delicacy, one is at risk of consuming his entire share in the world to come. If that is true of a silly delicacy, how much more so is the risk when enjoying blessings of such magnitude.

In truth, Dovid Hamelech already voiced this question in Tehillim. מה אשיב לה' כל תגמולוהי עלי -- Dovid asks what he can possibly do to repay or somehow absolve himself of the weighty debt incurred as the recipient of the kindness of Hashem. Thankfully, Dovid answers the question, כוס ישועות אשא ובשם ה' אקרא, that he will proclaim the greatness of Hashem and bring honor to his name. How, though, is this the answer?

The Malbim refers us to an earlier chapter of Tehillim (פרק ס"ג), where Dovid states כי טוב חסדך מחיים שפתי ישבחונך -- Your kindness is greater than life, so my lips will praise you. How can Hashem's kindness be greater than life; aren't all the blessings we enjoy only additional enhancements to life? The Malbim explains that life is not an end unto itself, but a means by which to come to the main goal of life, which is to praise Hashem (see Ramban, end of Bo). This means that Hashem's kindliness, which is the opportunity to thank and praise Him, is the ultimate purpose of life, while life itself is no more than a prerequisite. Hence, Hashem's gifts are truly better than life, since they bring about the ultimate purpose that my lips will praise you.

We named our daughter Chana, after the great Chana and her seven sons who gave their lives to bring honor to Hashem during the period of Greek oppression preceding Channukah. Incidentally, the Shla"h writes that Channukah (חנוכה) is the letters of Chana's name (חנה) plus כ"ו which is the numerical value of the name of Hashem. As if to say that the honor brought to Hahem's name through Chana and her sons' sacrifice, were part and parcel of the miracles of Channukah and the sanctification which came through them.

But how is it that young children, confined to solitude and without outside influence, should have the courage and conviction to give their lives instead of bowing to an idol? The answer is because they were raised with this ideal, that life is not an end unto itself but just a vehicle by which to thank Hashem and bring honor to his name. With such an outlook, instilled in the children by their illustrious mother Chana, it was obvious that a life of disgrace to Hashem wasn't even life and wasn't worth living for.

This is our blessing to our daughter Chana, and our hopes for all of us. That she should be the recipient of endless kindness from Hashem, but that she should perceive all that goodness not through a selfish lens, but as a means to thank Hashem and give honor to his name. And, as such, she should live a long and fruitful life of Kiddush Hashem.

Good Shabbos,

Yosef
