הִנֵּה מַה טוֹב וּמַה נָּעִים שֶׁבֶת אָחִים גַּם יַחַד

"Behold, how good and how pleasant it is for brethren to dwell together in unity!"

This week's parsha is the conclusion of the book of Bereshit. We wrap up the section of the Torah that deals with the Jewish Family and move on to the book of our aspiring nationhood, Shemot. In this week's parsha Yaakov prepares for his impending death by blessing his progeny and revealing what their roles in the Jewish Country will be. Yaakov then dies and is escorted back to Israel to be laid to rest with his forefathers in Ma'arat HaMachpelah. The parsha proceeds to recount the death of Yosef and presents his final request to be have his remains taken out from Egypt and brought to Israel as well. Before he dies he reminds his family of the promise that God has made to them saying, "G-d will surely remember you, and bring you up out of this land to the land of which He swore to Avraham, Yitchok and Yaakov."

When Yaakov begins blessing the future generations he does not start with his children. Instead, he blesses Efraim and Menashe, his grandchildren from Yosef, first. Only after completing their blessing does he move on to his children. Not only are Efraim and Menashe out of order generationally, but they are given the status of full blown tribes, effectively doubling the inheritance of Yosef and bestowing upon him the firstborn birthright.

In a further breach of procedure, Yaakov puts his right hand on the head of the younger son Efraim and his left hand on the head of Menashe. Surely Yaakov realized the terrible heartache and pain that was caused by preferential treatment in the previous generation. He was estranged from his beloved son Yosef for years because of the jealousy that that his favor aroused in his other sons. Furthermore, Yaakov experienced first hand the strife and conflict caused by the inequality between him and his own brother Esau.

With these two actions Yaakov seems to be upsetting the order of both his children and his grandchildren. How could Yaakov, with his incredible wisdom and foresight, risk agitating another generation with the same partisanship that had wreaked havoc in prior generations?

Yaakov's actions provide deep insight into the how a family can rise above its struggles to become great. The hardest challenges we face in life are often multigenerational struggles that we have seen play out before us. Like the color of eyes or hair these obstacles are passed down hereditarily. We are all too familiar with the consequences and fallout that result from succumbing to these pressures but a genetic chink our armor frequently prevents us from fully escaping the pressure. We see this clearly in our ancestral bloodline. From Yitzchak and Yishmael to Yosef and the Tribes the challenge of preferential treatment is a dominating force in our forefamily's history.

Yaakov surely understood this. In fact, he understood the magnitude of this problem so clearly that he realized if left unrectified, we could never sustain nationhood. This explains why he chose to bless Efraim and Menashe first. The relationship between grandchildren and grandparents is uniquely human. While most animals have parental relationships the concept of a multigenerational family is reserved for people. Ephraim and Menashe represented the perpetuation of Yaakov's heritage beyond a single generation, a continuation that is the bedrock of establishing a People. Additionally, these children would grow up in Egypt, away from the 'jewish natural habitat' that is Israel. This would expose them to the forces of a foreign, corrupt, and abusive culture with no easy way to insulate themselves. Yaakov needed to know that there was peace between them. If there was, they could cleave to each other and survive the coming diaspora. Peace would be our sustaining force through Egypt. Peace would keep us connected without a homeland. However, if they did not have the ability to dwell together in unity, the Jewish People would surely be lost and the blessing for each tribe to finds its place within our nation would be irrelevant.

Yaakov also knew that the solution was not to turn a blind-eye to the problem, to avoid confronting it directly. In the moment before he blessed his grandchildren he could have chosen ignore his premonition that the younger would exceed the older. He could have blessed the children as Yosef presented them, the older on the right and the younger on the left. Even further, Yaakov could have not blessed them at all and avoided the contentious decision to bestow the double inheritance on Yosef entirely. Yaakov understood that the Jewish Nation would emerge from these two generations. The first generation, Yaakov's children, lost their first battle to sibling rivalry in a pretty catastrophic way. Unless this vulnerability was entirely repaired on their part, it could be passed down to the generations without end. By giving Yosef the recognition that had originally caused him to be sold, Yaakov was creating an opportunity for his other sons to directly face this challenge once again. This time, however, they were grown men, with families, who had the benefits of a much broader perspective. And this time, they passed the test and Yosef was awarded the birthright without conflict.

The final test to determine if the "genetic" struggle had finally been overcome was to observe the grandchildren. If this flaw was completely rectified within the previous generation, the next would have an immunity to the conflict that can only come from a spiritual immune system that had developed resistance. Efraim and Menashe proved, through their peaceful acceptance of their respective blessings, that finally this particular challenge had been conquered.

Through hardship and heartbreak, slavery and servitude, may peace among brothers continue to bind us.
