In this week's Parsha, Shoftim, Moshe lays the groundwork for a Jewish judiciary. "Justice, justice shall you pursue." Moshe explains how to carry out laws without bias, how to investigate crimes, and the requirement of at least two witnesses to convict.

Moshe tells Bnai Yisrael that every generation will need people entrusted to interpret the law and issue rulings -- "you shall not turn away from the thing that they say to you, to the right nor to the left." It is important to have people in our lives whose rulings we do not feel the need to constantly re-litigate.

Shoftim also includes prohibitions against idol worship and sorcery, laws for appointing a king, cities of refuge for accidental killers, and the laws of war -- including exemptions for men who have recently built a home, planted a vineyard, married, had a child, or are simply soft-hearted. We are commanded to offer terms of peace before attacking a city, forbidden to destroy wantonly, and forbidden even to cut down a fruit tree.

The Parsha concludes with eglah arufah, the procedure for an unsolved murder near a town -- underscoring the responsibility of community leaders to examine and change whatever could have prevented the crime or led to a better outcome.
