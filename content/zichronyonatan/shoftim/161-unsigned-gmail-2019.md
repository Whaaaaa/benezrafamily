This week's Parsha begins with the dictum to appoint judges and police in all of our cities.

It follows with more commands of how to create judicial systems in communities.

The wording of the verse is literally, "place for yourself (singular) judges and officers in all of your (singular) gates."

The sages derive an interesting additional idea here. Using the singular "lecha", your, and "shearecha", your gates, seems to obligate each person individually. The problem is, this is a communal command. How can each individual accomplish this?

The answer lies in another understanding of "gates". Each person has gates in their bodies. Our eyes allow new information and images into our minds. Our ears bring in music, which influences our emotions, and allows us to bond and form connections with others. Our mouths give us the ability to experience all of the nuances of flavor Hashem has placed in the world and to put out thoughts into words that can change the world.

The sages tell us that on another level this command is telling us to create accountability systems for ourselves. To place judges and officers in our own personal gates. To judiciously examine what we take into our minds, bodies, and souls and to take actions that only let in what will help us to flourish and to only put out that which changes the world around us for the better.

If we take the time to examine and judge what we consume and speak about, and guard the gates between ourselves and the world around us, we can reach the closing of the Parsha and "do what is right in the eyes of Hashem."
