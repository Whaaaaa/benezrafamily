This week's Torah portion concludes with an intriguing law. This is the law of the Eglah Arufah. When a body is found abandoned on the outskirts of town, we learn that the leaders of the nearest city are obligated to sacrifice a calf as an atonement offering. They must slaughter the calf and proclaim, "We have done our duty, we did not spill his blood nor did we witness what occurred." After this process, either the elders or the priests of the city request forgiveness from god, saying, "Absolve your people and do not allow the guilt for the blood of the innocent to remain among your people."

The commentator Rashi questions why the Torah is requiring a statement of innocence from the elders and leaders. Do we seriously suspect that the elders of the community committed this murder? Rashi answers that when the elders say that they did not commit the murder or witness what occurred, what they are really saying is, "We did not see him leave without food or escort, we did not know that he had needs that were unfulfilled." The implication from Rashi is astounding. This explanation indicates that had they seen the person leave without adequate provisions they would be responsible as if they had committed the murder themselves!

This obviously raises the question that this law seems to put responsibility for the death on the hands of a governing body that shouldn't be culpable. Why should the elders of a town take responsibility for something that occurred outside of their jurisdiction, especially when they took no part in committing the act? Why must they profess their innocence when they were not involved in the incident and had no idea that it had even taken place. The ordeal was not in their community, instead it occurred outside of their city limits.

The Netziv answers this question by explaining that this ritual is designed to start a dialog among the townspeople and their leadership. When the community learns of what has happened they will immediately mobilize to ensure this never happens again. The shock and horror of learning that a soul was lost in their midst will surely lead them to consider how these situations can be prevented. Through this dialog they will take productive steps to become a closer more attentive community. The Netziv is clearly explaining the reason why a ritual to raise awareness is necessary, but why the large emphasis on communal responsibility?

The Lubavitcher Rebbe learns a tremendous principle from how the Torah views communal responsibility in the case of the Eglah Arufa. He says that this law is meant to teach us that a person never has the ability to say, "This is outside of my control therefore it doesn't concern me." He teaches that immediately when we become aware of something, the awareness alone creates responsibility. In the case of the Eglah Arufah it was the responsibility of the townspeople and the elders to watch over and protect their fellow, to ensure his safety. They were responsible to be aware and concerned for each other, always. From here it is clear that avoiding awareness of that which occurs in your surroundings can not absolve you from guilt. Here too, the elders were unaware of what occurred and although they did not know, they still required repentance.

In this difficult time, still amid Uncle Jonathon's Shloshim (30 Days of Mourning), we must remember that our tremendous loss need not be in vain. Now that we have completed our ceremonies and rituals and engaged in the dialog of shiva we are left to see what will really change. When hardships befall us in the coming year will we remember that we do not have the right to throw our hands up and say, "What could I do? How is this my responsibility?" Did we harness this time of mourning to repent and look inside of ourselves to see what are we can do to enrich and extend the lives of those around us? Are we asking the hard questions about our community involvement? Will we start showing up and taking responsibility for our family, friends and neighbors? Now is when we need to really assess how we are using our loss as an impetus for lasting meaningful improvement. And finally, as hard as it may be, we must remind ourselves that tragedy has the ability to inspire transformation and that from this adversity we will emerge more aware.

Shabbat Shalom from Israel, Noah Cali and Moony
