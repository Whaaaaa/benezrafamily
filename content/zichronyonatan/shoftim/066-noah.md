This week's Torah portion concludes with the law of the Eglah Arufah: when a body is found abandoned outside a town, the elders of the nearest city sacrifice a calf and proclaim, "We have done our duty, we did not spill his blood nor did we witness what occurred." Rashi asks the obvious question -- do we really suspect the elders of murder? He answers that their declaration really means: "we did not see him leave without food or escort, we did not know he had unfulfilled needs" -- meaning that had they seen this and done nothing, they would bear responsibility as if they had committed the murder themselves.

Why should a town's leadership bear responsibility for a death entirely outside their jurisdiction, one they took no part in and had no knowledge of? The Netziv explains this ritual is designed to start a communal conversation -- the shock of learning a soul was lost nearby mobilizes a town toward becoming more attentive and connected. The Lubavitcher Rebbe draws an even sharper principle: a person never has the standing to say, "this is outside my control, therefore it doesn't concern me." Awareness alone creates responsibility.

In this difficult time, still within Uncle Jonathan's Shloshim, we must remember that our loss need not be in vain. Now that the rituals of mourning are complete, what will actually change? Will we remember that we do not have the right to throw up our hands and ask, "how is this my responsibility"? Will we take this loss as the impetus for lasting, meaningful improvement in how we show up for our family, friends, and neighbors?

Shabbat Shalom from Israel, Noah, Cali, and Moony

The learning should be a merit for Yonatan ben Avraham Avinu a"h.
