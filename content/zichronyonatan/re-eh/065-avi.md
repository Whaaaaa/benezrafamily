In this week's Parsha, Re'eh, Moshe places before the people a blessing and a curse -- blessing for keeping G-d's commandments, curse for abandoning them. One commandment given this week concerns kosher meat, with a complex history that begins with Adam in the Garden of Eden.

At creation, Adam is taught: "Behold, I have given you every seed-bearing herb... it will be yours for food" -- humankind begins with no meat at all. Over 1,600 years later, after the flood, Noach is told: "Every moving thing that lives shall be yours to eat" -- and meat enters the human diet for the first time. Yet the Torah later commands that Shabbat be honored, in part, by eating meat -- suggesting the practice is not merely tolerated but honored.

Rabbi Yosef Albo, in Sefer HaIkkarim, suggests G-d's original plan was for humans not to kill animals for food at all -- that doing so is inherently a cruel act which, over time, embeds cruelty into our character. The generations before the flood, permitted no meat, nonetheless failed to distinguish themselves from animals, denigrating into violence and corruption -- precisely the chaos G-d sought to destroy with the flood.

When Noach stepped off the ark, the terms changed: he was not only permitted to eat meat, but obligated to sacrifice some of it to G-d -- a reminder that we are meant to be beyond animals, responsible for the world in ways that exceed mere survival. Meat becomes a luxury, and the laws of Kashrut exist, in part, to temper the materialistic drive that comes with it. This is why the sages caution that an empty-minded person has no business eating meat, and why we are told never to eat meat on an empty stomach -- better to start with bread.
