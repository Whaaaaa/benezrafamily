This week’s parsha introduces us to the concept of Maaser Sheni, or the second tithe. Judaism's rich agricultural tradition and laws form the backbone of our shared society. While we value the open market and self determination in the planting and harvesting processes, laissez faire economics were already recognized as a failure in many respects. From providing parity and sustaining spirituality to harboring humility and strengthening social bonds, our complicated cycles of tithes mandated the positive behaviors the invisible hand failed to value.

Some commandments form the basis for a welfare state. Leket, Shikheha, and Pe’ah or the forgotten ears of grain, forgotten sheafs, and the corners of the field all were earmarked for the poor. Additionally, the Maaser Ani or tithe for the poor was due in the 3rd and 6th year of the cycle and also distributed among the less fortunate. The 7th year in the shmita cycle forgave debtors and the Yovel or Jubilee year restored everyone's ancestral land. We see many different tools being implemented to ensure the Jewish economy is one that provides for all, regardless of individual wealth.

Next, we have sets of laws that are designed to protect and support our religious leaders. The Kohanim and Leviim were our teachers, rabbis, and scholars. They were responsible for the soul of the Jewish people. They ran the temple, the cities of refuge, and were our spiritual doctors, diagnosing our ailments and recommending teshuvah. The Torah acknowledged that as central as they were to our lives and survival as a people, their pay wasn’t great. For this reason we are commanded to give Teruma and Maaser Rishon, priestly portions.

Finally, we are commanded to bring first fruits to Jerusalem on the pilgrimage holidays. This is meant to remind our nation that its success and bounty is only by the gift of God. Without this reminder, an affluent society can become overly materialistic and self absorbed. Leaders can quickly forget their role is to serve and not to be served. These are the first signs of a civilization in decline. So the Torah remedies this threat as well by commanding us to take our first fruits and dedicate them to God.

With this understanding, we can observe how each halachic nuance in our agricultural laws is meant to compensate for ways that the market inevitably fails to provide for its participants. That's why the law of Maaser Sheni in this week’s parsha is so difficult to comprehend. Unlike the other donations and tithes described above, Maaser Sheni does not benefit anyone except the individual himself. While first fruits were accompanied by liturgical declarations and ceremonial pomp, Maaser Sheni can be used for almost anything, as long as it is consumed within Jerusalem. What hole in the agricultural economy could this law be addressing?

The Rambam in the Guide for the Perplexed explains, “As he was not able to use it (Maaser Ani) otherwise than by way of eating and drinking, he must have been easily induced to give it gradually away. This rule brought multitudes together in one place and strengthened the bond of love and brotherhood among the children of man.”

From the Rambam’s explanation we are able to see that Maaser Sheni combatted an incredible threat to peaceful society, individualism. The great driving force of a free economy is individual identity and the right to self determination. As much as this is an engine for the expansion of our economy, the quest for individual achievement comes at a massive cost to the common good and public spirit. As we become more focused on ourselves and our lot, even while paying our other dues, we lose the social capital on which our society is built. This is the brilliance of Maaser Sheni. We are commanded to flood the streets of Jerusalem with open hearts and full pockets. Once there, we have the opportunity to lose ourselves, willingly, in the needs and desires of the whole. This experience rejuvenates the connections that form communities and ensures we can not recede into icey isolation.

These lessons ring even more true in the post industrial age. As the costs of capitalism become more apparent through rising income inequality, sharp declines in religious practice, and a government that increasingly only legislates in its own interest, we must heed the warning of this week’s parsha. We cannot let individualism consume our communal consciousness. Now, more than ever, we need to come together in joy and celebration and strengthen the bond of love and brotherhood among all of mankind.

Shabbat Shalom,

Noah BenEzra
