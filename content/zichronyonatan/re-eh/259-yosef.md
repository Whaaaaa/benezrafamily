While in the desert, camping around the Mishkan, the Jewish people were commanded only to eat meat that was part of a korban, a sacrifice to Hashem. Since everyone lived in immediate proximity to the ‘House of G-d’ it was a slight to Hashem’s honor to eat meat without offering its blood and the like to Him first. However, when the people were to come into the land of Israel and were no longer going to be in direct contact with the Mishkan, Moshe gave the word that basar taavah, meat not part of any korban, would become permissible.

What’s interesting to note is the preface which the Torah gives to this amendment: ‘When Hashem will broaden your boundaries with wealth¹ and you will desire to eat meat, you may eat meat as you wish.’

What does wealth have to do with the desire to eat meat?

Rashi comments that the Torah is teaching us derech eretz, which means etiquette or simple common decency. What’s the common decency? That one should not desire meat unless he has first been granted the wealth to obtain such delicacies. If one cannot afford meat, he shouldn’t even have a desire for it. It’s not in his dreams or his aspirations – it is simply something outside of his reality. This is why the Torah prefaces the desire to eat meat with the blessing of wealth because until wealth comes his way, he has no business with such desires.

For our purposes, we must point out the obvious that meat is only a parable. In our days, thank G-d, I’m confident that everyone reading this can afford meat on at least some sort of regular basis. However, The broader lesson is perhaps more pertinent today than ever.

The entire American culture is based on longing for things out of our reach. Every advertisement, every social media post, and so many of our conversations revolve around the things we cannot afford. Be it that picturesque vacation in paradise, that bright and flashy new car, or that dream house big enough to fit three of your own, we are perpetually programmed to dream and desire every unattainable indulgence known to man.

But here we find an eye-opening window into the stark contrast between the timeless truth of the Torah and the distressing degeneration of Western society. Here we learn that the very baseline of human decency in the Torah’s view is something antithetical to our whole ‘American’ way of life. In the Torah’s eyes, the desire for material things beyond our grasp is a violation of the most basic human principles. One who harbors such aspirations is lacking in his status as a contributing part of society.

I want to stress that Rashi is not telling us about lofty levels of piety but about simple ‘derech eretz’, the most basic definition of human morals and conduct. He is teaching us the playbook for successful living and the path to a life of fulfillment. This is the inescapable truth; it is up to us only to decide whether we would like to face that truth and introduce it into our thought patterns. We need to retrain ourselves to stop all of those wants and desires at their very root before they have a chance to settle in our hearts and minds. And as we learn to desire only those things which are already within our means, we will find a much happier and more fulfilled existence.
