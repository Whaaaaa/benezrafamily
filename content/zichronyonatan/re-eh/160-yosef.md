It was just before Sukkot during the second World War, and bombs were falling everywhere. Fear reigned. But the Brisker Rav, Rabbi Yitzchak Zev Soloveichik, had something else on his mind. He was totally absorbed in finding Hadasim for the chag of Sukkot; not just kosher ones, but the finest Hadasim available. Someone asked the Rav if the dire situation didn't warrant a slightly lower standard than usual. Wouldn't it be okay this year to use regular kosher Hadasim and forgo the added stringency of finding the most beautiful ones?

Rabbi Soloveichik gave an incredible response which is pertinent to all of us, in the best and worst of times. What if Hashem would tell just one person, that he wants just one thing, right now. And that one thing would be to find beautiful Hadasim. Of course in such a case everyone would make it their business to fulfill the command, no matter the circumstances. After all, G-d asked a personal request of me, right now.

Why do we have trouble doing the mitzvot in the face of adversity? We don't think of the mitzvot in the frame of reference of G-d Himself asking. We don't feel personally responsible because all Jews were commanded, not just us. We don't realize the importance of the specific mitzvah in front of us because there are so many other mitzvot. And the command isn't current but rather from thousands of years ago.

It is for this purpose that we say the following every day in the Shema: "And this thing that I command you today shall be upon your heart." 'This thing,' as if it's the only single mitzvah. 'That I command', that G-d Himself is the one talking. 'You,' as if you are the only subject of the command. 'Today,' as if I just commanded this very day. We have to view the Mitzvot as current and personal objectives which we received today. Then His service will be real and relevant, and we won't easily shrug off such a specific request.

This, says Rabbi E. B. Finkel, is the message contained in the beginning of our Parsha. "Behold (single tense) I am placing before you (plural tense) today a blessing and a curse." Why does the verse begin with the single tense of seeing, as if it was said to a single person, wasn't this said to the entire assembly together, as the verse finishes in the plural? The answer is that even though it was said to everyone together, Hashem wants us to know that in His eyes every person is treated as an individual. He wants us to know that every mitzvah is not only a national objective, but the personal responsibility of each and every individual. As the omnipotent G-d, the significance of a single person is in no way mitigated by the existence of others doing the same thing.

Let us realize our very personal connection to G-d and to each of His mitzvot, let us realize the importance and vitality of our own service, and let us thereby redouble our efforts and performance, no matter the circumstance.
