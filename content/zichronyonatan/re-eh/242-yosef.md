'See that I am placing before you today a blessing and a curse.' Famous words, but they seem to be grammatically incorrect. 'See', or Re'eh in Hebrew is written in the single tense, whereas 'before you, lifneichem, is written in the plural form.

The Rebbe of Alexander explained with a powerful one-liner. He said it in Yiddish, but we'll do our best to translate without losing the flavor. 'Things are presented before everyone, but only a select few see the opportunities.' Thus the presentation is before the plural you, but the seeing is written only to an individual.

All of us receive endless invitations and opportunities to become great – the hard part is being the individual who sees, realizes, and capitalizes on that which is presented.

But why is it so difficult to see? What is this blindness which impairs most of the world from tapping into their potential? And how can we avoid the pitfall and count ourselves among the few possessed of vision?

The Netivot Shalom (Parshat Va'etchanan) says in the name of a certain tzadik that when, at the end of his life, he looked back over the years, he found an incredible thing. He saw then that all the periods which had seemed to him as the 'good years' were really very simple and unaccomplished. On the other hand, those times which had seemed dark and treacherous, when he felt like he was faltering, were in fact days of tremendous growth and achievement.

Why the paradox? It would seem that greatness is built through hard work and striving against the darkness. Greatness doesn't come from places we'd expect it, from times when everything is going right and we feel like we're on top of the world. Rather, greatness is something that blossoms from times of trial, from times when things aren't going the way we'd like when we feel like we just can't overcome.

Those trying times are the 'presentation' that is afforded to everyone, and which precious few are able to see. And perhaps with that realization, we can understand why many of us are opportunity-blind. When we find ourselves face-to-face with a situation we dread when all decks seem stacked against us, how do we react? Do we roll up our sleeves, take the bull by the horns, and brace ourselves for victory? Or are we so busy feeling upset about the situation, so scared of suffering defeat, and so wrapped up in self-pity that we check out and turn a blind eye to greatness being presented to us?

If you identified with the latter thought process, then I think we've identified the problem. But how do we combat it?

An Israeli soldier was once captured by terrorists and was being tortured for information. He was a very good soldier and was committed not to disclose anything, but as the days wore on and the torture became worse and worse, his stamina began to wear thin, until one night he decided he just couldn't take it anymore. He made up his mind that tomorrow he would tell them everything. But later that night he overheard the most peculiar thing. His captors, thinking they were out of earshot, were conversing in Hebrew! Immediately, he realized that these were no Arabs, but fellow members of the IDF. They were testing him. As soon as the truth hit him, he was filled with new strength – if this was just a test, then he would pass, no matter what it took. He stood strong, and before long he was thrilled to be told the truth and to find himself promoted to the highest echelons of the army.

If we would only ingrain in our hearts that all the tribulations that come our way are none other than tests and opportunities to propel us to new heights, we too would be filled with the power and strength to carry on and overcome. So let us change the lens through which we view every challenge. Let us not waste our time being upset by the situation, but recognize it for the treasure trove that it is. Let us not be scared by the chance of failure, but thrilled by the opportunity for true greatness. And let us never be wrapped up in self-pity, but filled with gratitude for each stepping stone that is sent our way.

Good Shabbos,
Yosef
