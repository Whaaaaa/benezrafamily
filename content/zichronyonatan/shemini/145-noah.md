This week's Parsha, Shemini, is the culmination of seven preceding parshiot instructing the Jews on the construction and service of the Mishkan. Now, on the eighth day, all is finally ready. The Mishkan is standing in all of its glory, Aaron and his sons have been consecrated and the time to begin the service has finally arrived. "Moses and Aaron went into the Tent of Meeting and when they came out, they blessed the people. God's glory was then revealed to all the people."

Just at the pinnacle of this narrative, something horrifying takes place.

"Aaron's sons, Nadav and Avihu, took their censers, put fire into them and added incense; and they offered unauthorized fire before God, which He had not instructed them to offer. Fire came forth from before God, and it consumed them so that they died before God. Moses then said to Aaron: "This is what God spoke of when he said: Among those who approach Me I will show myself holy; in the sight of all the people I will be honored."

Celebration is immediately replaced with mourning, elation with depression.

On the most basic level, Nadav and Avihu were guilty of offering a fire that was not commanded. Why doesn't the Torah view this as an act of tremendous initiative? How does their fiery enthusiasm warrant a death sentence while other religious acts of passion are extolled?

Understanding the nature of holiness is essential in understands it's dynamic with human will. In order to create the world, God had to hollow out a part of creation in which the divine will would be concealed. Like a parent who holds themselves back, allowing a child to independently learn and make mistakes, God holds back his presence. And as a parent never abandons his child entirely, God has allowed for times and places where his presence is revealed. We have shabbos, shemitta, yovel, and the chagim set aside as times of special connection to God's presence. We have Israel, Jerusalem, and the Makom HaMikdash as places in which God's presence is more revealed. These places are holy because they are where we encounter God's presence through the concealment. This is achieved through elimination of our will, "just as God makes space for man by an act of self-limitation, so man makes space for God by an act of self-limitation." This is not because God does not value our will and initiative, this whole process is only necessary because God wants us to become partners in creation with our will and initiative.

However, we must be willing to let go of our will entirely if we want to experience God in the holy realm. Our initiative is the most powerful tool in fixing the world around us but in the world of the divine, it is an affront to the very system that enables it to exist.

To conflate the will of God with our own is to make the mistake that inspires war and conflict under the guise of religious dedication. We must approach God in a state of Bitul, where our desires and priorities are silent in the overwhelming presence of God.

Shabbat Shalom,

Noah
