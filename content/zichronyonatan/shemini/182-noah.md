This week's Parsha contains the tragic deaths of Nadav and Avihu and their father, Aaron's, reaction to hearing of the tragedy. Rashi explains:

And Aaron was silent — He received a reward for his silence. And what was the reward he received? That the subsequent Divine address was made to him alone and not to Moses also — for to him alone was spoken the section dealing with those who are intoxicated by wine.

In light of Rashi's praise for Aaron's silence, the Chasam Sofer contrasts Aaron's response to tragedy with that of Iyov and Rebbi Meir. Iyov, does not remain silent in the face of a tragedy like Aaron and instead proclaimed the famous phrase, "the Lord giveth, and the Lord taketh away" followed by a frequently forgotten benediction, "May the Lord's name be blessed." The Chasam Sofer explains that speaking was not a mistake on Iyov's part, in fact the next posuk makes this clear, "For all that, Iyov did not sin nor did he cast reproach on God." To understand the difference in mourning, the Chasam Sofer points us to the Midrash about Rebbi Meir.

'A valiant woman, who can find - They said, "There was a story about Rabbi Meir who was sitting and expounding in the study hall on Shabbat afternoon when two of his sons died. What did his mother do? She placed both of them on the bed and spread a sheet over them. At the end of Shabbat, Rabbi Meir came home from the study hall. He said to her, 'Where are my two sons?' She said [back], 'They went to the study hall.' He said to her, 'I scanned the study hall and I did not see them.' They gave him the cup of Havdalah and he separated [the days of the week with the closure of Shabbat]. He repeated and said, 'Where are my two sons?' She said, 'They went elsewhere and they are coming now.' She placed food in front of him and he ate and blessed. After he blessed, she said to him, 'I have a question to ask you.' He said to her, 'Say your question.' She said to him, 'Rabbi, before today, a man came and deposited something with me, and now he is coming to take it. Should we return it to him or not?'
He said, 'My daughter, one who has a deposit with him must return it to its owner.' She said to him, 'Were it not for your consent, I would not have given it to him.' What did she do? She grabbed his hand, brought him up to that room, had him approach the bed and took off the sheet from upon them. When he saw both of them dead and laying upon the bed, he began to cry and say, 'My sons, my sons, my teachers, my teachers - my sons in the way of the world, my teachers in that they would enlighten my eyes with their Torah.' At that time, she said to Rabbi Meir, 'Rabbi, is this not what I told you - do I not need to return the deposit to its Owner?' He said, '"The Lord has given and the Lord has taken; may the name of the Lord be blessed"' (Job 1:21)." Rabbi Chanina said, "With this thing, she consoled him and his mind became composed - that is is why it states, 'A valiant woman, who can find.'

The Chasam Sofer goes on to explain that a God-fearing mourner must adopt the attitude of a borrower that was lent a great treasure. He must appreciate the time he had with it, especially upon its return. He explains further, "Until the time of its return, the borrower doesn't appreciate the good and the good itself is barely noticeable. Only in the absence of a treasure can we perceive its true worth." This was the sentiment expressed by Iyov and Rebbi Meir when they cried out, "The Lord giveth and the Lord taketh away, may the name of the Lord be blessed." May God's name be blessed in appreciation of the original giving that I only can truly appreciate now that it is gone. This response to tragedy is the highest level as it prioritizes appreciation over grief. That's why Iyov's words were regarded with the same infallibility as Aaron's silence. But why isn't Aaron censured for not offering praise in this personal moment of bereavement as Iyov and Rebbi Meir could? The Chasam Sofer
explains that Aaron wanted to. He wanted to cry out in love and appreciation for the time he had with his beloved children. But Aaron knew that if he allowed himself to express appreciation for what he was given, it would inevitably lead him to resent that it was ever taken away. That's why Aaron's silence meant so much.

Everyone's lives have been turned upside down and people are losing loved ones all around us. It is very hard to know what to say and how to react in these times. I think the Chasam Sofer's message is perfectly applicable in this era of personal and communal suffering. While there has been so much taken away from us, jobs, hobbies, family gatherings, Shabbos meals, minyanim we have been given an opportunity to realize how lucky we were when they were a part of our lives. When we open our mouths to talk about this crisis it shouldn't be with expressions of complaint and reproach to God. Instead, we should address the situation with appreciation and recognition of the good that still permeates every aspect of our lives. And if we find it impossible to do this, to see the silver lining, to use absence to realize presence, to bless God on the giving even at the time of the taking, then we should follow in the way of Aaron HaKohen and remain silent.
