This week's Parsha is called Shemini, "the eighth." For seven days Moshe, Aharon, and the Jewish people inaugurated the Mishkan; on the eighth day Moshe and Aharon began their priestly duties, and Nadav and Avihu brought a "strange fire that G-d had not commanded," and were consumed by it.

We are also taught the laws of Kashrut -- land animals must have split hooves and chew their cud; fish must have fins and scales; certain birds and locusts are listed as kosher and non-kosher. We learn the laws of ritual impurity, and that immersion in a mikvah restores purity after contact with certain forms of death.

Nadav and Avihu's death was not punishment, but G-d granting their wish -- they sought a level of transcendence beyond what is possible for earthly beings, and their souls rose to rest beside G-d. While we all wish to be closer to G-d, their story teaches that transcendence is not a worthy pursuit unless used to bring others up and spread wisdom in the world; transcendence is a means, not an end.

Kashrut and ritual purity are deeply personal and physical -- what we eat, what we touch, where we go, all become tools for a transcendent consciousness. This Parsha teaches us how to manage ecstasy without destruction: instead of floating up like Nadav and Avihu, there are things we can do within our bodies to draw closer to G-d.
