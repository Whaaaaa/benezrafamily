Bnai Yisrael runs free from Mitzrayim, but Pharaoh, changing his mind, gives chase. At the sea, Moshe lifts his staff and the waters split; Israel crosses on dry land, protected on all sides, while the sea closes behind them to drown Pharaoh's army. Bnai Yisrael sings the Song of the Sea, which we still recite each morning.

But gratitude fades quickly in the desert -- the people grow hungry and thirsty and complain to Moshe. G-d sweetens the bitter waters of Marah, draws water from a rock, and sends quail and manna to nourish them, instructing that a double portion be gathered on Fridays since none will fall on Shabbat. At the Parsha's end, Amalek attacks, and thanks to Moshe's prayer and Yehoshua's army, Israel prevails.

This Shabbos is called Shabbat Shira, for Miriam's Song of the Sea. Miriam's name means "bitter" -- she was born into exile, watching her infant brother float down the Nile. Her unique experience as a woman in exile gave her song the power to move an entire nation into song and dance. Our tradition counts ten notable songs of redemption across Tanach; the midrash says a tenth, "new song," will one day mark the final redemption -- not merely a new melody, but an entirely new musical language we cannot yet imagine.

Each and every Jew alive today is the result of a miracle, and throughout life's challenges we learn which coping mechanisms carry us through. But if all we do is cope and forget to move forward, we remain in Egypt.
