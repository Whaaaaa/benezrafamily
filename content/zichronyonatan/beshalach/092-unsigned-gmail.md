After Israel's darkest exile Pharoh finally lets his slaves go. As Jews rush toward the border nobody is sure of their future. Will Pharoh change his mind and dispatch his army to catch up with the Jews at the Red Sea? Will the sea in fact split? How will we survive in the desert after our escape? Can we go to war? Can we just sell ourselves back into bondage? All this uncertainty is met by the most miraculous display of walls of water and columns of fire Egyptians and chariots drowning and Israel bursting into songs of praise. Women and men sing and dance with Miriam and her tambourine. We are led into the desert where manna falls from heaven and quail fly by slow and, around dinner time. We are clearly God's chosen people until Amalek cuts into our path where we are weak and all goes into question. Moses himself renames the spot on the path calling it "challenge and strife" as if to say "is God amongst us or not?"

This is not only the question Pharoh wanted to know before letting us go but it is also a question a quarter or more of the Jewish population wanted to know. For our sake God answered doubt with miracles; for Pharoh, he answered with plagues, and for us wonders and signs. At each moment of doubt, Pharaoh's heart grew colder, I can only assume that the quarter of jewish doubters also had icy hearts. There were those who said, "Let us throw ourselves into the sea." A second group said, "Let us return to Egypt." A third faction argued, "Let us wage war upon the Egyptians." Finally, a fourth camp advocated, "Let us pray to G-d."

When fear and doubt are rampant chaos ensues. When fear and doubt are used for political leverage this is exile and slavery. the consequences are long and drawn out and every day becomes a battle to remember being free and the promise of living a good life. However in the barracks of a slave camp hope is hard to find. Miriam and the women of israel are attributed with sustaining hope in the community. When moses was born his parents put him and his basket in the Nile hoping he may live a better life than their own. Miriam his older sister could not hold back her fears she had doubt and watched and followed the baby until he was in the right hands. Instead of accepting her tormented reality she saw it through. When women have a fear they don't turn around until they see a resolution. As a result they go through infinitely more suffering than those who hope for the best, this is why when resolution does come at the splitting of the sea she and the women sing a more elaborate praise to god because though she was afraid miriam stayed tuned with her hope for a new generation.

In the aftermath of our escape from bondage, we are met with fear in the desert. What is there to eat? God gives us manna but we are skeptical the term "mon" has a few translations, some say the word was adopted from Egyptian. "Mon" means "what?" no really that's what it means, so it is no wonder why god wanted us to eat our doubts before leading us to Sinai. It is also no wonder why some of us wanted Egyptian slave food, we cleave to our doubts as a source of freedom in times of inescapable dominance.

At this point jews have just witnessed the greatest, most out right miracle short of the giving of the Torah, likewise, the world had witnessed what God did for us. This gave us reassurance in our displacement but this confidence was not enough to scare amalek. This is the nature of doubt, some doubt comes logically with no motive, other doubt comes with the sole motive of clouding our rational mind. Amaleks doubt in our relationship with the creator is definitely not logical because nations stood witness to the signs and wonders of exile. Their doubt could not be coming from personal motives because we were a brand new slave nation with no political past. Amaleks desire to wage war is nothing more than a cynical shrug of skepticism, that shook us to our core. "To what is the incident (of Amalek) comparable? To a boiling tub of water which no creature was able to enter. Along came one evildoer and jumped into it. Although he was burned, he cooled it for the others. So, too, when Israel came out of Egypt, and G-d split the sea before them and drowned the Egyptians within it, the fear of them fell upon all the nations. But when Amalek came and challenged them, although he received his due from them, he cooled the awe of the nations of the world for them." We are at war for generations with Amalek because what they represent is the archenemy of Jewish thought. Nobody is safe from baseless doubt when faced with fear without a bond to their creator.

Moses's response to each jews uncertainty at the shore is befitting. To those who wanted to jump into the ocean in a leap of faith moshe pointed out that even if it worked it would only work for them, these men are tzadikim their trust absolute yet entirely impractical unless everyone levels up. They live in a spiritual atlantis.

The stockholm Jew thinks it is great to be a chosen people but we are a minority if we want representation we need pharaohs political regard. It will be hard having two masters we will give it a try.

The warrior Jew does not isolate or hide from his wretched reality. He knows his path is not only just, but "chosen". He's got a holy chip on his shoulder and battles sinners, jew -haters, un- jewish-jews and non-fighting jews alike. He will knock some sense into the world that spat him out.

The spiritualist when thrown into an ocean of doubt takes a moment to pray. They are not quick to try to defy nature, yet they believe there is a place for jews beyond the constraints of exile, he does not violently tread water in an attempt to rise out of the ocean victorious. He takes a pause and reminds himself life in the future gets better, we prefer the law and order the torah puts forth and fighting anything ends up damaging one's self. So the spiritualist prays and when the order comes down from heaven we all go in unison. This is a miracle less because of the wonders and more because of our survival, when one's very existence goes into question our trust in prayers is challenged, if we seize the opportunity and name our situation and desired outcome no matter what comes into play seems predestined and miraculous. Maybe this is why we were attacked by amalek, by God allowing the amalekites to attack our caravan he made us reaffirm the ground we are standing on and often that is all it takes to witness a miracle. Good Shabbos!
