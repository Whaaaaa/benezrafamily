The spies returned from touring the land of Israel with a stunning report, as far as the bounty was concerned. They carried samples of the fruit of the land; fruits which required multiple men just to lift. And they told of a land literally flowing with milk and honey, a land lush and beautiful beyond belief.

But then, they made an abrupt about-face, declaring unequivocally that the plans to invade must be forsaken. True, it was a beautiful land, but the inhabitants were incredibly powerful giants living in fortified cities.

לא נוכל לעלות – 'We cannot go up.' It is too dangerous, too difficult, and we are too weak and frail.

Only Yehoshua and Kolev dared stand up to the spies' bleak forecast, as Kolev responded עלה נעלה כי יכול נוכל, 'We shall rise up, for then we will surely be capable.' His point was clear. We are capable of exactly how much we perceive ourselves to be. If we give up in the night, we'll never see the light of day, but if we will only choose to fight then, with Hashem at our side, there is no limit to what we can accomplish.

The message of the spies, their call of despair and hopelessness, is not a one-time event in history, but the oft-repeated attack of the yetzer hora, the evil inclination, which takes many forms throughout the generations.

At a great many junctures in our lives and in our national history, we face tests and trials which seem insurmountable. We may see the opportunity for greatness contained in overcoming the challenge, but we lose hope of being able to rise to the occasion. There's a voice, mocking and derisive, telling us lo nuchal la'alos – you can't, you won't, and you shouldn't even try.

Our job is to recognize the yetzer hora of the spies again rearing its ugly head, and to identify that this is nothing new, but the age-old cry of lo nuchal la'alos. And as we distinguish exactly who the source of that voice is, we are empowered to respond with the secret that Kolev once entrusted us: alo na'aleh ki yachol nuchal.

We can. And we will. If we'll only endeavor to try.
