This week's Torah reading is Parshas Shlach, literally translated as 'Send'. The Jews are in the desert after receiving the Torah and are heading toward the Land of Israel. As they are traveling, doubt begins to bubble to the surface and questions plague their minds.
What if the land is not fruitful? What if the inhabitants prove to be too difficult to fight and conquer?
They express these fears to the leader, Moshe, who answers, "send for yourselves spies so they may check out the land and see that it is good."
Moshe had hoped that when they saw how comfortable he would be with them investigating the land, they would gain confidence that there is nothing to be afraid of. However, that was not the case, and a representative from each tribe was tasked with infiltrating the communities and evaluating the strength of their soon-to-be opponents.
While traveling the Land of Israel, they encounter a family of giants. The spies make a very interesting statement. They say, "we were in their eyes like grasshoppers".
They bring this back to the rest of the Jewish people in their report, stating that the men who live there are so big and strong, it would be impossible to defeat them.
I'd like to explore an interesting matter. The spies contend that in the eyes of the giants, they were as small and insignificant as insects.
But how would they know how they appeared to the giants? We actually learn that the mountainous men in question were busy arranging a funeral and didn't spare a glance at the representatives of the tribes.
The verse continues with the words, "and so we were in our own eyes". Once they believed that this was how the giants saw them, that was how they then saw themselves.
The mind has a way of convincing ourselves that we know how other people perceive us.
We assume that others have a certain judgment of us and feel we are confined by those conclusions. We give them credence and an ability to confine us to those barriers.

There is a solution though. When we realize that we don't know and we learn not to care what other people think about us, we remove those shackles and allow our wings to fly over the gates that jail us in.
There is someone who we were privileged to see putting this into practice. Uncle Yonatan truly did not care what others thought of him. He wanted to be comfortable. He wanted to do the right thing. He wanted to push himself to be better. And he never once checked if anyone was watching.
May we all learn to live lives that are not dictated by what society expects of us, but what we know is right in our hearts.

Good shabbos, Shira
