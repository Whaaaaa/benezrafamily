The story of the spies is one of the most painful in the history of our people. We were on the brink of entering the Holy Land while still under the leadership of Moshe, and our sages tell us that had he brought us into the land we would have immediately achieved the purpose of creation. Had Moshe been at the helm as we entered the land of Israel, the temple would never have been destroyed and the Jews would never again have tasted exile. All of that was snatched literally out from under our noses with the sin of the spies. Tragedy of tragedies.

But perhaps the most troubling part of the whole story is that we received permission from the Almighty to send them. Admittedly it discouraged, but Hashem did acquiesce to the nation's request to send spies. How could our omnipotent Father, fully knowing the destruction which would result from such an initiative, have given us the go-ahead?

Rabbi Moshe Feinstein offers an incredible explanation that is highly relevant to each of us in our own lives. And, as with all good Torah answers, he prefaces with another question.

The generation of the Exodus was privy to the most incredible and undeniable revelations of G-d's power in all of history. They saw the ten plagues, which demonstrated Hashem's absolute mastery over all aspects of creation. They themselves marched through the Sea of Reeds upon dry land. And they personally witnessed the giving of the Torah at Sinai. These are all the cornerstones of emunah, or Jewish belief, which provide the base for Jews throughout the ages to believe in G-d and His Torah. And they didn't merely hear these things from their fathers, as is the case for the rest of history, but rather they saw it with their own eyes. We would hence expect that they would be the paragon believers in Hashem and His boundless power.

But countless stories in the Torah show a lacking in their emunah that begs an explanation. Let's take just two examples. The first is the episode in last week's parsha, when the Jews, unsatisfied with the angelic food called Manna, demanded to be fed meat. Our sages tell us that this was not a simple craving for a steak, as they had plenty of cattle from which they could have eaten. It was rather a challenge – to see if Hashem could really supply them with meat or if He was r"l limited to Manna. After all, they had seen, they were somehow still unconvinced that this feat was within heavenly reach.

Likewise, right here in the story of the spies the Jewish people were scared of a war with the nations of Canaan. They had seen Hashem disrupt every facet of the very formidable Egyptian society, all without the Jews lifting so much as a finger. They witnessed as the entire might and majesty of Egypt drown miraculously in the Sea of Reeds. And yet somehow all of this didn't equip them with the belief that He could just as handily quash the armies of Canaan.

How are we to understand such a seemingly incongruent worldview?

The answer, says Rabbi Feinstein, lies in the miraculous fashion by which they came to believe in G-d. Because they had witnessed such open miracles which left no room for doubt, and because they were served emunah on a silver platter, they therefore never had the need to invest their own thought and feelings into that belief. When we receive knowledge or belief without having a personal stake in them, they remain on the surface and never penetrate to the depths of our identities. As such, their emunah was limited to exactly that which they had seen, but anything beyond their prior knowledge was clouded in uncertainty. They knew Hashem could provide Manna because they had seen it, but perhaps meat functioned differently. They knew Hashem could defeat a single, albeit mighty King, but they were unsure about the prospects of the many Kings of Canaan.

This state of belief could not possibly remain intact throughout the generations; they would have to spread their own wings and work on their own emunah in order to give it an element of permanence. It was for this reason that Hashem allowed the Jews to send spies, because after they would fall they would finally need to work on themselves and acquire their own well-founded belief which would persevere even in areas which they had not yet witnessed. In their teshuvah process, they would need to realize their error and invest in themselves to become confident of Hashem's ability to conquer the nations of Canaan. Only in this way would the Jewish nation be able to remain strong throughout all the ages.

May we learn the lesson of the spies and commit to put our whole selves into everything we learn, never accepting knowledge on a silver platter but rather working to fully acquire and incorporate our learning into our deepest selves. And especially in the arena of belief in G-d, let us not be satisfied with the superficial lip-service which is so prevalent, but toil always to make it part and parcel of who we are.

Good Shabbos,
