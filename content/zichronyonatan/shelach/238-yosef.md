When the spies came back from their tour of the Land of Israel they brought an ill report. Gathering the people, the spies began to viciously discourage them from continuing into the land. They told of giants and fortified cities, saying that their small nation would never be able to overcome such enemies. At that point, Kalev stood up and silenced the crowd, convincing them that there was hope and that with Hashem's help they would indeed conquer the land. Yet his accomplishment was incredibly short-lived. In the very next verse, the spies repeated their message and won back the hearts of the people, ultimately succeeding in turning them against Moshe and G-d.

From our perspective, it would seem that Kalev accomplished nothing. His efforts were heroic, but ultimately a failure. That short moment where he refuted the spies had no lasting effect and was forgotten almost as soon as he finished speaking. Yet we find that Hashem took great pleasure in Kalev's actions, even granting him the holy land of Hebron (the burial place of our forefathers) as a personal estate.

Why is it that Hashem saw this as such a great deed when we see it as no more than an act of futility?

I'd like to answer with a parable from real life:

Picture a mother as she watches her precious baby turn over for the first time. She is overjoyed! Whooping from excitement and smiling ear-to-ear, she calls her various relatives to share the news. "The baby turned over!" In the course of the child's development, this scene repeats itself, again and again, the first smile, the first word, the first step, and each time the mother's joy and excitement are boundless.

Why? Doesn't she know that these are standard things that almost every child achieves; small achievements in the scale of things?

The answer is that when it comes to your child every small milestone is reason for celebration. Because of our overwhelming love for our children, even a small step in the right direction brings us immeasurable happiness.

We are all children of the Almighty. In fact, our sages tell us that Hashem, in his infinite capacity to love, views each and every one of us as his only child. We should know that just as we are overjoyed with the smallest improvement that our children achieve, so too Hashem derives immense joy from even the smallest steps we take to become better people.

This is why Hashem so appreciated Kalev's short-lived accomplishment. Yes, Kalev only saved the people from sin for a tiny amount of time, but for that minute the Jews reaffirmed their loyalty to G-d. They fell back down a minute later, but for that moment they made a baby step in the right direction and our loving Father derives immeasurable joy even from our baby steps. So much so that Kalev, who facilitated that baby step, was deserving of great honor and the priceless gift of Hebron.

Often, we have a desire to improve, but we tell ourselves that we can never be good enough anyway. We let these thoughts lull us into complacency, thinking that if we can't achieve perfection we might as well just stay how we are. Let us think of our own children's baby steps and how proud they make us, and let us know that Hashem is all the happier with our smallest achievements. Every small improvement is worth millions to our loving Father. Let us empower ourselves to eat the elephant one bite at a time and step by baby step we can become better and bring G-d and ourselves so much pride.

I believe it is obvious how such an outlook will not only encourage us to make those baby steps, but in the end, all those baby steps will add up to true greatness and we will truly accomplish things we never dreamed we could.

Good Shabbos,
Yosef
