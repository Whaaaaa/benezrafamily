This week’s Parsha, Chayei Sarah, marks the death of our primary matriarch. In his essay on this week’s Parsha, Rabbi Sacks ZT’L describes Avraham’s reaction to the death of his partner:

“Sarah, his constant companion throughout their journeys, has died. He is 137 years old. We see him mourn Sarah’s death, and then he moves into action. He engages in an elaborate negotiation to buy a plot of land in which to bury her. As the narrative makes clear, this is not a simple task.”

When great people face tragedy, they respond with action. In this same piece, Rabbi Sacks contrasts the differences in detail between the purchase of Ma'arat haMachpelah and Yitzchak's hunt for a wife with the Akedah. While the former is described in elaborate detail, the latter leaves much up to interpretation. His explanation of the difference is that Avraham’s purchase of land and the betrothal of his daughter were fulfillments of covenants that he had already made with God and that they carry extra significance. When Sarah dies Avraham realizes that the legacy he was promised, abundant land and children, is far from reality. Others would throw their hands up at age 127 and absolve themselves of further responsibility. Lesser men would blame God for not upholding his end of the bargain and allow themselves to turn bitter. But not Avraham.

Avraham understood that a covenant isn’t a promise so much as it is a relationship. As Rabbi Sacks so eloquently explains, “God promises, but we have to act. God promised Abraham the land, but he had to buy the first field. God promised Abraham many descendants, but Abraham had to ensure that his son was married, and to a woman who would share the life of the covenant, so that Abraham would have, as we say today, “Jewish grandchildren.”

This perspective on covenant transforms a concept that can be used to buck responsibility, into one that mandates we do all we can to make God’s will a reality in our lives. This approach is both profoundly inspiring and intensely demanding. We are required to strive for Godly results while simultaneously being confident in human means of achieving them. This is the attitude of leaders. As Rabbi Sack explains, “That is what leaders understand, and it is what made Abraham the first Jewish leader. Leaders take responsibility for creating the conditions through which God’s purposes can be fulfilled. They are not passive but active – even in old age, like Abraham in this week’s parsha.”

Rabbi Sacks was among the Jewish leaders that had deeply internalized this teaching. Until months before his death, he was still writing, speaking, and teaching Torah. He was not afraid to wade into conflict when he felt it was necessary for the community and stayed incredibly active in Jewish life until his final days. His teachings reached millions and his reputation and renown were an unparalleled kiddush hashem.

Throughout this year that has been so full of loss Avraham’s response to the death of Sarah can be a guiding light. If we use loss to inspire us to achieve and to tap into our personal covenants with God we can strive to become great leaders as Avraham and Rabbi Sacks were, each in their own time.
