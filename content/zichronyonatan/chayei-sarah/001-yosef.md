When a person arrives in a new town and is looking for a kosher place to eat, it becomes readily apparent just how seriously he takes his religion. A person who is marginally observant might be satisfied with a simple sign in the window reading 'kosher'. One who is more observant would probably insist on seeing an actual certificate from the local Kashrus Agency. And a truly devout man might not even be satisfied with that, but would rather ask the local Rabbi where the highest level of Kosher food can be found.

But let's turn the subject at hand to something more worldly. After grabbing a bite to eat, our friend begins looking for a venue in which to invest ten-thousand dollars. Could we imagine him seeing an ad in the paper with the simple statement 'guaranteed returns' and promptly wiring over the money? Of course not! Money is something everyone takes seriously and you can rest assured that our friend, no matter who he is, will not part with his hard-earned cash before doing some serious research about the trustworthiness and track-record of the said investment.

Unfortunately, this clearly illustrates that our spiritual well-being is something that we take much less seriously than our material standing.

So let's try to learn a proper outlook from the very best -- our forefather Avraham.

Avraham sent his slave Eliezer to search for a suitable match for his son Yitzchak. Now, Eliezer was not an ordinary slave but a man of incredible piety and trustworthiness. So much did Avraham rely on Eliezer, that he is described as the one who governed everything in Avraham's colossal estate. Not only that but when he was sent to find a match, Avraham handed him a document of ownership for everything, intended to show the value of the estate to the prospective match. Throughout all of this, with such a huge amount of money on the line, Avraham didn't ask for any sort of security -- he trusted Eliezer implicitly.

However, later in the same conversation, when Avraham expressed his desire that the match not be from the daughters of Canaan, we find something very peculiar. This was a matter that concerned the spiritual well-being of his son Yitzchak, as the Canaanites were known to be a people severely lacking in character. And so, after casually handing all of his worldly possessions over, he suddenly grew wary that something may go wrong. Now Avraham insisted that Eliezer swear to him that he would follow his directions and not take a Canaanite girl. For spiritual matters, only an oath would suffice.

Here we see what a proper perspective of life should be. Money and all worldly matters are things of temporal value, good for only a few decades and relatively unimportant in the grand scheme of things. But Torah and mitzvot are things of eternal value, things that can put us in good standing forever! So let us try to keep things in perspective and attach at least as much importance to our Judaism as we attach to our livelihood.
