“I'm too young.” “I'm too old.” “I'm way too busy.” “I just had a baby!” “Now is definitely not the right time.” We all have plenty – very legitimate – excuses why now is not the ideal time for us to involve ourselves in spiritual growth.

In this week's Parshah, the Torah eulogizes our matriarch Sarah, by saying:

“Sarah's lifetime was one hundred years, twenty years, and seven years; the years of Sarah's life.” (Breishit, 23:1)

Why doesn't the verse simply state that Sarah's lifetime was 127 years? Why must we split each group of years into its own number? The Ramban explains that each group surpassed the group preceding it – the last twenty years found her at greater spiritual heights than the previous hundred, and the last seven found her even greater than the previous 120.

What an incredible lesson! How often do we give up on ourselves and the potential greatness that we can achieve? How often do we tell ourselves “Oh, I'm too old, what can I accomplish at this point?” How often do we assume that the years in which we could have attained greatness surely have long passed? Yet, we learn from Sarah that such an excuse is not at all valid! At the age of 120, she continued to work on herself and grow to new levels of spiritual achievement!

The Ramban further explains that the seemingly superfluous use of the phrase “the years of Sarah's life,” is written to teach us that all of the years of Sarah's life were equally good. The phrase “equally good years” obviously cannot refer to her actual spiritual level, since that level was constantly changing for the better. Rather, the Chasam Sofer explains, the phrase refers to the diligence that Sarah applied to her spiritual growth. Sarah's years were equally good in that she approached every age and stage with the same excitement and zeal for growth. Every year of her life, from the time she was a child to the time she was an old woman of 127, she climbed the rungs of her spiritual ladder, climbing higher and closer to G-d. Her life was one of consistent and unrelenting growth.

The Chasam Sofer writes that the concept of continual spiritual growth is not relegated to only great people of the past, but is a fundamental requirement of every single Jew.

From the countless, inspiring stories that I've heard from Yosef, Uncle Yonatan exemplified this attribute of consistency in growth. No matter where he was, or what challenges came his way, he steadfastly continued to grow in his service of G-d. He never used circumstantial excuses to absolve him of spiritual obligations, and went to great extents to serve G-d in the most difficult situations.

Let's learn from Uncle Yonatan to follow Sarah's example and constantly work on ourselves, taking baby steps to climb the ladder closer to G-d, no matter what situations we are in.
