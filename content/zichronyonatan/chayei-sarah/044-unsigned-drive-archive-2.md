When one door closes another door opens... "Well if that's the case, I'm not buying the car!" "The Life of Sarah," Uncle Jonathan's bar mitzvah parsha, opens with the death of Sarah, Avraham finding a burial space for her, remarrying Hagar and finding a spouse for Isaac. All seemingly mundane things considering the families recently turbulent path. Avram had just marched his son off to be sacrificed and the Midrash explains this is the very thing that killed Sarah, like any good mother- she died worrying about her son. Why don't we call the parsha "The Death of Sarah?

Sarah spent a lifetime traveling and spreading the word of monotheism side by side with Avram, despite her early inability to bear a child. Upon her death, many long-time goals were established in ways that we still see today. With Sarah's infertility, the Jewish population was running thin, to say the least, without her endeavoring Abraham's prayer that they begat Isaac the Jewish family tree would have ended. After birthing at 91 Sarah could not have lived to see Issac be the last of her lineage; that why in this very parsha Yitzchak marries Rebbeca, who our sages say had similar virtues of Sarah. Our parsha acts as an extension of a life whose story will be everlasting. Sarah's prosperity perpetuates at Maarat Hamachpelah as her burial plot and much more. This is the first time a jew acquires property in the holy land once again making the promise Hashem made into reality.

The gap between heaven and earth was so distant in their generation that Avraham is accredited for bridging it. He was known as the lone Hebrew or "Ivri" - literally "from the other side". He made it his life's work to oppose popular theory and push monotheism. Rashi says that Avram acknowledges his accomplishment while speaking to his servant Eliezer. After Avraham assures Eliezer that he will succeed in finding a wife for Issac he says: The L-rd, G-d of the heavens, Who took me from my father's house and from the land of my birth, and Who spoke about me, and Who swore to me, saying, "To your seed will I give this land," He will send His angel before you, and you shall take a wife for my son from there. Whereas in an earlier verse he called God by "the G-d of the heaven and the G-d of the earth." Rashi writes that Avraham was telling Eliezer that when he left from his birthplace to go to Canaan, God was only of the heavens; Because his name was not on the tongues, and his Torah not in the minds of the generation god remained bound away. Avraham managed to break away from idol worship and go off on a limb and open a new door. From the moment God tells him to leave his home, he is on God's mission totally binding himself and family to his goals and G-d. Anyone who was privileged to know our uncle grew an interest in understanding uncle, in many ways he was the lone Hebrew defining jews every kiddush Hashem along the way. The man from the other side only put himself there to grow. Uncle continues to live on like Sarah, but like god himself will only be in heaven if we do not hinge our lives to his will.

This week's parsha describes Avraham as old and advanced in years. We see other people called old or advanced in years but Avraham's maturity paralleled his age. His lifestyle moved with the calendar with his excitement toward the next good day, his growth was circular. Passing points of encounter, enveloping every practice. Yes we can learn from others and yes we can get ahead of ourselves - this is why we do not endure like our fathers. Our peak experiences, the ones we mention on Yom Kippur are only glimpses into outliers, to learn from ourselves we must take a step back and seek a smooth steady familiar path opening every door along the way. The early Avram was not familiar with god he went door to door questioning the sun, moon, and earth and found nothing remained. Through a driven routine, we find ourselves in uncharted territory, learning and growing.
