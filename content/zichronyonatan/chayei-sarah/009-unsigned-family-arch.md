"When one door closes another door opens..." "Well if that's the case, I'm not buying the car!" "The Life of Sarah," Uncle Jonathan's bar mitzvah parsha, opens with the death of Sarah, Avraham finding a burial space for her, remarrying Hagar, and finding a spouse for Isaac. All seemingly mundane things considering the family's recently turbulent path -- Avraham had just marched his son off to be sacrificed, and the Midrash explains this is the very thing that killed Sarah; like any good mother, she died worrying about her son.

"The years of Sarah's life" could not all have been equally good -- she spent years barren, was abducted and held captive twice -- so how can Rashi say her years were equally good? The Gemara tells of Rabbi Nachum Ish Gam Zu, whose gifts of jewels were stolen and replaced with sand by thieves; when he discovered it he simply said, "This too is for the best" -- and the sand was later used, miraculously, as munitions that won a war. When Rashi tells us Sarah's years were "equally good," he means she took her challenges in stride, able to say "this too is for the best" amid serious trouble.

Avraham is called the lone Hebrew, "Ivri" -- literally "from the other side" -- for making it his life's work to oppose the idolatry of his generation. Anyone who was privileged to know our uncle grew an interest in understanding him; in many ways he was the lone Hebrew, defining what it means to be a Jew in every kiddush Hashem along the way. Uncle continues to live on like Sarah -- but, like G-d Himself, only in heaven if we do not hinge our own lives to his example.

May we all find the strength and fortitude to emulate the resilient Jews who came before us.
