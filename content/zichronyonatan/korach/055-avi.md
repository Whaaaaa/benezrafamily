This week's Parsha is Korach, in this familiar story of rebellion, Korach and his 250 statesmen challenge Aharon's priesthood, claiming, "why take all the holiness for the Kohanim when the whole nation is holy?" Moshe suggests a test: both sides offer incense, and whoever's is accepted holds the priesthood. Before the test, Moshe tells G-d, "I have not taken a donkey from a single one of them, and I have not harmed a single one of them."

At first glance, Moshe seems to be defending himself -- but the rabbis explain the opposite: Moshe, secure in his own credibility, is instead admitting he could have done more to win Korach's crew over. He had never interacted with them personally, never done business or asked favors -- and he acknowledges to G-d that this very distance may have contributed to the rebellion.

Moshe's reaction teaches us how to respond when people around us make poor choices -- if we bear some responsibility for them, that may include accepting some share of the blame. It is easy to see people make bad decisions and conclude they are simply bad people; it is much harder to examine the circumstances that led them there.

Judging people favorably is a core tenet of Jewish practice, even toward those who oppose us. Moshe teaches us not to avoid such people, but to intertwine our lives with theirs -- to understand what led them to believe as they do, and, if we hold any influence, to try to improve their circumstances. By judging our enemies favorably, we humanize them, dampen tension, and address issues before they become disasters.

When Uncle Yonaton taught me for my Bar Mitzvah, he stressed: if you had been there, it would have been hard to know it was even a rebellion -- Korach was learned, and at times in history his tribe had legitimate claims to priesthood. G-d instructs that each tribal leader place a staff before Him, with Aharon's staff blossoming, blooming, and bearing ripe almonds overnight, passing through every natural stage an almond branch would take on the tree itself.

The Rebbe distinguishes two types of miracles -- confrontational ones that bypass nature, and harmonious ones that work through it. A miracle that works through nature has the power to permanently rewrite what we understand nature to be. Good Shabbos! -- Avi
