Following the story of the spies, which ended with a decree to wander in the desert for forty years, a rebellion began to rise. The rebel group was led by Korach, a very great man and a close cousin of Moshe himself. He gathered a group of followers and plotted, in the name of righteousness, to dethrone Moshe. In his mind, Moshe had abused his power and he truly believed that Hashem Himself would choose him in place of Moshe. In fact, he was so confident of Hashem's approval that he knowingly brought an incense offering which carried a heavenly death sentence if brought by anyone other than Hashem's personal elect. We all know the end of the story; the ground opened up and swallowed Korach and his entire assembly, thus abruptly ending the rebellion and restoring authority to Moshe.

I once heard an intriguing question. We know that the Jews in the desert were sustained by the manna which fell outside their tents each morning. This manna didn't fall by chance but was carefully placed in varying proximity to each person's tent. It's location depended on one thing only – the spiritual standing of the recipient. The more righteous one was, the closer it fell, and vice versa. Let's imagine Korach's experience. Before he initiated the rebellion, the manna surely fell right outside his door, as befitting such an incredibly great man. But with each thought or action he took against Moshe, the manna fell further and further away. After such a clear sign that his mission was against the will of Hashem, how could he possibly still have thought that he was acting in the name of G-d?

Rabbi Chaim Kanievsky, he should live and be well, gives a chilling explanation. Of course Korach realized that Hashem was upset with him, but he was so blinded by self-righteousness, that he couldn't possibly believe that his mission was evil. To uphold his facade as the champion of good, he chose to believe that Hashem was chastising him for *not fighting hard enough!* As the manna fell further and further from his tent, he only redoubled his 'holy' efforts to overthrow Moshe.

We see here a terrifying portrayal of just how much one can be blinded if his worldview is somewhat skewed. Such a person can be the flag-bearer of all that is evil, and never for a minute doubt that he is a servant of G-d. Even the purest of intentions, when improperly guided, can lead a man to destroy himself, to destroy his family, and to bring the whole world down with him.

And the examples of such are endless. Each of the greatest atrocities in history were committed in the name of righteousness. The crusaders, Hitler, Stalin, and all the greatest villains lived out their lives and indeed died believing all the while that they stood for the good of humanity. They lacked not good intentions, but the values and wisdom to properly guide those intentions.

Of course, on our own we are almost doomed to such a fate. One cannot pull himself out of the pit, and it is nearly impossible to perceive our own faults. For this we must subject ourselves to the timeless knowledge of the Torah and to the guidance of our Rabbis and teachers.

Let us not repeat the greatest mistakes of history, but rather learn this lesson now. Let us recommit to learning and acquiring a proper set of values, to seek guidance from those greater than us, and to never stop questioning the sincerity of our own intentions.

*Good Shabbos,*

*Yosef*
