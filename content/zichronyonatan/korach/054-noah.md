The parsha begins, "And Korach took..." -- our introduction to arguably the most infamous mutiny in our people's desert wanderings. Korach incites a rebellion against Moshe and Aharon's leadership, joined by 250 distinguished members of the community. The commentators are puzzled by the verse's opening: "took" what, exactly? The verb has no object.

To understand this, we must consider the mindset of a generation constantly on the move, with little control over its own future, prone to projecting instability onto every gift given to them by G-d. It was amidst this anxious backdrop that Korach rose. Unlike quieter critics, Korach challenged loudly, publicly, with derision -- and unlike marginal firebrands, he was wealthy and socially distinguished, which greatly amplified an otherwise questionable message.

The Talmud says of Korach that "riches were hoarded to the misfortune of their owner" -- his wealth could not buy him the spiritual status he actually craved, and it was this thwarted greed that drove him to threaten the foundation of his own nation. Rabbi Eliyahu Munk explains what Korach actually "took": the feelings of envy and resentment of his followers. His outrage was not his own -- he echoed the legitimate grievances of each disadvantaged faction around him to build support for his own self-serving agenda. Each group heard exactly what it needed to hear, and saw only a wealthy, fearless advocate, never a conman.

Had the Israelites been more content and secure, they might have been less susceptible to scapegoating. Had Korach appreciated his own good fortune, he might not have coveted what he could not have. But perhaps most importantly, had Korach's comrades remained critical and genuinely examined his platform, they might have seen an advocate was, in fact, a fraud.

We should all merit to be content, appreciative, and always critical of authority.
