Did you ever hear of the great ladder which stands right here on earth but reaches into the highest heavens?

In last week's Parsha, we read about Yaakov taking the blessings from his father. Now, Yaakov's brother Esav, who feels that the blessings rightfully belong to him, vows to kill Yaakov. Yaakov follows the advice of his mother Rivkah to flee to her brother Lavan's house. However, despite being Rivkah's brother, Lavan is hardly cut from the same cloth. Rivkah is the paradigm of sanctity while Lavan is one of the most depraved men in history; a swindler and idolater of the highest order. His house is a place of great tribulation for Yaakov, as it defies every ethic he holds dear.

As Yaakov sets out on this most perilous of journeys, he has a prophetic dream. In the dream, he sees a great ladder, rooted on earth, yet rising all the way to the heavens, with angels ascending and descending upon it.

What is the lesson of this prophecy, and what message was it meant to convey to Yaakov? Rabbi Shlomo Wolbe explains that this dream was meant to teach Yaakov, as well as all of his future descendants, that besides for doing all of the mitzvos, there is another task with which we are charged. That is the task of always climbing, always growing, and never but never resting on our laurels. A Jew can never look at himself and say he's satisfied with his spiritual level -- that there's no more need to grow. Rather, we must always continue climbing the great ladder of life in an eternal ascent heavenwards.

One may ask, isn't this is a lesson vital to be learned at the very beginning of life, as one starts his journey through the world? Why was Yaakov told this now, when he was already sixty years old?

The answer is that Yaakov was well aware of this concept long before his dream, but as he set off towards the spiritually depraved house of Lavan, this lesson took on new meaning. There was ample room to think that in the house of his father, Yitzchok, where there was such an abundance of godliness, it was necessary to constantly absorb all the growth that was so available to him. But here, as he entered into a spiritual wasteland, to the hands of one bent on destroying everything Yaakov stood for, here it would be sufficient to maintain the level where he stood.

It is for this reason that specifically here came the message that even under such duress, there can be no such thing as staying put. Even here, Yaakov was charged with continuing to steadily climb that ladder. Even against the most fearful tests, one may face in life it is imperative to ever strive for greatness. And even here the ladder still reaches the highest heavens, if one will only continue to climb.

But the lesson goes further. Not only can one achieve greatness despite being in a difficult situation but actually it is only through difficulty that one can attain true growth. Specifically in the house of Lavan was Yaakov able to reach his full and awesome potential. It is only through standing up to these great tests that Yaakov achieved such spiritual greatness as to have his image engraved on God's Throne of Glory.

There's a beautiful story that I always remember when thinking of this concept of growth through hardship:

Amir, an Israeli teenager who had recently become a baal teshuva, avidly attended Rabbi Shalom Shwadron's nightly class. Over time he became quite close with the Rabbi, and there was almost nothing in the world that could make him miss the class. It, therefore, came as quite a surprise when suddenly he missed the class for a few days in a row without any call to the Rabbi. Concerned, Rabbi Shwadron went to Amir's house one afternoon to inquire after him. Amir greeted the Rabbi sheepishly and fumbled out an explanation for his absence. He explained to the Rabbi that he was an avid soccer fan, and this week was the championship, being held in Israel. He said that he just couldn't bring himself to miss out on one of the games, but he assured the Rabbi that he would be back next week with all of his previous enthusiasm.

Rabbi Shwadron, known for his wit, asked the Amir if he could please explain to him what soccer is, so he could better understand its great draw. Amir excitedly told the Rabbi how there are two teams on a field, each one with a goal, and one ball which they each try to kick into the other team's goal. Hearing that the goal was quite large, the Rabbi said that it sounded far too easy to score. Amir told the Rabbi that it wasn't so simple because there was a goalie whose job it was to block any attempt to get the ball inside. The Rabbi laughed and told the boy confidently that he could easily score a hundred points. He'd outsmart the other team and go to the field when nobody was around; that way there would be no goalie and he'd easily win the game. Amir tried to hide his amusement and patiently explained that this would miss the whole point -- you can only score when the goalie is blocking the goal.

The Rabbi smiled and told Amir to listen what he himself was saying. In life, we have many things that we must accomplish. Sometimes we wish that we could achieve all of them through peace and serenity, living on easy street with it all coming our way. But that's just not the way things are. Next week, when the championship is over, the Rabbi explained, it will be easy to come to class. But if you wait until it's easy, you haven't gotten yourself anywhere at all. If you want to grow and achieve greatness, then it is specifically when it's difficult that you have to show your mettle, step up to the test, and fight tooth and nail to reach greatness. After all, you can only score when the goalie is blocking the goal.

Uncle Yonatan personified this great lesson. Throughout his life, he faced countless challenges which would have broken a lesser man ten times over. Yet Uncle knew the secret. He knew that in the face of pain and suffering, in the face of trial and tribulation, the only way to go is up. He knew how to utilize every test to strive higher and higher still. He taught us the power one man can wield and the incredible effect he can have on his whole world. I pray that we be able to learn his lesson and bring him pride, as we continue the path he set us on -- growing through our challenges and never ceasing to reach for the stars.
