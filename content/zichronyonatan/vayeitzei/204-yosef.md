The Midrash records a puzzling conversation between Yaakov and Leah, immediately following their marriage. Yaakov questions Leah's integrity, asking how she could have deceived him by saying that she was Rachel, to which Leah responds that he had also lied when he told his father that he was Esav.

At face value, this would seem like a petty argument between two children – a tit for a tat. It is obvious that such great people could never have engaged in such an immature squabble, and it behooves us to understand the deeper intent here.

Let us begin with another question. We know that already in the womb Yaakov was attracted to the study halls and Esav to the houses of idolatry, but this appears to run contrary to the basic Jewish premise of free will. How could it be that still before birth Yaakov was naturally righteous and Esav was naturally evil?

I heard a beautiful explanation from my Rosh Kollel, Rabbi Shmuel Fasman shlit”a. He explained that Yaakov is described as a 'yoshev ohalim', one who dwells in the tents of Torah, whereas Esav is deemed the 'ish sadeh', the man of the field. These descriptions bring out the calling of each brother and his potential to perform the will of Hashem – each in his respective domain. Yaakov, as the dweller of the tent, was charged with the service of aseh tov (doing good) by proactively pursuing Torah and mitzvos in their natural setting. Esav, however, was to serve Hashem in the field, representing the physicality of this world. There he was charged with the service of sur mei'ra (avoiding evil) by engaging and triumphing over the lusts and desires of this world.

Each brother was equally equipped and expected to reach the highest levels of righteousness, albeit from two opposing realms and through different approaches. This is the explanation of Yaakov and Esav's fetal attractions, each towards their own domain of service. Esav's urge to engage the house of idolatry was not a display of any innate evil, but simply a yearning to face his calling.

But on the fateful day when Esav came back from that proverbial 'field', he is described as ayef, worn out. This means that his reservoirs of strength had been depleted and he would no longer be able to fulfill his destiny as man of the field; he was already overcome by the very physicality which he was meant to defeat. When Yaakov saw that Esav could no longer play the part which was meant for him, he put a bid on Esav's firstborn right. In doing so, Yaakov effectively stepped into the shoes which Esav was meant to fill and acquired the additional role of being the man of the field.

At that point, Yaakov became personally responsible for the dual role of dwelling in the tent of Torah as well as conquering the physical world. This two-fold calling is apparent throughout the life of Yaakov, as he switched seamlessly between the two arenas, in quest of perfecting both. For example, Yaakov first spent fourteen years of uninterrupted Torah learning, the paradigm of a tent-dweller, and then immediately was thrust into a twenty-year sojourn in the idolatrous house of Lavan, where he engaged the 'field' of evil and handily triumphed over it.

We can now begin to understand Leah's position in the story. Our sages say that the older sister Leah had really been destined for the older brother Esav while the younger Rachel was meant for the younger Yaakov. This means that Leah's calling was aligned with that of the man of the field; it was her potential to vanquish the evil within the physical domain. But when Esav sold away his role, Yaakov additionally became the man of the field, thus redesignating Leah to him as the quintessential helpmate in this particular mission.

Thus, when Leah's integrity was questioned, she pointed to the defining point which had aligned her destiny together with Yaakov, when Yaakov had clothed himself as Esav. If we examine this facade with a deeper understanding, we can see that Yaakov wasn't wearing a mere Purim costume, but rather he had clothed himself in field-clothes as an expression of his newly acquired mission as the man of the field. Leah explained that since Yaakov had taken upon himself the part of Esav, whereby he was entitled to call himself Esav, she was henceforth destined for Yaakov. In this light, she had the right to call herself Rachel, the one originally designated for Yaakov, since Leah now shared that designation.

On a practical note, as the children of Yaakov we are all endowed with this ability to straddle both ends of the spectrum. The Jew is to live in the tents of Torah, meaning to be fully focused on the spiritual service of Hashem in Torah study, prayer, and introspection. But at the same time he is able and expected to engage the physical world, overcome it's tests, and ultimately prevail in elevating it to the service of his Creator.
