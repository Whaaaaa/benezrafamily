In Parsha Vayeitzei, Yaakov leaves Be'er Sheva to work for his Uncle Lavan as a shepherd. On his journey he sleeps with his head on a stone and dreams of the famous ladder reaching to the heavens, with angels ascending and descending upon it.

The classic question is why Yaakov -- unlike his father and grandfather -- needed to leave the land of Israel at all in order to build the next generation of the Jewish people. The answer lies in the very nature of the ladder: it teaches that holiness is not confined to sacred places, but can be built anywhere a person is willing to climb, rung by rung, toward heaven, even -- especially -- in a place as spiritually treacherous as Lavan's household.
