**ADAPTED FROM AN AUDIO SHIUR FROM A RENOWNED TALMUD CHACHAM**

In this week's parsha we have the famous incident of Yaakov and the ladder.

Reb Yerucham Olshin says that the ladder is a parable to our individual obligation of continual personal growth, a separate obligation aside from our standard obligation to the mitzvot.

Yet this is seemingly a strange lesson to be teaching to a 77-year-old Yaakov. Yaakov definitely understood from his time growing up in Yitzchak's house and learning with Shem V'ever that you always need to be growing. Why is this lesson applicable at this point in Yaakov's life?

He answers that Yaakov was now confronted with the unique challenge of Lavan who wanted to uproot everything! In such a dire and difficult circumstance there is room for a mistake to be made. One could think that in such a situation it is enough just to maintain their current level of spiritual achievement and not necessarily continue to focus on growth. This is alluded to in the posuk:

וְשַׁבְתִּ֥י בְשָׁל֖וֹם אֶל־בֵּ֣ית אָבִ֑י וְהָיָ֧ה יְהֹוָ֛ה לִ֖י לֵאלֹהִֽים
and if I return safe to my father's house—the LORD shall be my God.

On which Rashi comments:

IN PEACE, perfectly free (שלם) from sin, not having learned evil from Laban's ways

This seems to indicate that Yaakov was content to leave his association with Lavan just having not descended from his previous level.

That's why now this lesson is so relevant. Even in the bleakest and hardest situations, we are still obligated to strive for growth. And in fact, these are the circumstances where the most growth is possible!

The Medrash says that Hashem tested the Forefathers:

נסיון אחר נסיון, וגידולין אחר גידולין, בשביל לנסותן בעולם, בשביל לגדלן בעולם
Test after test, opportunity for growth after opportunity for growth, in order to make signs out of them and to make them great.

Reb Yerucham Levovivtz says this is where we clearly see that it was through the trials and tribulations that our forefathers were formed.

Ohr Gedalyahu writes that when Yaakov was going to Lavan the Torah writes;

וַיִּפְגַּ֨ע בַּמָּק֜וֹם וַיָּ֤לֶן שָׁם֙ כִּי־בָ֣א הַשֶּׁ֔מֶשׁ וַיִּקַּח֙ מֵאַבְנֵ֣י הַמָּק֔וֹם וַיָּ֖שֶׂם מְרַֽאֲשֹׁתָ֑יו וַיִּשְׁכַּ֖ב בַּמָּק֥וֹם הַהֽוּא׃
He came upon a certain place and stopped there for the night, for the sun had set. Taking one of the stones of that place, he put it under his head and lay down in that place.

"וַיִּפְגַּ֨ע" implies surprise. On his way down to Lavan seeing the angels made a significant impression on him but after his time in Lavan's house the Torah writes:

וְיַעֲקֹ֖ב הָלַ֣ךְ לְדַרְכּ֑וֹ וַיִּפְגְּעוּ־ב֖וֹ מַלְאֲכֵ֥י אֱלֹהִֽים׃
Jacob went on his way, and angels of God encountered him.

This verse uses the same word as above (וַיִּפְגְּעוּ) again implying a surprise or seeing something of significance. But after the arduous stay with Lavan, it wasn't Yaavok impressed by the angels, the angels were impressed by him.

When it is time for Yaakov to return to Cnaan, he informs Rachel and Leah and they reply:

תַּ֤עַן רָחֵל֙ וְלֵאָ֔ה וַתֹּאמַ֖רְנָה ל֑וֹ הַע֥וֹד לָ֛נוּ חֵ֥לֶק וְנַחֲלָ֖ה בְּבֵ֥ית אָבִֽינוּ׃
Then Rachel and Leah answered him, saying, "Have we still a share in the inheritance of our father's house?
הֲל֧וֹא נׇכְרִיּ֛וֹת נֶחְשַׁ֥בְנוּ ל֖וֹ כִּ֣י מְכָרָ֑נוּ וַיֹּ֥אכַל גַּם־אָכ֖וֹל אֶת־כַּסְפֵּֽנוּ׃
Surely, he regards us as outsiders, now that he has sold us and has used up our purchase price.

Reb Meir Simcha asks what Rachel and Leah mean when they say that their father has sold them. He married them off the same way as every father has for generations! What is their complaint?
He explains that Yaakov really married Rachel and Leah with his years of work for Lavan. We ask in the Gemara Kiddushin, when someone labors for another does the credit for his labor accumulate daily or only once it is completed? This answer determines whether or not the promise of labor is a valid dowry for marriage.

If the credit is only owed at the end of the service, at the time he completes the work, then it is not a loan at all but a single lump sum that can be used like a ring or other object of value.

If the credit constantly accumulates, by the time the labor is done the laborer will have essentially given a loan to the contractor for every day's work since the commission of the project. And because a loan is not a valid method of betrothal, contracted work cannot be used for marriage.
We concluded that the credit constantly accumulates. If so, how could Yaakov marry?

Reb Meir Simcha answers that there is a fundamental difference with a Jew. A Jew cannot be a permanent slave and females can only be temporarily enslaved to someone that is a suitable marriage partner at the end of the servitude.

So Rachel and Leah's complaint was that they weren't married like Jews should be! They were just sold as slaves!

At the end of the parsha Yaakov is told by Hashem to leave:
וַיֹּ֤אמֶר יְהֹוָה֙ אֶֽל־יַעֲקֹ֔ב שׁ֛וּב אֶל־אֶ֥רֶץ אֲבוֹתֶ֖יךָ וּלְמוֹלַדְתֶּ֑ךָ וְאֶֽהְיֶ֖ה עִמָּֽךְ׃

Then the LORD said to Jacob, "Return to the land of your fathers where you were born, and I will be with you."

When Yaakov goes to inform his wives, however, he preempts telling them about God's command with summarizes the hardships they have to endure with Lavan.
Rav Elya Lopian says from here can learn an important lesson about our perspective toward our service of God. The people who say that they are going to sacrifice all the pleasures in the world and give up on the good life have totally missed the point! The ultimate satisfaction is living a spiritually informed and meaningful life and those chasing meaningless pleasures are not achieving happiness in the least.
That's what Yaakov is teaching us. Yes, Hashem gave us a command and we will definitely follow. But we can do it without feeling resentful or like it is a great sacrifice. We can look at all of the positive changes that will result from this command and move into this next phase with a full heart.

The Shelah comments on the posuk:

וְדוֹרְשֵׁי יי לא יַחְסְרוּ כָל טוֹב
Those who seek Hashem won't lack anything good.

The verse doesn't say that those who seek Hashem will have a Cadillac. It says that those who seek Hashem will not be missing anything.

This is analogous to the man who walks into his friend's house, looks into his medicine drawer, and upon seeing it is empty becomes distraught. His incredulous friend has to explain to him that due to the family's consistently good health they don't need medications! Likewise, the tzaddikim are known to live in sparse conditions with just the essentials. This is not a sign of great need but a sign of so much contentment there is no need. These people don't need a Caddilac, they already are not lacking any good.
