It's hard to find the words to start this Dvar Torah. It's hard to believe it's been four years without Uncle Yonatan. I feel the loss like it was yesterday.

I'm going to break the rules and go off of the Parsha for this week, instead revisiting one of Uncle Yonatan's favorite Dvar's. The one that I take with me most often. The grasshopper one. :) Hopefully we can uncover another layer this year.

I have spent many hours delving into the depths of the story of the spies and it was particularly on my mind this past week due to Tisha B'Av which has its roots in the story of the Spies, or the Meraglim, as the Sages called it.

To recap, Hashem told Moshe to send spies to the Land of Israel to bring back a report "If it is good or bad; fortified or unprotected, etc." Moshe does just this. He selects the leader of each of the 12 tribes and sends them on a reconnaissance mission. They return and give a "bad report" about the land saying that the fruits are huge, the cities are heavily fortified and the people there are giants. "We were in our eyes like grasshoppers and so we must have been in their eyes." The Jewish people listened to the report and cried that night, beginning the cycle of mourning that still has a hold on this date in our history.

I heard an amazing new idea that I'll attempt to excerpt before we return to Uncle's favorite part. (Full version here: https://www.alephbeta.org/playlist/tisha-bav-origin-story) In the interest of brevity, I will skip the bulk of the question answer format that drives these observations.

The story of the Meraglim relates directly to the story of Yosef and his brothers. We find the same unique words that link these two portions together.

Early in the story of Yosef, he went to spy on his brothers His father's request. This immediately followed a bad report that he gave Yaacov about his brothers and the repeating of his dreams which increased the jealousy and bad feelings that were already rampant in the family. As he was approaching, they saw him from afar and decided what they would do with the "dreamer", ultimately throwing him in a pit and selling him to some spice traders on their way to Egypt. (The same word "dibbah" is used to describe bad reports in both stories).

Rabbi Fohrman's question on both of these stories is what the intention of God was in sending the spies and what the intention of Yaacov was in sending Yosef? Were these both not reconnaissance missions doomed to failure? Didn't Moshe ask them to see if the land was good or bad? Was this some sort of trick question? Were they not supposed to answer honestly? How could these stories have ended well?

He posits a very deep and important truth To make sense of these stories:

We are not supposed to ignore the "negative" parts of a person when we view them. We are allowed to see fault. But we are meant to view the person as a whole being. And we should understand that many traits are package deals. The giants who were so threatening to the spies were only giants due to the fruits being so prolific, nutrient dense, and bio-available. The problem came in when they passed judgment on the entire package as not worth claiming. But noting the fortification of the land and the military aptitude of the current residence was part of their mission. And they did nothing wrong by noting the bad along with the good. The problem was when they allowed the bad to overshadow the good that was inherently part of the package.

With this in mind, the goal that we have toward each other should be to view each other objectively. We must understand that the same creativity that expresses itself as disorganization also creates the seeds of incredible creative change. That the flexibility we enjoy in loved ones being easy-going about rescheduling might come along with some lack of timeliness.

I think through this lens, Uncle's favorite Dvar about the grasshoppers allows us to reflect the same idea inward. The Spies saw themselves as grasshoppers and only once they were viewing themselves that way, did they assume that they were also being viewed by others in that manner. We tell the world what to think about us. Everyone of us gives off the vibrations of our own self judgment. The more that we can view ourselves as a mixed bag and appreciate the gifts that Hashem uniquely gave us, the more we can build on the positive aspects of ourselves, mitigate the negative effects of those same traits, and ultimately appreciate the sublime beauty of those around us in their technicolor glory!

I love you all so much! You make Yonatan proud! See yourselves as the Awesome lights that you are!

"Be excellent to each other!" And "Party on Dude's!"

Good Shabbos!
