In this week's Parsha, Moshe tells the children of Israel that he will not enter the Land of Israel -- he will only see it from a distance. Continuing his recap of their journey, Moshe recounts the Exodus and the giving of the Torah, overcome by its splendor: "Has there ever occurred this great thing, or has the likes of it ever been heard? Did a people ever hear the voice of G-d speaking out of the midst of fire... and live?"

Moshe predicts that Jews will, in the future, turn away from G-d and be scattered among the nations -- and also that they would ultimately search Him out and return. The Parsha concludes with the Shema and the Ten Commandments, along with the mitzvot to love G-d and study His Torah -- the words we bind to our arms and heads with tefillin, and affix to our doorposts with mezuzot.

Idol worship posed a threat to Judaism that is hard for us to understand today; when it began with Enosh, it served a very tangible purpose -- his generation believed in G-d, but worshipped constellations as intermediaries capable of warping His will, so that even a punishing drought could be undone by an idol, making it impossible for G-d's judgment to actually reach them. The evil of idol worship is that it removes the point of life -- growing closer to G-d through self-perfection. If we don't suffer through a drought, we may never improve our watering systems. Idol worship does not exist today in the same form it did five thousand years ago, but many things today still keep us from confronting reality.
