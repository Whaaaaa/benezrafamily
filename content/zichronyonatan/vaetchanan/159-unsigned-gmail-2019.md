"Let me cross over," Moshe implored God as the people of Israel camped on the eastern bank of the Jordan, "and let me see the good Land." But God refused. Moshe, who had taken the people out of Egypt, who had climbed Mount Sinai to receive the Torah for them and returned to teach them the word of God, who for forty years tended to their hungers and thirsts, their doubts and complaints and rebellions—Moshe was to die and be buried in the plains of Moab, and his disciple, Yehoshua, was to lead a new generation into the Promised Land.

But upon closer examination, Moshe prayer was at least partially answered. Moses asked to cross over and to see the Land; God refused the first part of the request and granted the second. "Ascend to the top of the summit," God said to Moshe, ". . . and see it with your eyes; for you shall not cross this Jordan."

This week's Parsha contains the heartwrenching culmination of the saga of the greatest leader we have ever had. This confounding conclusion and its accompanying teachings reveal the essence of the Moshe Rabbenu. Why was he barred from leading the nation he built into the next phase of their existence? Why did it require God's interference to end his imploring? Why did we have to lose the leader we loved?

The simple answer is that Moshe sinned by failing to obey God's instructions. When the people thirsted for water, God told Moshe to speak to a certain rock and it would bring forth its waters. Instead, Moshe struck the rock. As a result, God informed him that both he and Aaron would not be allowed entry into the Land but would perish in the desert. (See Numbers 20:1-13.)

This event revealed a facet of Moshe's leadership that was incompatible with the next chapter of the Jewish nation. Moshe carried the nation from its birth to this point. Just as an infant cannot understand language and must be physically reprimanded when endangering itself, so was Moshe's approach to Israel's spirituals follies. Similarly, Moshe did not allow Israel to face the consequences of its actions. When we sinned he shouldered the burden and prayed for us. When we complained he provided. Part of this leadership style was rooted in Moshe's selflessness. His direct unfiltered connection to God could make him aloof and unrelatable, hence Aaron's role as a more human interface, but this was the only person that could handle Egypt, Mount Sinai and 40 years of wandering. He was the only vessel pure enough to both receive and transmit the Torah to the generations that would follow. Only the complete absence of id exhibited by Moshe could allow enough room for a nation to be conceived and delivered.

The next stage of Israel's development would necessitate a leader that was accessible and empathetic to the mistakes we would surely make, leaving the nest and settling the land. For Moshe to be that leader he would have to dial down the intense relationship with God he had devoted his life to forging. With this understanding, God's single command to Moshe after his protracted appeal makes much more sense. "It is much to you; do not continue speaking to Me about this matter further" (Deut. 3:26).

Yehoshua's leadership is compared to the moon and Moshe's to the sun. Moshe's brilliance will always be the source of our connection but other leaders will arise to reflect it in ways that are practical and applicable. Yehoshua will be tasked with sustaining the flame lit by Moshe and ensuring it ignites another generation to serve God.

On Uncle's 2nd Yahrzeit it is clear that he was our Moshe. Through his unique intensity and selflessness, he brought us the Torah. He carried us through the beginnings of our religious development and brought each of his children to the edge of their promised lands. Like Moshe he got to see it with his eyes, the families being brought up with his influence, the Torah homes established because of his passion. And like Moshe, he didn't get to enter the land. He was denied a chance to see the grandchildren living his legacy come to bar and bat mitzvah. He never saw all the fruits of his labor.

But in his absence, we are each accountable to be Yehoshua. To lead our respective families by example and reflect Uncle's light onto the next generation. While we may never reach the same spiritual and intellectual heights he did, we have the opportunity to build sustainable futures on the Torah he gave.

Shabbat Shalom
