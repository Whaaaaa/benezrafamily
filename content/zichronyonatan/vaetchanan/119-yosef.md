This Friday marks the first yahrtzeit of our beloved Uncle Yonatan. It is hard to believe that it's been a full year since he took leave of us, as his memory is so real and vivid. For many of us the wound is still raw, but more importantly, the memories are still very much alive.

Much was said at the funeral about the fact that it took place on the festive day of Tu B'Av, the fifteenth day of the month of Av. Also notable is the fact that Uncle passed away in the week of Shabbos Nachamu, or the Shabbos of Consolation. And, as Uncle always taught us, it would serve us well to understand how his passing corresponded to this week's Parsha, Va'Etchanan.

Let's start by understanding this Shabbos of consolation. The last few weeks have been the saddest in the Jewish calendar. Beginning with the 17th of Tamuz and culminating with the 9th of Av, this is the time of intense mourning for the destruction of the Temple. But on the Shabbos right after Tisha B'Av, we make an about-face and it is suddenly a time of consolation. What is the consolation? Aren't we still here in exile just as before?

The Chasam Sofer (1762–1839) explains that the very fact that we are still mourning the Temple's destruction is the biggest reason for consolation. If we would have stopped mourning and it would have passed from our memories, then it would essentially be lost forever, never to be rebuilt. The fact the we are still mourning, some two-thousand years later, means that the temple and what it stood for lives on in all of us. Our dedication to its ideals and lessons gives it eternal life and guarantees that one day it will again stand tall.

Uncle may no longer be with us in physical form, but his memory and all that he gave us is etched forever in our hearts and minds. The indelible mark which he made upon us and on the world gives him eternal life. Perhaps this is the reason his memory is so vivid to us – because we continue to embody his ways. Just like the temple lives on, so does he live on.

Now I'd like to address Tu B'Av, and why I see it being so appropriate as the day Uncle returned his soul to his Creator. You see, the 15th of Av is a day which is supposed to mark a renewed dedication to Torah study. Because the nights, which are considered the main time for study, begin to become longer from here on out, this is the time when everyone is expected to redouble their efforts in Torah learning. Uncle loved the Torah with his whole heart and soul. There was nothing he wanted more than to be free to study Torah day and night without any other responsibilities. Alas, between work and all the other things which one must attend to, he didn't have nearly as much time as he would have wanted. We are taught that, in the world to come, the righteous sit together with God in the heavenly Yeshiva, plumbing the depths of the Torah. On Tu B'Av, Uncle Yonatan was finally able to ascend to the heavenly Yeshiva, where he is able to constantly rejoice in the study of Torah,
finally unhindered by all the distractions which are the lot of mortal men.

This means that not only here does he live on in a more figurative sense within the hearts of those who loved him, but in the next world he lives on in a very literal sense. There he has finally achieved the goal towards which he strove for all his life – closeness to God and to His Torah. There he exists in a plane of spiritual ecstasy doing what he loved most.

And this ties us back to our Parsha. The verse says (4:4) “And you who cleave to the Almighty are all living today.” Our deeper sources tell us that this does not only refer to those who were then alive whom Moshe was talking to, but rather it is a sweeping statement that applies for all time. Those who are connected to God, the omnipotent Source of all life, are connected to eternal life. By attaching to The Source, mortal man can lift himself above his mortality and indeed live forever.

So on Uncle's yahtzeit, let us understand and take consolation in the fact that Uncle lives on, both here in our hearts and minds, and in the next world, as one connected to the divine. And let us also learn the lesson of eternity, learning how we too can live forever by teaching and giving to others, as well as connecting ourselves to God.

Have a wonderful Shabbos of Consolation,

Yosef
