This week is Shabbat Nachamu, our first Shabbat out of the three weeks and nine days and immediately following Tisha b'Av. At a time when our spirits have been crushed by reliving the horrors our people have endured, we are given this Shabbat as a consolation. Our calendar is arranged so we always read Parashat Vaetchanan and its accompanying haftorah this week and can find solace in their words of unity and comfort. This is also the Shabbat right before Tu b'Av, a holiday characterized by intimacy with God to the extent it is compared to Yom Kippur, and so joyful that the Mishnah writes "the daughters of Jerusalem would go out in white clothes to the vineyards and dance." It was when, on multiple occasions, the individual tribes of Israel reunited and started building families together again. It is an auspicious day to get married and it has many segulot related to finding a spouse. We have barely removed our sackcloth and washed the ashes from our hair and we are already expected to don our finery and find our dancing shoes. What is the power of this Shabbat that gives it the ability to serve as such a seamless bridge from tragedy to triumph?

While Tisha B'Av represents our recognition of the impermanence of even our deepest treasures, Tu B'Av represents our appreciation for the things that are eternal. We mourn the temples and the exiles on Tisha B'Av but celebrate God's forgiveness and the establishment of new Jewish families on Tu B'Av.

This theme is found deeply throughout our Parsha. Moshe reveals that he will not be permitted to enter Israel but will only be able to observe the foundation of our undying connection to the land from afar. There is a profound sense of loss brought on by Moshe's departure but also a deep sense of hope in the establishment of our eternal homeland.

Moshe also explains that we will sin and experience exile. Again we are struck with sadness at how fragile and temporary our connection with God can be. But Moshe also reassures us that we will forever continue to seek God and do teshuvah. Providing us another eternal consolation for the loss of something ephemeral.

We also repeat the Ten Commandments, our immortal code of conduct, and the Shema, declaring our continuous unity as a people.

This Shabbat is our chance to feel the deep void left in ourselves after reflecting on our losses. It is a chance, while we are still raw, to try and zoom out and see the bigger picture. We lost a temporary temple but we never stopped seeking god, just as Moshe promised. We faced internal schisms and divisions yet we always managed to create families together. We have been exiled and tormented at every turn but we still declare our unity as a people. This contrast between sorrow and hope enables us to feel both our anguish and our excitement more intensely. It is this emotional dichotomy that gives Shabbat Nachamu its incredible power.

Shabbat Shalom,
Noah
