This Friday marks the first yahrzeit of our beloved Uncle Yonatan. It is hard to believe it's been a full year since he took leave of us -- the wound still raw, but the memories still very much alive.

Much was said at the funeral about the fact that it took place on the festive day of Tu B'Av, and that Uncle passed away in the week of Shabbos Nachamu, the Shabbos of Consolation -- and, as Uncle always taught us, it serves us well to understand how his passing corresponds to this week's Parsha, Va'Etchanan.

What is the consolation of Shabbos Nachamu, when we remain in exile just as before? The Chasam Sofer explains that the very fact we are still mourning the Temple's destruction, two thousand years later, is itself the consolation -- if we had stopped mourning, the Temple would be lost forever, never to be rebuilt. Our ongoing dedication to its ideals gives it eternal life and guarantees it will one day stand again.

Uncle is no longer with us in physical form, but his memory is etched forever in our hearts, giving him eternal life -- perhaps this is why his memory feels so vivid: because we continue to embody his ways. The 15th of Av marks a renewed dedication to Torah study, as nights begin to lengthen again; Uncle loved Torah with his whole heart, and on Tu B'Av he was finally able to ascend to the heavenly Yeshiva, where the righteous learn Torah unhindered by the distractions of mortal life.

This ties back to our Parsha: "And you who cleave to the Almighty are all living today" -- a statement that applies for all time, not only to those Moshe was addressing. Those connected to G-d, the Source of all life, are connected to eternal life. By attaching ourselves to the Source, mortal man can lift himself above his own mortality.

So on Uncle's yahrzeit, let us take consolation that he lives on -- here in our hearts, and there, connected to the divine -- and let us learn the lesson of eternity: that we too can live forever by teaching and giving to others, and by connecting ourselves to G-d.

Have a wonderful Shabbos of Consolation, Yosef
