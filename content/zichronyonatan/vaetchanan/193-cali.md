This year, we have all seen loss and devastation. There is a global pandemic, governments seem to be crumbling, and we are just starting to see the beginnings of an economic downturn. Times are hard and I find myself feeling helpless and asking myself, "how will anything ever get better?"

For me, the months of isolation were hard and feeling connected to my community and Judaism felt even more difficult. When I read the first line on Eicha this year, it seemed eerily relevant to the months past and I realized that this Tisha B'Av hit a lot closer to home than I could have ever imagined.

"How solitary lies the city that was once so full of people!" (Eichah 1:1)

I am so blessed to be part of such an awesome community. We have friends, women's Torah classes, shul, and everyone is ready to help each other out. But, in the past few months, this community moved online and I found that it was much harder for me to connect. It made me ask myself, why does Hashem throw these hurdles at us, and what makes him so sure that we will actually make it out the other side?

In Yirmiyahu 31:28 it's written: "and just as I was watchful over them to uproot and to pull down, to overthrow and to destroy and to bring disaster, so I will be watchful over them to build and to plant—declares the LORD." Yirmiyahu saw the destruction and devastation before it happened, and yet, he also saw that we would have the strength and resilience to rebuild.

But how was Yirmiyahu so sure? Rabbi Sacks [1] asserts that this is because of Hashem's promise in Vayikra 26:44, "Yet, even then, when they are in the land of their enemies, I will not reject them or spurn them so as to destroy them, annulling My covenant with them: for I the LORD am their God."

I think we can see from these two sources that destruction is inherently tied to rebuilding and that Hashem is ultimately in control. So I was wrong, now isn't a time to "why is this happening," but rather, it is a chance to ask ourselves "where do I have room to improve." Even if we can't see the positive side of the situation in the moment, things will get better and we will become stronger and more resilient than before.

Shabbat Shalom!
Cali
