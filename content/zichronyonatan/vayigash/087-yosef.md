From all of the forefathers, Yaakov alone experienced prophecy in the form of מראות הלילה, a vision of the night. The Meshech Chochmah (1846-1923) explains that Yaakov experienced such to send a message regarding the Egyptian exile which he was about to enter, as well as all of the subsequent exiles of the Jewish people. The message was that even in the darkness of night and even in the most bitter exile, Hashem's presence would still be with Yaakov and his progeny.

Yaakov was the paradigm for a Jew in exile; always fighting off the darkness of night. He was the one who faught and overcame the angel of Eisav in the dead of night. He was the one who instituted Maariv, the prayers of night. Likewise, the four overarching trials of Yaakov's life -- the stories of Eisav, Lavan, Dina, and Yosef -- parallel the four exiles of the Jewish people.

As a nation, we just celebrated the holiday of Chanukah, commemorating the miracles which accompanied us even into Greek exile. We light the menorah in the darkness of night -- testimony that the *shechinah*, the Heavenly Presence, is with us right here in exile. It's also no happenstance that Chanukah always falls out on the winter solstice, the darkest day of the year, or that the Menorah (when lit outside) is supposed to be lit lower than ten handbreadths to the ground. The idea is all one -- we may be in the darkest, lowest, most unholy of places, but the light of Hashem and His Torah is with us even here.

The Meshech Chochmah further tells us that while Yaakov's nighttime prophecy signaled that Hashem will accompany us into the exile, there are some strings attached. He explains that generally prophecy was limited to the land of Israel and only one who first experienced it in the Holy Land was able to retain the ability in exile. Yaakov was only able to have a revelation in the dark of night because he had experienced such during the brightness of day.

It follows that in order for the Jewish people to have the *shechinah* with us in exile we must be able to claim that we have it with us from previous times. But, after thousands of years of exile, how are we supposed to make such a claim?

The answer is that as long as we act and view ourselves as scions of our fathers, we are tied to all the greatness of our people. When we follow in their footsteps, then they live on through us and we are able to claim membership in a nation that lived with G-d while yet in the Holy Land. As such, we have access to the divine presence grandfathered in and we are able to take it with us even into the deepest darkest places. But if we do not hold sacred the link to our people; if we stray from the paths of our fathers, then we are no more than our small and pitiful selves. If we cut ourselves off from the timelessness of our people, then we cannot hope to be joined by Hashem. Such a relationship can only begin in the Holy Land of antiquity.

We've already put away our Menorahs for the year, and now is the time to make sure that the deeper light of the menorah, the light of Hashem, will stay with us throughout the night. So let us cling to the Torah and cling to the ways of our glorious and ancient people. Only as we stand atop our predecessors' shoulders can we hope for that light to find a place in our lives. And very soon, may our flickering candlelight give way to the light of dawn with the coming of Moshiach.

*Good Shabbos,*

*Yosef*
