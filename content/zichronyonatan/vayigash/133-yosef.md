After months of drama, accusations, and blackmail, Yosef finally reveals himself to his brothers. “I am Yosef. Is my father still alive?” With this sudden realization, the brothers recoil as if bit by a snake, unable to say a word.

On this the Medrash comments, “Woe is to us for the day of judgment, woe is to us for the day of rebuke. Yosef was from the youngest of the brothers, yet they could answer when he rebuked them. How much more so when Hashem will judge each person according to who he is, that we will be unable to answer.”

The Beis Halevi asks a famous question. The entire preceding discussion between Yosef and the brothers centered around their father's well-being. The brothers were begging him to spare Binyamin so as not to cause distress to their father Yaakov. Where, then, was there room to turn around in the middle of the conversation and ask if Yaakov was still alive?

The answer is that this was not really a question, but rather an accusation in question format. With this short sentence, Yosef was showing them the incongruence of their entire life story. Here they were, begging and pleading that Yaakov could not bear to be separated from his precious son Binyamin. They went on and on about how unthinkable it was to cause pain to their elderly father. To that Yosef responded, “I am Yosef.” I am the beloved son of Yaakov that you yourselves sold into slavery. It is me whose death you staged, devastating our father for the last twenty-two years. “Is my father still alive?” How is it that he could possibly survive such intense grief after the story you've spun of his inability to be separated from Binyamin?

With these short but stinging words, Yosef effectively disproved all that they had done for the last twenty-two years – and from their very own actions. He showed the glaring contradiction between their words and their actions. They themselves had disregarded the feelings of Yaakov in the face of their own interests, yet when it came to the viceroy's interests suddenly this was unthinkable.

This was the criticism to which the brothers had no reply. They may have had myriad answers for the sale of Yosef, but when faced with their own opposite reasoning they had no way to dispute their guilt.

With this, the Beis Halevi goes on to explain the words of the Medrash. The Medrash says that Hashem will judge each person “according to who he is”. What it the implication of these seemingly superfluous words? Also, the Medrash lists two things which we must worry about. The “day of judgment” and “the day of rebuke”. What are the meaning of these two separate concerns?

He explains that judgment refers to the actual sins a person committed, whereas rebuke refers to how our own actions disprove our defenses. For example, one who does not give enough charity may have many ways to explain and justify his actions. Money is tight, he has a large family to feed, and he just can't afford to give anything more to the poor. These answers, while in a vacuum, may serve to partly vindicate him. That is “judgment”. But then there is the “day of rebuke”. The Judge turns to him and asks, if you really couldn't afford to give anymore, then how is it that shortly after you sent that pauper away empty-handed you splurged on a not-so-kosher and extravagant form of entertainment or the like. To this, the man is silenced. He himself has disproved the purity of his intentions.

This is what it means that we will be judged “according to who we are”, meaning with our own actions setting the bar for unacceptable defenses.

So let us reexamine our actions and our reasoning, ensuring that we are honest with ourselves. Let us be aware of the standards we ourselves are setting every day, so that we not be haunted by those very standards when we later cross them.

Good Shabbos,

Yosef
