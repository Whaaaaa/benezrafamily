Rabbi Sacks points out that the narrative spanning Bereshit 37 to 50 stands as the Torah's lengthiest unbroken story, with Yosef at its core. Despite Yosef's apparent destiny for greatness, it is another brother, Yehudah, who ultimately shapes the fate of the Jewish people. The Jewish people, known by various names, find their identity through Yehudah, who becomes the forebear of Israel's greatest king, David, and the lineage of the Moshiach.

Yehudah's significance is underscored by a pivotal moment in the narrative when he suggests selling Yosef into slavery, displaying a callousness that contrasts with the nobility of Reuben. However, over time, Yehudah undergoes a profound transformation. In a later episode, he willingly offers himself as a slave to spare Binyomin from the fate he once proposed for Yosef. This dramatic shift in character highlights Yehudah's newfound concern and courage, illustrating his capacity for change.

The turning point in Yehudah's life is traced back to the Tamar story in Bereshit 38. Recognizing his mistreatment of Tamar, Yehudah undergoes a significant change, marked by the acknowledgment of his guilt. This episode becomes the genesis of teshuvah, the ability to recognize wrongdoing, feel remorse and change. Yehudah emerges as an exemplar of teshuvah, earning him the name "he who acknowledged his sin."

The significance of Judah's transformation is encapsulated in the saying that "in the place where penitents stand, even the perfectly righteous cannot stand." This challenges the prevailing notion that Yosef, consistently regarded as righteous, holds a superior position. Instead, Yehudah, as the first penitent in the Torah, becomes the progenitor of Israel's kings, underscoring the profound power of penitence and the potential for personal growth and change.

May we all be able to follow in the steps of Yehudah and humbly acknowledge our mistakes so that we can all achieve greatness.
