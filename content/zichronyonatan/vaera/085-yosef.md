For as long as Ehud could remember, he'd been riding the buses in Jerusalem. And for almost as long, he'd been entranced watching the bus drivers effortlessly handling the flow of passengers, the sharp turns, and the steep hills. And so, as Ehud grew older, he dreamed of one day getting his license and joining Egged [the largest Israeli bus company]. Finally the day arrived, Ehud received his license and soon after signed a contract with Egged -- in just a few days his dreams would finally be realized. He could barely sleep at night as he imagined driving the busy Kotel route, with a bus packed literally to the rafters at all hours of the day. He dreamed of the endless flow of passengers, raking in the bus fares too fast to keep track of. He envisioned capably keeping track of both entrances while opening the bottom compartment for anyone in need. Yes, he would be the greatest bus driver that ever lived!

His first day came and he was up early, dressed in his uniform and ready to conquer the world. But as he received his bus route, his heart dropped. Instead of the big number 3 assigning his duties to the Kotel, there was some route he'd never even heard of servicing the outskirts of Jerusalem. Not wanting to make a bad impression, he swallowed hard, thanked the boss, and hurried off to his bus. That day was the longest day of Ehud's life. No throngs of clamoring passengers, no traffic to navigate, no cash flow, and no fun. Ehud knew that he hadn't even earned enough for the company to pay for his gas, and he felt less than worthless.

Ehud cried himself to sleep every night that week, but over the weekend he developed a grand solution. On Monday, he reported as usual and received his expected depressing orders. He got into the driver's seat and started toward his route, but halfway there he changed course and adjusted the sign on his bus. It now read "Number 3 -- Kotel". As he arrived at the first stop on the new route, he was overjoyed. At the stop waited tens of people, all pushing their way to get a seat on his bus. He deftly took everyone's payment and provided the necessary change and tickets, while simultaneously navigating the traffic to the next stop. He loved the noise, the money flying in, the excitement -- every bit at grand as he had dreamed! For all eight hours of his shift, the smile never left his face. At the end of the shift he put all the money into a huge and sagging bag and proudly marched into the boss's office, placing it with a satisfying thud on the desk. Beaming, he told his boss how he had manned the Kotel route, making a veritable fortune for the company while providing unparalleled customer service.

As Ehud neared the end of his speech, he noticed his boss's face turning beet red. "You WHAT?!" bellowed the boss. "I was trying to contact you all day! Dozens of people were unable to get to their jobs, students missed school, and I've never received so many complaints as I did today! You're route may be quiet, but its an essential part of the system and is necessary for the city to function. By switching your role for the role of another you ruined the greater picture that we must always keep in mind!"

Sometimes the Torah lists Moshe before Aharon, and sometimes Aharon comes before Moshe. According to Rashi, this teaches us that they were equal. The Kli Yakar further points out that in a verse regarding prophecy Aharon is listed first, whereas in a verse about advocating to Pharaoh Moshe is first. This is very puzzling because we know Moshe was the greatest prophet to ever live, whereas Aharon did almost all the talking with Pharaoh. How then can we say that they were equals?

The answer is that each of them had something that they were expected to do, and they each fulfilled their duties to their utmost. Yes, Moshe's place was to be the greatest prophet to ever live while Aharon was supposed to take a back seat. Yes, Aharon was to be the main spokesman to Pharaoh while Moshe was more subdued. But, like the bus driver, each role was of vital importance to the Master Plan and therefore they were considered equals.

Each of us has our own, very specific role which we have to play in the world. To that end we are set up with our place, our family, our capabilities, and and our limitations. Our physical qualities, our intelligence, our inner leanings, and our financial standing are all custom made for us to be able to fill that role to perfection. If we will accomplish that which we are supposed to then we are a shining success, equal to Moshe himself. If not, no matter what glory we pursue in its stead, we are ultimately a failure, leaving a gaping hole in the Master Plan.

Sometimes we look around and see others who are stronger, taller, smarter, or wealthier than us. We see their shiny position in life and wish we could switch places. Let us remember Ehud's dreadful mistake and how absurd it was to think he'd be more successful driving the route assigned to another. Let us realize that so it is with our route in life -- it may seem quieter and less glamorous than our friend's, but it is just as vital and important to the Master Planner. *That* is the definition of success.
