This week's Parsha represents the first seven of the ten plagues. And among them, my favorite. Not because it demonstrates the great power of the one above, although it obviously does that. Not for how well the punishment matches the crime, although it does that as well. But this plague I love most for what it teaches us about ourselves. I'm talking about frogs. The slimy, green, hopping creatures that literally blanketed all of Egypt. Indoors and outdoors, in the outhouses, even in the ovens that they baked in. They were literally everywhere.

The funny thing is, the midrash tells us it didn't begin this way. It all started with just one frog in Pharaoh's palace. One slimy green pest that belonged outside. And Pharaoh took a staff and hit it with all his might to kill it once and for all… And it multiplied. So he hit it again. And again. And again. With greater anger each time, he struck the frogs thereby enlarging his problem.

And I listen to the story and think, "What an imbecile! Stop hitting it!"

It's easy to criticize and ridicule the Egyptians for approaching their problem with anger. I once heard a line, no one in history has ever calmed down because someone told them to calm down. When the fierce grip of rage overtakes someone, it is almost impossible to not respond in kind. It becomes a problem that escalates until it is completely beyond repair.

The only solution is to take a step back and think logically. Count to ten before taking any action.

Our sages teach us that someone who gives in to anger, it is as if they have committed idolatry. It is worship is the ego, belief that we should be the ones in charge and loss of control over our inclinations.

The Egyptians were notorious idol-worshipers, bowing down in submission to everything from the Royal King to the ordinary housecats. It is no surprise to me that a nation who was so ensconced in idolatry would not stop when it came to worshipping themselves. I am so much more mature and mindful than they were, so I am a completely different story. Right?

Until I think of all the problems I've hit over and over, only making them worse. When we're angry, we react in ways that sane people think insane. Even when we ourselves look back, we can see the insanity of what we've done, but in the moment it's very hard to hold back. So the frogs teach us about human nature. When we feel ourselves becoming upset, before we sabotage our own goals, we can stop. And respond rather than react.

I love this Parsha because it reminds me to look for the frogs in my life. And to stop, take a deep breath and slowly stop multiplying them. Good shabbos! Shira and Adam
