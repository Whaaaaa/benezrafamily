Moshe came onto the scene in last week's Parshah, and from here on out he is the central figure for the rest of the Torah. Yet we find three places in our Parshah where Moshe takes a background role and gives the spotlight over to his brother Aharon. These are the plagues of blood, frogs, and lice, which were all brought about by Aharon. The reason given for this switch in roles is that Moshe could not be the one to bring a plague upon the Nile or the dirt of Egypt. Why? Because Moshe had been saved in the Nile as a baby and protected by the dirt years later when he killed an Egyptian and covered the evidence with soil. Since he had benefited from these things, his appreciation would not allow him to damage them with the plagues.

This needs to be understood. It is obvious that because the Nile and the dirt are inanimate objects, there is no need or implication of 'returning the favor'. They need not and cannot be repaid and they feel no lack of gratitude either way. What's more, they didn't act in any way to benefit Moshe but were merely the vehicles through which people protected him. Where is the need for gratitude here?

The answer is that gratitude is not only something we express in order to 'make it up' to our benefactor -- it is an internal trait that should give us a personal need to show appreciation. Moshe was not concerned for the well-being of the Nile, but it was unthinkable to him to act callously toward something which had benefited him. Had Moshe smote the Nile, it would have been a personal breach in character, even though the Nile would have felt nothing.

In a similar vein, I was taught not to throw away a used garment in the garbage amongst all the dirty diapers and banana peels, but to first wrap it in a bag. For after benefiting so much from that shirt, how could you so disdain it to treat it like the rest of your refuse?

I believe that this is a beautiful portrayal of how the Torah's ideals, even in things which are part of the basic human conscience, are on a plane above human theory. Appreciation is a universal ideal, and any decent person will tell you that it is morally incumbent upon the recipient of goodness to thank and try to repay his benefactor. But here we are learning that this is not enough. Here we are learning a much more delicate and refined form of thanksgiving. The Torah is teaching us that we cannot be internally complete if we can bring ourselves to disdain or hurt our source of benefit -- even if it makes no difference to the person or item in question. It is obvious to me that one who acts according to such an ideal will have achieved a greatness of character far beyond the general public.

On this note, am I reminded of how Rabbi Yaakov Bender shlit"a used to constantly reiterate that the American catch-phrase 'There is no way I can possibly thank you' is no excuse not to try. Rabbi Bender was telling us that even when we find ourselves completely inadequate to repay a favor, we still must do everything in our power to show appreciation. Will it aid our benefactor? Perhaps not. But for ourselves we must become people of thanks; people who yearn and pine and wish to repay every ounce of goodness which we receive.

While we may be used to thinking that appreciation is relegated to the realm of interpersonal relationships, we are learning now that its scope extends even to our personal lives. The same follows that it is of paramount importance in our relationship with Hashem. In fact, the Chovot Hal'vavot bases his entire approach to mitzvah observance on the trait of appreciation. He explains how one is supposed to observe the endless amount of goodness which Hashem bestows upon him -- his every breath, his body, light, sound, family, friends, a house, clothes, food, and the list goes on. As one begins to think of the magnitude of the debt which he 'owes' to G-d, it should spur him on to serve Hashem with every ounce of his being, as an act of thanksgiving. Here too, man's service is to One who lacks nothing and to Whom he cannot possibly benefit -- so what good is his appreciation? The answer is as stated; it is true that we cannot possibly 'make it up' to Hashem, but we are expected to become people consumed by thanks, even when it will not help the one who we are thanking.

So let's make it our business to become people of thanks. Let's keep our eyes out to realize every good thing which we enjoy, from Hashem, from people, and even from inanimate objects. As we learn the habit of recognizing the good, let's strive to not let any goodness go by without a show of appreciation. It may be a thank you, it may be a gift or a card, it may be a favor in return, or it may be just a feeling of respect and indebtedness. Whatever it is, it will help us grow to become thankful people. Without a doubt, this will improve our relationships with man and G-d alike and will help us lead happy and fulfilled lives as we truly appreciate all the goodness bestowed upon us.
