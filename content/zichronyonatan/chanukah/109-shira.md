Happy Chanukah to all!
What a beautiful time of year! The festival of lights has been illuminating every Jewish home for millennia, and as the days pass and the number of candles increase, we feel more and more empowered by the Menorah and what it represents.
We can learn a lot about what Chanukah is here to teach us by examining the different laws in conjunction with lighting the Menorah.
We light the candles just after the sun has set. Ideally, we place the Menorah no higher than 40 inches off the ground.

The fight against the Babylonians seemed impossible to win. It started off with just five brothers against an army of thousands. Both once they had one victory, many more join the cause and they conquered their enemies and held on to the Holy Temple.
Chanukah always comes out in the winter months.

The date that we beginning celebrating is the day the war was over. The day of Chanukah literally means "They rested on the 25th day (of the month)". The fight was over, but now it was time to rebuild. with the Holy Temple in such disarray, it seems like an impossible task. But that tiny jug of oil lasted for 8 days instead of just one and encouraged the Jewish people that Miracles can happen.
It also takes place during the reading of the Torah Portion of Vayigash, during which the family of Yaakov goes down to Egypt, marking the beginning of the long and painful Exile.

It seems to be the most bleak and dour time of year.
Yet, there is a light. The light is the promise that we won't be in exile forever and that there will be salvation. All it takes is one small light and darkness vanishes. From one light, it doesn't take any extra effort to create a second light and it doesn't decrease or diminish from the first one. In fact, even if one explicitly states that no one may light their candle off of his, it is not considered stealing or a sin to do so and you have not wronged him. That is the power of fire. It is the power of hope, happiness, and hugs. When you share it, when you give it, you are both stronger. When you have the tiniest flame, you have the potential to light up the whole world.
Uncle Yonatan was known for being a ray of sunshine. He walked into the room and greeted everyone by name and with a smile and a hug. The sun lights up the whole world. But sometimes, it's dark outside; the sun is not ready to rise. And that is when you light a candle. Uncle Yonatan was also that candle, that beacon of light that assured us that there was good in the world, good people, kind souls, honest and thoughtful, who are there just to provide light where there is none.
Although we miss him terribly, just remembering what a special person he was, brings light to our hearts and hope to every soul. May his memory be a blessing.

Shabbat Shalom,
Shira
