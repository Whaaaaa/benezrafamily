During the Greek occupation of Israel, a handful of Talmudic scholars -- men of the book, not of the sword -- waged war against the might of the Greek empire, and won. To their dismay, they found only one small, pure jug of oil for the Menorah -- enough for a single day, though making new oil would take eight. They lit it anyway, and it burned for eight full days.

Yet the Temple was filled with daily miracles for nearly eight hundred years -- heavenly fire, showbread that stayed hot for a week, an entire nation finding room to prostrate in the courtyard. Why no holiday for any of these? Our sources explain we only celebrate miracles on which the very existence of the Jewish people hinged -- Pesach created our nation, Sukkot ensured our survival in the desert, Shavuot defined us as a people of Torah. Why, then, does Chanukah -- commemorating oil that "merely" saved a week's wait -- warrant its own holiday?

The answer lies in when the miracle occurred: at the end of the prophetic era, just as G-d's presence in the world became newly hidden, a plunge into darkness for a people who had always related to Him through open miracle and direct prophecy. It was at precisely this moment that G-d performed the miracle of Chanukah, proclaiming that even in the darkest, most hidden times, He remains with us, lighting the way.

This message was essential for a nation about to enter a long exile in which spirituality would remain veiled -- carrying on only with the firm knowledge that even when we cannot feel G-d's embrace, He is watching over us, guiding us through the darkness.

So this year, as we light the Chanukah candles: even when our lives are shrouded in darkness, even when things seem bleak and hopeless, G-d is still lighting our path and leading us exactly where we need to go.

Good Shabbos and Happy Chanukah, Yosef
