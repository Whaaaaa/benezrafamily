Other than the fact that Chanukah paraphernalia can now be bought at Hobby Lobby, there is something else that makes Chanukah feel very different from other holidays -- we can go to work. On the shalosh regalim and the High Holidays, we stay home and keep Yom Tov, prescribed by the Torah to help us safeguard and pass on miracles to future generations. There is no such Yom Tov prescribed for Chanukah or Purim -- and yet the rabbis tell us the light of Chanukah and the celebration of Purim will outshine every other holiday in the Messianic era.

Neither Chanukah nor Purim is mentioned in the Torah; both were accepted by Jewish communities long afterward. On holidays like Yom Kippur, a divine energy enters our presence through the observance of Yom Tov itself. On Chanukah we light candles to remember that the Greeks defiled our oil and forbade our observance -- and that despite their elephants and archers, we fought for our place and won.

Being a Maccabee, waging war against the Greeks, was nothing short of suicidal -- these Jews acted beyond logic and reason. This is precisely why no Yom Tov is observed: their motivation is beyond our comprehension, and simply ceasing work would not help us understand it.

One might think that because Chanukah falls on ordinary weekdays, its miracles are somehow less significant. The opposite is true: the light of Chanukah is bright enough to penetrate even the mundanity of work. It is unavoidable, even at your local grocery store.
