As we approach the final days of Chanukah, one might feel compelled to ponder what it is we have gained from this festive week. It's been quite an ascension, steadily increasing the number of lights as we have worked to compound illumination in our homes.

One of the reasons we light the menorah is because of the Torah commandment, Pirsumei Nisa, publicizing the miracle. By igniting the chanukah lights in our windows, doorways, and in some communities, outside, we bring attention to the glorious salvation Hashem granted us in those days at this time. People may inquire about the custom of lighting the menorah and we eagerly educate them regarding our victory in war and the jug of oil that lasted for eight days.

Acknowledging that the opportunity to spread the word of the miracle is a key reason for lighting, one may ask a question.

What about an individual who lives in a remote location, or who is only able to light long after anyone is around to witness? Is he still obligated to light the Chanukah candles?

We are told that he still meant to kindle the Menorah, for the one he is publicizing the miracle to is himself.

But if he is lighting the menorah, surely he is already aware of the miracle, so it seems superfluous for him to do the action.

This question clues us in to an important component of Judaism not just in regard to lighting the Menorah, but every commandment and custom.

Hashem doesn't want lip service. He doesn't need our light. He is the one who created light. There is nothing we can do for Hashem that He can't do for Himself.

What Hashem wants is the one thing that we alone can offer: our hearts.

Our obligation to bring light into the world is not a physical flame that is required. It is the light of our souls and the fire that we feel when we choose to do something, even when it's hard. It is the responsibility to focus our attention on our own growth and constant improvement.

We light the Menorah to inspire ourselves and to remind ourselves that all it takes is one little spark to banish darkness.

May we all merit to see the Light of the Truth in our times, and may we be so fortunate as to be the ones to light it.

Have a wonderful Shabbos!

Shira Freedman
