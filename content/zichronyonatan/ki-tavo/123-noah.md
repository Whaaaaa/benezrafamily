At the end of this week’s parsha, we find the pasuk, “cursed is the one who doesn’t uphold the words of the Torah.” This warning is provided to the Jewish people at the of the series of blessings and curses that were given at Har Grizim/Har Eival. At its face value, the curse seems to apply to anyone who disregards any of the commandments. However, the Ramban interprets this curse much more specifically. He learns, based on a Gemara Yerushalmi in Masechet Sotah, that this curse is directed at a chazan that does not do hagbah, or the lifting and displaying of the Torah. Many poskim use the words of the Ramban to prove that this mitzvah is of biblical origin or, “close to biblical.” Let’s understand more about the origins, importance, and variations of this commandment.

Traditionally done by Sefardim at the beginning of Kriyas HaTorah and Ashkenazim at the end, this ceremonial display is normally accompanied by congregation pointing, often with their pinkies, at the spinning text. The Gemara in Masechet Soferim is the primary source for this custom.

מסכת סופרים יד:יד

מיד גולל ספר תורה עד שלשה דפין ומגביהו ומראה פני כתיבתו לעם העומדים לימינו ולשמאלו ומחזירו לפניו ולאחריו שמצוה לכל האנשים ולנשים לראות הכתב ולכרוע ולומר (דברים ד) וזאת התורה אשר שם משה לפני בני ישראל

[The Chazan] Immediately unrolls the Sefer Torah, revealing 3 sections of parchment which he shows to the assembled congregation to his left right front and back. [This is because] it is a mitzvah for all of the men and women to see the writing and to bow and say, “That is the Torah that Moshe placed before the Children of Israel.

The Shulchan Aruch quotes this Gemara almost verbatim when presenting the Halacha. The Mishnah Berurah does not argue and only adds that it is beneficial for a person to see the letters of the Torah as the kabbalists write that it pulls a great light onto the person.

Immediately these sources contradict the prevailing minhag. Very rarely do we see people bow during this part of the service, instead pointing has taken over which is not mentioned at all. Most authorities hold there is a commandment to at least bow the head, but the Ramo mentions the minhag recorded by the Maharil to prostrate oneself fully during hagbah. If this was such a foundational part of the service, why is it not practiced nowadays?

Rav Asher Weiss brings the Shiltei HaGiborim on Kiddushin 14b quoting the Riaz to justify the practice of not bowing in today’s age:

קידושין ל"ג ע"ב:

וחייבים לעמוד מפני הס"ת "אם מפני לומדיה עומדים מפניה לא כ"ש" ונראה בעיני שלא אמרו אלא לעמוד מפני התורה ולא להשתחוות לה ולא נמצא בכל התורה שמשתחוין אפילו לארון הקדש

One is obligated to stand before a Sefer Torah. "If we have to in front of those who learn it, all the more so before it" (Kiddushin 33b). But it appears in my eyes they only said to stand, but not to bow and in the whole Torah we never saw an idea to bow, not even to the Aron Kodesh.

R’ Yaakov Castro מהריק"ש however, disagrees:

א. אין כונת הריא"ז אלא שאין חיוב השתחויה אלא חיוב עמידה בלבד אבל ודאי ראוי אף לשיטתו לכרוע.

The Riaz was only implying that there is not an obligation to prostrate oneself in front of a Sefer Torah but it is still a fitting thing to do.

The Birchei Yosef also disagrees with the Riaz saying:

והברכי יוסף כתב דהריא"ז מיירי רק ברואה ס"ת עובר, ולא אמרו חכמים אלא שחייב לעמוד ולא לכרוע, אבל בשעה שרואה את הכתב אכן נהגו לכרוע וכך ראוי, עי"ש

The Riaz’s Psak only applied in a case where a Torah was passing, not when the writing was held visible. In such a case one certainly should bow.

Rabbi Asher Weiss accepts these refutations and says that today we should still be bowing during this auspicious time. This sheds light onto the lost custom of bowing but where did pointing come from and why the pinkie?

The Me'am Lo'ez quoted by R' Chayim Falaji point to a Medrash on Shir HaShirim as the source:

שיר השירים רבה ב:ד

“וְדִגְלוֹ עָלַי אַהֲבָה"

אָמַר רַבִּי חוּנְיָא לְשֶׁעָבַר אָדָם מַרְאֶה אִיקוֹנִין בְּאֶצְבָּעוֹ וְהָיָה נִזּוֹק, וְעַכְשָׁיו אָדָם מַנִּיחַ יָדוֹ עַל הָאַזְכָּרָה כַּמָּה פְּעָמִים וְאֵינוֹ נִזּוֹק, וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא, שֶׁאָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא וְגוּדָלוֹ עָלַי אַהֲבָה

The Pasuk says, “And his banner of love was over me.”
The Medrash explains, “In the past, if a person displayed an image of the king on his finger he would be punished. Now a person places his hands on the king's insignia many times and is not hurt. Moreover, Hashem declares, “Vdiglo Alai Ahava.” Do not read the verse “vdiglo” meaning banner but rather “vgodlo” meaning pointer finger.

From here we see that we show appreciation for our ability to serve God by pointing at the Torah. But this scriptural source for applies specifically to the pointer finger.

A second source is offered as well:

תנא דבי רבי ישמעאל שלשה דברים היו קשין לו למשה עד שהראה לו הקב"ה באצבעו ואלו הן מנורה וראש חדש ושרצים מנורה דכתיב (במדבר ח, ד) וזה מעשה המנורה ראש חודש דכתיב (שמות יב, ב) החודש הזה לכם ראש חדשים שרצים דכתיב (ויקרא יא, כט) וזה לכם הטמא ויש אומרים אף הלכות שחיטה שנאמר (שמות כט, לח) וזה אשר תעשה על המזבח:

Gemara in Menachos 29a says: 3 things were called “Zeh” because it was difficult for Moshe to understand God's directive. Menorah, Rosh Chodesh, Sheratzim. In all these cases the Rabbis taught that Zeh means Hashem “pointed at the object with his finger.” So too, when we say V’Zos Hatorah we should point.

Like the previous source, this analogy of God pointing is understood to be referring to a metaphorical pointer finger. The scriptural significance of using the correct finger is such that R’ Chaim Kanievsky addressed this question and said that the original Minhag was with the index finger and that it is correct. There are other variations of Hagba behavior that have deeply rooted sources but none of them seem to advise pointing with the pinkie. So where did this custom originate? I could not find a definitive answer but someone who did a bit of research on the subject claimed that the reason people used their pinkie was that their pointer and thumb were reserved for snuff/snus during services and pointing at the Torah with a tobacco covered finger was deemed disrespectful.

With this deeper understanding of Hagbah, we should all merit to pull the spiritual light mentioned by the Mishnah Berurah. Our practice allows us to become a part of the chain of great sages that have upheld the Torah throughout the generations. And through the lens of even this individual mitzvah, we can understand that all of the holy debate is the greatest fulfillment of upholding the Torah imaginable.
