In the middle of a long and harrowing list of the curses which will befall the Jewish nation during our sojourn in the exile, the Torah spells out for us the reason for the curse. 'Because you did not serve Hashem your G-d with joy and a good heart from a position of all good things.' Simply rendered, this is saying that if, when showered with all good things, we lose sight of Hashem, then the good will be taken away. Hashem showers us with so much blessing and he expects that we will use those blessings as catalysts to grow in our love and fear of G-d. We are meant to utilize the myriad presents which Hashem gives us to recognize Him more and to redouble our efforts in His service.

Too often, though, all the blessings and good of this world just serve as distractions that stand between us and Hashem. When that happens, our loving Father takes away the blessing and delivers a slap to get us back on track and refocused on the true purpose of life.

This lesson is certainly true and important, but Rabbi Meir Simcha of D'vinsk takes it one step further. He explains that, when read in a different light, the Torah is actually coming to reveal what exactly it was that led us astray. The reason that you did not serve Hashem, says the Torah, is because you derived your 'joy and good heart' from 'all good things'. Your sense of happiness and fulfillment came as a result of your physical possessions and earthly pleasures. This is not the Jewish way of life. Quite the opposite; a Torah Jew finds 'all good things' sourced in his 'joy and good heart'. He may not have much in the way of physical possessions or pleasures, but his internal happiness makes him lack nothing. In effect, he indeed has 'all good things', but it's not confined or decided by how much stuff he has, rather by the wellsprings of happiness that he finds deep inside his soul. Like it says in Pirkei Avot, 'Who is truly rich? One who is happy with his lot.'

When we make the silly mistake of thinking that our success and happiness in this world must be defined in terms of worldly achievement and good times, we have already begun our descent away from Hashem. This is a hoax, and we all know it to be a hoax, yet somehow the whole world runs on this mistake. If you ask anyone in an honest moment if material possessions and pleasure seeking is able to bring happiness, I don't believe you will find a single intelligent person who will answer in the affirmative. But just the same, you'll find him a minute later chasing the American dream like all the rest. And you'll find me too; each in our own way and to differing degrees. It would seem like our knowledge of this falsehood is still not clear enough to govern our behavior.

But this lifestyle is completely antithetical to the service of our Creator. As long as we perceive, consciously or not, that happiness comes from worldly pursuits, they will always stand in the way of our true purpose in life. Torah learning will be a challenge because it takes away time from recreation and fun. Charity will be a challenge because it diminishes our net worth. Shabbos will be a challenge because we can't earn any money on Shabbos. And the list goes on and on. Such a worldview will inevitably curtail our service of G-d, besides for the fact that we will never achieve the imaginary dream which we so covet.

If we want to be able to live a life of godliness and of true happiness, then we need to deeply internalize and integrate the Torah paradigm of 'wealth' into our lives. We need to realize that it's not stuff that brings happiness, but happiness which reveals to us how much blessing we truly possess. Under that light, the pursuit of happiness doesn't stand in the way of our service of Hashem, but encourages it as a source of inspiration and fulfillment. And, as a bonus, when material success will no longer pose a threat to our Torah observance, there's no reason our loving Father shouldn't grant us a healthy dose of worldly blessings as well.

Good Shabbos,

Yosef
