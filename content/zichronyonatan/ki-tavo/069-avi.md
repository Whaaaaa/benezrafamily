Ki Tavo opens with the mitzvah of Bikkurim -- upon entering Israel, one brings the first ripened fruits to the Beit Hamikdash and declares gratitude for all G-d has done. The Parsha continues with tithes, the blessings and curses proclaimed on Mount Gerizim and Mount Eival, and concludes with the tochacha, warning of the suffering that follows abandoning the Torah.

Every morning we say Modeh Ani, offering thanks while still half-asleep, in pajamas, before even a sip of coffee. Why offer gratitude at our least prepared, least contemplative moment? Throughout the day our conscious mind filters reality through logic; at night, in sleep, that filter falls away, and a more elemental, unfiltered form of knowing takes over. What we know at our very essence -- our faith, our commitment -- remains constant through every state of consciousness, and it is precisely at the threshold of waking, in Modeh Ani, that we express some of our innermost, unfiltered convictions.

Bikkurim, like Modeh Ani, is a declaration of gratitude offered at a moment of transition -- from wilderness to promised land, from slavery to a life flowing with milk and honey. The Talmud holds that bikkurim could not be brought until the entire land was conquered and divided; the Sifri holds they could be brought immediately upon arrival, valuing the awe of a new, unpracticed gratitude even above the depth of a seasoned one.

Crossing the borders between slumber and wakefulness, desert and Zion, expectation and the good life -- these are the paramount moments of transition, opportunities to specify who we are and what we want to become. We are blessed each morning with a prayer that says what we already know: my soul is boundless, and I am lucky to have a loving King who returns it to me each morning and receives it back each night.

May Hashem's presence return to Jerusalem speedily and in our days.

-- Avi

The learning should be a merit for Yonatan ben Avraham Avinu a"h.
