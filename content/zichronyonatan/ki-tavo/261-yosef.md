This summer, my family and I took a wonderful trip to visit family in Denver. Of course, a major highlight of the trip was visiting the Rocky Mountains, where we spent two beautiful day trips. Since we were a large group, it was important to us to find free or inexpensive trips, and we were thrilled to find two such free experiences in the Rockies.

The first was Red Rocks, a world-famous venue with the most incredible rock formations colored a deep red hue, as its name would suggest. There we found a public porch big enough for our entire party, with stunning views of the park, complete with tables and chairs; but for some reason it was completely empty – as if reserved for us. We set up camp and enjoyed dinner there, wondering aloud about how we had chanced upon such a dining experience completely free of charge.

Our next trip was to Breckenridge, where there is a free cable car to the top of the mountain. Everyone enjoyed to views and the ride, again marveling at the fact that we could do this for free. From there we drove to Blue Lakes Trail, a place of otherworldly beauty and splendor. My words cannot possibly describe the magnificence of this mountain lake and waterfall, sitting almost as high as the timberline, where we spent two unforgettable hours in ecstasy. And again, we felt fortunate to be enjoying all of this completely free of charge.

A wonderful vacation. Great times. And life goes on.

Back home in Los Angeles, I was reminded of a statement from Rav Yisroel Salanter that got me thinking. He said that this world is like a very expensive hotel. If one drinks a cup of coffee at home it costs a few cents, in a cheap restaurant it’s a dollar or two, in a fancy coffee house it might cost five dollars, and in a world-class hotel it could cost ten. Why do the prices differ so much? Because you’re not just paying for the coffee, but for the whole setting – the ambiance, the décor, the service, the music, and so on.

If the ambiance in a hotel elevates the coffee to ten dollars, how much does Hashem’s world add to the price tag? Let’s go back to the coffee you’re drinking in your kitchen or your back porch for a few cents. All around you there is sweet, life-giving oxygen. There are birds chirping in the background and a gentle breeze rustling through the assortment of trees. Perhaps your children or grandchildren are playing in the background, and pictures of your family grace the walls. The sun might be shining brightly, or perhaps there’s a fresh blanket of snow all around. Your hands effortlessly lift the coffee and your lips adjust perfectly to the curvature of your mug. And of course, we all understand that this extensive list hasn’t even scratched the surface.

So, let’s revisit the question. How much is that cup of coffee worth, backdrop included?

Priceless. Truly priceless.

That ‘simple’ cup of coffee is worth more than any mortal fortune. In fact, Rav Yisroel says that if we would spend our entire lives serving the Creator of all that goodness, we could never repay Him for even that one cup of coffee. He warns that with one worldly pleasure we are at risk of consuming the entire reward waiting for us in the next world!

This is a very alarming statement from one well-qualified to make such assertions. For one pleasure? What then is one to do?!

And if this is true of a ‘simple’ cup of coffee in my own back yard, what kind of bill does a dinner overlooking Red Rocks rack up? Or an afternoon at Blue Lakes? Or a cable car in Breckenridge? There truly is no such thing as a free ride, and these things are far far beyond our means.

Thankfully, Rav Yisroel didn’t leave it up to us to devise a plan of action; he finished off the hotel analogy with a lifeline for all of us. If one is on a business trip and the company puts him up at the hotel, he can enjoy all of the amenities without a worry about the bill. After all, he’s working for the company and all of his expenses are on the house.

This is our lifeline. If our lives are centered on doing the will of our Creator, if we see ourselves as ambassadors for Hashem, if our ultimate purpose in life is Torah and mitzvos, then we are working for the company. As an emissary of Hashem, we can enjoy all the pleasures which we cannot begin to afford, and it’s all on the house.

And perhaps we can add an additional idea. We certainly should strive for Rav Yisroel’s ideal of living our entire lives focused on the service of Hashem, but if we fall short, I think there is a more case by case tactic that may help us. Say that one isn’t working for the company, but he receives a sample in exchange for writing a review. This would also be free of charge, since his enjoyment is of interest to the manufacturer. If we can utilize the enjoyments of life as vehicles to increase our gratitude to Hashem and as catalysts to renewed and reinvigorated service of Hashem, then each individual pleasure is of interest to Hashem, and is thus provided free of charge.

So this Elul, let’s make sure we’re working for the right company by centering our lives around Torah and mitzvos. Let us seize every pleasure, from the daily joys to the one-time experiences, as a means of growing closer to Hashem. And as such, let us truly enjoy life, while secure in the knowledge that it’s all on the bill of the Almighty.

Good Shabbos,

Yosef
