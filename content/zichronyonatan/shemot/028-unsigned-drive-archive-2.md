A group of women met together to learn, and came across a verse in Malachei (3:3) referring to G-d: "He will sit as a refiner and purifier of silver."

This verse puzzled the women, and they wondered how this statement applied to the character and nature of Hashem.

One of the women offered to find out more about the process of refining silver.

The following week, the woman called up a silversmith and made an appointment to watch him while at work. She didn't mention anything about the reason for her interest, beyond her curiosity about the process of refining silver.

As she watched the silversmith work, he held a piece of silver over the fire and let it heat up. He explained that in refining silver one needed to hold the silver in the middle of the fire, where the flames are the hottest, as to burn away all of the impurities.

She asked the silversmith if it was true that he had to sit in front of the fire the entire time the silver was being refined. The man answered that yes, not only did he have to sit there *holding the silver*, but he also had to *keep his eyes on it the entire time* it was in the fire. If the silver was left even a moment too long in the flames, it would be destroyed.

The woman was silent for a moment. Then she asked the silversmith,

"But how do you know when the silver is fully refined?"

He smiled at her and answered,

"Oh, that's easy -- *when I see my image in it*."

This week we start the book of Shemot, Exodus, and with it the beginning of the Jews' difficult exile in Egypt. Egypt is often referred to as the "iron furnace," because the hardships that we endured in Egypt, refined us and allowed us to become a pure and holy nation.

Yet, just as the silversmith never casts his gaze away from the precious piece of silver in his hands, G-d lovingly held onto His people throughout their hard exile, never taking His compassionate eyes away of their pain. Indeed, the book of Shemot, which describes the Jews' sojourn into Egypt and the various afflictions that they encountered there, is nearly overflowing with clear demonstrations of Hashem's loving kindness.

In the beginning of the Parsha we learn about an incredible phenomenon. Pharaoh becomes king and sets out to get rid of the Jews who are inhabiting his land. Yet, at the same time, we find that the Jews start multiplying at an unprecedented rate, miraculously having six babies at one time! In fact, Rashi tells us just as the Egyptians set out to afflict and get rid of the Jews, so Hashem set out to bless and increase them. Despite the Egyptians' evil intents and the intense fire of oppression, G-d openly displayed His ever-present love for His people, showering them with blessing in the hardest of times.

Another mind-boggling portrayal of G-d's guiding hand, which never releases its loving hold of the Jewish nation, takes place in Pharaoh's palace. Pharaoh's astrologers tell him that the Jews will have a savior who will help free them from their bondage. Pharaoh thus sets out to have every Jewish baby boy thrown into the Nile so that their savior will never be able to rescue them. However, despite the Egyptians' great efforts to kill out all of the Jewish boys, the Jewish savior himself, Moshe, ends up being raised in Pharaoh's own house! This unbelievable twist of events is such a beautiful demonstration of G-d lovingly manipulating events for the best of His people, thus showing them that the difficulties that they were encountering were likewise for their ultimate best.

Later on, as the afflictions in Egypt get worse, the Jews are literally crying out from anguish, and the verse tells us that Hashem hears their painful cries. Rashi explains that G-d did not close His eyes to their sorrow, but rather took their cries to heart. Much like the silver refiner, Hashem did not take His eyes off of His precious people for even a moment, carefully and lovingly watching them to ensure that the painful oppression that they had to endure should not destroy them.

Perhaps, with this understanding of the Jews' exile in Egypt, we can appreciate why G-d chose to appear to Moshe in a burning bush. G-d was demonstrating to Moshe, and to the entire Jewish nation, that not only was He watching over them, but He was actually with His people in the burning fire, and feeling their pain.

Here we are, centuries later, but sadly, still in Exile. It is imperative for us to constantly remember that even when the going gets rough, Hashem is with us, lovingly guiding us through every step of the way.

*If today you are feeling the heat of the fire:*

*Remember that you are in Hashem's hand,*

*He has His eye on you,*

*And will keep holding you and watching you*

*Until He sees His image in you.*
