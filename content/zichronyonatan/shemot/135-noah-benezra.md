This week's Parsha introduces us to a new Egyptian Pharaoh who we are informed, “didn’t know Yosef.” How is it possible that the king of Egypt didn’t know about the chief “central banker” that saved them from economic collapse. Asserting that the Pharoh did not know Yosef is as unbelievable as asserting that the current president doesn’t know Ben Bernanke. How are we to understand the massive change that enabled Yosef and his descendants to go from the progeny of a respected technocrat to the slaves upon which the Egyptian empire would be built? What changed so drastically that all of the good that Yosef did was forgotten?

One answer that is commonly provided to this question is that the new king knew who Yosef was. The name was more than a little familiar. The knowledge of Yosef that was suddenly lacking was hakares-hatov, or appreciation. While Yosef the character was still renown throughout the land, the new king no longer felt a sense of appreciation toward him or his descendants. While the previous attitude was based on the axiom that Yosef was working for the betterment of Egyptian society as a whole, a new attitude, fueled by a combination of Yosef’s families success and hateful speculation arose. Suddenly appreciation was replaced by fear. What if they outnumber us? What if they join with our enemies? These hypothetical fears took the place where appreciation used to reside. These fears worked to undermine all the benefits brought to Egypt with Yaakov and his sons. Fear resulted in years of enslavement, untold misery and the eventual massacre of our baby boys.

The book of Shemos is the transition point of our story as a family to our story as a nation. No longer are we an insulated group of 70 souls. We are numerous and many, with smaller families, clans and division within our ranks. Despite this, we are to be molded into a unified nation, forged in the fiery destruction of the greatest empire the world had ever seen. Each time the Egyptian nation falters and crumbles the future Jewish nation is watching and taking notes. We have the opportunity to learn how to be a successful nation-state by learning from the failures of Egypt. And what is the first lesson we are provided on our outset? Appreciation instead of fear.

A nation cannot thrive and prosper built upon a foundation of distrust and fear. We cannot achieve stability and tranquility unless we open our eyes to the kindness and good being done all around us. Acknowledging that we have needs that can only be filled by others, outsiders, the stranger in our land, and sincerely appreciating them, fixes faults and strengthens society. But when we are afraid, constantly worried and distrusting, we become like the Egyptians and are capable of committing unbelievable atrocities. An inability to see the good done by individuals enables stereotyping and hate. Only by opening our minds and hearts to the positivity that exists all around us can we stave off the inevitable fear of the unknown.

Shabbat Shalom,

Noah BenEzra
