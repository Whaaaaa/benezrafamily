In this week's Parsha we are introduced to the protagonist of the Exodus saga, Moshe Rabbenu. His miraculous entrance into the world is described in Sotah 13a.

ואומרת עתידה אמי שתלד בן שמושיע את ישראל וכיון שנולד משה נתמלא כל הבית כולה אור עמד אביה ונשקה על ראשה אמר לה בתי נתקיימה נבואתיך וכיון שהטילוהו ליאור עמד אביה וטפחה על ראשה אמר לה בתי היכן נבואתיך והיינו דכתיב (שמות ב, ד) ותתצב אחותו מרחוק לדעה מה יעשה לו לידע מה יהא בסוף נבואתה
And as a child, Miriam would say: In the future, my mother will give birth to a son who will save the Jewish people. And once Moses was born, the entire house was filled with light. Her father arose and kissed her on her head. He said to her: My daughter, your prophecy has been fulfilled. And once they put him into the river, her father arose and hit her on her head. He said to her: My daughter, where is your prophecy? And this is as it is written: "And his sister stood afar off, to know what would be done to him" (Exodus 2:4), i.e., to know what will be the ultimate resolution of her prophecy.

This story begs many questions. Where did Amram's faith in the prophecy go? He just experienced a miraculous birth! Furthermore, it was known the whole time that there was a decree against the Jewish males. Why his drastic change in demeanor from a kiss to a potch?

The Beer Mayim Chaim explains that Amram's frustration wasn't rooted in a lack of belief that the salvation would come, rather he wanted to know how it would arise. He wanted to know the exact way his son would be saved and eventually rescue the Jewish people. His initial positive reaction was caused by the confirmation of the prophecy while his second more aggressive reaction was from the frustration of now knowing how the prophecy would be fulfilled.

This is fleshed out even more generally by the Kedushas Levi. He explains that when get bogged down in the physical details of a situation we lose the heavenly perspective necessary to recognize gods plan. This is alluded to by Miriam's reaction, "she stood far off." She distanced herself from the immediate situation to regain the perspective from which she could still see her prophecy come true.

Each child is a miracle. We all know the feeling of being in a house filled with light emanating from the bassinet in the corner. But so quickly, this experience of endless potential is bounded by the how. Sure your a perfect creature capable of anything but how will you get there? How will you learn to sit up and walk and read and socialize and assimilate into Egyptian royalty without losing sight of your people? I believe you will, I really do! But HOW?

And we have to take a lesson from Miriam and step back and view the situation from a distance. We have to remember the overwhelming light that the child brought into the world with them and that original promise and potential. From this perspective, we can escape how and again realize that this is part of God's plan.

Shabbat Shalom
