A group of women, studying together, came across a verse in Malachi referring to G-d: "He will sit as a refiner and purifier of silver." Puzzled, one of them visited a silversmith to understand the metaphor. He explained that refining silver requires holding it in the hottest part of the flame -- and watching it every moment, because left even an instant too long, it will be destroyed. "How do you know when it's fully refined?" she asked. "That's easy," he answered, "when I see my image in it."

This week we begin the book of Shemot, the beginning of our difficult exile in Egypt -- often called the "iron furnace," because the hardships we endured there refined us into a holy nation. Just as the silversmith never looks away from the silver in his hands, G-d never took His eyes off His people through their affliction.

Even as Pharaoh works to destroy the Jews, they multiply miraculously; even as Pharaoh orders Hebrew infants drowned in the Nile, the very child prophesied to be their savior is raised in Pharaoh's own house. Later, as the Torah tells us Hashem "heard" the cries of the enslaved, Rashi explains He did not merely hear, but took their cries to heart -- watching over the affliction to ensure it would not destroy them.

Perhaps this is why G-d chose to appear to Moshe specifically in a burning bush: to show that He was not only watching from a distance, but present within the fire itself, feeling the pain alongside His people.

If today you are feeling the heat of the fire: remember that you are in Hashem's hand, He has His eye on you, and will keep holding you and watching you until He sees His image in you.

Good Shabbos!
