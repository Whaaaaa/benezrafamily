In this week's Parsha Pinchas, A grandson of Aaron is responsible for single-handedly halting a plague and defending the morals of Bnei Yisroel when he stabs an immodest Midianite and her prince husband Zimri for publicly flaunting their intimacy. Pinchas famously is struck by Zimri's immodesty with his wife in public. Pinchas may have seemed to act on impulse, but the Gemara tells us the opposite. Instead of jumping into action Pinchas looked to his elders, Moshe and Aaron were equally taken aback by the scene. Pinchas respectfully reminded Moses that he himself taught the principle that one who behaves as Zimri may be executed by the zealous. At which point Moshe ordered Pinchas to carry out the killing, all in one instant.

Why is Pinchas credited for doing something required by law in such an informal fashion and What made Moshe forget his own doctrines?

When destiny beckons, and there is a situation that we personally can see to resolution an obligation falls upon us to do so. This is Pinchas's test. Moshes inability to draw quick conclusions, set the stage for Pinchas to act rash. If Pinchas would not have looked to his elders and just went ahead he would surely be a murderer. If he looked to his elders and saw that they were speechless and remained speechless the plague would have never lifted. Pinchas knew his place as a young man and his place in a royal family involved in carrying out divine procedures. This was far out of his job description but asserting himself with the oversight of his superiors is what made his trial a success. The Lubavitch rebbe reminds us that when we see the people we look up to most speechless, overrun or out of breath, these are the times our involvement is most meaningful.
