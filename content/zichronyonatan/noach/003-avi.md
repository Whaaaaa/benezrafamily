This week has been particularly difficult for Jews around the world. This week's Parsha however does not focus on Jews and it instead focuses on Noach, the only righteous man in a world steeped in violence and corruption. G-d speaks to Noach and instructs him to build an ark because G-d is going to end humanity with a great flood.

Noach follows G-d's instructions and gathers a pair of every living creature, his direct family, and the waters begin to rise. It rained for forty days and nights and the water churned for 150 days. Once the water calmed, Noach sent doves to find dry land. One year after the beginning of the flood, Noach and his family were finally instructed by G-d to leave the ark and repopulate the drying land.

Noach builds an altar and offers sacrifices to G-d. G-d pledges to never erase humanity again and the rainbow is the sign of His testament. Noach plants grapevines and becomes drunk; two of his sons are commended for covering their father's embarrassment, and one is criticized for taking advantage of it.

Noach's descendants live as a single people with one language for ten generations, until they unite to build a tower to the heavens as a symbol of their own invincibility. G-d humbles them by confusing their language, and they are scattered into seventy nations.

Why did G-d destroy the Tower of Babel? After the flood, humanity's culture became one based purely on survival -- and a shrine built to survival alone is hollow. Our purpose must not be a higher wall and a stronger tower; we must give meaning to our survival by remembering the "why" of our existence. I pray that Jews around the world sanctify G-d's name by being exemplary human beings.

Shabbat Shalom.
