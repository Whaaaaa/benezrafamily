Noach is a patriarch that we can all emulate. This week's Parsha tells us that Noach had doubts about the flood and never entered the ark until it started raining. Noach is not credited with birthing a nation or leading his people to the promised land. Unlike Moshe and Avraham, Noach remains an unsung hero of humanity, and not just of humans -- of all life on earth.

Noach's strength is in surviving and persevering through harsh criticism and doubt. Noach's superpower is not letting even his own doubts distract him from the job -- and the job was not pretty; much of his time on the ark was devoted simply to tending to animals.

There is a Yiddish saying, "Fun a kasha shtarbt men nit" -- "Nobody ever died of a question." While we are torn or doubtful, we cannot become paralyzed. Noach was faced by the doubts of his neighbors and his own doubts, but the stakes were too high not to act.

Noach's people speak one language and share one culture for ten generations, until his descendants build a monument to their own invincibility -- their ultimate downfall, as G-d splits their tongue and scatters them across the land. Noach's gift of action in the face of doubt is a double-edged sword, but a gift nonetheless. Assertive people bring life into a room with them; Noach's ability to be assertive saved humanity.
