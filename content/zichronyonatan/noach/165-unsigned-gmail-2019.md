In this week's Torah Portion, Parshas Noach, there are two major events that take place.

The more famous one is the incredible story of a man named Noach who was instructed by Hashem to build an ark. The people of his time were acting in terrible ways and deserved to drown in a flood. Only Noach, his family, and two of every species of animals (except for the kosher ones, of which seven were selected) would be saved. Why did the entire world need to be destroyed and all the people wiped out? Because they were sinning against each other. Specifically, they were stealing from each other and performing acts of sexual immorality.

The other event that takes place in this week's reading is the story of the Tower of Babel. In this event, the citizens of the world decided that they wanted to build a tower that would reach the heavens. To what end? To fight with God Himself! Their punishment was that they all suddenly spoke different languages and couldn't understand each other. With no way to communicate any longer, the project was abandoned and they dispersed to the four corners of the Earth.

This seems rather strange if one takes a close look. When the people were sinning against each other, the entire world needed to be obliterated. But when they attempted to fight and defy God Himself, they only needed to be dispersed and separated so they could no longer rebel against Him?

Perhaps the story could be better understood if I share one of my own. When I was a little girl, I shared a room with four brothers. One night, we were sent to bed and given permission to read on our own until we fell asleep. A brother who was not particularly interested in reading took a puzzle to his bed. It soon became too difficult to put it together on a surface that was not wide and flat so he moved it to the floor and invited us one by one to come and help him. I was very engrossed in a book and was the last one to join. Only moments later, my father walked into the room to check on us. Imagine his surprise when he saw us not in bed at all but with the light on and working on a puzzle! But then his face softened.

He said, "Had only a few of you been out of bed I probably would have been very angry, but you are all together and collaborating and there is no greater joy for a parent than to see his children working in unity."

It happened to have been that that night was the 9th of Av, the saddest day in the Jewish calendar. It is the anniversary of when the Holy Temple was destroyed. We are taught that the reason why it was destroyed was because the Jews were not getting along with each other and were acting hateful towards one another. It was a time that was especially important to my father that his children love each other and he was very happy to see that.

That is the explanation to our earlier conundrum. When the humans of the Earth were sinning in their interactions with each other, they didn't deserve to live. They needed to be completely wiped out. But when they were acting in unity, even against Hashem, they can live. God loves all of his creations, and what he wants from us is to love each other.

Let us try our best to love, not judge. To help, not deride. To support, not tear down. Let us do right by each other and Hashem will shine His blessings down upon us.

Good Shabbos
