This week's parsha is that of my namesake and I have always taken it personally. There is an ongoing theme found in the commentary throughout this parsha that has always been difficult for me to understand and accept. Noach is contrasted to Avraham in multiple places and this almost always results in Noach being viewed as the lesser of the two.

The first words of this week's Parsha say, "These are the offspring of Noach, Noach was a righteous man, perfect in his generations; Noach walked with G-d." No other person in the Torah is described as "righteous." But the sages compare these praises to the exaltations of Avraham: "Avraham walked before G-d." While Noach was in lock step with the Creator, Avraham was running ahead. Rav Eliyahu Dessler zt"l explains that while Noach is called a man of righteousness, Avraham is a man of kindness -- Noach's acts stemmed from obligation, Avraham's from a burning desire to give.

The sages further ask why Noach is called righteous "in his generations" -- implying his greatness was only relative to the corruption around him, while Avraham's was absolute.

I once heard an alternative reading of Rashi's comment that Noach "believed and didn't believe" the flood would come -- that the comma should move, so that it reads: Noach believed in those who lacked faith, and (because he was confident they would repent) did not enter the ark until the water pushed him. According to this, Noach's flaw wasn't too little faith in G-d, but too little urgency in trying to save others.

On a personal level, I feel much better having studied this. My brother's namesake, Avraham, was never in competition with mine -- he took the example Noach set and put fresh passion behind it. God willing, we should all merit to share in Avraham's example of being a light to others.

Shabbat Shalom, Noah
