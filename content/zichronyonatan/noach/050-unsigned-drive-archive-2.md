This week has been particularly difficult for Jews around the world. This weeks Parsha however does not focus on Jews and it instead focuses on Noach the only righteous man in a world steeped in violence and backwards corruption. God speaks to Noach and instructs him to build an ark because God is going to end humanity with a great flood.

Noach follows God's instructions and gathers a pair of every living creature his direct family and the tides began to rise. It rained for forty days and nights and the water churned for 150 days. Once the water calmed Noach sent doves to find dry land. One year after the beginning of the flood Noach and his family were finally instructed by God to leave the ark and repopulate the drying land.

Noach builds an alter and offers sacrifices to God. God pledges to never erase humanity again and the rainbow is the sign of His testament. God commands Noach about the sanctity of life and murder is designated as a capital offense. While man is permitted to eat meat he is not allowed to eat living flesh.

Noach plants grapevines and becomes drunk on wine. Two of his three sons Shem and Yapheth are commended for covering up their fathers embarrassment. Ham is criticized for taking advantage of his fathers shame.

Noachs descendants live as a single people with one language and culture for ten generations. They unite in the effort to build a tower to the heavens as a symbol of their invincibility. God humbles them by splitting their tongue making it impossible for them to understand eachother. They abandoned their project and divided into seventy nations.

The Parsha concludes by recounting the ten generations between Noach and Abram (Abraham) and Abram's journey to the land of Cannan.

They said to one another: ". . . Let us build for ourselves a city, and a tower whose top shall reach the heavens; and we shall make for ourselves a name, lest we be scattered over the face of the entire earth . . ."

G-d dispersed them from there across the face of the earth, and they stopped building the city. Therefore its name was called Babel (confusion), for there G-d confused the language of the world . . . (Genesis 11:3-9)

Why did God destroy the tower of Babel? After God destroyed humanity their culture was one based on survival. They built a shrine to the cause of survival and that was the beginning of their descent. The cause of survival is hollow and the purpose of human life goes far beyond survival. All too often we Jews find ourselves fighting for our very survival and in times like this we must remember the lesson of the tower of Babel. Our purpose must not be a higher wall and stronger tower rather we must give meaning to our survival by reiterating the "why?" of our existence. We must give our name meaning, our city significance and crown our towers with a higher purpose. I pray that Jews around the world sanctify Gods name by being exemplary humans. Shabbat Shalom.
