Noach is approached by God and informed of his intention to destroy the earth. As an exceptionally righteous man in a wicked world, he alone has been selected to build a boat that will ensure the continuation of humanity. He does not argue or ask questions, "Noach did so, just as God commanded him, so he did."

Then the rain starts to fall. At 600 years old Noach gathers his family in the foyer to depart their home and enter the vessel he has dedicated himself to creating. They have their bags packed, the animals are caged away. Their house is empty and life savings have gone to gopher wood. Looking out the front door window they see the drops start getting bigger. Noach's wife Naamah is standing beside her three sons and daughters-in-law. It is silent. There is palpable anticipation hanging in the air. Everyone is waiting for Noach to open the door and lead the way into the ark. But he is standing there frozen, gaze transfixed on the puddles rapidly accumulating in the front lawn.

Rashi alludes to this mysterious moment. After 600 years of faithful service, and the commitment and investment that goes into an ark, why would Noach be hesitating now? According to Rashi, "Noach was of those who lack faith, he believed and didn't believe that the flood would come and did not enter the Ark until he was pushed by the water." Despite demonstrating his faithfulness and listening to God's every word consistently through this whole process, somehow he still lacked some deeper belief. Even though he had reoriented his entire life around this goal when the moment of truth arrived, he choked.

This perspective on Noach's moment reveals that true faith is expressed in the less significant daily decisions we make and confirmed on the occasions that require complete trust. Building the ark demanded the former, which Noach was comfortable with, but when his emuna required blind faith he couldn't give himself and his family over. From an outside perspective, it seems unbelievable that Noach would go so far only to stall at the finish. But I know that I expend an inordinate amount of energy on goals that I believe are important while not allowing that belief to propel me to take a risk or make a significant change.

While I was here in Yeshiva I heard another take on this dramatic moment. Instead of reading Rashi the way it is translated above, by moving a comma (and ignoring a little grammar) his comment can be read, "Noach also believed in those who were lacking belief so he did not enter the Ark until he was pushed by the water."

In this reading, Noach had so much faith in those around him that he believed they would come to their senses and abandon their sinful ways. His faith in humanity was so great it could overshadow all of the toil of building the ark and the torrential rains. Clearly, we can see the peril of having too much faith in harmful people and not allowing ourselves to get away until we're in over our heads.

I hope that I can internalize the lessons of this episode from both perspectives. I want the courage to take a leap of faith and the sense to know when I am letting my faith in others drag me down.
