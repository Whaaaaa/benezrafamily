We now stand, quite literally, on the brink of the world. *On Rosh Hashanah they are inscribed, and on Yom Kippur they are sealed.* On Rosh Hashana, the world was recreated from scratch. All that we have taken for granted in the past is no longer a given. Rather, if we wish for a good and prosperous year it has to be rewritten now, and it is up to us to decide what will be written.

But how? What is the secret to achieving a positive judgment and a year full of blessing? The answer, we are taught, is *teshuvah.* Simply translated, *teshuvah* means repentance. But a more accurate translation would render it "returning". *Teshuvah* means returning back home, back to our loving Father in heaven.

I heard a beautiful story about a rebellious teenager, Sam, who ran away from home; shirking his responsibilities at the family farm in order to explore the world. Sam's father was livid with his son's behavior, and vowed that if he chose to leave home he would never be welcomed back again. Sam stuck to his guns and set out, with just a few coins to his name. He loved the adventure and for a while he was content scavenging for food and sleeping in the streets. The freedom thrilled him and he found it worthwhile to live the lowly life of a beggar, so long as he could do as he pleased. But as the months went on, Sam began to feel homesick. He missed his mother's warm meals and the security of his father's home. He longed for his parents embrace, but he remembered his father's threat all too clearly, and he feared that he'd never again be welcome back at the farm. In desperation, Sam penned a letter to his mother describing his regret and his wish to come back. He asked that if his father was willing to let him return, they should tie a white handkerchief around the apple tree in front of the house. If he would see the handkerchief as he rode by on the train, he would know that he was wanted back home.

As soon as he saved up enough money, Sam boarded the train and set off towards the place he had once called home. As he neared, Sam began to shake with trepidation. He willed there to be a white handkerchief on the tree, but he was too scared to look. Not knowing what to do, Sam closed his eyes and asked the man sitting next to him to tell him what he saw. As they passed, the man clapped him on the back and said, "Son, there are white handkerchiefs tied around every branch of that tree."

We may have gone astray, forsaking the warmth and comfort of G-d's embrace in order to chase after our wants and desires. We may have wandered far from home, sullying ourselves in the muck of our sins, all in the name of freedom. But despite it all, on Yom Kippur every Jew longs to finally pick himself up and return back home, back to our loving Father's embrace. And lest we fear that we are no longer welcome, G-d has hung white handkerchiefs around every branch of the tree. He gave us the entire month of Elul, Rosh Hashanah, the Ten Days of Repentance, and Yom Kippur -- forty days imbued with the tremendous power to cleanse and lift ourselves up. He is begging us to come back and has opened the door wide for anyone who's willing to take the invitation.

As we're looking to do *teshuvah*, it is certainly imperative to repent for our individual sins as enumerated in the Yom Kippur prayers. However, the basis of our *teshuvah* must be in its most literal sense - returning to our loving Father, making Him part of our lives, and living with Him on a daily basis. This is much like the relationship with any loved one. When you share a relationship, you keep in touch. You communicate on a regular basis, in order to keep your loved one involved in your life. So must we try to build a relationship with G-d Himself.

This is what *teshuvah* means at its most fundamental level.

This may sound lofty at first, but it's really not that hard. When you're running late and you need a parking spot, take a quick moment and ask G-d to help. When you're making supper, ask Him to make it delicious. If you're taking a test at school or an assessment at work, He can help you do your best. And when you find that spot or perform well, don't forget to say thank you. This is a very practical way to rebuild a relationship with our Creator, to truly make Him part of our lives and thus fulfill the most basic element of *teshuvah*.

May we all merit a complete *teshuvah*, and thereby may we be blessed with a Shana tovah umetukah. A sweet new year filled with all the blessings that come from being home, within the embrace of our loving Father. *[Inspired by the writings of Rabbi Shlomo Wolbe and Rabbi Yissochor Frand.]*

*Good Shabbos and Good Yom Tov,*

*Yosef*
