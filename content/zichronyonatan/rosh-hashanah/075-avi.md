As the month of Elul comes to an end and we approach Rosh Hashanah, we are filled with a sense of dread. Elul is an acronym for the phrase in Shir Hashirim, "Ani Le'dodi Ve'dodi Li" -- "I am for my beloved and my beloved is for me." In a month associated with fear and strict justice, it seems strange to address G-d as our beloved -- why does G-d even need to judge us? Why celebrate?

G-d, by definition, is perfect and complete; His judgment is not for revenge, but for our benefit. On Rosh Hashanah we remember that G-d cares about everything we do, say, and think -- and that this personal attention is itself proof of love. As any relationship counselor knows, indifference, not conflict, is the true sign of a lack of love; G-d shows no indifference at all.

This is laid out in the Rambam's thirteen principles: the tenth, that G-d knows the actions, speech, and thoughts of every person and does not ignore them, and the eleventh, that G-d rewards and punishes accordingly. Why does the Rambam need both principles separately? In theory, G-d could have built a world where reward and punishment happen automatically, without His constant awareness required. The Rambam insists on the tenth principle specifically to teach that G-d prioritizes His relationship with us over the mere mechanical fulfillment of mitzvot.

On Rosh Hashanah we celebrate how much G-d loves us, and the strange privilege of standing before a Judge who is both full of mercy and strict in judgment. We do not know why G-d allows bad things to happen to good people, but we are reminded of Avraham and the binding of Yitzchak, and of the sacrifices Jews have made throughout history to sustain our relationship with G-d.
