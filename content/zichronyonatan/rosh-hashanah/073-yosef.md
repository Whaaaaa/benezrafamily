We now stand, quite literally, on the brink of the world. On Rosh Hashanah they are inscribed, and on Yom Kippur they are sealed. On Rosh Hashanah the world is recreated from scratch -- nothing we have taken for granted is a given; it must be rewritten now, and it is up to us to decide what will be written.

The secret, we are taught, is teshuvah -- more accurately, "returning": returning home, back to our loving Father in heaven. I once heard a story of a rebellious teenager, Sam, who ran away from his family's farm to see the world, and whose father, furious, vowed he'd never be welcomed back. Homesick after months of freedom, Sam wrote his mother, asking that if his father would take him back, they tie a white handkerchief on the apple tree by the house, so he could see it from the passing train. Too frightened to look himself as the train approached, he asked the man beside him to look instead. "Son," the man said, "there are white handkerchiefs tied around every branch of that tree."

We may have wandered far from home in pursuit of our own wants -- but G-d has hung white handkerchiefs around every branch. He has given us Elul, Rosh Hashanah, the Ten Days of Repentance, and Yom Kippur -- forty days with the power to lift us up -- and He is begging us to take the invitation.

The basis of teshuvah, at its most fundamental level, is returning to a real, daily relationship with our loving Father -- checking in, communicating regularly, the way you would with anyone you love. This is not as lofty as it sounds: when running late and needing a parking spot, ask G-d for help; when cooking, ask Him to make it delicious; and when it works out, remember to say thank you.

May we all merit a complete teshuvah, and a Shana Tovah U'Metukah -- a sweet new year filled with the blessings that come from being home, within the embrace of our loving Father.

Good Shabbos and Good Yom Tov, Yosef
