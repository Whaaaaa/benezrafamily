As I stand at the threshold of the incoming year I am overwhelmed with disappointment, remembering how many commitments went unkept this year -- how I am still, in countless ways, the exact same person I was last year. These feelings are not conducive to a holiday atmosphere.

Fully understanding Rosh Hashanah as both a festival and a day of judgment requires a real perspective shift. If the guilt described above were what the day was meant to elicit, our liturgy would be full of groveling and pleading. Instead, the lengthy service contains almost no references to personal judgment at all -- it is focused on the state of the world as a whole, on G-d's kingship, on the fact that a true King loves and does what's best for His people.

The Torah reading for Rosh Hashanah offers a clue: G-d hears Yishmael's cry "where he was" -- not his location, but his exact moment. God did not weigh Yishmael's descendants' future conflicts, or his own past, in judging him. The Talmud learns from this that a person is judged only according to his actions in the exact moment of judgment. Rosh Hashanah is not about the past or the future -- it is about reclaiming the present.

This is why Rosh Hashanah, "the birthday of the world," is not observed on the day the world was created, but on the day humanity was created -- because the defining human capacity is free will, and free will operates only in the present.

Rosh Hashanah's purpose, then, is to gauge our intent, not our track record. Are we curled up in self-pity over past failures, or have we dusted ourselves off, filled with excitement for the year ahead? This is why our day of judgment comes before our day of atonement -- we cannot ask forgiveness for the past until we've freed our free will from its grip, as the twelve steps teach: "coming to believe" comes before "making amends."

Mishlei says the righteous fall seven times and rise again. I am exchanging my dread for this attitude: I am not defined by the times I fell last year, but by the fact that I have risen again to celebrate a new cycle. I still believe sanity is attainable in this world, and I will look in the mirror and see the same hopeful face, resilient and strong, ready to fight the same battles with renewed vigor.

Shana Tova U'Metuka and Shabbat Shalom, Noah
