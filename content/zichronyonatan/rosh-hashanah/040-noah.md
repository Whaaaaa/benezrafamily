As I stand at the threshold of the incoming year I am overwhelmed with disappointment. Reflecting on the past year I remember how many times I made commitments that went unkept. I recall the rush of excitement and pride in imaging how different I would be this year and how much I would change for the better. But looking in the mirror on Erev Rosh Hashanah morning the face staring back at me looks frighteningly familiar. Here I am, in countless ways the exact same person I was last year. I am still struggling against the same character defects, fighting the same desires and caught up in the same old vicious cycles. So many of the improvements that I was committed to making in my life have not come to fruition. These feeling are not conducive to a holiday atmosphere and suck the aspiration out of me.

That's why fully understanding how Rosh Hashanah is both a festival and the day of judgement requires a huge perspective shift. If the attitude described above was what Rosh Hashanah was meant to elicit, our liturgy should be full of groveling, pleading, and begging for divine forgiveness. But instead, the lengthy Rosh Hashanah service contains almost no references to the personal judgment that is occurring. It is focused on the state of our world as a whole, declaring that there is a God that is aware of everything, and that through his continued sustenance of this world he demonstrates his deep love and concern. We focus on the aspect of Kingship in the sense that a true King loves and does what's best for his people. This is the first tip that the judgment that occurs on Rosh Hashanah is much deeper than the judgement we are used to seeing.

The Torah reading for Rosh Hashanah offers more clues into the nature of the day. We read about Yishmael pleading to be spared. The Torah says that, "God heard the voice of the boy where he was." The redundancy of the phrase "where he was" is meant to teach us a crucial lesson. It clearly does not refer to Yishmael's physical location, rather it is coming to say that God heard the voice of the boy in that moment. The future conflicts that would arise between his progeny and the Jewish people did not weigh into the equation, nor did his previous actions. God did not compare Yishmael to who he was last year or to his future self to determine his fate. From this the Talmud learns that a person is judged only according to his actions in the exact moment of his judgement. Our Rosh Hashanah is not about looking to the past or the future but it solely about reclaiming the present.

The importance of our present is also found in the name of the holiday. Despite being called, "the birthday of the world," Rosh Hashanah is not celebrated on the day the world was created. Rather it is observed from the day that mankind was created. If the preexisting world, already colorful and mountainous, blooming with life and illuminated by a sun moon and stars, wasn't enough to merit commemoration, what did humans add to the mix that was so special? The answer is found in the defining characteristic of a human. This is our ability to make choices, to exercise free will. Free will is our method of interaction with the present, there is no ability to choose or decide the future or the past.

For these reasons, it is apparent that Rosh Hashanah's purpose is to gauge our intent and not our actions. After another long, taxing year are we curled up moping around in our own self-pity or have we dusted ourselves off, cleared our heads and filled ourselves with and excitement for the year ahead? Are we going to allow that failings of the past, and the fear of the future to define our decisions and destiny, or can we harness this opportunity to start anew?

This change in perspective perfectly explains why our day of judgement comes before our day of atonement. As each year passes we find ourselves caught in some of the same vicious cycles. When we allow our free will to be bound to the past we end up unable to make real lasting change. We become comforted by our vices and begin to lose our will to fight against them. We cannot ask for forgiveness and try to atone before we have freed ourselves from the shackles of the past. The twelve step program beautifully conveys this point. To escape a destructive habit the twelve steps require "Coming to believe that a Power greater than ourselves can restore us to sanity" way before "Making a list of all persons we have harmed, and becoming willing to make amends to them all." Rosh Hashanah is when we state that we believe there is sanity that will be restored to our world and that we no longer have to engage in behaviors that are harmful just because we have in the past. Only after this process can we begin to make amends, the culmination of which will be Yom Kippur.

Mishlei famously states that the righteous fall seven times and rise again. This is the attitude that I'm exchanging my dread for. Upon entering Rosh Hashanah I am going to remember that I am not defined by the times I fell last year but by the fact I have risen again to celebrate the start of a new cycle. Once again I have the opportunity want to be better, to want to make the right choices and to escape the pitfalls along the way. I still believe that the entire creation exists to help me grow and that sanity is attainable in this world. When I look in the mirror I will see the same hopeful face that imagined a better future and will be proud that I am that same person, resilient and strong, ready to fight the same battles with renewed vigor.

Shana Tova U'Metuka and Shabbat Shalom, Noah
