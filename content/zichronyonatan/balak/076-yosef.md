Bilam, someone infamous for the profound potency of his curses, tried multiple times, with many different tactics, to curse the Jews as they marched toward the land of Canaan. Although his employer, Balak the king of Moav, only wanted the curse to keep the Jews from invading his land, Bilam wickedly decided to do away with the Jewish problem once and for all -- shooting for their total annihilation.

Hashem, however, had different plans. He forewarned Bilam that even if he were to attempt to follow Balak and curse the people his efforts would be unfruitful -- his mouth would only pronounce the words placed there by Hashem. This arrangement resulted in some of the most beautiful blessings our people have ever received being bestowed upon us by our arch-enemy. Still, our sages say that from the blessings we can infer that the intended curses must have been the same point reversed.

Given these two points, that Bilam was only interested in our complete destruction and that all of his blessing mirrored the intended curse, it is rather puzzling to understand one of the most famous verses in his liturgy. He says, 'How good are your tents, Yaakov, and your tabernacles, Yisroel.' The sages explain that these are references to the shuls and the Yeshivos where Jews unite in the study of Torah. According to the principle above, we can infer that Bilam must have tried to curse the Yeshivos and to hamper our ability to learn Torah. This is a surprising attack from one seeking blood alone. It would be like Hitler neglecting his gas chambers in favor of ensuring that the Jews shouldn't speak of Torah in the barracks.

What did the Yeshivos have to do with Bilam's dastardly scheme?

Rav Elchonon Wasserman zt"l explained with a war analogy. When two countries go to war, there may be a great many battles fought and a great many casualties suffered. One country may far exceed its enemy when it comes to individual battles won, but that still doesn't define the victor. At what point can they claim victory? When they conquer the capital city. When the capital falls to the enemy, the war is over. There may still be remaining towns to overtake and battalions to vanquish, but these are just the finishing stages -- the war has already been won.

Bilam knew that the Yeshivos, the halls of Torah learning, are the capital of the Jewish nation. Were he to focus only on the physical lives of the Jews, he knew that he would never be able to claim victory. Only by targeting our lifeblood, our core, and our capital fortresses of Torah, could he achieve his ultimate goal of annihilation.

If the wicked Bilam understood the centrality of Torah learning not only for our spiritual existence but for our physical vitality, then we, the Chosen Nation, must certainly learn the lesson as well. So, let's redouble our efforts in the pursuit of Torah learning; through personal study, through teaching, and through supporting our institutions of Torah. In this way, and only in this way, will we succeed in rebuilding the glory of our nation as it finds it center in the capital fortress of Torah.
