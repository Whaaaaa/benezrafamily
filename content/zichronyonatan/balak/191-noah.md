In this week's parsha we learn about the infamous curses of Billam against the Jewish people. This conflict is archetypal of the us versus them mentality that has taken root around the world. First, King Balak and the people of Moab begin having anxiety about the rapidly growing neighboring Jewish population that is consuming large amounts of resources, "as the ox licks up the grass of the field." This anxiety results in monumental cooperation between historical rivals, Moab and Midian, and true fulfillment of "the enemy of my enemy is my friend." With this alliance established,
וַיִּשְׁלַ֨ח מַלְאָכִ֜ים אֶל־בִּלְעָ֣ם בֶּן־בְּע֗וֹר פְּ֠תוֹרָה אֲשֶׁ֧ר עַל־הַנָּהָ֛ר אֶ֥רֶץ בְּנֵי־עַמּ֖וֹ לִקְרֹא־ל֑וֹ לֵאמֹ֗ר הִ֠נֵּה עַ֣ם יָצָ֤א מִמִּצְרַ֙יִם֙ הִנֵּ֤ה כִסָּה֙ אֶת־עֵ֣ין הָאָ֔רֶץ וְה֥וּא יֹשֵׁ֖ב מִמֻּלִֽי׃
[Balak] sent messengers to Balaam son of Beor in Pethor, which is by the Euphrates, in the land of his kinsfolk, to invite him, saying, "There is a people that came out of Egypt; it hides the earth from view, and it is settled next to me.
Let's unpack the Balak's reasoning for hiring this hitman. First, the posuk mentions the "land of his kinsfolk" again stressing the sense of relatedness that now existed between these nations. Next, the posuk mentions, "to invite him." Rashi explains that this is a hint to the large cash payment that Balaam received for his willingness to act against this perceived threat. Balak now mentions the Exodus from Egypt. The Aderet Eliyahu asks why would Balak mention a time of Israel's incredible triumph while trying to rally the troops against them? What is the connection between this and the incitement Balak is attempting? The Kli Yakar explains that this was to make it clear that we were not a settled people, but migrants. This made the Jewish people seem more vulnerable and Balak's suspicion blinded him from even noticing the incredible triumph that was their departure. But agitating against the vulnerable isn't always enough to convince others to join your cause. Rashi explains;

עם יצא ממצרים
[THERE IS] A PEOPLE COME OUT FROM EGYPT — and if you ask: What harm does that do you?
If you are like the questioner in Rashi and aren't convinced yet, Balak will now roll out his final justification for cursing a nation. "It hides the earth from view." In Hebrew this phrase literally means, cover the eyes of the earth. Rashi explains that this is an allusion to Sichon and Og, the giants that we conquered in the previous parsha. Sifsei Chachamim says that Rashi isn't sticking with the simpler meaning that the Jewish camp was massive because we know it covered only 12 mil, a relatively small area. The problem wasn't really about overpopulation or resource consumption, but because they saw that we took away something they relied on. The giants are referred to as the eyes of the world because Balak relied on them for protection like his own eyes. The insecurity that our success provoked was the final justification for Balak's enmity toward the Jewish people.

In the end, we know that Balaam was not successful. The many merits of our communal structure, its modesty, and commitment to holiness, saved us from the impact of these curses. Still, we have to learn from this attempt against us. We have to learn to identify the patterns of "us versus them." It starts with the consolidation of a silent, previous fragmented, majority. This group, motivated by economic anxiety, has a leader promise riches while directing this worry toward a vulnerable minority. This minority fits the accusation as they have already caused insecurity by threatening the integrity of GIANT systems of oppression. Sound familiar?
