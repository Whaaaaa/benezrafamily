Bilam is standing on a mountain overlooking a portion of the Jewish camp in the desert, Balak alongside him, hoping this third attempt will finally produce a curse. Instead, the words "Mah Tovu" emanate from Bilam's mouth -- praise so enduring it was written into our siddur, mandated to be said upon entering a synagogue. What do Bilam's words teach us about the essence of a Jewish community?

Rashi explains that Bilam noticed the entrances to the Jewish tents were offset, so no one could see directly into a neighbor's home -- a Jewish community is modest. Rav Hirsch reads "Tov" in the moral sense: regardless of a home's outward appearance, its real value lies in its spiritual atmosphere -- a Jewish community makes spirituality its focus. The Gemara in Sanhedrin explains that Bilam had originally intended to attack our shuls and study halls, understanding them as the centers of communal spirituality -- a Jewish community values its shuls, yeshivas, and schools.

The Ohr HaChaim reads the blessing's imagery -- "brooks stretching out," "gardens along the river," "aloes planted by G-d," "towering cedars" -- as describing four types of people who make up a community: those who spread Torah across the land, those who teach near its center, those who study purely for its own sake, and those who support Torah study financially though not scholars themselves. All are described as deeply rooted in sanctity -- a Jewish community is rooted in sanctity, no matter which role each person plays.

Why would Balak think this particular mountain, this particular vantage point, would finally produce a curse? Perhaps because a curse is simply the choice to notice the negative and hope for its continuation. When Bilam finally saw the Israelites as one impenetrable community, he could find nothing to curse at all.

Shabbat Shalom, Noah
