Bilam is standing on a mountain overlooking a portion of the Jewish camp in the desert. A frustrated Balak is alongside him, hoping he can deliver a curse on his third attempt. Instead of the prophetic words, Ma Tovu, emanates from Bilam's mouth, extolling the virtues of a Jewish community. This blessing would go on to be included in the siddur (possibly a first for a non-Jewish/Israelite author) and be mandated to be repeated upon entering the synagogue. What do Bilam's words teach us about the values and essence of a Jewish community?

מַה־טֹּבוּ אֹהָלֶיךָ יַעֲקֹב מִשְׁכְּנֹתֶיךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל׃

How fair are your tents, O Jacob, Your dwellings, O Israel!

כִּנְחָלִים נִטָּיוּ כְּגַנֹּת עֲלֵי נָהָר כַּאֲהָלִים נָטַע יְהוָה כַּאֲרָזִים עֲלֵי־מָיִם׃

Like palm groves that stretch out, Like gardens beside a river, Like aloes planted by the LORD, Like cedars beside the water;

The most famous explanation of these verses is found in Rashi. He says that Bilam was noticing that the entrances of the tents in the Jewish encampment were offset, obscuring any direct view into the neighbor's abode. A Jewish community is modest.

מה טבו אהליך. עַל שֶׁרָאָה פִתְחֵיהֶם שֶׁאֵינָן מְכֻוָּנִין זֶה מוּל זֶה

In the same vein as Rashi, Rav Hirsch explains that the posuk means Tov in a moral sense. While our homes may differ in appearance the real mark of value in the Jewish home is the spiritual atmosphere. He goes on to explain that Ohalecha is referring to more humble dwellings and Mishkenosecha to more lavish ones. Bilam is commending us for making spirituality the central focus of our lives regardless of the material situation we currently find ourselves in. A Jewish community makes spirituality its focus.

The Gemara in Sanhedrin teaches that from Bilam's blessings we can ascertain what his true intentions were. The Gemara says that because Bilam wanted to attack our shuls and study halls his curse was flipped to Ma Tovu Ohalecha Yaakov. Bilam understood that these locations are the centers of communal spirituality and that spirituality itself was the core of the Jewish community. This is why he selected it as his first target. The Sforno explains that the wording Bilam used reflected a deep understanding of the influence our shuls and study halls have on our community. He comments on the unusual plural formulation of the word Tovu saying that it indicates these places are not just of supreme value to those who frequent them but their very existence is a benefit to the whole community. A Jewish community values Shuls, Yeshivas, and Schools.

Finally, the Jewish community is comprised of individuals. To understand who these people are the Orach Chaim says we need not look farther than the first verses of Bilams blessing. The first type of person is the people who go across Israel year after year to spread Torah knowledge. They are alluded to by the phrase, "brooks which stretch out." The second is people who make headquarters near the temple mount and judge and teach. They are like, "gardens along the river." The third, study Torah entirely for its own sake and are "Aloes planted by G'd." Finally, there are those who provide financial support for people who are studying. The Torah calls them "towering cedars" because although they do not have the fruits of their own Torah they are still mighty spiritually. The Orach Chaim concludes that "the entire blessing is meant to describe all of the Israelites as deeply rooted in sanctity, even the section of the population which devotes itself to the pursuit of material goals in order to
share the results with the Torah students." A Jewish community is rooted in sanctity.

Why would Balak think that on this mountain Bilam would perform differently? Why was the Bilam's power to curse dependent on the vantage point from which he observed the nation? The answer may be that Bilam's curses were merely observations. A curse is a choice to notice the negative and hope for its continuation. When Bilam saw the Israelites we were one impenetrable entity. When our communities function as they should, "He sees no sin Jacob no woe for Israel."
