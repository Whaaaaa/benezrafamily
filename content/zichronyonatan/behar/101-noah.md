This week's Parsha Behar lays out some of our most fundamental rights and responsibilities around land ownership and wealth accumulation. These laws are not merely dry and technical regulations but instead focus on the human experience of property exchange. The first mitzvah in the Parsha immediately illustrates this point. We are instructed, "When you enter the land that I assign to you, the land shall observe a sabbath of יהוה." According to the Klei Yakar, the Shemitta year is not about allowing the land to rest so it will be fertile in the coming seasons. If that was the case it would not be called a "Shabbath of Hashem."

The Klei Yakar explains that rather, it is "to implant the character traits of faith and trust in Hashem in the people of Israel. Hashem was concerned that perhaps when they would come to the land they would work the soil in a natural way, and when they would be successful they would forget Hashem and remove their trust from Him. They would think that 'their might and the power of their hand have made them this wealth,' and the world goes along in its natural way. They would think that the land belongs to them; they are the masters and no one else."

This theme is repeated throughout the Parsha. Our laws around property rights are not about protecting ownership as much as about fostering trust, realizing that Hashem truly owns everything and that property is meant to be used equitably. The next mitzvah, the Yovel, makes this point clearly.

"And you shall sanctify the fiftieth year. You shall proclaim liberty throughout the land for all its inhabitants. It shall be a jubilee for you: each of you shall return to your holding and each of you shall return to your family."

This year reverses many of the largest financial decisions people have made over the last 50 years and we start it by proclaiming freedom? This is one of the most interventionist economic policies in the Torah and it is justified with the same principle that is used to advocate for laissez-faire economics today.

I think this drastic change in perspective is deeply rooted in the character traits the Klei Yakar outlined above. Someone who subscribes to the philosophy that 'their might and the power of their hand have made them this wealth,' would see the Yovel as a limitation on freedom. They worked and toiled for material gain that all can be taken away! But someone who has had the 'character traits of faith and trust in Hashem' implanted in them may view this reversion to an economic mean differently.

When we have faith in Hashem and the people around us and the humility to realize that what we own is just on loan from Hashem, there is nothing more freeing than the opportunity to begin again. From this positive perspective, freedom from material things can be achieved whereas the disposition that the Klei Yakar warns about can only result in servitude to stuff.

While we see that trust in Hashem is created through Shemittah observance, where is our interpersonal trust derived from? The next verse states, "When you sell property to your neighbor or buy any from your neighbor, you shall not wrong one another." This warning is reinforced with the subsequent bans on usury and interest.

Taken all together we see that the Torah's perspective on regulating property ownership is designed to create an economic framework that is trustworthy, equitable and human-centric. This is a high bar to set but by following the Klei Yakar's advice to trust in Hashem and by conducting our own business fairly and faithfully we can always improve.
