This week's parsha, Behar, opens with G-d instructing Moshe, from Mount Sinai itself, to safeguard the Shmita cycle -- six years of working the land, a seventh of complete rest.

Rashi asks: weren't all commandments given at Sinai? Why does the Torah specify Sinai here? To teach that just as Shmita's complex details were given at Sinai, so were the details of every mitzvah. But why teach this foundational lesson through Shmita specifically, rather than through Shabbat or any other command?

Shmita is unique: unlike Shabbat, which forbids nearly all interaction with the material world, Shmita permits normal life to continue -- travel, commerce, daily routine -- forbidding only the cultivation of one's own field for future gain. This gives us an unparalleled opportunity to elevate the mundane rather than simply abstain from it, but only if we relinquish the illusion of control and accept that our sustenance ultimately comes from G-d alone.

This same lesson lies at the heart of the revelation at Sinai: that G-d's will governs the world, and every mitzvah is an opportunity to make that truth visible in the details of daily life. The Gemara notes that G-d descended to the low, humble Mount Sinai rather than raising the mountain to Himself -- teaching that G-d comes to meet those willing to make themselves small.

Shmita, then, is chosen precisely because every detail of it gives us the chance to relinquish perceived control and make room for G-d -- without requiring any drastic change to our daily lives, only a constant awareness that we are not the center of the universe.
