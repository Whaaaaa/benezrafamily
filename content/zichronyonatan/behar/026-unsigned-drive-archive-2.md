This weeks parsha, Behar, opens on Moshe speaking with God on Mount Sinai. Specifically at this summit of spiritual experience God instructs us to safeguard the Shmita cycle. We are told that for 6 years we may work our fields and prune our vineyards but the seventh year, "the land shall have a complete rest a Sabbath to the Lord." The parsha continues with the complex agricultural restrictions surrounding Shmita. It explains that we may eat from the produce that continue to grow during this year, however we cannot sow new seeds or prune existing crops.

Rashi immediately ask a question. Weren't all the commandments given on Mount Sinai? Rashi explains that the Torah emphasizes that this mitzvah was given on Sinai to teach us that just as all of the complex details were taught as Sinia, so too all of the mitzvos had their technicalities and specifics explained at Sinai.

If the lesson from Shmita really is applicable to every other commandment, why would the Torah choose to teach it to us through Shmita? Why not have the first or last mitzvah as the example? Why not choose to teach us this lesson from Shabbos? What about Shmita is so fundamental to our mission as Jews that it meritted to validate all other commandments, and their minutia, as originating from the Revelation at Sinai?

Shmita is unique in that it commands a type of dedication to God that is more nuanced than any other. While Shabbos and Holidays offer us a similar respite from labor, this is a complete rest. We are forbidden from engaging with the material world except to fulfill our most basic needs. On Shmita we are allowed an opportunity to still go about our daily routine. We can travel, spend money, and engage in business, all of which are forbidden on Shabbos and Yom Tov. We are only prohibited from toiling in our fields for the sake of the next years harvest.

The Shmita year gives us an unparalleled opportunity to elevate our daily lives, our "mundane" routines. Instead of refraining from materialism to attain spiritual connection, we take spirituality and transform the temporal. But this process can only take place if we relinquish our sense of control and truly believe that everything comes from God. This perspective shift is achieved through the singular prohibition on cultivation. Our focus can no longer be about accumulating wealth and securing the next year's abundance. We have to accept that the future is in God's hands alone. For a full year, we are reminded daily that our efforts are not what sustain life and create wealth. We only exist and are sustained through the compassion and good that God bestows upon the universe constantly. Every aspect of our lives, every detail of our routine is only possible because God has given us the opportunities and resources to maintain it. The resulting humility enables us to appreciate and elevate all of the blessings God gives us daily instead of worrying about what will be.

Many of these same lessons are found during the revelation at Sinai. The giving of the Torah was the culmination of God's demonstration that his will controls the world. Our acceptance and performance of the mitzvos is an opportunity to transform every aspect of our lives into a testament to this fact. From when we wake up to when we go to sleep, we are constantly able to choose to do the right thing, creating a life of holiness and connection. Furthermore, the choice of Mount Sinai as the setting for this event shows that God can only share his will with those who make room for him. The Gemara teaches that God specifically came down to Mount Sinai instead of raising the mountain up. From this the Gemara learns that God himself descends to the humble and engages with them. When a person believes that their will determines their future, they cannot achieve the surrender necessary to make room for the will of God.

WIth this understanding, it is clear why Shmita was chosen as the mitzvah to teach us the profound message that every detail of every commandment came from God at Sinai. Every detail of Shmita is designed to give us the opportunity to relinquish our perceived control and make room for God in our lives. We don't have to make any drastic changes to our lifestyles yet they can become infused with a constant reminder that we are not running the show, we aren't the center of the universe. These are the essential prerequisites on which all other commandments rest. The commandments and all of their particularities and strigencies are an exercise in accepting that our will is not paramount and incorporating this awareness into every moment of our lives.

We should all merit to lead lives that are replete with this awareness and the resulting peace of spirit.

Shabbat Shalom
