Every morning a Jew awakes with the declaration of Modeh Ani on his lips:

"I give thanks before you, O Living and Eternal King, for mercifully returning my soul to me. How great is your trustfulness."

With this, we acknowledge that our soul was entrusted to Hashem as we slept and that it is He who has returned it to us. Furthermore, we state that we did not deserve another day – it was granted to us just because of His abundant mercy. This means that although we did not live the previous day correctly, He has still given us another chance to achieve and become meritorious.

In light of this statement, that we did not deserve to have our souls returned but rather received them as a charity, the final sentence is perplexing. "How great is Your trustfulness." Simply understood, this is a reference to the fact that Hashem has returned what is ours and is thereby described as a trustworthy watchman, as it were. But what does this have to do with trust? We didn't deserve it back anyway, so this is more a display of His kindliness than His trustfulness.

I believe the answer is that we are not referring to the degree that we can trust Him, but to the degree that He places trust in us. We realize that yesterday, and the day before, and the day before that, we failed in our mission. We don't deserve to wake up in the morning and yet everyone reading this did wake up. He gives us a second chance after second chance for years. Long after we have given up hope in ourselves, He keeps on trusting. He trusts that today we'll be different. Today we'll turn our lives around. Today we'll finally begin to place proper ideals before our short-term wants and desires. Today will finally be a day deserving of another.

As soon as we wake up, before we even get out of bed, we take note of Hashem's great trust in us, and we are empowered to regain trust in ourselves as well. With the Almighty's own vote of confidence, it dawns on us that perhaps we do have it in us. And, hopefully, we resolve not to let Him down, but to really attain quantum change. After all, G-d believes in us. In Parshat Pekudei, the Torah gives an accounting of each and every material used to build the Tabernacle. Nothing went to waste. Not one ounce of metal, not one thread of fabric, and not one single stone. Each and every piece was fully utilized to fulfill the will of Hashem.

Rabbi Moshe Feinstein [1] says that this is to teach us that we too should take account of everything given to us and make sure that whatever we have is properly used. We each have been given so many resources with which to achieve in this world. From our talents and material possessions to our family situation and our intellect, everything was measured out to us with precision. They are exactly what we need to reach our personal form of perfection, and every little bit must be utilized.

But above all, the most prized possession entrusted to us is life itself. Every day that we are granted is a goldmine of endless potential and possibility. So let us realize the great trust that Hashem puts in us with every morning that we awake. Let us capitalize on that trust and believe in ourselves. Let us use every day to its fullest so that we will be able to make an accounting of each of our days. And when the time comes to take a final account may we be able to say that this was a life worth living.

Have a wonderful Shabbos,

Yosef

[1] Quoted in Sefer Kol Ram.
