As a Nation we all stood under Mount Sinai, overcome with awe, we all accepted the Torah. God appeared face to face with us, as fire blazed our discerning consciousness left us, and our most primal instincts were inspired to accept the Torah.
The Gemara explains that it is not for another thousand years that our covenant with God was sealed. A contract made under duress cannot stand, and under Mount Sinai, God held the mountain above our heads like a jar to an insect, as if to say accept the Torah or let this spot be your grave.

A thousand years later, after the destruction of the first Beis Hamikdash Jews are in exile and stand to be annihilated by Haman. No longer witnessing open miracles in the temple, God's hand is hidden but the Jewish commitment to God is as public as ever. Not a single jew in the Purim story assimilated out of fear, the resistance was strong and empowered Jews. How did this spiritual blackout change our relationship with God? What was the point in God's coercion at Mount Sinai?

Our relationship with God is not based on reason. Nor is it based on our awareness of its existence. Our relationship with God is so rudimentary it resides in the very core of our souls. This is why we were "compelled" to accept the Torah, a conscious and thought out acceptance would not have covered that which is so elemental.

In the Purim story, we have the opposite situation, a seemingly godless exile and a nation that wants to destroy us. The awe is in the Jewish people who grow stronger and more united through said darkness. This polarity allowed for the contract that was drawn at Sinai to finally be sealed, now as sober people we embrace the Torah and its commandments, reflecting the light of God unto the nations in a dark exile. On Shavuot, we affirm that our coercion at Sinai was not against our will, but in complete harmony with our true desire.
