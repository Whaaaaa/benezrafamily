For millennia, we have been known as the "people of the book". Our nation's greatest leaders are men who spend every waking moment poring over the worn pages of the talmud and it's commentaries. Yeshivas and Kollels across the globe are brimming with thousands of young men, many of our best and brightest, who spare no effort to gain some mastery of the Torah. Even our laymen can be found early in the morning or after a long day at work, fighting back their weariness in order to learn the Daf Hayomi (daily page of Talmud). The Torah is the lifeblood of our people, our defining point and the essence of our being.

As we approach Shavuot, the day on which the Torah was given to our people, let us try to understand a little bit of the Torah's overarching importance and why so many of our finest minds are prepared to give up everything else the world has to offer in the pursuit of Torah study.

If one were to say that Torah study is important because it is necessary in order to know how to perform the mitzvot, it would be understandable. As Jews, we have 613 mitzvot to fulfill, and without learning we wouldn't know what to do.

But such an answer would in no way justify the importance attached to Talmud study, which, more often than not, discusses far-fetched cases which have almost no bearing on the practical law. Moreover, the Talmud is full of arguments and discussions, each one leading to a single decision at the bottom line, yet we study even the opinions and thought processes which are eventually discarded in practical law. Also, the practical law is relatively simple -- do this, don't do that -- whereas the Talmud and its commentaries are exceedingly difficult. Why should we work so hard if it doesn't have any practical application?

While the deeper sources talk about the incredible and far-reaching effects of Torah study on every realm of creation and how it is the source code for the entire universe, I'd like to offer an explanation which is a little easier to grasp on our simple level.

As a nation, we are charged with being a G-dly people and with spreading godliness throughout the world. It is through us that the entire world is to be elevated and imbued with Hashem's light. It follows that the only way we are able to attain our purpose in life is if we can connect ourselves to G-d in such a profound way that His light will shine through us. This requires a very powerful and unique bond. Mere contact will never suffice to transform ourselves into conduits of His holiness.

So how are we to achieve such depth of relationship to the point that we reflect the light of Hashem? Just like any relationship, bonds are built through knowledge and understanding of the other. Torah is the words and will of the Creator. Through its study we can begin to know G-d, and thereby begin to form a true relationship with Him. Still, a casual knowledge of the Torah will get us no further than that - a casual relationship with Hashem. Hardly the depth and intimacy needed to radiate His own aura from within ourselves. To achieve such a profound bond we need to attain an acute understanding not only of G-d's edicts on a practical level, but of His entire way of thinking, so to speak. This is why every thought process of the Talmud deserves our full attention; because it allows us to understand His thought processes, as it were. Only through intensive study of every facet of Torah, can we become so connected to the Almighty that we will be able to cast His light on all of creation.

This is the reason that our nation has always been, the "people of the book". This is why our leaders are the Talmudic masters. This is why many of our best and brightest give their lives to the singular study of Torah. And this is why our laymen find it so necessary to make time in their schedules for intensive Torah study. We are in the pursuit of creating the most profound relationship with Hashem that can possibly be achieved.

So this Shavuot, as we celebrate the giving of the Torah, let us realize its overarching importance to our people and find its importance in our personal lives as well. Let us make an effort to increase our dedication and connection to the Torah, as well as our understanding of what Torah truly is.

*Good Shabbos and Good Yom Tov,*

*Yosef*
