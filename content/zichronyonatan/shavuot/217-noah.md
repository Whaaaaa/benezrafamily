Shavuot is uniquely enigmatic. While the other chagim have clear historical and agricultural significance written in the Torah, Shavuot is only explicitly related to agriculture. It is only through reasoning and the Oral Torah that we learn that the Revelation at Sinai occurred on Shavuot.

At the introduction to the giving of the Torah it is written, “In the third month after the Israelites had gone forth from the land of Egypt, on that very day, they entered the wilderness of Sinai” (Ex. 19:1). We know that Shavuot is the only holiday in the third month so we make the connection. But this implicit connection is very different than the explicit agricultural and historical connections made with Sukkot and Pesach. It is a connection that requires the Oral Torah.

Rabbi Sacks explains that the essence of Shavuot can be explored by investigating the holiday from the perspective of the Sadducees in the time of the second temple who didn’t recognize the validity of the Oral Tradition. There is a famous argument about when the counting of the omer starts, starting the countdown to Shavuot. The Sadducees interpret the verse requiring the counting of the omer to start on the “day after shabbat” as meaning Sunday, while the oral tradition understands Shabbat in that context to mean the first day of Pesach. The gemara recounts an exchange in which a Sadducee explains to R. Yochanan ben Zakkai why, according to them, Shavuot is always on a Sunday: “Moses our teacher was a great lover of Israel. Knowing that Shavuot lasted only one day, he, therefore, fixed it on the day after the Sabbath so that Israel might enjoy themselves for two successive days.”

From the Sadducee's perspective, this results in a holiday that does not have a fixed day. It would be hard to commemorate the Revelation at Sinai, a concrete event with a set date, with a holiday whose date fluctuates annually. Instead, Rabbi Sacks posits that the significance of Shavuot was much more closely associated with the harvest, the land, and entrance into the Land of Israel in the times of Yehoshua. This harvest-centric view is reinforced by the remark said to R. Yochanan ben Zakkai. After an exhausting harvest, all anyone could want in a long weekend to rest.

With the oral tradition, we were able to root this holiday in something much more eternal, the giving of the Torah. While the Sadducees all but disappeared at the end of the second temple period, our religion rooted in the Oral Tradition continues to thrive. When the process of a miraculous Exodus followed by arduous wandering in the desert leads to land, when that land is taken away so is the tradition built by those experiences. But when the culmination of our formation as a nation is in receiving the Torah, something divine and eternal that we can take with us anywhere, we are left with a much more resilient tradition. One that allows us to appreciate the land and the harvest but one that retains its meaning and significance even when we can’t.
