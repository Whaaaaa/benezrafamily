On Shavuot we celebrate the day that Hashem gave the Torah to Israel in favor of all the other nations of the world. The Midrash tells us that this was not just the way it had to be, but rather G-d offered it first to each of the other nations; only when they all declined did he give it to the eager Jewish nation. With a little speculation on the words of the Midrash, we can learn a very vital lesson for all of us to experience our own acceptance of the Torah this very Shavuot.

The following is a loose translation: When Hashem revealed himself to give the Torah, He went to all the other nations first. He went to the children of Esav, and they asked what is written in it. He responded that it says one may not murder. They said, 'Everything about us and our forefathers is murder.' He went to the people of Amon and Moav and told them that the Torah forbids immorality. They said, 'We are rooted in immorality as our people were born from the union of Lot with his daughters.' He went to the children of Yishmael and told them that the Torah forbids stealing. They said, 'Our forefather was a robber.' When Hashem saw all of this, he went and gave the Torah to Israel.

The Midrash spells out that the complaint of all the nations was not that they themselves could not part with their sinful ways, but rather that their predecessors were sinful. They said that this is the way we were raised, this is how our nation has lived for all time. We are therefore unable to accept the Torah and change.

We must understand that Hashem certainly knew that these nations were steeped in evil from their very origins, and yet he offered them the opportunity to have the Torah. This is a fundamental principle in Torah, that mankind is imbued with bechira, or the ability to choose between right and wrong. It doesn't matter how you were brought up, what your natural dispositions are, or what circumstantial pressures come your way. It doesn't even matter how big a ditch you have dug for yourself in the past. No matter what, you and only you are in control of your destiny, and you alone must bear the responsibility for your choices.

The other nations complained that they were victims of their circumstances. They fully understood the tremendous value of the Torah and [as another Midrash states] they longed to be able to receive it. Yet they were shackled by their perception of determinism. They said, 'We were born this way, we were raised this way, and there's nothing that we can do about it. The Torah is simply beyond our scope.' It was this fundamental mistake, the failure to recognize man's ability to rise above his circumstances, that caused them to lose out on the Torah for the rest of eternity.

But let us not think that this is just something for the history books. The giving of the Torah was not a one-time occurrence but rather a yearly event for every single Jew. On the day of Shavuot we are each charged with accepting the Torah anew, and the amount of Torah growth we can achieve this year will be according to the level of that acceptance. If we wish to merit the Torah this year, one of the lessons we must learn is that which the other nations failed in. There is no room to blame our shortcomings on anything outside of ourselves; we alone are in charge of our lives.

Uncle Yonatan lived this lesson with every fiber of his being. He had a million and one excuses to explain away a life of complacency and stagnation. He wasn't raised with Torah and he had to sacrifice everything in order to attain it. At every turn of his personal and religious life he faced tremendous opposition. He fell many times, and fell hard. But never did he give up, never did he say "I can't", and never did he stop fighting.

We must stop blaming our situation or our past mistakes for our present state. We must accept full responsibility for our future and realize that no matter which hand we've been dealt we are never victims of our circumstances. We must resolve to never be complacent but to constantly strive to reach beyond ourselves. In this way, may we merit to receive a heaping portion of Torah in the coming year, along with all the blessings that the Torah brings.

Good Shabbos and Chag Sameach,

Yosef
