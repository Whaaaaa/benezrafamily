For millennia we have been known as the "people of the book." Our nation's greatest leaders spend every waking moment poring over the worn pages of the Talmud; yeshivas and kollels across the globe are brimming with young men giving everything to master Torah, and laymen everywhere fight back their weariness to learn the daily page.

As we approach Shavuot, the day the Torah was given, it's worth asking why so much devotion is poured into study that so often has little bearing on practical law -- the Talmud discusses far-fetched cases and preserves opinions eventually discarded in favor of a single ruling. Why work so hard through material with no practical application?

As a nation, we are charged with spreading G-dliness throughout the world, and this requires forming a bond with G-d profound enough that His light can shine through us -- more than mere contact, a true relationship. And just like any relationship, that bond is built through knowledge and understanding of the other. Torah is the words and will of the Creator; through its study we begin to know G-d, and thereby begin to form a real relationship with Him. A casual knowledge would yield only a casual relationship -- to achieve real depth we need to understand not just G-d's edicts, but His entire way of thinking, so to speak, which is why every twist and turn of Talmudic reasoning deserves our full attention: it allows us to understand His thought process itself.

This is why our nation has always been the "people of the book" -- why our leaders are Talmudic masters, why our best and brightest give their lives to Torah study, and why our laymen find room in busy schedules for it too. We are in pursuit of the most profound relationship with Hashem that can possibly be achieved.

So this Shavuot, as we celebrate the giving of the Torah, let us realize its overarching importance, and find room for it in our own lives as well.

Good Shabbos and Good Yom Tov, Yosef
