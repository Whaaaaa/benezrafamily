Terumah means "lifted apart" -- this week's Parsha teaches all the ways the Torah makes our lives more beautiful. G-d says to Moshe, "Make me a sanctuary and I will rest there," and the Parsha enumerates the materials for the Mishkan: gold, silver, copper; blue, purple, and red wool; flax, goat hair, animal skins, wood, olive oil, spices, and gems, donated by each household according to their means.

Today we build and design synagogues in the image of the Mishkan and Beit Hamikdash -- some magnificent, some in a basement or the sixth floor of an apartment building. The responsibility of having a place to pray falls on the community as a whole: if they cannot afford to own, they must rent; if they can afford to own, they must buy.

Ideally the synagogue would be the tallest building in a city; because that is unreasonable today, some are careful not to build a home taller than their synagogue. Beauty and aesthetics work hand in hand. When designing a room for oneself, we choose comfort and purpose; when designing a room for G-d, beauty comes first. Building a synagogue from scratch gives us the rare opportunity to permanently construct the walls, curtains, and boundaries that direct our lives as Jews.

Good Shabbos!
