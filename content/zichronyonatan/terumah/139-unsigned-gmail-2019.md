Trumah focuses on the architecture of the Mishkan and the contributions that made it Gods temporary dwelling on earth. Thirteen contributions came from each household; gold, silver, copper, blue, purple, and red dyed wool flax, goat hair, animal skins, wood, olive oil, spices, and gems. God said to Moses "They shall make for Me a Sanctuary, and I shall dwell amidst them." the Mishkan was beautiful and every facet played a key role in building a home for Hashem. This week's Parsha is about designating living spaces. God is everywhere around us but only home in the places, we uplift for the divine.

A home is a place that greets you as you enter, it is a place you frequent and have established. A place devoted to your needs that allows the space for your privacy. This is the environment the Mishkan created for God and B'nai Yisrael. The Mishkan appealed to all our senses, the Mishkan's furnishings or "vessels," are seen as representations of the various organs and faculties of man: the Ark (containing the Torah) corresponds to the mind, intellect and speech; the Menorah, to the eyes and the sense of sight; the Table that held the "showbread," to the sense of taste; the Inner Altar on which the Ketoret (incense) was burned, to the sense of smell; and the Outer Altar on which the animal and meal offerings were brought represent the digestive system and other "functional" organs. The rituals in the Mishkan existed to excite all of our senses, in the desert in a tent this was necessary to dignify a holy place. Our senses are our only way to spirituality. Today synagogues and schools play the role of Gods designated places in our world, we are expected to keep a high level of decorum when visiting and wear the right dress code. We all come as individuals but while we are there we are indivisible.

When sharing space like this there are many rules, it becomes an obligation for a community of a minyans size to own a synagogue. It should be the tallest building in the town! Today that is unreasonable so one should be careful not to buy a home taller than the local synagogue. Contributions for the building and weekly herring should come from community according to households financial needs. The building should look like a holy temple, it should be highly ornamented with 12 windows that face Jerusalem. There must be an entryway so people can wash and compose themselves before walking into the back of the sanctuary. I division of men and women is appropriate in these places and the Chazan's bimah should not be directly in front of the ark for modesty's sake. The Torah is kept in the ark which faces the community, it has a curtain and doors like in the courtyard of the Beit Hamikdash. No art is hung in the sanctuary. These are a few of the facets of modern shuls but the Mishkan had many more! Good Shabbos!
