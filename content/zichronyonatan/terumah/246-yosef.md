The Mishkan was, so to speak, a house in this world for Hashem's presence. It was the holiest place in the world, the place most central to the service of G-d, and one of the greatest undertakings ever attempted by man. It was built with every vestige of wisdom and craftsmanship possessed by the Jewish people, as well as a huge amount of wealth, lovingly donated by the nation.

It would stand to reason that such great expense and effort were required because nothing less could possibly be fitting to 'house' the King of kings. Where else would the Shechina, the Divine Presence, reside if not in the most fantastic palace man can dream of?

But further examination reveals that the reality is not so. There is, in fact, another 'house' for the Shechina that is possibly even greater than the Mishkan.

Hashem commands, "Speak to the Jews that they should take a tithe for me; from each person whose heart moves him [to donate] you shall take my tithe... And you shall make for me a Mishkan and I will dwell in them."

The Alshich famously notes that the verse does not say that Hashem will dwell in it, meaning the Mishkan, but rather that he will dwell in them, meaning within the Jewish people. This means that the ultimate abode for the Shechina is not in the holy Mishkan, but rather in the hearts of the Jewish people!

However, this begs the question that if ultimately the 'home' for Hashem is not in the Mishkan but rather in our hearts, what do we need the Mishkan for in the first place?

Another question. The mitzvos in the Torah are not optional. Hashem doesn't say that whoever wants should keep the Shabbos, eat kosher, and don tefillin. Mitzvos by definition are commandments which we are to keep to whether or not we are in the mood. Why then, in this mitzvah of taking a tithe for the Mishkan, does the Torah specify that it should be taken only from those whose hearts move them to donate?

The Vilna Gaon offers a beautiful understanding, which changes our whole perspective of what the Mishkan was.

He explains that the Shechina was to dwell in the collective heart of the entire Jewish people, joined together as one. No single individual could merit housing the Shechina, but as one great collective Jewish heart, that heart could merit being a 'home' for Hashem. The only problem was how to achieve that unity of so many individual hearts, each with their own thoughts and feelings.

This was the role of the Mishkan, which served as a place where all the hearts of the Jewish people could come together as one, in one unified effort to bring Hashem's presence into this world. And so each person donated their most prized possessions, the things they loved and cherished most, with hearts brimming with love and devotion. They were not just giving money, they were giving a piece of their hearts. And, as each and every Jew came bearing their heart of hearts, they became unified as the ultimate abode for Hashem's presence.

So yes, the Shechina did dwell in the Mishkan, which is why it had to be such an impressive edifice, but the real beauty of the Mishkan was not the gold and the gemstones, but the heart of a nation expressed and contained within them. And of course, it goes without saying that such a tithe could only be brought by those driven from within – gold can be taxed, but hearts can only be gifted.

If we wish for the coming of Moshiach and the rebuilding of the Beit Hamikdash, then we must strive to lay the foundation for such a 'house' for Hashem, by joining and uniting our hearts with the hearts of each of our brothers.

Since this is much easier said than done, I'd like to conclude with one practical and fresh idea that has been on my mind. Perhaps all of our troubles, grievances, and arguments with our fellowman stem from a lack of thought and understanding. After all, if I am always convinced of the righteousness of my feelings and actions, then it would be likely that the person across from me feels the same way about his position. Most functional people don't do things that they feel are wrong, so if they are doing something, they probably have a reason for it. Just that I am so consumed with my own view that I am blind to any other vantage point.

What, though, would happen if we would spend some time putting serious thought towards understanding the people around us? Maybe it could be a quiet five minutes sitting on the couch, maybe just some contemplation while driving to work, or perhaps even some time thinking with a pen. Whatever it is, some dedicated thought trying to understand our friends is bound to have wonderful effects. What challenges does this person face? Which background does he come from? What does his family, financial, and social situation look like? In which areas does he excel? And then, how does the object of our disagreement fit into that bigger picture?

After such an exercise, I think we may just have a chance of seeing his side of the story; where he is justified or even correct in his actions. This regimen would take a decent amount of work, but I think it could make a drastic change for the better in our world. Through heightened understanding, we could all lead happier, kinder, more peaceful lives. And we can work towards achieving that unified Jewish heart which can indeed be the dwelling place for the Shechina.

Good Shabbos,
