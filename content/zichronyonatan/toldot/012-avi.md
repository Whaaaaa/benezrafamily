In this week's Parsha, Yitzchak and Rivka conceive after twenty barren years; Rivka's pregnancy is difficult because "two nations struggle inside her." She gives birth to Esav, then Yaakov. Esav is favored by Yitzchak for his hunting; Rivka loves Yaakov, a dweller of the tents of learning. Nearing death, Yitzchak asks for Esav to receive his blessing -- but Rivka arranges for Yaakov to receive it instead.

Rivka's deception teaches us when it may be permissible to bend the truth, and when we may even be obligated to withhold it. The rabbis instruct us to distance ourselves from lying -- liars, in seeking only the favor of other people, ignore the all-knowing G-d. Yet the Torah only holds a person strictly accountable for a lie that leads to death: "Distance yourself from words of falsehood; do not kill an innocent or righteous man."

The rabbis are lenient toward certain kinds of untruths: to preserve someone's humility, to maintain peace between spouses (even the Kohen Gadol would mediate disputes this way), or to spare someone painful and unnecessary truth. Lying to restore peace is itself considered a mitzvah.

Now that we understand the spectrum of untruths, it is worth remembering that even white lies should be avoided where possible, so that we never come to say one thing while believing another in our hearts. There are worse lies and better ones -- but there is no lying to G-d.
