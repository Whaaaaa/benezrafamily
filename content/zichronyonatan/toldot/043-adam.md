This week's Parsha includes the famous account of Yitzchak preparing to bless his sons. He sends Esav, the firstborn by birth, to bring delicacies back from the field before he bestows the blessings upon his son. We are familiar with the saga, depicting Rivka, the sons' mother commanding Yaacov to bring her food to prepare. She then dresses him in his brother's clothes and covers his hands and neck with fur so that Yitzchak, now blind at the end of his life, would bestow the firstborn blessings upon Yaacov, the younger son.

When we read this story as a fictitious tale, it seems like a classic story of family difficulties and trickery. Two brothers competing for their father's blessings before his death.

But the Torah is not a book fables, but a historical account, in this case leaving us with many questions.

First of all, the Torah tells us of the terrible moral state of Esav leading up to this point. With the Medrash depicting Esav as a murderer, rapist, and idolator, how is it possible that Yitzchak could be blind to his son's nature? He may have been losing his eyesight, but it seems that as a father, he would have some idea of the lack of morality his son was displaying. Why was he looking to bestow the priceless blessings on his devious son?

Furthermore, didn't he know that this wasn't Esav? Was Yitzchak, the brilliant sage, so easily fooled by some lambskin and clothing? He seems to notice that something is up; three times he questions Yaacov before actually blessing him. Was he truly convinced by this cunning disguise?

And in any case, if he blessed the wrong child due to trickery, wouldn't he be able to change this after the fact? The Jewish concept of a "mistaken sale" seems applicable. One is not simply able to keep the things he steals (or even those he is inadvertently given); if Yaacov was not supposed to receive the blessings, shouldn't Yitzchak have simply blessed Esav (and possibly cursed Yaacov for taking what was not his)?

To begin to understand this story I believe we need to understand the differences between Yaacov and Esav. Yaacov is described as an Ish Tam Yosheiv Ohalim, a man of completeness and simplicity, a dweller of tents (study halls). And Esav is an Ish Yodea Tzaid, Ish Sadeh, a man who knows weapons, a man of the field. Yaacov interacted primarily with ideas, staying indoors. Esav was out in the world, working fields and learning how to hunt.

Chassidus teaches that everything in this world progresses through stages. First, there is tremendous power and light that enters the world with insane force, called Tohu and then that power is restrained and contained so that it can be effective, called Tikkun. We see this on a small scale in the way that ideas are brought into reality, someone throws out a grand idea in a boardroom that is brilliant but crazy (Tohu) and others "reel it in" and contain it (Tikkun) until it is able to be a positive force for change. In the progression of childhood learning, we see a similar pattern. When a child first learns of a new concept it is all-consuming (Tohu) and only later is he able to contain it and utilize it for good (Tikkun). Most crazy ideas in the world are founded on something positive, they are just properly contained to be a usable force for good yet. Even communism, we can see is based on a beautiful idea of unity and oneness. But as long as it is in a state of Tohu, or uncontained potential, it is a destructive force, a light so bright that it burns or destroys. It needs to be restrained in the containers of Tikkun in order to be constructive.

Yaacov is referred to as a Neshama from the world of Tikkun and Esav a Neshama of Tohu.

The reason Yitzchak wanted to bless Esav, was not because he was blissfully unaware of what his child was doing. Chassidus teaches that he was very aware of what Esav was capable of. And it was this very awareness that drove him to want to bless Esav. Yaacov knew that what one Esav could accomplish in this world, ten Yaacov's could not! And he desired to bless him that he be able to utilize his brother's talents of Tikkun to contain the light and turn it into a positive force in the world. He wanted his sons to together join forces and bring the world to its ultimate perfection.

But Rivka knew, in her superior vision of the spiritual realm, that this was not to be. And she intervened in order to save both the future of the Jewish people and the rest of the world.

The sages say that on a deeper level, Yitzchak did know what was going on and the fact that he was able to give the blessings indicated that they were truly meant for Yaacov. This is why he wasn't able to "take it back" as Esav requested. They had reached their true destination and there the blessings remained.

May we all merit to see our potential as the beautiful, awesome light that it is and be able to find the proper containers for that light so that we can utilize it to help ourselves, those around us and ultimately the world to reach true Emes a knowledge of Hashem with the coming of Moshiach!
