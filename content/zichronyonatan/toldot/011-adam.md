This week's Parsha includes the famous account of Yitzchak preparing to bless his sons -- and Rivka's intervention to ensure the blessing intended for the firstborn reaches Yaakov instead. Read as a simple story, it seems a classic tale of family rivalry. But the Torah leaves us with real questions: how could Yitzchak, aware of Esav's moral failings, still intend to bless him? How was he fooled by lambskin and clothing when he questions Yaakov three times before blessing him?

Chassidus teaches that everything in creation moves through two stages: Tohu, raw and uncontained potential, and Tikkun, that potential properly restrained so it can be a constructive force. Yaakov is a neshama of Tikkun -- Ish Tam Yosheiv Ohalim, simple, a dweller of tents. Esav is a neshama of Tohu -- Ish Sadeh, a man of the field, of raw and untamed capability.

Yitzchak wanted to bless Esav specifically because he understood Esav's raw potential -- ten Yaakovs together could not accomplish what one properly-channeled Esav could. He hoped his sons would join forces, Tikkun containing Tohu, to help perfect the world. Rivka, with clearer spiritual sight, knew this was not to be, and intervened to protect both the Jewish people's future and the world's.

On a deeper level, the sages say Yitzchak did know what was happening -- and that the very fact the blessings could not be reclaimed afterward proves they were always meant for Yaakov.

May we all merit to see our potential as the beautiful light it is, and find the proper vessels to turn that light into a force for good in the world, until we merit the coming of Moshiach.
