As Yom Kippur came to an end a little over a week ago, I found myself in a mix of emotions.

Elated by the closeness I felt from moving through Rosh Hashana, the Ten Days of Repentance and the culmination of it all at Neilah, the extra prayer on Yom Kippur.

Awe at the realization of how fragile our lives are and how great and vast God is.

Gratitude at having made it through the process once again.

And sadness that it would be a full year until I would stand here again.

But a moment later, I remembered that we weren't all done yet. We still had Sukkot only 4 days away. Sukkot is called by the sages the "Time of Our Happiness" and I have always felt the easiest connection to Simcha, or happiness, on Sukkot.

My mind began to fill with memories of singing and dancing in the Sukkah. Eating and sleeping each day under the canopy of pine, bamboo, or palm branches depending on where I spent the Chag. And I felt the sadness leave. Replaced by hopes and longing for the Sukkah we would soon inhabit.

But what is the internal work that we do on Sukkot? It seems hard to create happiness. The Days of Awe may be for many, the easier holiday to connect to emotionally.

Rabbi Shimshon Pincus said that there's a very deep concept in this progression from the Days of Awe to the Time of our Happiness.

In the Torah, building takes place in units of ten. And seven represents intrinsic completeness. God used ten sayings to create the world and ten plagues to remove the Jews from Egypt (and Egyptian-ness from the Jews). And we have ten days between Rosh Hashana and Yom Kippur. This is time to do Teshuva, which as Uncle Yonatan frequently reminded us means to return more than to repent. But this process is a *ten day process* indicating to us that it is a building process. That we must start somewhere and slowly raise our level of Fear and Awe of the Almighty and thereby develop within ourselves the will to return to him. The more we focus on His vastness and our smallness, His power and our fragility, the more we build toward this experience of Teshuva. This is what the sages call Teshuva Me'Yirah, or Returning/ Repenting out of Fear/ Awe.

The holiday that follows is Sukkot, a seven day holiday (for Noah and Cali at least). Seven, says Rav Shimshon Pincus, is the number of completeness. Seven days in the creation of the world, seven days in a week and seven thousand years of human history. The uniqueness of sevens is that the full experience is encapsulated in every moment. The potential is there to experience the entirety of what is contained, throughout the entire process. And Sukkot is all sevens. Seven days of the holiday, seven pieces in the Lulav and Etrog set we shake (one Etrog, one Lulav, two Aravot, and three hadasim), seven directions that we shake toward (front, right, back, left, up, down, and center), etc.

What can we experience during Sukkot? Returning from Love, or Teshuva Me'ahava. This is a higher level of Teshuva in which we, out of complete love and devotion, return to Hashem because we value the relationship that we have damaged. And this level of Teshuva has the power to turn our misdeeds into merits. It may sound like magic, but through the lens of relationship it actually makes perfect sense. When a husband and wife quarrel, and then fix things up out of love and devotion to each other and value for the relationship, they create a level of closeness which could only exist *because* of their previous misdeed. So to, when we come back to Hashem out of love for Him and the desire to be close again, our mistakes become a positive part of that process, retroactively.

So for seven days, we can at any moment step into the closeness that is love from the Almighty and we needn't build up to get there. We must only choose to experience it.

But these seven are also almost at an end. And we move into a new holiday.

Shmini Atzeret, the Eighth day of Gathering.

So what do we have to experience in this day? What does eight represent?

The sages say that Hashem gave us this additional holiday because He said, "It's difficult for me that you are going away." Can you believe? It's hard for Hashem that we are going back to "regular" life! Back to school, back to our jobs, back to the day-in day-out running around.

So He gave us one more day to celebrate together! During Sukkot, the nations of the world are represented in the sacrifices, for they too are His children and He wants a relationship with them as well. But on the eighth day, Hashem says, "It's just for you and me. No Shofar, no fasting, no Lulav and Etrog, no sukkah, no matza. Just *you* and *me*."

Before we go back to life we get one more day that is exclusively for connecting and experiencing our relationship with The One.

With one difference that Uncle Yonatan first taught me. Eight is the number that transcends the natural. All of nature exists in patterns of seven. He could have given you a hundred examples. But the number eight transcends what's possible in the natural world. It is a time when we can experience a closeness that can only exist between God and the Jewish People. When we take a Torah in our arms, and use our voice and our body to call out to Hashem and connect. When we can ask Hashem for anything because nothing is too much for him. And we can experience the true culmination of the last two months of preparation.

May we all merit to experience Hashem's closeness to us and find the way to reach out to Him and give Hashem a hug. Through the Torah and through the other Jews around us.

Good Yom Tov, Chag Sameach, Good Shabbos.
