As Yom Kippur came to an end a little over a week ago, I found myself in a mix of emotions: elated by the closeness felt through the Ten Days of Repentance, awed at how fragile our lives are, grateful to have made it through the process again, and sad that it would be a full year until I would stand there again. But a moment later I remembered: we weren't done yet. Sukkot, the "Time of Our Happiness," was only four days away.

But what is the internal work of Sukkot -- how do we create happiness on command? Rabbi Shimshon Pincus taught that in the Torah, building takes place in units of ten, and seven represents intrinsic completeness. The ten days between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur are a building process -- teshuvah me'yirah, returning out of fear and awe, developed gradually. Sukkot, seven days of completeness, offers something different: teshuvah me'ahava, returning out of love -- a level so complete it can retroactively turn our past misdeeds into merits, the way a couple who reconciles out of love for each other creates a closeness that could only exist because of, not despite, their earlier conflict.

For seven days we can step into that closeness at any moment; we need not build up to it, only choose to experience it. And then, almost at an end, comes one more holiday: Shmini Atzeret, the eighth day of gathering. The sages say G-d gave us this additional day because "it's difficult for Me that you are going away" -- one more day just for the two of us, no shofar, no fasting, no lulav, no sukkah, no matzah. Just you and Me.

Eight, Uncle Yonatan first taught me, is the number that transcends the natural -- all of nature exists in patterns of seven, and eight is what lies beyond what's possible in the natural world. It is a closeness that can only exist between G-d and the Jewish people, when we take a Torah in our arms and connect with nothing held back.

May we all merit to experience Hashem's closeness and find the way to reach out to Him -- through the Torah, and through the other Jews around us.

Good Yom Tov, Chag Sameach, Good Shabbos.

The learning should be a merit for Yonatan ben Avraham Avinu a"h.
