Mishpatim is densely packed with mitzvot addressing interpersonal obligations, financial responsibilities, and respect for authority. Together these comprise a large section of our moral fabric and are the backbone of a just society. It is curious then that the first commandment presented in this Parsha is the process of acquiring slaves. What about this process is representative of our judicial system and why is it our introduction to building an ethical society.

The Or HaChaim touches on an answer in his remarks on the first verse of the Parsha. He says that this mitzvah is not necessarily easy for the purchaser. He may feel entitled to keep the slave longer than the maximum six years set out in the Torah. But this six-year limit is still a benefit to him, as he may be in a similar situation someday. Every slave ended up where they are through financial desperation. And money has a way of rolling around and faced with desperate times the purchaser would act no different than the slave he bought. That is why these mitzvot are, "set in front of us" (תָּשִׂ֖ים לִפְנֵיהֶֽם) because they are for our future benefit and it is incumbent on all of us to feel as though the kindness in our systems touch us directly.

This lesson is played out over and over again in the Parsha. The extreme importance of empathy and trust in each other is an inescapable theme throughout all of these laws. We are instructed, "You shall not wrong a stranger or oppress him, for you were strangers in the land of Egypt." which asks us to look to our past to find compassion in the present. And immediately afterward we are commanded, "You shall not ill-treat any widow or orphan. If you do mistreat them, I will heed their outcry as soon as they cry out to Me, and My anger shall blaze forth and I will put you to the sword, and your own wives shall become widows and your children orphans." Here we are directed to imagine a painful future to use as a source of sympathy.

Later we are warned again: You shall not oppress a stranger, for you know the soul of the stranger, having yourselves been strangers in the land of Egypt. And Rashi succinctly expresses this same sentiment in his comment on the words, "The soul of a stranger." He writes, "You know how hard it is for him when he's oppressed."

In light of the incredible focus on empathy throughout the Parsha, it is more evident how slavery is a perfectly representative process for our ethical system. We had just been freed! Slavery was the national trauma that was still resonating deeply enough to evoke our sincerest sensitivities. We needed to connect to this experience so that we could truly build a society based on compassion and mutual respect and only through amending what it means to be a slave could we start this endeavor.
