In Parsha Mishpatim, Bnai Yisrael are commanded in many laws that remain applicable to daily life -- damages, rentals, loans, and how judges are appointed. If a man's ox gores someone, he is liable for only half the damages the first time, because an ox is a "tam" -- a simple, low-risk animal. But if it gores a third time, it becomes a "muad," a warned animal, and its owner is liable in full.

The Rabbis explain that each of us has an ox and an owner inside us -- the ox is not necessarily destructive, but it is selfish. If that selfishness becomes destructive, we assume it is an aberration, not the rule. Left unsupervised, though, the ox settles into habits, and its owner becomes fully responsible for the negligence.

Strikingly, the Mishnah tells us a "warned" ox can regain its "innocent" status simply by being sold to a new owner. How could a sale change an ox? Because a single bad habit is nearly impossible to overcome in isolation -- but a new environment, fresh hay, fresh grain, can make all the difference.

Along with these most mundane rules of ox-keeping, we are also commanded in this Parsha not to mix meat and milk, and given the festival calendar and our daily prayers -- a reminder that rules and laws govern every aspect of our existence, and without them we have not freedom, but nothing. Good Shabbos!
