This week we read Parshat Mishpatim and its expansive list of laws covering a broad range of civil topics, including personal injury, property damage, theft, and various types of social obligations. The list is introduced simply:
"And these are the laws that you must set before them."

Rashi comments on this, "God said to Moses: It should not enter your mind to say, "I shall teach them a section of the Torah or a single Halacha twice or three times until it will become current in their mouths exactly according to its wording (i.e. until they know the text verbatim), but I shall not take the trouble to make them understand the reason of each thing and its significance"; therefore Scripture says, אשר תשים לפניהם, "which thou shalt set before them" like a table fully laid before a person with everything ready for eating."

Rashi is understanding that this phrase is emphasizing the importance of learning the underlying reason behind these mitzvot. While we have other categories of Mitzvot that have very abstract reasons or meanings beyond comprehension, these civil laws must be taught with their reasons. Rashi explains in Eruvin that this is meant to avoid a situation where a teacher can say to a student, "This is what I heard from my teacher, go and understand it for yourself."

The importance of transmitting a logical understanding of Mitzvot may seem strange when we have the Parah Adumah (Red Heifer) and other Chukim where we seem to abandon logic in favor of maintaining a tradition.

The Gemara learns another lesson from this first line as well, "The school of Rabbi Elazar taught: The verse states with regard to the civil laws: "And these are the civil laws that you shall set before them" (Exodus 21:1). The reference to "them" in the verse is referring to all those who stood at the revelation, both men and women. The verse thereby equates a woman with a man with regard to all civil laws in the Torah."

This lesson of equality seems to run counter to other hierarchical systems that the Torah establishes elsewhere. Kohanim have different laws that govern them, women are generally exempt from time-bound mitzvot. Despite the importance of equality stressed in our Parsha we still have legal stratification built into our society.

To understand the tension between each of these values we have to understand what the point of these laws is. Conflicts between blind faith and reasoned understanding are not unique to the Jewish experience and the struggle to balance equality and individuality has been a primary force in shaping civilization forever. The Mishpatim in our Parsha form the bedrock of justice on which our particular Jewish society can be built. Our unique traditions and abstract religious ideals can only survive when they are protected by a logical framework. Our priests and social hierarchies must be predicated on a justice system that acknowledges fundamental equality.

This lesson can further be seen by the placement of these laws before Moshe even ascended to Mount Sinai to receive the Torah. These prerequisites were necessary for us to be able to enter a contractual relationship with our creator.

In a time when it can feel like logical governance and commitment to equality are out of vogue we have to remind ourselves that these values support the rich beautiful traditions that define our lives as Jews.
