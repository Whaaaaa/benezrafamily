Moshe was just a small child during the holocaust, and yet he miraculously survived the inferno until the camps were liberated. There he stood, destitute, robbed of his family, robbed of his friends, robbed of any sense of security. Completely at the mercy of his liberators, but alive. We fast-forward a few decades to find Moshe in America, where he has become a wealthy and powerful businessman. A true rags to riches story. From time to time, Moshe avails himself to interviewers, sharing with them the stories and the horrors of the camps. He can remember each and every facet with vivid detail, and as he speaks, the listener is able to perceive the experience to its smallest details. It seems as though nothing has been forgotten. But as much as he can relate the tortures of Auschwitz, he understandably only conducts such meetings from the comfort of his executive office. He sits in an oversized and luxurious leather armchair, with an impressive mahogany desk in front of him and a butler waiting on him hand and foot. He speaks only from a position of power.

Moshe shares his story generously, but there is one place he isn't willing to go. He will not allow himself to recount, or even remember, that feeling of helplessness and dependency which he felt standing there upon liberation. He simply refuses to see himself as once having been so unable to care for himself.

On Pesach, we are charged with the uneasy task of viewing ourselves as if we personally left Egypt. But what does that mean? Must we dress up, pick up a walking stick, and throw our matzos over our shoulder in order to fulfill the mitzvah?

Rabbi Uren Reich explains that it is this very feeling of dependency which we are asked to envision and experience. As the Jews left Egypt, they understood with utmost clarity that they were totally dependent on Hashem. They felt with every fiber of their being the hardships with which they were faced before G-d's salvation, and they knew without a doubt that they had nothing without Him. On Pesach, each of us needs to work on achieving this feeling of dependency. We need to realize that on our own we have nothing; that our intelligence, strength, charisma, and hard work are not the catalysts for whatever success we may enjoy. It is only the will of our Creator which decides what one will achieve.

Last year, we were afforded the opportunity to experience human frailty at its finest. We were shown up-close and personal how limited we truly are and how, but for the grace of G-d, we are unable to do as much as leave our houses. Last Pesach was one filled with pain for so many – those who were sick, those who lost loved ones, those who spent the seder all alone.

But this Pesach is different. No, Coronavirus is not yet in our rearview mirrors, but the severity is certainly beginning to wane. Many are breathing a sigh of relief after receiving the vaccine, many of our businesses, synagogues, and other destinations are beginning to go back to normal, and most of us will be able to spend Pesach in the presence of family.

But let us not slip back into the facade which was so strikingly shattered last year. Let us not be fooled once again into thinking that we are in any position of power. But let us see ourselves as if we left Egypt, and let us truly feel the dependency of one liberated towards his savior.

Chag Kasher V'sameach,
Yosef
