This Shabbat coincides with the enigmatic chag, “Shevii Shel Pesach.” Celebrated on the last day of Pesach, this one-day holiday has very little mentioned about it apart from the general Pesach festival. The last day of Sukkot ends in Shemini Atzeret, a chag with its own unique identity. In davening, it is called by a separate name, Shemini Chag Haatzeret, and its offerings deviate from the pattern established by the beginning of Sukkot. Shevii Shel Pesach has no such distinctions. Its details are the shortest of any other holiday, simply;

וּבַיּוֹם֙ הַשְּׁבִיעִ֔י מִקְרָא־קֹ֖דֶשׁ יִהְיֶ֣ה לָכֶ֑ם כָּל־מְלֶ֥אכֶת עֲבֹדָ֖ה לֹ֥א תַעֲשֽׂוּ׃

And the seventh day shall be a sacred occasion for you: you shall not work at your occupations.

Additionally, it is the only day on which work is forbidden that doesn’t have a full Hallel. What is the significance of this finale to Pesach?

To understand this holiday we will start with the event it is meant to commemorate. This defining moment is the splitting of the sea, kriyat yam suf. This is mentioned by Rashi, quoting the midrash who says;

“On the night of the seventh day they went down into the sea and on the following morning they (the Israelites) sang the Song of Praise and this was the seventh day of Passover. And that is why we read “The Song” as the Scriptural lesson on the seventh day of the Festival”

Yet this explanation for the celebration does not appear in the chumash, and the Torah mentions that Pesach will be a seven-day holiday well before the splitting of the sea. In fact, the Mechilta that is quoted by Rashi does not explicitly say that kriyat yam suf happened on Shevii Shel Pesach, but only gives the numbers of days needed to travel which Rashi uses to prove the date of the event. Where the seder has an aspect of pirsumei nisa, publicizing the miracles of the exodus, on Shevii Shel Pesach we seem to be doing the opposite.

The reason that we don’t say a full Hallel is the key that can begin to unlock the nature of this holiday.

“In principle, we should really recite Hallel every day of Pesach, just like Sukkot, but since on the seventh day of Pesach, i.e. Yom Tov acharon, the mitzrim were drowned in the Yam Suf, it is inappropriate to recite full Hallel on that day. Moreover, since we do not recite full Hallel on Yom Tov acharon of Pesach, we also refrain from reciting it on chol ha-mo'ed Pesach, since otherwise, chol ha-mo'ed will appear more significant than Yom Tov.

This explanation immediately infuses the chag with a sense of deference. While on the Seder night we drink four cups leaning to symbolize each way we were redeemed, on shevii shel pesach we have a much more toned-down experience. Our raucous renditions of dayenu would be disrespectful now that our enemies' bodies lay scattered before us by the thousands.

Despite this transition in tone and attitude, Shevii Shel Pesach still is not regarded as an independent holiday but entirely a part of “chag hamatzot.” This too is greatly informative about the nature of the holiday. An appreciative yet somber assessment of victory is essential to freedom. When we lose touch with the humanity of our enemies we don’t comprehend the collateral damage that our salvation entailed. This reflection is not separate from the redemption process but a fundamental part of it.

Exodus and redemption rarely have no costs. We know about the multitudes of Israelites who didn’t make it out of Egypt and the complete annihilation of Egyptian society. Taking an honest and sober assessment of the collateral effects of our salvation is essential in coming to a full appreciation of the miracles that were necessary for us to persevere. Shevii Shel Pesach is an opportunity to conclude our exodus and establish our national identity on empathy and restraint, not schadenfreude and jingoism.
