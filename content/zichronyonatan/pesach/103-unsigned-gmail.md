As Bnai Yisroel runs out of Egypt they meet the sea, under pursuit by Pharoh and his chariots the tide is high and Moshe says to the people of Israel "Fear not; stand by and see the salvation of G-d which He will show you today. For as you have seen Egypt this day, you shall not see them again, forever. God shall fight for you, and you shall be silent." God told Moshe G-d said to Moses: "Why do you cry out to Me? Speak to the children of Israel, that they should go forward."

But the children of Israel were divided. We all face challenges differently at this moment at the banks of the sea there were four factions. One group felt despair and was ready to hurl themselves into the sea. Another group felt defeated and was ready to turn back to Egypt. Another group felt rage and was ready to go to war, and the final group felt physically helpless and took refuge in prayer.

Moshe rejected all these ideas and in the phrase quoted above, he seeks to comfort each faction while motivating them to move forward. "Fear not; stand by and see the salvation of G-d which He will show you today." this is the answer for those who were ready to drown in despair. For those who wanted to turn back and submit to the aggressor, Moshe said "you have seen Egypt this day, you shall not see them again, forever" for those emboldened to fight Moshe insists "God shall fight for you" and to those praying Moshe said, "you shall be silent."

What then is a Jew to do when caught between an aggressor and tumultuous waters? These factions still exist among us, like the Jews who felt despair religious Jews plunge into the depths of the Torah, isolating themselves in the laws mythologies, and customs that bring us closer to god. Like a chassid in a fur coat. A man once asked a chassid in a fur coat why don't you light a fire that can keep a few people warm? Why invest in a coat that will only warm yourself? The chassid answered without stuttering. It is simply not possible to warm everyone in the world and such an undertaking would result in nothing but frustration so I keep myself warm with fur.

Those who want to return to Egypt are also engaging in escapism but rather than escaping the aggressor they deny the good God did for them in the execution of the plagues. Assimilation may be a route to having a better relationship with the aggressor but it is sure to end the heritage that Moshe worked so hard to maintain

A third response is to fight, this Jew will not escape, this Jew will not submit but this Jew is hopelessly outnumbered by the pharaoh and his chariots.

The praying Jew may feel despair but they are engrossed in a world beyond the material one they may feel like submitting but their trust in prayer makes them more resilient to such a temptation they will not fight because God's ways are good and peaceful but to the other factions they appear to be doing nothing and people doubt them.

God tells Moshe to tell the children of Israel to go forward. And as Bnai Yisroel wades in the water, despair, uncertainty, rage and helplessness are all present but just as the water reached our nostrils the water rose to let us pass. Each and every jew living is a result of a miracle and throughout our lives' challenges, we learn about what coping mechanisms help us through. However, if all we do is cope and forget to take steps forward we will remain in Egypt.
