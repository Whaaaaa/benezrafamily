I was asked by a friend at the local office Minyan, "If I told you that I know a Doctor who came over to me with a two by four and whacked me across the head, but immediately offered to stitch it back up perfectly, and was successful, would you be willing to schedule an appointment?"

Being a new Oleh, with limited Hebrew and little exposure to the Israeli healthcare system, I asked the only relevant question, "Does he speak English?"

All jokes aside, my friend was pointing out the absurdity that we spend a week appreciating our redemption from a situation that was seemingly avoidable. Why should we say Hallel and exalt God when he was the one that put us in this mess, rather Yaakov was forced down to Egypt by famine to begin with? We did not end up in Egypt on our own.

We say in the Haggadah,

וַיֵּרֶד מִצְרַיְמָה – אָנוּס עַל פִּי הַדִּבּוּר

"And he went down to Egypt" - helpless on account of the word

Rebbeinu Avrham Chadida explains that this exposition is meant to deny any claims that Yaakov's descent to Egypt was willing.

Many other Rishonim explain that this drasha is based on God's promise to Avraham, during the Brit bein HaBetarim (Covenant of the Pieces)

"וַיֹּאמֶר לְאַבְרָם, יָדֹעַ תֵּדַע כִּי-גֵר יִהְיֶה זַרְעֲךָ בְּאֶרֶץ לֹא לָהֶם, וַעֲבָדוּם, וְעִנּוּ אֹתָם--אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת שָׁנָה."

"And He said unto Abram: 'Know of a surety that thy seed shall be a stranger in a land that is not theirs, and shall serve them; and they shall afflict them four hundred years"

Others prove that our forefathers were unwilling to make this trip from God's reassurance to Yaakov Avinu,

"וַיֹּאמֶר, אָנֹכִי הָאֵל אֱלֹהֵי אָבִיךָ; אַל-תִּירָא מֵרְדָה מִצְרַיְמָה, כִּי-לְגוֹי גָּדוֹל אֲשִׂימְךָ שָׁם."

"And He said: 'I am God, the God of thy father; fear not to go down into Egypt; for I will there make of thee a great nation."

The Spanish kabbalist Rebeinu Yosef Chiquitilla explains that God would not have told Yaakov to not worry unless he was indeed very worried about the upcoming descent. He explains that Yaakov was afraid that his progeny would never merit redemption and that the strict forces of justice that govern our universe would make it impossible for Bnei Yisrael to deserve to be retrieved. It was only with God's promise that he would change the laws that govern the universe, from the laws of physics all the way to the laws regarding judgment and merit, that Yaakov finally acquiesced to descend.

These commentaries seem to enforce my friend's question. God clearly put Klal Yisroel in subjugation against their will. If this is the case, how is redemption praiseworthy? It seems like the least God could do for us after enslaving his chosen people.

To ask this question is to ignore the fundamental transformation that takes place during the process of subjugation and redemption. This is a process that often described as transitioning from individuals to a people, but I think it has to be much more than that. The Hagaddah says shortly after describing our descent to Egypt:

"וַיְהִי שָׁם לְגוֹי. מְלַמֵד שֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מְצֻיָּנִים שָׁם."

"And he became there a nation" - [this] teaches that Israel [became] distinguishable there.

The Ran in Nedarim (:כִ) "Says that Bnei Yisrael were extraordinary in Egypt and distinctive from the surrounding population like the Malachei HaSaares. They conducted their lives independently and separately from the Egyptians."

Before the redemption, we were clearly already a people. We already had a national identity that we were committed to in the face of oppression.

It is known that Jews thrive when we have a contrasting society to work against. This contrast is what shapes who we are and what our values are. But at this point, our identity is still defined by our hosts. Sure, we are deliberately different but this holds no independent value.

Despite having already achieved distinction we still were required to endure years of backbreaking labor and constant suffering. There must be more we needed to gain from the process than just being different from the Egyptians.

The Gemarah in Sotah (.ה) explains:

א"ר יוסף לעולם ילמד אדם מדעת קונו שהרי הקב"ה הניח כל הרים וגבעות והשרה שכינתו על הר סיני (והניח כל אילנות טובות והשרה שכינתו בסנה)

Rav Yosef says: A person should always learn proper behavior from the wisdom of his Creator, as the Holy One, Blessed be He, disregarded all of the mountains and hills and rested His Divine Presence on the lowly Mount Sinai. And similarly, when appearing to Moses, He disregarded all of the beautiful trees and rested His Divine Presence on the bush.

From the beginning of our redemption, Moshe receiving his message at the burning bush, to the ultimate culmination of our journey at Har Sinai, Hashem has been communicating an incredibly strong message about humility.

The Gemarah further states:

אמר רב חסדא ואיתימא מר עוקבא כל אדם שיש בו גסות הרוח אמר הקב"ה אין אני והוא יכולין לדור בעולם

Rav Ḥisda says, and some say that Mar Ukva says: Concerning any person who has arrogance within him, the Holy One, Blessed be He, said: He and I cannot dwell together in the world.

In order for us to be truly redeemed from Egypt to become a nation that would be defined solely by our relationship to the creator of the universe, we had to create space for God. The only way we can vacate this area to allow Hashem to become the backbone of our identity is to make ourselves lower.

We need to develop an identity that is free from the shackles of subjugation. Throughout the explanation of the work that Bnei Yisrael was forced to do you see a resounding theme of making them lose themselves above all else.

The Gemara Sotah explains that the city of Ramses is an allusion to the word, "mitroses" which means to collapse. The cities built by the Bnei Yisrael would immediately sink into the earth below them requiring them to start from scratch and never allowing them the satisfaction of seeing the fruits of their labor.

Furthermore the Gemara states:

"את כל עבודתם אשר עבדו בהם בפרך. אמר רבי שמואל בר נחמני אמר רבי יונתן שהיו מחליפין מלאכת אנשים לנשים ומלאכת נשים לאנשים."

"The verse concludes: "In all their service, wherein they made them serve with rigor." Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says that Rabbi Yonatan says: The meaning of befarekh is that the Egyptians would exchange the responsibilities of men and women, giving men's work to women and women's work to men, requiring everyone to do work to which they were unaccustomed."

Even if it would result in a lack of productivity or no productivity at all we were expected to keep working. No attention was given to individual skills or achievements. No preferences were honored and people were not allowed to excel in a craft. Our individual identity was stripped away.

And at this same time the haggadah relates that our taskmasters forced us to work under pressure:

וְאֶת לַחָצֵנוּ. זֶו הַדְּחַק, כְּמָה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר: וְגַם־רָאִיתִי אֶת־הַלַּחַץ אֲשֶׁר מִצְרַיִם לֹחֲצִים אֹתָם

"And our duress" - this [refers to] the pressure, as it is stated; "And I also saw the duress that the Egyptians are applying on them."

The hard work we did destroyed our sense of self. We no longer had time to ourselves, choices in our lives, or any independent pursuits. We were worked down to the point all we could do was moan out for Hashem.

And only at this point of total degradation,

וַיּוֹצִאֵנוּ ה' מִמִצְרַיִם בְּיָד חֲזָקָה, וּבִזְרֹעַ נְטוּיָה, וּבְמֹרָא גָּדֹל, וּבְאֹתוֹת וּבְמֹפְתִים.

"And the Lord took us out of Egypt with a strong hand and with an outstretched forearm and with great awe and with signs and with wonders."

וַיּוֹצִאֵנוּ ה' מִמִּצְרַיִם. לֹא עַל־יְדֵי מַלְאָךְ, וְלֹא עַל־יְדֵי שָׂרָף, וְלֹא עַל־יְדֵי שָׁלִיחַ, אֶלָּא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בִּכְבוֹדוֹ וּבְעַצְמוֹ

Once we were relinquished of any form of ego, there finally was the room necessary for God to retrieve us from the depths and to change the entire order of the universe to create an eternal bond between creator and nation.

At this moment we were finally able to forge a bond with G-d that would serve as the foundation of our national identity. And we see that from Kriyas Shema to Shabbos, so much of what we do and how we believe as a Jews refer back to this pivotal moment.

With this, I have answered my friend's question. We are grateful for the opportunity to be separated from all exterior connections and distractions to allow our relationship with God to reclaim its rightful place as our central motivation and concern. After a year of allowing our feelings and personalities to become inflated and push godly service from our lives, we are given the holiday of Pesach. It comes with many fine laws and restrictions regarding chametz, to the extent that we feel overwhelmed and incapable. But with bleach and perseverance, teamwork and tinfoil, we break ourselves down bit by bit. The house will be a mess after the Seder and it can feel like this whole exercise is fruitless labor enforced with unnecessary pressure. But this was really our opportunity to scrub the schmutz from our souls and to lose ourselves in the service of God. After weeks of cleaning, at our wit's end, we sat together with our loved ones in our pristine homes with deflated egos and reaffirmed our love and appreciation to God.

And from this point, and this point only, we can begin counting the Omer. Each night we acknowledge a corresponding pair of emotional attributes that, combined, represent the entire human psyche. The period of Sefiras HaOmer is our chance a to rebuild the qualities that we symbolic incinerated with our bagels. But this time, instead of using our talents and passion for the service of self, we have an opportunity to dedicate them to our creator. Like the offering of the Shtei Halechem that was given on Shavuot, we should merit that what was chametz in our personalities just weeks ago is now a becoming offering to God.
