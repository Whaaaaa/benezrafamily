I was asked by a friend at the local office minyan: "If I told you a doctor came over to you with a two-by-four, hit you across the head, but immediately, successfully, stitched it back up -- would you schedule an appointment?" All jokes aside, my friend was pointing at the absurdity of a week spent praising G-d for a redemption from a situation He seemingly put us in Himself. We say in the Haggadah: "vayered Mitzraymah -- anus al pi hadibur" -- Yaakov went down to Egypt, forced by the word -- denying any claim that the descent was willing.

To ask why we're grateful for such a redemption is to ignore the fundamental transformation that took place during the process. The Haggadah says shortly after describing the descent: "vayehi sham legoy -- melamed shehayu Yisrael metzuyanim sham" -- "and he became there a nation" teaches that Israel became distinguishable there. We were already a people before the redemption, already committed to a national identity in the face of oppression -- but this identity was still defined only in contrast to our hosts, not by anything independent.

The Gemara in Sotah explains that G-d rested His presence specifically on the lowly Mount Sinai, and on a humble thorn bush, to teach humility -- and that anyone with arrogance cannot coexist with G-d in the world. To become a nation defined solely by our relationship with the Creator, we had to make room for Him by making ourselves smaller. The hard labor of Egypt was designed to strip away individual identity -- the cities of Ramses sank back into the earth as fast as they were built; men's and women's work was deliberately swapped, denying any sense of individual mastery. Only once we were relinquished of ego did G-d retrieve us -- "Vayotzi'einu Hashem miMitzrayim... lo al yedei malach... ela HaKadosh Baruch Hu bichvodo uv'atzmo" -- not through an angel, not through a messenger, but G-d Himself, in His glory, directly.

We are grateful for the opportunity to be stripped of every distraction so our relationship with G-d could reclaim its rightful, central place. After a year of inflated egos, we are given Pesach -- overwhelming in its restrictions, but really an opportunity to scrub the schmutz from our souls. And from that point, and that point only, we begin counting the Omer: rebuilding the qualities we symbolically burned away with our chametz, this time dedicating them not to the self, but to our Creator.
