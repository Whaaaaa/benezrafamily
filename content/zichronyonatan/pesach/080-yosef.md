This Shabbos, the Shabbos before Pesach, is called Shabbos Hagadol, or the Great Shabbos. The reason for this prominent name is because of the 'Great' miracle which occurred then, as the Jews in Egypt set aside sheep for their Pesach offerings. They tied the sheep to the posts of their beds, and when the Egyptians asked them the purpose of this practice they revealed their plans to slaughter these animals in the service of Hashem. The Egyptians' teeth chattered with outrage upon hearing of the plans to slaughter their gods, but they were unable to do a thing about it. In commemoration of this 'Great' miracle, this Shabbos is forever called Shabbos Hagadol.

But the questions begs to be asked -- what is so special about this miracle? Yes, it was certainly miraculous, but we're talking about a time in history when miracles were happening every day. The ten plagues, the splitting of the sea, the countless miracles in the desert, to name just a few. What singles out this miracle to be called 'Great' when observed in the context of so many wonders?
Rabbi Mattisyahu Salomon explains with a very deep and piercing thought. The Gemara states that there is a difference between an incomplete tzadik (righteous man) and a complete tzadik. The incomplete tzadik is not permitted to incite an evil man for whom the times are 'laughing', meaning one who is enjoying good fortune. Conversely, a complete tzadik is permitted to incite and openly defy such a wicked man.

Rabbi Salomon explains that the underlying reason why an incomplete tzadik may not defy this wicked man is because his good fortune shows that Hashem has granted him favor for the time being. Because, for whatever reason, this wicked man is enjoying the good favor of Hashem, the tzadik must also follow suit and may not openly defy him. The times 'laughing' for him, connotes that G-d himself is laughing, so to speak.

He goes on to explain that there are two types of laughter that are not wholehearted. The first is laughter of flattery, by which one laughs to trick another with a false sign of approval. The second is laughter of mockery, where one laughs in another's face in order to highlight his disdain. Sometimes, these two forms of laughter combine into a single devastating laugh -- one of outward flattery, all the while coupled with inner mockery.

The divine 'laughter', the good fortune, which the evil sometimes enjoy is this double form of laughter. Still, it takes a great deal of clarity to perceive it that way, and we'll take it step by step.

The most basic understanding one must have when observing the good fortune of the wicked is that this cannot be a 'laughter' of true favor. Hashem is running the world and there is no way that the wicked receive any good because of true favor on G-d's part.

The next level of understanding is to perceive the element of flattery. This is the perception level of the incomplete tzadik, who realizes that this cannot be true favor but rather sees it as a temporary show of approval. Since he perceives the temporary favor which heaven is granting this wicked man, he is obliged to follow suit and may not treat him with contempt.

Only the complete tzadik reaches the depth of perception that this laughter is really a double-edged sword -- one of outward approval laced with inner mockery. The complete tzadik sees right through the success of the wicked to the ultimate demise which this very success will bring upon him. He perceives that the wicked man's success in this world is only a scheme to use up any merits he may have in order to destroy him eternally in the World to Come. Because the complete tzadik perceives this so clearly, there is no reason for him to refrain from defying a wicked man for whom the times are 'laughing'. He sees no hint of favor from on high -- just a laugh of contempt and mockery, as the evildoer revels in the digging of his very own grave. As such, he is permitted to follow suit and defy the wicked just the same.

This, says Rabbi Salomon, is the greatness of Shabbos Hagadol. As the Jews staked out the Egyptian gods for the slaughter and openly advertised their intentions, they were a prime example of one who incites an evildoer for whom the times are 'laughing'. The Egyptians were still on top, still the masters, and were still enjoying a divine 'laughter'. Yet here we were commanded to openly defy and incite them in the most provocative way. Here we were charged with rising above the fickle outlook of this world and viewing the universe through the lens of the the World to Come. We came to the perception of a complete tzadik, and were thereby permitted and indeed required to mirror the true feelings of G-d by openly defying the wicked.

Because of the lofty and elevated level we reached through this miracle, it is singled out as 'Great' and commemorated for all time.

On this Shabbos Hagadol, let us strive to attain the clarity we reached then. Let us strive to view the world not from the shallow vantage point of this world, but from an understanding that everything has a purpose in the eternal plan of the World to Come. In this way we can begin to find the hand of G-d in every facet of our lives and constantly perceive His guiding light.

*Good Shabbos,*

*Yosef*
