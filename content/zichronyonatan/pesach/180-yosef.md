My friend just told me a personal vignette that taught me an important lesson from the new reality that we're all learning to deal with. He had a flight scheduled for Monday, and, when asked about his plans, he replied that 'with the help of G-d' he would fly Monday. Not too surprisingly, he was told just a little while later that his flight had been canceled and he would have to wait until Tuesday. He related to me that even though he had said 'with the help of G-d', he really had meant that he was going, and G-d's involvement had really been more of a formality.

I was struck by the fact that in these times it is so much easier for all of us to realize how little we are in control. Who today makes firm plans more than a day in advance? Who really believes that they will certainly be able to do anything come tomorrow? We can't really assume that we will have work tomorrow, that we will be able to travel tomorrow, or that we will even be able to leave our houses. In fact, we can't even plan to get simple items like toilet paper or noodles at our supermarkets. And unfortunately, we are beginning to learn that there's no guarantee on life itself either.

There seems to be a very powerful message bombarding us on all sides, which I'd like to bring out with a lesson from the matzo we are about to eat.

Matzo would certainly seem to be the central focus of Pesach, but what is it that is so important about these flat pieces of flour and water?

Nachmanides explains that matzo reminds us of two points. On the one hand, it is called a poor man's bread, reminiscent of our destitute state in Egypt when we ate only hard bread akin to matzo. But at the same time, it symbolizes the matzo which we ate upon the exodus from Egypt, which reminds of Hashem's abrupt salvation which did not even allow us the time to let our bread rise.

Rabbi Mattisyahu Salomon explains that these are not two disparate ideas, but rather each one builds on the other to bring out an incredible realization. The 'poor man's bread' shows us how, when left to our own devices, we had absolutely nothing; just dire poverty, slave labor, and untold torment at the hands of our masters. We ourselves are absolutely powerless to achieve any kind of success, to acquire any wealth, or to execute any action simple though it may be.

And yet, the moment that we merited the help of Hashem we were suddenly and abruptly on top of the world. Immediately, before we even had eighteen minutes for our bread to rise, we went from the depths of slavery to the ecstasy of freedom. We marched out with a high hand as Pharaoh, so long our tormentor, came begging in his pajamas for us to get out.

This is the message for all of us to this very day. With all of our modern-day affluence, our technological advances, and our medical and scientific knowledge, it is very easy for us to imagine that we are in control. If I would have told you two months ago that there would come a day when you wouldn't be able to get your hands on simple household staples, you would have told me I'm crazy. And the same if I would have suggested that every department store, mall, restaurant, etc., could suddenly close down. Or that 6.6 million Americans could suddenly lose their jobs. And that barely scratches the surface of the upheaval which has transpired in such a small amount of times across the entire globe.

And with all of this upheaval we are forced to raise up our hands in submission and admit that, indeed, we are not in control.

Coronavirus is a very painful, even calamitous reality which has certainly shaken us all up. One day, however, it will come to an end, the world will move on, and I imagine the people of the world will do their very best to forget that it ever happened. But isn't that a tragic waste? Should all of the lives lost, all of the businesses destroyed, and all of the pain suffered be for naught?

Let us not allow such suffering to go to waste, but rather take the message of coronavirus to heart and allow it to affect an everlasting change in our worldview. Let us come to the firm realization that we are entirely dependent on the grace of G-d, and that on our own we are incapable of even the smallest success.

And, as we eat our matzos on the Seder Night and throughout the days of Pesach, let us remember the double identity of the matzos. That is, the poor man's bread when left on our own, and the bread of immediate salvation when we merit Hashem's help.

And, as we work to achieve greater awareness of human frailty and divine mastery, let us also merit immediate salvation, both from COVID-19 and from our long and bitter exile.

Chag Sameach,

Yosef
