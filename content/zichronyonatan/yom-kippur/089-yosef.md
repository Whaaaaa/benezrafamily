Yom Kippur. The holiest day of the year. The day set aside by G-d, in His infinite kindness, to forgive us for all of our misdeeds.

On this day, every Jew, no matter how estranged, somehow finds himself drawn to a house of prayer. But why is that? What is it about Yom Kippur that is so much more attractive than all the other wonderful days on our calendar? It certainly isn't about the delicious food, the spirited singing, or the beauty of family which we enjoy every other holiday. So what is it that gives Yom Kippur such a magnetic pull on the Jewish soul?

The answer, says Rabbi Shimshon Pincus, lies in the words of Maimonides: '[On Yom Kippur,] the very day itself atones.'

Rabbi Pincus explains that this is the fundamental difference between Yom Kippur and every other holiday. Every other holiday is a time of heightened opportunity for *us* to tap into the power of the time. For instance, Pesach is a time where we can tap into the spirit of redemption, Shavuot is a time where we can tap into the holiness of Torah, and Rosh Hashanah is a time where we can tap into the Kingship of G-d. But all of these are mere opportunities and times of divine assistance -- we alone are responsible for the heavy lifting.

Not so Yom Kippur. On Yom Kippur, the day itself atones. Meaning that the holiness with which Hashem Himself imbued the day works on its own.

However, there are certain things we must do to make ourselves available for that divine intervention. Teshuvah, repentance, is like the ticket to gain entry. We must improve *something*, we must take *some* steps in the right direction. We have to show that we're interested in being helped. Next, we have to refrain from the five restrictions of Yom Kippur -- eating, drinking, anointing, wearing leather shoes, and marital relations. These also are to open ourselves to receive the incredible heavenly assistance knocking at our doors. By loosening our hold on the physical world, we enable ourselves to be lifted up to the immense spiritual realms offered to us on this awesome day.

But, all of these laws are no more than prerequisites to allow the power of the day to act on us. The incredible power of the day is Hashem's doing alone. This is why even the most estranged Jew feels a tug on his heartstrings. We don't need to connect for the day to work. Hashem is reaching out to us and lifting us heavenwards -- on His shoulders, not ours.

This is the tremendous power of Yom Kippur, and it's this power that makes it so pivotal a day in our personal lives. When Hashem is doing the lifting, anything is possible.

Imagine a gigantic steel beam, one-hundred feet long and weighing several thousand pounds. When the beam is sitting on the ground, an army can't move it. But suppose that beam is suspended from above by a crane. Now, all that colossal weight is like nothing, and the beam can be easily maneuvered with no more than a hand.

Such is the Jewish condition on Yom Kippur. We are suspended, as it were, high above the ground. Lifted by Hashem himself out of the gravitational pull that holds us down a whole year. All of the weight of past mistakes and poor self-control which roots us firmly in our stagnant state is suddenly of little consequence. Floating above ground, we can turn ourselves around with minimal effort, so that we land exactly where we'd like to be.

It doesn't matter how far from home we find ourselves today. On Yom Kippur, it's not up to us, but to Him. Let's just open ourselves to His mighty hand and allow ourselves to be hoisted up and out. To become the new people that we always wish to be. And while we're enjoying the ride, let's make sure to adjust our position while the going is easy and start our new lives on the right foot.

*Good Shabbos and Shana Tova,*

*Yosef*
