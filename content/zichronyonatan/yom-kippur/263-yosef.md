Yom Kippur is the one of the greatest gifts ever given to mankind. It is the day described as an ocean of compassion, the day when Hashem purifies and forgives us for all of our misdeeds, the day that we are elevated from the level of mortal man to the heights of the angels.

There is just one caveat; one precondition which must be met in order to gain entry to such an awesome power of love and compassion. Yom Kippur atones for those who repent and do teshuva alone – if one fails to do teshuva, he is left untouched and unaffected by the holiness of the day. In fact, the Meshech Chochma comments that should one fail to do his part in unlocking all the treasures of Yom Kippur, this itself is the greatest prosecution against him, as he is thus charged with willingly confirming a year’s worth of sin when he could have so easily had it erased!

As such, it is of paramount importance to realize that going to shul, reciting the entire machzor, and beating our chests, is still insufficient to gain the atonement of Yom Kippur. We must each sit down by ourselves and go through some internal work which isn’t formally printed in the machzor. We must each find our way to teshuva and thereby gain access to the wonders of the day.

So, what is teshuva? In its most basic form, teshuva is a three-step process of regret, verbal confession to Hashem, and a concrete affirmation not to repeat the deed.

Regret is relatively easy to attain. Sure, it requires a certain amount of learning, since we cannot regret something while unaware of its folly. And it also requires an awareness of Hashem’s greatness and the necessity to obey his word. But, each of us according to our own level, can achieve some level of regret.

Confession is also not too difficult, as it is the one part of the teshuva process which is included in the machzor. The only things to be careful with is to understand what we are saying, and to know that not everything is included in that list. If we know of a specific sin, we should add it in our personal confession outside of the formal prayers.

But now we come to the hard part – the avowal not to repeat our actions. This is where too many of us throw up our hands, since, unfortunately, our list of shortcomings is impossibly long and most of our failings are things deeply entrenched in our natures. How are we supposed to honestly declare that we will never return to our sins?

Thankfully, Rav Yisroel Salanter, founder of the mussar movement, has thrown us a lifeline. He explained that nothing in the heavenly court is black and white, and we cannot assume that one who transgresses a specific sin is simply defined as a sinner until he rids himself of it entirely. Rather, every misdeed can be split into many different parts, and a teshuva even on a single part can already define a person as one who has done teshuva and thus earned entry to Yom Kippur.

Let’s take Rav Yisroel’s own example – Shabbos. If one is not careful with the laws of Shabbos, we might have assumed that so long as he isn’t a full-fledged ‘Shomer Shabbos’, he hasn’t done teshuva and his efforts are yet unfruitful. But in reality, his Shabbos observance, or lack thereof, is a much more nuanced issue where individual actions are divided based on the difficulty involved. Maybe he drives to work, cooks lunch, and plays a dumb game on his phone. The easiest one to avoid would be the game, since it’s relatively uninteresting and there are many forms of permitted entertainment to engage in instead. Next would probably be lunch, as eating is necessary and not frivolous like the game, but it also could be avoided with just a little forethought. Work might be the hardest since he sees it as necessary for his livelihood and dropping it would take bigger arrangements and a larger-scale lifestyle change. As such, the most serious transgression is the game; since it is the easiest to avoid, its performance is the worst show of apathy and the biggest disrespect to Hashem.

As things rank higher in terms of difficulty, they rank lower in terms of severity, and vise versa.

Rav Yisroel exhorts us to start small and take the easiest part of our sins to fix. On that smallest, most limited area, each of us is indeed capable of change. Since it doesn’t carry any special lure, we can honestly endeavor to change once and for all. And, through that minimal effort, we will have actually fixed the most severe part of our sin. With time, such baby steps can subtly chip away at every level of the sin until, eventually, we can rid ourselves entirely of it.

So, let’s all grab hold of the opportunity being given to us. Let’s not throw out the baby with the bathwater, assuming that without perfection we may as well give up, but rather let us take the baby steps which can unlock such spectacular results. And, as we all strive towards the highest teshuva we can reach, one step at a time, may we all enjoy the bounty of compassion and atonement being offered to us on Yom Kippur.

Good Shabbos and a Shana Tova to everyone,

Yosef
