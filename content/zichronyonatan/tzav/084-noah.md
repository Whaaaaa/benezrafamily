We are now in the second parsha of the book of Vayikra, Parshas Tzav. We find Moshe telling over all of the laws of the first offerings in the temple.

Moshe also places the garments on the Kohanim, the priests. He dresses Aaron, the first Kohen Gadol, or High Priest in the eight garments to be worn by every successive High Priest. Four are the same as a regular Priest and the other four are special garments only to be worn by the Kohen Gadol.

Yosef shared with me a very powerful thought that Rashi, our foremost commentator mentions in last week's Parsha regarding these garments. Rashi says that if a regular Kohen wears the extra garments, his service is invalid. And if the High Priest lacks the extra garments specific to his position, his offerings is likewise invalid! This stands true, however, even if the Kohen Gadol is performing an offering that any Kohen is allowed to perform, not only when he carries out an assignment specific to his role.

We find here a very potent thought that is counter to much of what we find in today's society's lifeview but that gives us a real idea of the Torah's understanding of an individual's purpose in life.

A Kohen Gadol is actually just a regular Kohen that has been anointed to take on the role of Leading Kohen for the Jewish people. A Kohen Gadol can do any regular Kohanic service in the temple whenever he likes but Rashi tells us that in order to perform even these tasks not specific to his position, he must wear the garments assigned to him.

We see that a person has specific tasks and roles to play in the world and that he cannot ever compare himself to the yardstick of another to measure his success even when he is carrying out the mundane tasks that fall upon him.

We find a similar idea that Uncle Yonatan taught me regarding Pesach. When the Jews left Egypt, we imagine the splitting of the sea as it's demonstrated in "The Ten Commandments" with one giant path that the Jews walk on through the sea. But in fact, the Medrash tells us that when the Jews left, there were twelve paths and every individual left in the special path for his tribe! Imagine the miracle!

Every person in this world has a purpose and tasks specific to him! Be that person a Jew or a non-Jew! From the tribe of Judah, or that of Shimon! And walking down the path of others, or comparing ourselves to others, only hinders our ability to perceive our unique talents and roles we are meant to play in shaping the world!

If you are meant to be the Kohen Gadol, the clothing of a regular Kohen does not suffice! And if you are meant to be a "regular" Kohen, all the garments you add to your costume only serve to further you from your ultimate purpose!

Interestingly, for those searching for their purpose, Rav Tzadok Hacohen in his magnum opus, Tzidkas Hatzadik shares that all one must do is look at what challenges him most in this world. To find that which is most difficult for him and work to overcome it!

Every person in the world is here to fight his or her demons and to become the greatest YOU that you can be! As Reb Zusha famously said, "When I get upstairs, they're not going to ask me why I wasn't more like Avraham our forefather or Moshe our esteemed teacher, they're going to ask me why I wasn't more like Zusha!"

Good Shabbos!
