The Gemara states, "Where is Esther (אסתר) hinted to in the Torah? Because it says 'And I (G-d) will surely hide (אסתיר) my face on that day.'"

This is very puzzling. The deeper sources tell us that G-d hiding his face from us is the worst of curses. With the shine of Hashem's countenance comes all blessing and only when he hides can evil exist. Why then are Esther and the Purim miracles which came through her mentioned here of all places? Wouldn't it be more fitting to mention her in a place of heavenly good-will and blessing?

In good Jewish form, instead of answering let's ask another question. We find a very peculiar thing in the climax of the Megillah, during Esther's second party with Achashveirosh and Haman. Queen Esther finally feels ready to beseech the King to spare the Jewish people, and she opens with an impassioned and eloquent speech. "If the King pleases, let him give me my life as my request and my people as my desire. Because we have been sold, myself and my nation, to be annihilated, killed, and destroyed." The speech of a lifetime. And from then on out, the rest is history. Achashveirosh, infuriated, inquires as to the identity of he who would want to harm his precious Queen, Esther incriminates Haman who is promptly hung, and the Jews are freed of their arch-nemesis. Just as you would expect things to unfold, in perfect succession.

But for some reason, right between Esther's request and the King's response, the Megillah has a break. In the scroll, this break is an elongated blank space and in the books, it's noted with a letter "ס". Either way, such breaks are usually reserved to separate different topics, kind of like a new paragraph. So what is it doing here smack in the middle of their conversation?!

The answer encapsulates the primary lesson of the entire Megillah. What looks to us like the simple and expected way of the world, as innocent cause and effect, is none other than the hand of G-d. To the naked eye, it appears that a beautiful and beloved Queen succeeded to sway the heart of her adoring King through the power of persuasion. But in reality, the two are separate and unlinked. The cause did not have to achieve the effect, and Esther was truthfully unequipped to bring salvation. The only One running the show was, and always is, G-d himself.

This is why Esther is hinted at in the curse of Hashem hiding his face from us. Because Hashem may be hiding and we may not see his actions openly in the world, but truthfully it is Him running the entire show from behind the scenes. The miracles of Purim, unlike those of the Exodus or even of Channukah, didn't contain any outright breaches of the 'laws of nature', but anyone following the story would have to be insane to blame the uncanny chain of events on mere happenstance.

And so it is in our everyday lives. Yes, Hashem may be hiding, and no, He's not splitting any seas for us, but it is Him and Him alone who is lining everything up. Hiding, but never far away.

Good Shabbos and Happy Purim,

Yosef
