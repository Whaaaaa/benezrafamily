A "filler" usually refers to something that doesn't have much of its own worth; it is just to make something else seem more full. It might refer to the rice or potatoes which are supposed to make the meal go further in place of the meat or fish that is the true centerpiece. It can describe the extra paragraph you stick into your essay just to meet the required number of words. Or the green leaves florists put into an arrangement of flowers to make it appear bigger. All these things lack personal worth or interest and are thus no more than fillers for the main part.

It's surprising, then, that the magnificent gems which appeared in the choshen, the High Priest's breastplate, were called avnei milu'im, or "filler stones". These gems included twelve of the world's most precious stones; diamond, sapphire, ruby, pearl and the like. Additionally, the gems were, rendering them more valuable than all of the gold and silver that was put into the tabernacle. If these stones were the most precious items of all, how is it that they are referred to as no more than simple "filler stones", as if they had no personal value?

The answer, says Rabbi Shmuel Berenbaum, contains a remarkable lesson in what the Torah considers true value. According to the Torah's benchmark, the true stature of a man is not according to his personal accomplishments and greatness, but rather by the degree to which he fills another's void. This is surely true with materialistic acquisitions; if one has attained great wealth but does not bestow kindliness upon others according to his means, then his wealth is essentially a waste. But even in spiritual attainments, the rule holds true. If someone has achieved great and lofty spiritual heights but does not share his wellsprings with others, this cannot be called true greatness. Only insomuch as one positively affects his fellowman can he be truly great.

It is for this reason that these most precious of gems are ranked not by their own incredible worth but by that which they fill the choshen, bringing it to its state of fulfillment.*

Of course, in order to make a true and lasting impression on another one must begin with internal growth and perfection. The goal should be to fill up one's own cup with knowledge and greatness of spirit, so much so that his cup overflows and naturally nourishes those around him. To spill out from one's cup without ever having it filled will only leave the giver as well as the receiver devoid of fulfillment. Just as the gems started with tremendous self-worth, so too we must look to help others from a place of self-perfection and achievement.

I'd like to close with a phenomenal thought from Rabbi Ephraim Shapiro of North Miami Beach. Throughout the Torah, the right side is always considered the more prestigious. It represents mercy as opposed to judgment and is almost universally favored by the Torah. We wash our right hand first, hang the mezuzah on the right side of the door, and even put our right shoe on first, to name just a few instances. Why, then, is the heart placed on the left side of our chest. Shouldn't this most central and important organ, both in a physical sense as well as a spiritual one, be accorded a place of honor on the right side?

The answer is that your heart was not created for you but for your fellow man! Yes, your heart is placed on your left, but when you face your friend it is on his right! It is for him that you are given the capacity to feel and to love, and it is therefore fittingly placed on your friend's right side.

Uncle Yonatan fully embodied this lesson. He determinedly strove for mind-boggling personal achievement but lived for anyone but himself. All of his many accomplishments were universally shared with each and every one of us who merited to be in his circle of influence. He was a giant among men, but with every level attained, he saw to it to pull us all up with him.

Let us constantly remember that we aren't here for ourselves but for others. May our ambition in life never be centered upon ourselves but on those whose lives we may touch and change. And may we forever remember Uncle Yonatan's remarkable ability to change the lives of so many and strive to emulate him always.

Good Shabbos,

Yosef
