Parsha Tetzaveh and the holiday of Purim may seem to have little in common, but both share a theme of hiddenness and revelation. In Tetzaveh, Moshe relays G-d's commandments regarding the priestly garments -- yet Moshe's own name is not mentioned in the parsha, the only one in the Torah from which it is absent. During the sin of the golden calf, Moshe had told G-d, "If You destroy our nation, erase my name from Your book" -- and here, G-d addresses him only as "you," referring to Moshe's full and complete essence rather than his name.

Similarly, in the Purim story, G-d's name does not appear even once in the Megillah, and the events unfold as a chain of apparent coincidences -- yet as the story unfolds, we see G-d's hand at work behind the scenes, orchestrating the salvation of the Jewish people.

In both, hiddenness gives way to revelation: the priestly garments, anointed with oil at the parsha's end, come to represent the revelation of G-d's presence; the hidden hand of Providence in Esther's story is ultimately revealed through the people's miraculous salvation.

As we celebrate Purim and reflect on Tetzaveh, we can take comfort that even when G-d's presence feels hidden, He is still there, guiding and directing our lives -- and we can strive, like Moshe and the heroes of Purim, to reveal that presence through our own actions.
