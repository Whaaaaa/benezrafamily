The Urim Vetumim were two of the most precious artifacts in the tabernacle. These two precious stones bore the names of each of the tribes and served to bring their memory before the Almighty. Additionally, when properly placed inside the Choshen (the breastplate of the high priest), these two stones possessed the remarkable ability to communicate the prophetic word of G-d, as well as the divine answers to any questions which were posed before them. The Kohen Gadol (high priest) would stand facing the Ark, while the questioner would stand behind him and voice the question. Then, the Urim Vetumim's letters would be illuminated before the Kohen Gadol in response to the question. Among other things, this was how the Jews would decide which battles should be fought and could know what the outcome of the battle would be.

Now that we understand the powerful and lofty nature of these stones, we can appreciate the Medrash which goes looking for an explanation as to why Aharon merited to wear the Urim Vetumim. Such a lofty adornment requires special permission to wear, and the Medrash finds something special about Aharon for which he earned the honors.

The heart that rejoiced at the greatness of his brother should wear the Urim Vetumim. As it states, 'And they shall be upon the heart of Aharon'.

When Moshe first encountered prophecy, at the burning bush, he tried to refuse the position as the redeemer of the Jewish nation, arguing that it would be a slight to his older brother Aharon's honor. Hashem responded that Aharon would feel no ill, to the contrary 'he will see you and rejoice in his heart'. In this episode, Aharon showed how pure his heart was; not only did he not feel jealous of his younger brother's position over him, he went so far as to rejoice at his brother's success. In this merit, Aharon was chosen as the proper bearer of the Urim Vetumim.

This is a beautiful story, but why specifically were the Urim Vetumim tied to this merit of rejoicing over his brother's success.

I believe the answer lies in the end of that very same verse. The verse, in it's entirety, reads: 'And you shall place the Urim Vetumim inside of the breastplate, and they shall be upon the heart of Aharon when he comes before Hashem, and Aharon will carry the ''mishpat'' of Israel upon his heart before Hashem, constantly.'

Chizkuni explains that the word 'mishpat' here means the needs of the Jewish people. He thus explains that Aharon was charged with forever bearing and mentioning before Hashem the things which the Jews needed, as communicated to him by the Urim Vetumim.

It would seem that only one who can fully rejoice at the good fortune of his brother is capable of truly understanding and feeling the needs and the pain of his fellowman. Most people run to participate in the wedding of even a casual acquaintance, but they are far more hesitant to attend a house of mourning, particularly in the event of a tragedy. Is this because it is easier to connect to the joy of our fellow than it is to connect to his pain? I think the opposite is true. Just that it is not so difficult to feign joy -- one can dance and sing and smile even if he doesn't feel it in his heart. But sharing in one's pain is something that cannot be just on the surface; if our heart isn't there people will feel it in a second.

When Aharon learned of Moshe's authority, he didn't merely smile and clap for the cameras. He rejoiced in his heart! Down to his very core, Aharon felt nothing but the happiness of his brother. No trace of jealousy or competition; just pure and unadulterated joy. Only such a pure and selfless heart can take on the task of carrying the needs of his people on a constant basis. Only the heart of Aharon could connect to the pain of his entire nation as if it were his own pain.

Here in Los Angeles, we have suffered two terrible tragedies in the last few weeks alone. First, a 16-month-old baby suddenly didn't wake up, and, shortly after, a young mother was killed in a car accident. After hearing such news, every person is shaken up, we feel badly, but then we move on just too quickly. It hurts us, but do we feel as if it is our pain? Do we carry the searing, unbearable wounds of our people upon our hearts?

If not, then we need to question the purity of our hearts and how much we really care about our brethren -- both in times of pain and in times of happiness.

Good Shabbos,

Yosef

CORRECTION (sent two days later): I mistakenly wrote in this week's dvar that the Urim Vetumim were stones engraved with the names of the twelve tribes. This is incorrect. In truth, there is some question as to the exact nature of the Urim Vetumim, but the consensus among most commentators is that they were the written name of Hashem, possibly the ineffable seventy-two letter name. My apologies for the oversight.
