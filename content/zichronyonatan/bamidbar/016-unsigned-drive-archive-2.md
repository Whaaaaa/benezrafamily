In this week's Parsha, Bnai Yisrael is counted -- all men of draftable age in one count, the Levites who perform priestly duties in a separate count. Priestly service originally belonged to the firstborn, but after the sin of the golden calf that responsibility passed to the tribe of Levi -- which is why firstborns must still be redeemed today through pidyon haben, a payment of five shekels.

There are practical reasons to count a nation: military strength, dividing responsibility for transporting the Mishkan. Nachmanides explains that rather than count each person directly, each gave a half-shekel and was counted that way instead -- teaching that it is not appropriate to count Jews directly. G-d promised that Bnai Yisrael would be as numerous as the sand of the sea and the stars of the sky -- innumerable. The half-shekel is described as an atonement for the soul, because being counted, even indirectly, places a person alone on a kind of pedestal before G-d, without the communal merit that ordinarily surrounds them.

King Shaul counted his people using shards of pottery and kid goats; King David's more direct census led to the deaths of 70,000. It is inappropriate to count Jews without a specified purpose -- and even today, some Jews in Israel are reluctant to take part in a census, citing this prohibition.

On Shavuot we complete the counting of the Omer -- the days leading to the giving of the Torah. As Jews who emulate G-d, we count the days and the people who matter to us. Uncle Yonaton bound our family together with Judaism and uplifted us all -- he strengthened our sense of community while also investing in each of us as individuals, preparing us to receive the Torah. Some of us still stay up all night learning; it is Uncle who taught us how.
