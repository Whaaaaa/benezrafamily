In this week's Parsha Bnai Yisrael is counted. All men of draftable age are in one count and the Levites that perform the priestly duties are in a separate count. The priestly service was the responsibility of the firstborn males but after the sin of the golden calf the firstborns were stripped of their duties and the work was passed on to the tribe of Levi. This is why firstborns must be redeemed in a "pidyon haben" a transaction in which five shekels are donated to free the firstborn from their priestly duties.

There are many logistical reasons for counting a nation, knowing how strong the army is, and dividing up the responsibilities of transporting the Mishkan and all of its parts. God counts his people at some pinnacle moments throughout our journey. The completion of the Mishkan was one of these moments, God counted us to see if there were enough of us to earn his holy presence but he also counted us to determine our military might. Nachmanadies explains that rather than count us directly each person gave a half-shekel and that's how we were tallied. We learn from this that it is not appropriate to count Jews directly.

God made a commitment that we, Bnai Yisroel would be so numerous like the sand on the beach and the stars in the sky which shall never be measured or counted. God instructs us that the half-shekel is an atonement for our souls. The reason we need atonement is that even though we are being counted indirectly it is like we are alone on a pedestal before God. Without the merit of our communities, we need to do something extra to maintain our favor before God. when we daven with a minyan and engage in our communities we all bring each other up simply by pooling our strengths and merits. When we are counted however we are simply an individual as we stand before God.
King Shaul counted his people using shards of pottery and later kid goats. King David was responsible for the death of 70,000 Jews. Some say this is because he counted the Jews directly others say he counted them indirectly but merely to pique his curiosity. It is inappropriate to count Jews for no specified reason. Today there are Jews living in Israel who are reluctant to take part in the country's census citing these prohibitions.

On Shavuot, we complete the counting of the omer. These are the days leading up to the giving of the Torah. These days have certainly been precious and as Jews who emulate God, we count the days and people who are important to us. Uncle Yonaton bound our family together with Judaism and uplifted us all. He strengthened our sense of community and he also invested in us as individuals he prepared us to receive the Torah. Some of us may stay up all night learning and reading it is Uncle who taught us how to do that.
