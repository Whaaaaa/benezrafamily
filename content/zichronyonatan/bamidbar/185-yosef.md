When Moshe ascended Mount Sinai to receive the Torah, he was met with fierce opposition. The angels made an uproar - "What is mortal man doing here?" These angels reside in the uppermost heavenly spheres and possess a level of holiness which man cannot even fathom much less rival. How could it be, challenged the angels, that Hashem's most prized possession should be given into mankind's unworthy hands?

Our great teacher Moshe was charged with refuting the claim. He grasped the heavenly throne, in an effort to gain divine inspiration, and took the stage. He said, "The Torah states, 'I am Hashem who took you out of Egypt' – did you descend to Egypt, were you enslaved by Pharaoh, why should the Torah be yours? The Torah further states to keep Shabbos – do you perform work that you can be commanded to rest from? It further says to honor one's parents – do you have parents to honor?"

But with all this, the angels were still unsatisfied. This needs an explanation. After Moshe's very compelling argument, we need to understand what the angels' position was in the first place. Didn't they know that all the mitzvos of the Torah are things relevant specifically to our world? Our teachers explain that the angels actually had a most powerful point – and that is that the Torah in its essence is far far greater than what meets the eye in its simple understanding. Only in it's most basic level does the Torah speak of earthly matters, but in its deeper levels of understanding it can be understood in the most esoteric ways. In fact, the Vilna Gaon said that in the first verse of the Torah he was able to perceive all that would ever happen in the course of history. The angels, therefore, claimed that to give the Torah to man would be to limit it to it's the most basic meaning and strip it of its deepest essence. Such could not be tolerated. And it is for this reason that the angels
were unmoved by Moshe's first and seemingly compelling point.

And so Moshe moved on to the final and decisive defense. "The Torah states: 'You shall not commit murder, adultery, or theft.' Do you have any desires which you must quell? Do you have any evil inclinations with which you must do battle?" When the angels heard this they immediately backed down and bowed to mankind's right to the Torah.

This is an incredible story. The angels were alight with righteous indignation at the thought of degrading and devaluing the Torah to mortal confines, and for this declaration of human frailty they bowed their heads? Isn't the same answer they had to Moshe's first point valid here too? In the Torah's highest and purest form, these base prohibitions also take on an esoteric and exalted form.

The answer lies not in the nature of the aforementioned prohibitions, but in the point, Moshe made from them. Man's desires and inclinations, his greatest enemies, are in fact his greatest resource. It is precisely these limitations which equip him to bring the ultimate glory to his Creator. The angels are possessed of endless holiness and purity, as well as unparalleled proximity to G-d, and it is in this way that they bring honor to Hashem. But because they have no battles to fight and no challenges to overcome, they are unable to contest with man's service. It is specifically when we engage our lower elements and emerge triumphant when we are hampered by our frailty and yet stand strong, that we bring the ultimate honor to Hashem.

In these times of difficulty we are all met with a long list of struggles. Struggles with finances, struggles in health, struggles with our patience for family, struggles in our Torah learning and mitzvah observance, struggles of sadness. And the list goes on.

Let us not be defeated or demoralized by our struggles, but realize that it is precisely the struggles which made us the recipients of the Torah and which arm us with the ability to bring the greatest honor to Hashem.

Good Shabbos,
Yosef
