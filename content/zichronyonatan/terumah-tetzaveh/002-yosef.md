In the olden days, one who spoke badly about his fellowman was struck with leprosy. The leper was so sullied by his impurity that he was banished from the city and branded with this unmistakable mark of shame, his evil ways revealed for all to see. Only after his leprosy was healed was he allowed to rejoin civilization after an arduous purification process. Because he slandered his friend behind his back, as if no one would know, therefore he was punished with this public disgrace. The lesson so powerfully imparted is that every word we say is heard and recorded from on high.

And yet, despite the embarrassment heaped upon this man, it is exactly calculated -- beyond what's deserved we find an incredible sensitivity to his feelings. Tosafot tell us that each of the gates of the Beit Hamikdash were sanctified with the great holiness of the inner chamber (the azarah); a holiness so great that only one who was completely pure was permitted to enter. There was one exception to this rule -- the gate through which a leper was required to anoint his hands during his purification process. This gate was left on a lower level than all the others, allowing the impure to stand beneath the gate. Tosafot goes on to explain that this was not actually necessary. Just like they stood under the gate and stuck their hands into the inner chamber to be anointed, so too they could have stood outside the gate and extended their hands inside. Why, then was this gate left without the higher level of sanctity. The answer, says Tosafot, is for just one thing. That the leper shouldn't have to stand in the rain or the sun but should be able to find shelter beneath the arch of the gate.

This is mind-boggling. One can only imagine the effect every square inch of that great holiness had on the world. It was from that place that the entire universe drew sanctity and Godliness; surely every extra bit was precious beyond words. What's more, the process of anointing was quite brief, and he would only have had to wait a minute or so. Beyond that, who are we talking about? A sinner, one who badmouthed his fellow and payed no heed to the other's pain or discomfort. And for this petty concern and this lowly man's sake we're willing to forgo all of that Godliness in the entire universe?!

The answer is yes. Because this man has a history of disregarding an other's feelings, that is precisely why he needs this courtesy. As he comes to again join society, we enable him to learn the incredible sensitivity the Torah has towards every human being. We tell him that yes you are imperfect, and yes its just a minute in the heat anyway, but even so it is worth sacrificing so much just to care for you. Seeing such sacrifice for the honor and comfort of a single person, we hope that he can learn the lesson and begin again with renewed respect for others and right his previous wrongs. Furthermore, with this show of respect towards the leper, we aim to give him a true sense of self-respect, thereby removing his need to put down others in order to lift himself up.

So let's try to emulate the Torah's respect for our fellow, realizing that there is nothing more precious than another human being. Let's work on realizing our own self-worth and the respect the Torah accords each one of us, so that we need not knock other's to raise our own stature. And let's try to guard our tongues, taking great care not to ever hurt another with our careless or hateful words.

Have a terrific Shabbos,
Yosef
