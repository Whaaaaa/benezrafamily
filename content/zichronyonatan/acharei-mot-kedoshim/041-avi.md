In this week's Parsha, G-d warns Bnai Yisrael of the potential of the Kodesh Kodashim, the innermost chamber of the Temple. Only the Kohen Gadol may enter, and only on Yom Kippur -- and even then, at his own risk: should his mind wander or his intentions waver, he might not come out alive. On Yom Kippur we similarly confront two parts of ourselves -- the part fit to enter the holiest places, and the part we would like to discard -- symbolized by the two goats of the Yom Kippur service, one offered to G-d, the other sent into the desert.

We are warned against sacrifices made away from the Temple, commanded to properly drain the blood from our meat, and warned about illicit relationships. G-d tells us to emulate Him, and the prohibitions against idol worship sit alongside commandments of charity, honesty in business, and equality before the law -- all constructed in the image of G-d. Finally we are given the commandment perhaps most applicable to daily human relationships: love your fellow as yourself.

There is a common theme here: G-d warning of potential, and the importance of boundaries. A sacrifice offered in the Temple may atone for sin; the same act performed elsewhere is an abomination. Improperly cleaned food, meant to sustain us, can instead make us ill. As adults we take risks to accomplish our goals, and knowing the potential for harm can sometimes keep us from trying at all -- but by emulating G-d, we learn to see the dualities in our own actions, and to find meaning not just in what we do, but in how we do it.
