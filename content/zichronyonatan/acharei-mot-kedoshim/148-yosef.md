Imagine the world's most promising medical student. His genius has already gained him fame and recognition in every area of the medical field. From one side of the world to the other, his name is uttered with awe by all of the world's top doctors. While still a student, he has published research opening the door to an eventual cure to cancer, and the eyes of all humanity rest on him to find the answer. He graduates college and decides to take a well-earned trip touring Europe before embarking on his dazzling career. In an effort to 'get away', he takes no means of communication and no one hears from him for over a year. Eventually, one of his most optimistic professors attends an event in England and bumps into his star pupil. Not in the Oxford library, but in a burger joint... flipping burgers. He barely recognizes his student, hair grown long and wearing faded jeans and a t-shirt. "What happened to you," gasps the professor, disbelieving. "I'm happy here," comes the apathetic response, "who needs all that work with medical research."

Tragedy of tragedies! What vast potential, what glamorous dreams, all wasted for nothing other than laziness.

The parsha begins, "Speak to the entire assembly of Israel and tell them, 'You shall be holy for I, Hashem your God, am holy.'" Here we are charged with achieving no less than kedusha, loosely translated as holiness. In the classic work, Mesilat Yesharim, we find kedusha as the very top rung on the ladder of spiritual greatness. There he devotes a few chapters explaining what exactly defines kedusha, but in a few words, it refers to absolute unity with G-d Himself. Only one whose every thought and action is focused on Godliness has achieved kedusha.

We would think that such loftiness is expected only from an elite group, but surely not the benchmark for your average layman. Yet Rashi makes an astonishing comment – he says that Moshe was required to gather every single member of the nation; men, women, and even children, to communicate this mitzvah. That means that it is expected from each and every Jew. No matter who he is, he can and must achieve kedusha.

If the Torah expects such holiness from us, then rest assured that we all possess the ability to get there. The Jewish soul is a piece of G-d himself and in it is contained literally endless potential. We can do it if only we will believe in ourselves, strive ever higher, and never sell ourselves short.

Let us remember the parable of our tragic medical student, and feel the pain and sorrow of such wasted potential. Let us realize that the tragedy depicted in the parable is only a drop in the ocean next to a waste of our own spiritual capabilities. Let us understand what heights we are able to reach and use this understanding to bring our limitless abilities to fruition.

Good Shabbos,

Yosef
