Moshe was a good boy who lived in the Ramot section of Jerusalem. At the time of his Bar Mitzvah, Moshe heard an idea to choose one mitzvah in which to excel in and take to the highest level. This is one way to achieve greatness, because when one chooses one area in which he will take no shortcuts, eventually this will lead him to fix all of his flaws. Moshe, enamored by the idea, decided that his special mitzvah would be davening with a minyan (a quorum). He took upon himself never to miss a minyan and to work his life around this mitzvah, come what may.

For a few years, everything went smoothly. Sure, it wasn't always easy, but more or less Moshe was able to conveniently schedule life in such a way that his undertaking would be fulfilled. After a few years of never missing, though, Moshe faced a real test.

He had gone to a wedding with friends, missing his usual Maariv minyan, but planning to find a minyan at the wedding hall. Unfortunately he couldn't find one there, and resolved to go to a 'minyan factory' near his house as soon as he got home. He got carried away by the dancing and after a long evening finally boarded the bus home with a few of his friends. They chatted amicably, and in the midst of it all, it slipped Moshe's mind that he hadn't yet gone to Maariv.

He showered and finally climbed into bed, well after one o'clock in the morning, weary from a long day. As his head sunk into the pillow, he suddenly remembered that he hadn't yet davened Maariv. An average person would have managed a quick bedside prayer before retiring, but not Moshe. He had a special mitzvah, and he was committed to uphold it – no matter what.

He knew that the local minyan factory didn't run so late, so he decided that he would call a taxi and travel to the famed Zichron Moshe Shtiblach, quite a way from his house, where he was confident that minyanim would still be available. Moshe and his family were far from wealthy, so he pulled out his stash of Bar Mitzvah money to pay for the taxi – taking the whole stack just to be safe. He finally arrived at Zichron Moshe somewhere in the two's, and found, to his surprise, that it was empty. Not one person was waiting for Maariv. Dejected, he almost gave up, but as he took out a phone to call another taxi for the ride home, he had an idea. He asked the dispatcher to send him as many taxi's as he could. This company had four available, and from another few companies he managed to order a total of nine. They pulled up in front of the shul and Moshe instructed the irreligious Jews to please turn on their meters and come inside to help with the minyan. Nine taxi's would cost him the remainder of his assets, but to Moshe it was well worth upholding his special mitzvah. Each one managed to find some sort of Kippah, and Moshe led the services. For many of the drivers, this was the first time in a shul since their own Bar Mitzvahs. When they concluded the service, Moshe took out his stash and asked each driver to tell him the charge. One after another, however, the drivers declined payment and tearfully thanked Moshe for allowing them to be part of such a beautiful minyan.

This is an incredible story about the dedication of a young teenager and of the power of a mitzvah done to the fullest to touch the hearts of those far from Torah. But I heard an incredible lesson from this story which pertains to all of us on a daily basis.

Many times in life, we are given the choice between a mitzvah and some personal drive or desire. How we choose defines the value of all of our mitzvos. In effect, we personally rate each of our actions.

How much do you think every minyan that Moshe attends is worth? We can't really know, but it's worth more than his entire bank account and a whole lot of work in the middle of the night. He rated it as such.

But suppose we miss a minyan in exchange for a few extra minutes of shut-eye, to spare ourselves the inconvenience of cutting our supper short, or to some other minor deterrent. By doing so, we have unfortunately rated our minyan's value far below what it should be worth. After all, we ourselves have shown what silly things we are willing to trade our mitzvah out for. [This idea is brought out in the Ramban in this week's Parshah (18:5)]

So let's be ever cognizant of the value mitzvos have over worldly drives and pleasures, and let's make sure to retain the value of our actions and keep them at the premium they are truly worth.
