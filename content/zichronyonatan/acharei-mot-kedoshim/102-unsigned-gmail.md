After Nadav and Avihu are killed for bringing foreign sacrifices we are instructed that only the high priest may enter the holy of holies on Yom Kippur. In this week's Parsha we are taught the laws of Yom Kippur and the process of casting away guilt.

The idea of scapegoating in the Jewish sense is to dramatize a dynamic that is so universal that in relating the scene the participants live it. In our ceremony lots are cast to determine which of two identical goats is "To The Lord" and which is "to Azazel". Some say Azazel means a hard rocky place others agree that it is the name of a bad angel but the simplest answer is that it means ez-goat azel-sent away, hence the latin translation scapegoat. Maimonides says "There is no doubt that sins cannot be carried like a burden, and taken off the shoulder of one being to be laid on that of another being. But these ceremonies are of a symbolic character, and serve to impress men with a certain idea, and to induce them to repent; as if to say, we have freed ourselves of our previous deeds, have cast them behind our backs, and removed them from us as far as possible." Maimonides believes we are not rational and as humans framing our spiritual goals with physical behavior helps us make a change.
Whether it be casting crumbs into bodies of water or animal sacrifice.

As Jews, we are very familiar with guilt offerings but this particular offering distinguishes itself. Why do we use two goats? Because The Torah specifies two objectives: "On this day atonement will be made for you, to cleanse you. Then, before the Lord, you will be clean from all your sins" (Leviticus 16:30) the two distinct objectives are atonement and purification, atonement being for deeds and purification being for people. We are regularly given the chance to atone and assume responsibility, even if this is hard, purification is truly an opportunity to give us a clean moral slate. The goat that is sacrificed is "To The Lord" is for atonement and for acts we committed. The goat sent away "To Azazel" will make the long journey through the desert for our moral renewal.

In Gan Eden when we ate from the tree of knowledge we hid and blamed circumstances for our lust. In this week's Parsha we are taught to do the opposite "It was me, and I offer no excuses, merely admission, remorse, and a determination to change." These words are powerful and resemble the confessions the high priest makes on Yom Kippur on our behalf. In our renewed view of ourselves, we know sin is practically inevitable and preys on the spiritually weak. We won't have a Yom Kippur with nothing to atone for, by taking responsibility we invite the possibility of gaining higher perspective through watching our own growth and healing. By remembering the duality of or nature we remember the power of our yetzer hara can be captured and used as we choose. Being a nation that believes in one god, we believe that god is also captured by this duality feeling remorse and being torn in times of tough decision. This is why occasionally we must be purified. Fixing our problems is not enough we must free ourselves from sin's corrosive residue after correcting our behavior. Jewish scapegoating is the ultimate form of ownership of one's misdeeds.
