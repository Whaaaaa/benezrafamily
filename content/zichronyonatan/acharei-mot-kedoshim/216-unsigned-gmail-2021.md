One of this week’s parshiot is Parshat Kedoshim. It starts with a mitzvah that at first glance seems superfluous. After having been warned about forbidden relationships, seen the consequences of Nadav and Avihu actions, and right before more mitzvot relating to moral and spiritual growth we are commanded, “קְדֹשִׁ֣ים תִּהְי֑וּ“ (“You Shall be Holy”). Hasn’t that been the main point of the narrative and commandments spanning the last couple parshiot? What does this commandment add to our understanding of achieving the goal of holiness?

The Ramban asks a similar question on Rashi’s understanding of this posuk. Rashi states the command to be holy means, “הֱווּ פְרוּשִׁים מִן הָעֲרָיוֹת וּמִן הָעֲבֵרָה” (“To separate from forbidden relationships and from sin.”) The Ramban, citing other sources that refer to abstinence as a virtue unto itself, says this mitzvah must refer to something more. In his opinion, the mitzvah is referring to abstinence even from permitted temptations. He describes the “נבל ברשות התורה” (sordid person within the permissible realm of the Torah). This person indulges every pleasure to the point of excess. He is a glutton but only eats kosher meat, a drunkard but only drinks kosher wine, he is sex-obsessed but only for his wifes. While this character may seem to comply with the bare minimum necessary to be “Torah Compliant” his attitude and demeanor are themselves a violation. He is not using the regulations of the Torah as a tool to strive for holiness and closeness to God, but rather as a fig leaf to barely obscure his vile actions.

This mitzvah is teaching us an important lesson about how we achieve holiness. We can use the Torah and our observance as a form of moral licensing that allows us to indulge without restraint in the permissible. This perspective undermines the entire exercise of religious restriction and leaves us no holier than before. On the other hand, using our religious practice as a constant reminder and reinforcement of a lifestyle of moderation and self-restraint can result in us achieving greatness and even godliness. As the posuk concludes, “קְדֹשִׁ֣ים תִּהְי֑וּ כִּ֣י קָד֔וֹשׁ אֲנִ֖י יְהוָ֥ה אֱלֹהֵיכֶֽם׃” (“You shall be holy, for I, the LORD your God, am holy.”)

Shabbat Shalom
