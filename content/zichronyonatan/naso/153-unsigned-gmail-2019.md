This week's parsha, Nasso, includes the commandment directed to Aaron and his son's to bless the children of Israel. The verse preceding the command says, "And they shall place My Name upon Bnei Yisrael and I shall bless them." The concept of human conduits for heavenly consecration is confounding. Why is it necessary for us to receive a benediction via the Kohanim when God is surely capable of bestowing it upon us directly?

To shed light on this question, we can look to the Malbim's explanation of the parsha. He brings down a dispute from Chullin over who, "and I will bless them" refers to.

R Yishmael says, when it says, "and I will bless them," it means the kohanim bless Israel and God then blesses the kohanim.

R Akiva says, it means that the Kohanim bless Israel and God responds with his approval.

The Malbim says that this argument is parallel to the argument in Brachos:

It is said: "Hear Me, stubborn-hearted who are far from charity". While both agree that the verse refers to the righteous, Rav and Shmuel, and some say Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar, disagreed as to how to interpret the verse. One said: The entire world is sustained by God's charity, not because it deserves to exist, while the righteous who are far from God's charity are sustained by force, as due to their own good deeds they have the right to demand their sustenance. And one said: The entire world is sustained by the merit of their righteousness, while they are not sustained at all, not even by their own merit, in accordance with the statement that Rav Yehuda said that Rav said.

As Rav Yehuda said that Rav said: Every day a Divine Voice emerges from Mount Horeb and says: The entire world is sustained by the merit of Ḥanina ben Dosa, my son, and for Ḥanina, my son, a kav of carobs is sufficient to sustain him for an entire week, from one Shabbat eve to the next Shabbat eve.

On one side we have the position that the righteous who bring blessings into the world automatically receive from the blessing they bestow upon others. If this is the case, the bracha of kohanim makes perfect sense. They are given the opportunity to bless the people so they can be the first beneficiaries of said blessings. According to the second opinion, that the kohanim do not even necessarily benefit from the blessings they bring, what is the point?

This question can be resolved with a fundamental change in perspective. While we assume that the blessings are for the sake of the kohanim or based on their power the opposite is true. As explained by the Sefer HaChinuch, Birchas Kohanim is really an opportunity for the community to physically express their desire to be part of the divine source of blessing. The kohanim simply give us a forum to exercise our ultimate power, showing god we want to be blessed. This opinion seems to believe that the power to receive blessing isn't through being a tzadik or Kohen, but by simply seeking to be included in it.

This idea is even more powerful when put in the context of tefilla. It seems trivial to stand to before an omnipotent and incorporeal creator and pray that my car works. It seems delusional to believe that my paycheck is reflected in my shul attendance. But we see from the kohanim that davening isn't about demanding reality be a certain away, but opening ourselves up to be willing to see all the bracha around us even when it isn't. Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa received sustenance in a way that was, "concealed and required toil" according to Rashi. Despite this, he is credited with having some of the most powerful forces of prayer. Most notably, as recounted in Taanis:

On a Friday night, R Chanina ben Dosa noticed his daughter was sad and asked why. She responded that she had mixed up her vinegar with her olive oil and accidentally used the vinegar for the Shabbos candles. "Do not let it bother you. He who commanded oil to burn will also command vinegar to burn."

Sometimes we get so caught up in the difference between wealth and poverty, oil and vinegar, a working car and a lawn ornament, that we fail to recognize they all share the same source. To be blessed is to have this recognition and to believe whatever resources we are provided are enough to fulfill the will of God.

Shabbat Shalom.
