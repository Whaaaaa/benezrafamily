*From the Haftarah*

Manoach and his wife had been married for many many years, but had not yet been blessed with children. One day, however, everything changed in a miraculous way. When she was in the field alone, a man with a G-dly countenance approached her and shared the most wonderful prophecy. She would soon conceive and have a son who would be a savior to the entire nation. This son, Samson, was to be a *nazir* from birth -- never to cut his hair nor drink wine. In fact, even she was to abstain from any grape products for the length of her pregnancy.

Just as soon as the man had come, he disappeared, leaving a very surprised, if delighted, woman to share the news with her husband. When Manoach heard the news, he prayed that they be privy to a second revelation, in order that he could hear first hand the prophecy and directions for raising such a child. In response to his prayers, the man of G-d again appeared and repeated his message. Manoach offered the man to partake of a celebratory feast, but the man declined, suggesting instead that he make an offering to Hashem. As fire rose up on the altar, the man, in truth an angel, joined the flames and rose heavenward.

The Torah closes the story with a puzzling statement: *'And the angel never again appeared to Manoach and his wife; then Manoach knew that this was an angel of Hashem.'*

The glaring question is that if, after all the miraculous events, Manoach was still unconvinced of the identity of this 'man', how did he suddenly figure out that it was an angel when the man disappeared forever?

It would seem that in those times miracles were not so unheard of, and there were mortal men as well who could do the miraculous. As such, although Manoach was keenly aware of the godliness of his visitor, there was no clear indication of his immortality. Even as the man rose to the heavens with the flames, he still could have been a human capable of miracles.

So what blew the angel's cover? When he did something distinctly inhuman. For after being the bearer of such wonderful news, which human could withhold himself from coming back to enjoy the thanks and honor which were sure to greet him at the baby's *bris milah*? When the 'man' remained in hiding throughout all of the festivities of the baby's birth, Manoach finally knew that this was no mortal man.

We're not angels, but let's try just a little bit to fight back against this lowly human trait of being so inebriated with our own praise. The next time we perform a good deed, let's try as much as possible to hide it from others. If we achieve something impressive, let's try not to look over our shoulder for a pat on the back. And if we do someone a favor, let's do it for their sake and try to forget about the thanks we have coming to us.

*Good Shabbos,*

*Yosef*
