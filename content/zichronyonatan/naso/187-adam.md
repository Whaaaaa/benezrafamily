"May Hashem bless you and safeguard you. May Hashem light up His face to you and be gracious to you. May Hashem lift His face to you and give you peace."

These words echo in every synagogue daily in Israel as the Kohanim bless the Jewish people.
But interestingly, we use these same words to bless our children each Friday night. And just as the kohanim aren't allowed to give the blessing if they harbor ill feelings toward one of the congregants, so too we must focus our love as we bless our children.

At first glance, the blessing seems almost repetitive and strangely abstract. Light up his face? Lift his face? What is the deeper meaning?

Rabbi Fohrman suggests a beautiful understanding in regards to parenting that I believe we can use in every area of our lives. After all, when we're all grown up, we must still parent ourselves.

The first verse says Yevarechecha, may Hashem bless you. Blessing connotes expansion and multiplication. We find this all over the Torah when the word Bracha is used. One example is when the land will be blessed in the sixth year of the Shemitah cycle such that it gives forth 3 years of provisions. The numerical values of the letters in Baruch are 2, 200, and 20. All demonstrating multiplication.

This verse is connected to the growth of a young child even while in the womb as his or her cells multiply and continue growing exponentially while being safeguarded from the outside world. Parents are mandated to continue this with their children, boosting them and growing them while always protecting them from negative influences and dangers.

The second describes the uncontrolled joy we feel as we see our children live. Our faces light up at a smile, a giggle, a sound, and later an adorable one-liner they never even prepared. This joy must be communicated to our children. They should know the impact they make on us just by being themselves.

And the third part takes us into later life; "lift up" refers to being able to see "eye to eye" with them. To see them as separate and whole individuals who impact the world in their own right. If we have raised them up and properly performed the first two dicta, we should be looking at a healthy adult. One who is very clear and able to create healthy boundaries and who is capable of the unconditional self-love that leads to passing these blessings to yet another generation. We give them Shalom, Peace.

Peace amongst sameness is not true Peace. Shalom is only truly possible when there are differences. God is said to "Create Peace on High," referring to the plague of hail, when fire and water, possibly the most polar opposites, coexisted. Only when we allow our children to truly fly the nest, respecting their differences as they become their own unique selves (lift his face to you), do we reach for Shalom. Peace amongst sameness is not true Peace.

The words for loving-kindness, gemilas chesed, denotes this as well. Gemila means to wean. Chesed means kindness. True kindness is allowing for separation and difference. Giving in a way that allows the child or friend to grow and wean themselves, setting out on their own. True leaders create leaders, not followers.

May we all merit to experience the three-fold blessing ourselves, to pass it with love to our children, and to constantly be the light, lifting our own eyes up to see our families, communities, and the rest of the world. In such a way, may we experience the only true Shalom, heralding Mashiach speedily in our days!

Love from the Shtetl, Adam
