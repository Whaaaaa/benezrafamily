A man once traveled to a far away island on rumors of the opportunity for great business prospects. He planned to stay for a year and hoped to make enough money to last his family a lifetime. Indeed, when he got to the island he found that his skill set was perfect for making it in the local economy; before long he had climbed his way to real wealth and was regarded as one of the richest men in town. However, wealth on the island wasn't measured in modern terms of gold and silver or dollars and cents. It was measured in animal fat, which was a precious commodity on the far flung island, necessary for all parts of their way of life. As the date neared for our friend to return home, he began to plan just how he would transport all of his newfound wealth back to his shtetl in Poland. Sufficiently brainwashed by the local society, he began to invest all of his funds in the most stable form of local currency -- animal fat. Finally the big day came and a great team gathered to pack all of the fat onto a huge ship. The man boarded last, and with a feeling of triumph and pride he dreamed of the admiration he would enjoy from his family and townspeople when they would learn of his new status.

The rest of the story is as obvious as it is tragic. During the voyage home, the ship began to reek of rotting fat, insects began to swarm the boat, and when he ultimately reached land there was nothing but rot and stench to show for his year of toil. He knew from home what was really considered wealth, but he had been blinded by the primitive ways of the island-folk into amassing a boatload of garbage in place of the wealth which could have lasted him a lifetime.

What a fool this man was! It's almost painful to think about such folly. And yet, says the Chofetz Chaim, this story is a parable to each and every one of us.

Our neshama comes from the highest heavens, where it knows with absolute clarity that Torah and mitzvos are the only thing worth collecting. It travels down to this world for but a few years on a very clear mission of earning the spiritual gains to last it an eternity. But then it gets to our world, to the primitive and short-sighted value system which enshrines fickle material gains that won't last more than a few years -- certainly not beyond the grave. We become busy amassing this new kind of wealth valued on the island. We chase endlessly after fun and good times, pleasure, honor, and material riches. We may even perceive ourselves as successful individuals, but all in the primitive lens of worldly pursuits.

One day each person's time will come to travel back home and return his soul to its maker. If we succumb to the brainwashing of the world around us, then we will have nothing to load onto the boat but our cars and estates, our selfies and travel visas, our steaks and sushi and wines. And the rest of the story is again as obvious as it is tragic. Those things are at best useless in the real world, and more often than not they begin to reek with the stench of sin. When we reach our destination we are in danger of facing humiliation and remorse in place of the wealth and honor which we had imagined.

This is the message of the verse in our parshah, 'and that which man will tithe to the Cohen will be his.' The Chofetz Chaim explains that the balances in all of our bank accounts, brokerage accounts, and other assets, are not ours to keep. The time will come oh too soon when we will not be able to take them home. At that time we will realize that the only money that is truly ours is that which we tithed to the Cohen and to tzedaka. Only mitzvos will truly 'be his'.

Let us each take a few minutes to ponder this story, connect to the feelings of embarrassment and regret that the man feels upon returning home, and then turn our focus inward to the parallel in our own lives. With such an exercise we could gain a great deal of clarity and focus in everything we do, and we could rid ourselves of so much of the pettiness of this world.

Good Shabbos,

Yosef
