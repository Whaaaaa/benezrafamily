Every investor knows that there's one key thing to evaluate above all else when investing money and time. Return on investment, or ROI. If you have a good projected return on investment, it's worth figuring out all the other factors. But without it, it's better to walk away.

This week's Torah portion is very interesting from an investing standpoint. We have quite a few guarantees from Hashem if we "go in Hashem's laws, guard the mitzvos, and perform them."

We are promised rain in exactly the right times, ample crops that follow one another without delay, satiating bread that never leaves us hungry or wanting for more, as well as peace both internally in our society and from outside forces that may rise up against us.

Sounds like a good Return, question is, what is the investment?

Rashi jumps in immediately to ask the obvious question: "Guarding the mitzvos and doing them" would seem to refer to the positive and negative aspects of keeping mitzvos. Guarding means observing the negative commandments and doing them, the positive ones.

But what does "going in Hashem's laws" mean? Rashi says it refers to the toil in Torah learning.

The ROI on observing the Torah AND making time to learn and delve into its depths, yields the guarantee of all our physical needs being met and the societal security to enjoy the abundance we are granted.

Sounds like a pretty sweet ROI! Not only are we seeing the long-term return in the next world, we're guaranteed a healthy "cash flow" if you will, while we "build our nest egg."

And it doesn't stop there, we have the best insurance policy around to protect the abundance we're growing.

If you're a savvy investor, you probably want in on the deal!

But you'll see, it gets a bit more interesting in the next few verses. And you may get ready to call your attorney to review the documents on this one since the numbers just don't quite work out.

(Get your accounting brain on.)

Translation based on Rashi:

"...I will grant peace in the land; you will lie down to rest without fear, I will banish evil beats from the land, and no sword shall pass through your land (even to wage war against others). You will pursue your enemies and they will fall before you by their own sword. Five of you will pursue one hundred, and one hundred of you will pursue ten thousand, and your enemies will continually fall before you in supernatural ways…"

Did you catch the accounting problem? The numbers don't work out. If five are capable to take on one hundred, then one hundred should only be able to pursue two thousand, not ten! (Always read the fine print.

But before you get the attorney on the phone, let's look at Rashi's explanation.

He says one of the most important lessons we need to know in our generation, "this is teaching you that there is no comparison between a few who perform the Torah and many who perform the Torah!"

Sometimes we think that maybe our responsibility is to worry about ourselves. We'll take the deal because we see the obvious ROI's both in this world and the next. And if the next person doesn't see it or isn't interested, that's their business not ours.

NOT SO, says Rashi! The Torah is telling us that our own ROI is dependant on the number of people in the syndication! And it's an exponential gain, not a linear one. So we have to invest in changing the world, and helping others join us in our mission!

May we be granted the ability to internalize what we learned from Uncle Yonatan, who never thought it was enough that he was growing but looked for the exponential return from investing in all of us (and frankly, anyone who would listen, no matter their lot) and may we realize all of the returns in this week's Parsha along with the entire Jewish nation. Good shabbos!
