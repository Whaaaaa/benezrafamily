With Shavuos, the day we received the Torah, fast approaching, I'd like to take the opportunity to share a beautiful thought about the Torah. I understand that this may be a paradigm shift for many, and I believe it is one which, when truly internalized, can change our whole relationship to Torah and to Hashem.

I once had the privilege to learn with a very special young man as he was finding his way back to Torah Judaism. He had grown up in an orthodox family, but had struggled mightily in high-school and eventually found himself in the streets while heavily drug-addicted. When we met, he was taking the first steps back into society and Torah observance, and it seemed that the most powerful impetus in his miraculous return was the realization that Hashem really loved him. Today, that confidence in Hashem's love is still carrying him, and he's made a complete turn-around, beyond anyone's wildest dreams. In fact, I am thrilled to have tickets to attend his wedding in just less than a month.

What I'd like to share with you, though, is one of the main reasons he had gone astray in the first place. He grew up doing the mitzvos, learning the Torah, and believing in G-d. There was just one problem -- he pictured G-d as a menacing ruler, waiting to zap him for the slightest infraction. As he grew older, the demands of a Torah life, viewed only as rules and regulations, became burdensome to the point that he stopped conforming and rebelled.

I'd like to dispel the myth to which he, and unfortunately many others, fell prey.

Hashem is not bound to our finite notions, nor can we perceive his essence within the constraints of a mortal mind. Our only understanding of Hashem, our only windows into who He is, is through the facets by which He interacts with people. In such context, we do find certain descriptions of Hashem in scriptures and from our sages, drawing corollaries to human conduct. For example, at the splitting of the sea Hashem is described as a mighty warrior, and often He is described as a father or a king. These are not descriptions of His essence, rather they are depictions of the different ways in which He relates to us at different times.

How would we expect Hashem to 'appear' at the giving of the Torah? I hope none of us imagine the picture we painted before of the menacing ruler, but what would be a more reasonable guess? Perhaps as a sage or a judge, teaching law and wisdom? Or maybe as a king or general, giving us our directives and marching orders?

Rashi tells us the answer which I don't think any of us would have guessed. Hashem revealed himself at Sinai as a '*zaken malei rachamim',* a kind elder brimming with mercy. The Torah is not the law book of a judge nor the orders of a king, it is an outpouring of love and mercy. Every word of the Torah is dripping with kindness and goodness, like the advice of a loving grandfather equipping his grandchild with the means to achieve fulfillment.

This idea is found many times in the Talmud as well, as it often prefaces a quote from the Torah with the statement '*amar rachmana',* or 'the merciful One says'. The Torah is the worlds greatest expression of Hashem's mercy and love.

If we can picture that behind every verse in the Chumash and every law of the Torah, there stands not an iron-fisted ruler, and not even a wise judge, but a loving and merciful zaidy, then the Torah will take on a whole new dimension. These are not just laws and statutes, but rather the keys to success and fulfillment. They are not just rules and regulations, but opportunities and steps to create a beautiful relationship with that Loving Elder.

So for the remaining days of Sefirah, as we count up to the great day of the receiving of the Torah, let us realize just what sort of event we are anticipating -- a climactic outpouring of love expressed by G-d through the giving of His Torah.

And it is you, me, and the rest of our people who are so fortunate as to be the recipients.

*Good Shabbos,*

*Yosef*
