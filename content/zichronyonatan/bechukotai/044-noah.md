Every investor knows there's one key thing to evaluate above all else: return on investment, ROI. Bechukotai opens with the blessings promised for observing G-d's decrees, and the curses -- the tochacha -- that follow abandonment of them, one of the most difficult passages in the Torah to read aloud.

If life with Torah were purely transactional -- mitzvot in exchange for reward, like any other investment -- the tochacha would simply be the fine print of a business contract. But the relationship the Torah describes is not an investment; it is a marriage. The blessings and curses read less like the terms of a deal and more like the anguish of a covenant that has been betrayed and can still, painfully, be repaired.

We should all merit to see our relationship with Torah not as an investment we calculate, but as a covenant we honor -- one whose "returns" cannot be measured in any conventional sense at all.

Shabbat Shalom
