The Mishnah in Succah tells us:

לוּלָב הַגָּזוּל וְהַיָּבֵשׁ, פָּסוּל. שֶׁל אֲשֵׁרָה וְשֶׁל עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת, פָּסוּל. נִקְטַם רֹאשׁוֹ, נִפְרְצוּ עָלָיו, פָּסוּל.

"A Lulav that was stolen or that is completely dry is unfit for use in fulfilling the mitzva of the four species. The Lulav of a tree worshipped as idolatry [asheira] and a Lulav from a city whose residents were incited to idolatry [Ir Hanidachas] are unfit. If the top of the Lulav was severed or if the palm leaves were severed from the spine of the Lulav, it is unfit."

While on a simple level these appear to be halachic minutiae, their placement as the introduction to the mitzvah of lulav indicates a deeper significance. We are all familiar with at least one of the many metaphorical comparisons between the Lulav and a Jew's approach to divine service. These similes are seen in the species sensual characteristics, the sweet-smelling and tasting Etrog represents the tzaddik with both Torah and good deeds, the fragrant but tasteless Hadass represents those with good deeds but devoid of Torah, the Lulav has taste but no smell, symbolizing those who study Torah but do not possess good deeds, and finally the Aravah has neither taste nor smell, symbolizing those who lack both Torah and good deeds. In our action of binding together these different species, we acknowledge and unify Jews throughout the entire spectrum of divine service.

Alternatively, the species represents each of our human faculties that can be dedicated to divine service. The Etrog our hearts, the Lulav our spines, the Hadass our eyes and the Arava our mouths. Again the symbolism is deeply tied to a Jews approach to engaging with God.

B'lvavi Mishkan Evneh explains that this symbolism does not stop with the broad strokes of just what species to choose. He explains that the metaphor permeates deep into the halachic framework of the Lulav as well. The Mishnah in Sukkah is teaching us about the criteria for the acceptable service of God.

The first halacha disqualifies a stolen lulav. This is analogous to the inauthentic service of God. The Zohar teaches that Lulav comes from a combination of the words “lo” (“to him”) and “lev” (“heart”), to show that “one’s heart must be in his jurisdiction.” In other words, a person’s heart should not be swayed by the hearts of others, especially in relation to our way of serving Hashem. When we shuckle harder because the person next to us is, take on a chumrah that is not personally meaningful because it's the community fad, or mimic the behaviors of our Rabbis without understanding their significance, we are effectively offering a stolen service to God.

The second is a dried Lulav. This is our limp and lifeless Pesukei Dezimra the morning after a late night. It is our contentment with letting things dry out, rather than continuing to bloom.

The subsequent two disqualifications are Lulavim that were already dedicated to or used for Avodah Zara. When we use Torah to justify our own desires, when we rationalize rage with religion, we take a Lulav from the Asheria and shake it before God. Although it may look perfect on the outside, God isn't fooled. When we allow ourselves to be swept away by the current of pop culture and media we engage in public idol worship like the Ir Hanidachas. When we shake the Lulav and perform mitzvot are we thinking about the stock market or the impeachment inquiry, or are we focusing our effort and attention on spiritual pursuits?

Next, come physical abnormalities and deficits. A headless lulav has no direction. It can no longer hope to grow higher, to achieve more. An attitude without aspiration is not compatible with divine service. A Lulav with severed leaves is invalid. Rashi explains that this refers to a Lulav where the leaves have become separated from the spine so that there is nothing connecting the leaves to the source. Technically, the leaves could be tied to the spine or may even be able to survive independently without the spine. However, the Gemara learns from this that the Torah requires that the leaves of the Lulav must obtain their nutrients and energy from the spine, in order for the Lulav to be deemed valid for the mitzvah. Like the Lulav, our mitzvos and Avodas Hashem have to be connected. What source is connecting us to these actions? Do we feel that our mitzvah performance is the essence of our lives? Or are we just forcing ourselves to do the mitzvah while still remaining detached?

Coming off of Yom Kippur we are all granted a fresh canvas on which to paint another year of godly service. Sukkot is the time that we can choose our palette. Are we going to use bold meaningful colors to create a work of authentic expression, or are we going to choose the same dull and uninspired hues as the year past?

We should merit to learn the lesson of the Lulav and bring connection and intention to our Avodas Hashem this year.

Shabbat Shalom and Chag Sameach,
