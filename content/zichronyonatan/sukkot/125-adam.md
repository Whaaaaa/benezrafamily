Sukkos falls in an interesting place on the Jewish calendar. We just completed the Days of Awe and approached God from a place of fear and awe, looking to our Father and King and begging to be able to renew our relationship with him. We looked at our past mistakes, spoke them out verbally and resolved not to return to our prior misdeeds.

But we also did something very strange for Jews to do. We removed ourselves from physicality. We even say the verse of “Baruch Shem” aloud on Yom Kippur because we are like the angels for that day. Just as the angels exist in a spiritual plane alone, we stop eating, drinking, washing, and engaging in marital relations. These are all things that connect us to the physical world. And for one day we remove ourselves from the physical world and we stand in prayer all day long, connecting to the spiritual alone and building a bond with our Creator.

But now, a mere five days later, we utilize a different approach. Generally Judaism doesn't promote abstinence from the physical. Rather, we utilize the physical and elevate it to the spiritual plane. Sukkos is extremely physical. We take our entire bodies outside and into the Sukkah. We make Kiddush on wine and we eat and drink in celebration of the special bond we have.

Rabbi Shimshon Pincus brings an amazing explanation of the difference between our approach during the Days of Awe and Sukkos. He says that our avodah, our mission with the upcoming holiday, is to return to the deep relationship we have with the Creator but this time out of pure love. We have an opportunity to experience an even deeper connection than we could on Yom Kippur: to live within the embrace of the Sukkah. To take all of the physical and utilize it to fall in love with God again. Perhaps we could call Sukkos the Days of Love.

We just read Parshat Haazinu which is one of the ten songs in the world. Nine have been sung and the tenth will be sung with the arrival of Mashiach. If we read the parsha, however, it doesn't seem like the greatest song. There are some very difficult passages talking about the Jewish people disconnecting from God and chasing “other gods”. How could this be a quintessential song? Our sages say that what makes something qualify as a Shira, or song, is specifically that it encompasses the difficulties as well as the good. A true song is not all margaritas and basking on the beach. It's hiking a towering mountain, traversing difficult terrain, and arriving at the tallest peak to experience the breathtaking view at the top. It's the sublime mixture of seemingly incongruent emotions and experiences.

I think that's why we have songs surrounding this time of year. We need the mistakes we made in the past, the difficulties we faced, the awe that we ignited in our hearts, the fear of damaging the one relationship in our lives that matters more than anything else, and the love that we can experience with the Almighty all together to sing Shira.

May this Sukkos bring every one of us closer to each other and through that, to our Creator. May we all be able to experience teshuva me’ahava, returning out of love. And may we merit to bring the final song into the world with the coming of Mashiach!

I love you all,
