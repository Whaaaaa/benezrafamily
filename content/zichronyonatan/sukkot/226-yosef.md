Once, a wealthy American businessman had the opportunity to visit the Chofetz Chaim (1838-1933), the sainted leader of the Jewish people, at his home in Radin, Poland. When the businessman entered the tiny and decrepit shack, he could hardly believe his eyes. He took in the sparse and rickety furniture, the bare walls, and the general lack of any and all creature comforts. Could this really be the house of the greatest rabbi of the generation?!

Unable to contain himself, the man turned to the Chofetz Chaim and asked incredulously, “Where is all of the Rabbi's furniture?”

The Chofetz Chaim smiled and turned the question right back, “I don't see your furniture either. Where is all of it?”

Now the man became confused, thinking he had been misunderstood. “My furniture? I am just passing through here, but in my estate back in America I have all the finest things that money can buy.”

“Ah,” said the Chofetz Chaim, “I, too, am just passing through here. It will just be a few short years here in this world, after which I will arrive at my true destination – the world to come. Since I'm just traveling, I see no need for more than the bare minimum to furnish my home.”

This is a perspective which would serve each of us very well, to whatever degree we are able to internalize it. And while such is no easy task, we have been given the holiday of Succos to help us learn the lesson, hands on.

The Talmud sums up the mitzvah of sitting in the Succah for us in one sentence: 'Leave your house of permanence, and live in a temporary hut.'

This means much more than the external action of walking out the door and setting up your folding chairs. There is a paradigm shift here which we are expected to internalize and make part of our lives.

Leave your prior notion that the place where you reside is here in this world. Let go of the misconception that material achievements, pleasures, and comforts are the purpose of life. Instead, live with the mindset that your residence in this world is nothing more than temporal; no more than a means to merit your eternal abode of the world to come.

For a week, we are afforded the priceless opportunity to live the ideal by actually abandoning the sturdy walls and thick roof of our house in favor of our oh so temporal Succah. But we can't just sit back and relax, because when the week ends we are expected to take our newfound perspective back into our houses. This is no easy task, so we have to get busy using our minds throughout Succos to make the message of the Succah part of our psyche, in such a way that it will last even without the external assistance of the Succah.
