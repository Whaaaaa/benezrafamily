After the Jews perpetrated the unthinkable sin of the Golden Calf, Hashem was ready to destroy them entirely. Our leader Moshe stood up to defend his people and beg for their forgiveness. But among his heartfelt and eloquent pleas, we find one line that would seem counterproductive. He says, “Why Hashem should Your wrath burn upon Your people, whom you have taken out of Egypt with great strength and a mighty hand?” We would expect Moshe to recount the good deeds of the people, in order to arouse divine mercy upon them, but enumerating all the miracles which Hashem performed on their behalf would seem only to strengthen the case against them. After all, Hashem did for them, this is how they repay Him?!

Rabbi E. B. Finkel answers with a parable to the parents of a child suffering from cancer. They fight mightily on his behalf, taking him from specialist to specialist, in and out of hospitals, and across the country, to find him the best care. All that time, they put their lives on hold, barely holding down their jobs, barely finding time to eat, and certainly at the expense of any unnecessary pleasures they would have wanted. They can't sleep at night and are consumed with worry for their precious child. Such pain do they suffer on his behalf!

But when the child recovers, what happens to the relationship between that child and his parents? Is it worn thin and uncomfortable because of all the trouble he brought them? Of course not. Their love for this child knows no bounds, and their bond is stronger than that of any of their other children. Why is this? Because the more we give to someone, the more we come to love them. With every sleepless night, every ounce of worry, and every penny they spent on him, they came to love him more and more.

When Moshe came to arouse the mercy of Hashem, he chose to mention all that Hashem had 'invested' in their relationship, reminding Him of the great love which He acquired through these miracles.

This is indeed true of any relationship, parent to child, child to parent, spouse to spouse, and friend to friend. The more we give, the more we love. A relationship founded on the quest to get from another is doomed to failure, and only one rooted in giving can breed true love.

Notably, this is true in our relationship with G-d as well. If we want to achieve a true love of Hashem and find the beauty of a true connection with Him, we need to invest in that relationship. The more we give of ourselves in the service of Hashem, the more we are going to be fueled with burning love and desire for His closeness. With every mitzvah and every word of Torah, the bond becomes stronger. Especially when those mitzvos aren't easy to come by.

But, unlike human relationships, when it comes to Torah and mitzvos this outcome isn't guaranteed. Unfortunately, without proper thought, it is possible to perform mitzvos and learn Torah all day without once thinking about Hashem. If we want our mitvos to build a relationship, we need to think, with every mitzvah that we do, about Whom we are doing it for. If we act merely by rote, or even if we are motivated because it is just the right thing to do, we have not connected with Him. We have not invested in the relationship, because we don't even perceive for Whom we are doing the mitzvah.

So let's try to pause before each mitzvah and think for half a second that we are about to do Hashem's will. And if we find the mitzvah difficult, all the more should we focus on doing it as a labor of love and as an act of dedication towards our loving Father. This is a small exercise from which we can reap vast rewards. Imagine if we could live life with a constant feeling of love and connection to Hashem – what a difference it would make in our entire existence, and how joyous a life we could lead. And now let's turn that image into reality.

Good Shabbos,

Yosef
