Imagine the most magnificent building you have ever seen. Picture the ornate engravings which adorn every molding, the masonry and woodwork which take your breath away, the wondrous tapestries which hang from the walls. In this incredible edifice you find the finest artifacts, crafted from solid gold, more grand than any museum showcase. As you marvel at the artistry and precision, you try to calculate how many of the most skilled and practiced artisans it must have taken to create such a masterpiece; surely the work of the world's most elite experts in each of many fields. You ask your tour-guide who created this and where each of them studied their crafts, and he points to the simple construction team standing outside, people who obviously spend all their days doing coarse labor.

Unbelievable, right?

But in fact, this is exactly what took place with the building of the tabernacle. It was crafted of gold and silver, the finest cedar wood, silk and fur. Its artifacts, including the ark, the menorah, and the altar, would leave the most seasoned art critic in awe. This was one of the most breathtaking pieces of art ever created -- but did you ever wonder who possessed the skills for such an undertaking? For generations the entire Jewish people had been brick layers; hard laborers not unlike the construction team mentioned above. Besides for lacking any experience in the field, you can imagine their coarse hardened hands, hardly the tools for such finery. So how did they do it?

We can answer with the following anecdote. In the circus, there is usually a herd of elephants which are central to the show. How are these gigantic and powerful creatures contained when not under the trainers watch? The answer is fascinating -- they are tied to a simple wooden stake stuck into the ground. They are far stronger than the stake and could easily rip it out, but they never try and therefore it is plenty to hold them captive. The reason they don't try is because they have been tied to that stake every night since they were babies. They tried to uproot it when they were little and at that stage they learned that it was beyond their capability. And so, inhibited by their own underestimate of their strength, they remain captive to the stake for their entire lives. In fact, when Barnum and Bailey's once suffered a fire, the elephants panicked and broke their bonds. After seeing once that they could break loose, they could no longer be contained by their simple post in the ground and the circus was forced to set them free.

We find a similar thing with people. There are countless stories of people lifting huge loads in the face of emergency, doing things which are way beyond normal human capacity. It would seem that we don't really lack the physical strength for such things, but that we usually perform far below our true potential. We are used to selling ourselves short and thereby most of us never tap into our true inner power, much like the elephant who is hoodwinked into believing that the stake is stronger than he.

The greatness of the human spirit is nearly limitless. Indeed it is hewed from under the heavenly throne. A small piece of Godliness, as it were. We can do so much and go so far if only we'd believe in ourselves and give it everything we've got.

True, the Jews were unpracticed and ill prepared for such a daunting task as building the tabernacle. True, their hands were far from the hands of an artist. But they saw the need to build an earthly abode for G-d's presence and they dared to believe that they could do it. As Moshe told them of the work at hand, they came running. They didn't know how they would do it, but they would pour their heart and soul into the work and they were sure they'd be met with success. If we only put our heart to it, there is nothing we cannot do.

Uncle Yonatan was a living example of this greatness of spirit. He did so much and went so far. He touched so many and grew so much. He fell but never let it keep him down. His power was limitless, unbound by all the limitations we impose upon ourselves. He refused to shackle himself and he was therefore capable of the impossible. So let's look to Uncle, break all of our bonds, and strive for no less then absolute greatness.
