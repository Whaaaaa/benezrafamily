At the beginning of this week's parsha, we reiterate the commandment to keep Shabbos before the description of the building of the tabernacle. While previously we had read the divine blueprints, we will now be recounting the actual construction of the building. In both of these places, the tabernacle is juxtaposed with the commandment of keeping Shabbos. This relationship is the source of many laws, in fact, the 39 principles forms of prohibited labor on Shabbos come from the actions necessary to construct the Mishkan. These two commandments are fundamentally related, yet they appear in opposite order in each iteration. When originally presented in command form, Shabbos appears after the Mishkan. In our parsha, we receive the commandment to keep Shabbos again while the description of the Mishkan's construction doesn't start until next week's parsha. Why does the Torah change the order?

The Gemara in Shabbos (69b), raises the following question: what happens if you are far away from civilization and you forget what day it is. How do you observe the Sabbath? The Talmud offers two answers:

R. Huna said: if one is traveling on a road or in the wilderness and does not know when it is the Sabbath, he must count six days [from the day he realizes he has forgotten] and observe one. R. Hiyya b. Rav said: he must observe one, and then count six [week] days. On what do they differ? One master holds that it is like the world's creation. The other holds that it is like [the case of] Adam.

The difference in opinion between these Rabbis is rooted in the change of order mentioned above. There are two perspectives from which Shabbos can be viewed. R. Hiyya's opinion corresponds to the first instance of the Miskan and Shabbos. This is the divine perspective, from which Shabbos is the culmination of a week of creation. For this reason, R. Hiyya prescribes waiting 6 days before observing Shabbos and the Torah mentions Shabbos only after the commands regarding construction. The second perspective, that of R. Huna and the one reflected in this week's parsha and the next's, is from the human viewpoint. As Sixth Day creations, for us, Shabbos is the first day of the week. It precedes our labor and toil.

This dichotomy reveals a difference between Man's creative power and God's. Man works with no foreknowledge of the result of his actions. He is vulnerable to mistakes, unequipped to handle unintended consequences, and prone to distraction. God does suffer from these deficiencies. For this reason, Man needed to start with Shabbos. We couldn't be trusted to engage in the imperfect art of creative work and emerge capable of realizing the original idyllic utopia. We had to experience it first so we would have an untarnished reference toward which to aspire.

On a larger scale, every Shabbos is still that first Shabbos in Gan Eden. Our weekly celebration is intended to create a reference point that can guide us in doing the hard and complicated work of creating the ultimate utopia speedily in our days.
