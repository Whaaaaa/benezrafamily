In the first of this week's two parshiot we learn about the mitzvot of shmittah and yovel, our obligation to redeem our captured brothers, and our responsibilities to the destitute members of our communities. We are forbidden from charging interest within our tribe and commanded, "For your kin, no one shall rule ruthlessly over another."

These mitzvot share a strong tribal perspective that prioritizes the continuation of our communal identity over individual rights. Returning ancestral lands, freeing captives, and our unique responsibilities to Jewish slaves, are just some of the many mitzvot in the parsha that emphasize our communal identity over universal morality. These are the threads that make our social fabric distinctive.

This mix of mitzvot in parshat Behar is summarized by its final verse, "You shall keep My sabbaths and venerate My sanctuary, I am God."

Rabbenu Bechaya, quoting the Ramban, explains that this conclusion is a warning to us about selling a Jew to outsiders. Isolated from the community he is bound to stray from Judaism. The one who casts away a fellow is responsible for neglecting the communal mindset that is the foundation of our society.

This is the mindset that allows us to create sabbaths and sanctuaries.

Our second parsha paints the idyllic picture of what life can look like under these conditions. We are promised bounty "so that the earth shall yield its produce and the trees of the field their fruit"

and security "I will grant peace in the land, and you shall lie down untroubled by anyone; I will give the land respite from vicious beasts, and no sword shall cross your land." Finally, we are told that God will "establish [his] abode in your midst, and will not spurn you."

This is the promise of the Land of Israel. Our security is dependent on our internal cohesion. Respecting the individual sacrifices that we all make for the sake of our tribe can be a way of venerating God's sanctuary and inviting him to abode in our midst.

The opposite is also true, "And if, for all that, you do not obey Me, I will go on to discipline you sevenfold for your sins, and I will break your proud glory. I will make your skies iron..." And if this isn't enough of a warning to make us change, "And if you remain hostile toward Me and refuse to obey Me, I will go on smiting you sevenfold for your sins."

For months, we have found ourselves in the middle of one of Israel's largest internal conflicts. Both sides have taken to the streets in record numbers, some to voice their opinions on the issues, and many to confront and demean the other side. The situation feels intractable with each of Israel's "four tribes" digging in their heels and receding deeper into their respective corners. Each one casts the other as having malevolent intentions and an unwillingness to compromise. The rhetoric has become so pessimistic that newspapers from Yisrael Hayom to Haaretz have featured headlines about the prospect of a Civil War.

And then this week, the day after lag b'omer, all schools within 40 km of Gaza were closed until further notice. The arguing pundits on the radio were supplanted by interviews of displaced families punctuated by the command to immediately seek shelter. Video footage of the Iron Dome shooting down the latest barrage is everywhere. The external threat has temporarily quieted our internal conflict.

As we watch the news unfold, I am grateful that our skies are iron. I just hope it is enough for us to recommit to making this place a sanctuary for everyone, from the inside. Only then can we be assured that the divine will dwell among us.

Shabbat Shalom
