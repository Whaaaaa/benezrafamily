Bechukotai, the second of this week's parshiot opens with, אִם־בְּחֻקֹּתַ֖י תֵּלֵ֑כוּ וְאֶת־מִצְוֺתַ֣י תִּשְׁמְר֔וּ וַעֲשִׂיתֶ֖ם אֹתָֽם - If you follow My laws and take heed of My commandments." This vague message is the subject of many interpretations. What are we to learn from this posuk? Rashi offers the classic explanation, based on the midrash,
AND TAKE HEED OF MY COMMANDMENTS -- "Study the Torah laboriously with the intention to take heed and to fulfill its teachings, as it is said, (Deuteronomy 5:1) "and you shall learn them and take heed to do them."

Many commentators follow in this same vein, interpreting the verse as a coming to emphasize the importance of both learning Torah and practically utilizing the knowledge by either teaching or practicing the mitzvot. The Ibn Ezra says it most succinctly:
אם בחקתי. תשמרו ועשיתם. מצוה ללמוד וללמד ולעשות

AND TAKE HEED OF MY COMMANDMENTS- It is commanded to learn, to teach and to do.

But the Aderet Eliyahu drives the point home:
ואת מצותי תשמרו. הלמד שלא לעשות נוח לו שלא נברא:

AND TAKE HEED OF MY COMMANDMENTS- The one who learns Torah but doesn't do anything with the knowledge, is better off to have never existed.

The marriage of intellectual achievement to real-world action is so fundamental that each alone is worthless. But why does this invalidate a person's entire existence? This can be explained by looking to the next verse,
וְנָתַתִּ֥י גִשְׁמֵיכֶ֖ם בְּעִתָּ֑ם וְנָתְנָ֤ה הָאָ֙רֶץ֙ יְבוּלָ֔הּ וְעֵ֥ץ הַשָּׂדֶ֖ה יִתֵּ֥ן פִּרְיֽוֹ׃

I will grant your rains in their season so that the earth shall yield its produce and the trees of the field their fruit.

The apparent promise of prosperity to the pious is problematic. We know that the correlation between our spiritual advancement and material wealth is tenuous at best. What does it mean and why is this promise made specifically made in regard to the commandment of learning Torah?

The Chizkuni and the Bechor Shor explain that we receive bracha in the form of rain and produce for laboriously learning Torah because to the same extent that a cloud exists to fulfill its mission of providing rain, our mission is to plumb the depths of Torah understanding. Just as an apple tree bearing fruit has achieved the central goal of its existence, when we learn Torah and perform the mitzvot we connect to the central goal of our existence. This perspective also illuminates Aderet Eliyahu's sharp Mussar. One who has learned but not utilized their learning to teach or do mitzvot is akin to an apple tree that never bears fruit. Each year it grows larger and larger taking up more space and resources without ever fulfilling its essential purpose.

Now it is easy to get lost in this metaphor and think that like apple trees we are all supposed to produce the same fruit. The Or HaChaim lays this misunderstanding to rest, saying that the Torah has 4 paths (Pshat, Remez, Drush, Sod) and each path has 70 faces which each branch off into innumerable trails and ways. This is hinted to in the plural form of the words "paths" and "you (plural) shall walk." The Torah is commanding us to explore all of these paths and forbidding us from proclaiming that there is a single path for everyone.

This week's Parsha is calling us to do the laborious work of creating a unique relationship with the Torah and exploring it vigorously. Then we are instructed to make the insights we acquire to change the way we behave and to share them with those around us. This process is our fundamental purpose in this world and its results are the fruit we yield.
