The Israelites cried out for lack of water in the desert, and Moshe and Aharon were commanded by G-d to speak to a rock, whereupon water would gush forth from the rock. There was a certain confusion (which is the subject of debate and is not the topic of our discussion) and they hit the rock instead of merely speaking to it. *'Hashem said to Moshe and Aharon, "Because you did not believe in me to* ***sanctify*** *me in the eyes of Israel, therefore you will not bring the nation into the land which I have given them."'*

We find here a fascinating concept which Uncle Yonatan was very fond of discussing. Our great leaders were charged here with passing up the opportunity to sanctify Hashem's name. Rashi explains that if they would have spoke to the rock, the people would have understood that if a rock obeys the mere command of G-d, then certainly they too must heed His word alone. By hitting the rock, they watered down the lesson, implying that it is only necessary to obey G-d in order to avoid punishment. Because of the exalted level of Moshe and Aharon, this was considered for them a very grave sin and they were denied the right of entry to the Land of Israel.

The incredible thing to note is how the Torah sums up the event. *'These were the waters of Meriva with which Israel challenged G-d, and He was* ***sanctified*** *through [the waters].'* How was Hashem sanctified through the waters? Rashi tells us that it was through the punishment of Moshe and Aharon itself. He explains that when Hashem enacts judgment with His holy ones, His name becomes revered and feared. We are meant to realize that if even such lofty individuals sometimes fall short of G-d's expectations, then how much more so must we never be satisfied with our accomplishments. We must constantly be working on ourselves as we strive ever higher. That lesson itself is a great sanctification of His name.

This means that they were faulted for failing to sanctify Hashem's name and thereby they were punished. What happened through their punishment? Hashem's name was sanctified.

Rabbi E. E. Dessler explains that the purpose of the world and all that is in it, is to bring about the sanctification of Hashem's name. That is the purpose of each mitzvah and the central point of the entire Torah. We are charged with causing this sanctification through our every action. But say that one loses track of his mission in the world and he decides to sin against G-d, thereby defiling His name. Has he thwarted the divine plan? Not at all. All he did was to choose not to align himself with the will of G-d, and not to play an active and willing role in picture. As such, he will be punished, in this world or the next, and in this way Hashem's name we be sanctified just the same. The will of Hashem will be fulfilled *no matter what*, we can only choose what kind of role we'd like to play. We can proactively sanctify Him through His service, while reaping endless reward for ourselves, or we can break ourselves against His will, sanctifying Him through our very suffering.

Choose wisely.
