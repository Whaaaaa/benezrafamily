This week's parshah includes the painful story of *Mei Meriva*, when Moshe and Aharon sinned by hitting the rock to produce water instead of talking to the rock as commanded by Hashem. This seemingly minor infraction cost our great leaders their lives and the right to lead the nation into the land of Canaan, and much has been said to explain exactly the nature of this sin and why it was treated with such harshness.

I'd like to share a penetrating explanation from Rav Yeruchom Levovitz, whose deep words I will attempt give over.

Our sages tell us that one who says things over in the name of it's source brings *geulah*, or redemption, to the world. On the surface this seems difficult to understand. When one hears a nice thought from his friend and tells it over to another, it is simple decency to quote the source instead of taking the credit for oneself. To do otherwise is called plagiarism and, at least in writing, is even against the law. How is this a ticket for the ultimate redemption?

To understand, let's preface with a perspective on what *geulah* is really all about. We've said many times in this forum that the ultimate purpose of creation is to bring about *kiddush Hashem*, the sanctification of Hashem's name. In *golus,* or exile, Hashem's mastery of the world, as well as His power and honor, are concealed. There is a thick smokescreen of nature which hides Hashem's involvement and threatens to fool us into thinking that we run the world. *Geulah*, however, is the full revelation of Hashem's might and omnipotence, when all of His glory becomes crystal clear and undeniable. *Geulah,* then, is the ultimate manifestation of *kiddush Hashem* and the climactic culmination of the purpose of the world.

On a deeper level, one who says something in the name of it's source, doesn't just mean one who avoids plagiarism. It is a deep-seated trait of disgusting any undeserved credit and an exalted level of humility wherein a person is able to take himself and his ego out of the picture and realize that even his 'own' accomplishments are truly rooted in what he was handed from others.

To illustrate, a master of this trait is might be one who delivers a blockbuster performance in front of an enamored audience thousands strong, yet remains unimpressed with his own accomplishment. As the crowd cheers, he, in his heart of hearts, feels no surge of pride and no pleasure at his glory. Instead, he is thinking that his charisma is God-given, his prowess is the product of his teachers, his wisdom is from his parents, and so forth. Everything which would make a lesser man feel pride, evokes in him nothing but gratitude. Every greatness which he possesses is ascribed to those above him, with no sense of ego to steal any of the limelight.

Because *geulah* is the ultimate manifestation of Hashem's glory, it is only this person devoid of any ego and self-interest who can be the vehicle for the *kiddush Hashem* of *geulah.* Anyone else would cloud the picture by taking some of the credit for himself. As he would lead the people to salvation, he would feel some modicum of haughtiness and grandeur, thereby diverting some of the glory to himself instead of pointing it all to it's true source as the honor of Hashem.

Moshe was the paradigm of one who attributes everything to its source. While he was the greatest man that ever lived, he took no credit for himself. It is Moshe who said ונחנו מה, 'what am I'. It is Moshe who is hailed by G-d Himself that the most humble of all men. Moshe knew who he was, but he took no credit for his achievements, sourcing everything back to Hashem. As such, Moshe was the quintessential vehicle to bring the ultimate *geulah,* and it was he who was charged with bringing the Jewish nation into the land of Canaan. Had Moshe been the one to lead them in, our sages tell us the world would have reached its purpose and the temple would have never been destroyed. He was the only person armed to bring about the full and complete *geulah* because he was the one who could do so while sourcing everything to Hashem and enacting the ultimate *kiddush Hashem.*

Thus, when Moshe was commanded by Hashem to speak to the rock and he veered from the source and did something of his own, this was a catastrophic glitch in the master plan. By doing something, however minuscule, that was not a perfect representation of the Source, he had lost the ability to bring about the complete *geulah.* No longer was he able to be the transparent agent who could focus all the honor on it's source. By straying from the Source, he had lost the ability to be the bringer of *geulah*, and for this reason he was denied the position of bringing the nation into the land.

We are standing on the brink of *geulah*, with Moshiach just waiting to step over the threshold and usher in the final redemption. Each and every one of us can and must bring that great day by exerting ourselves in Torah and mitzvos, but we must do so with an emphasis on ascribing everything to the Source. We have to strive and fight to accomplish all that we can, but when we are met with success we have to realize that it's not thanks to us. By doing so, we can diminish our egos and become the transparent people capable of evoking *kiddush Hashem* and the ultimate *geulah.*
