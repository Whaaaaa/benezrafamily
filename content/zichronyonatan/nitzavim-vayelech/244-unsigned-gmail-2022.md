This week's parsha details the mitzvah of Hakel. The King of Israel is instructed to convene the entire community on the first day of Sukkot after a shemittah year, "Gather the people—men, women, children, and the strangers in your communities—that they may hear and so learn to revere your God and to observe faithfully every word of this Teaching." The King would ascend to a platform in front of the assembly and would read from the Torah written by Moshe. The Sefer HaChinuch describes the reason for this gathering," The talk of all the nation – men, women and children – would then be: "Why have we assembled for this large gathering?" And the answer would be: "To hear the words of the Torah—our essence, glory and pride!" This would lead them to praise the Torah and speak of its glorious worth, and implant within their hearts a desire and motivation to study and know G‑d. Thus they will merit the ultimate good, and G‑d will rejoice in His creations."

As this year is a Hakhel year the mitzvah has special significance now. But its relevance is even more pronounced today as we are preparing for Shabbat Shuvah. In his commentary on the Mitzvah of Hakhel the Kli Yakar reveals that this mitzvah is actually the culmination of the entire teshuva process that we started in Elul and that escalated on Rosh Hashanah as we entered the 10 Days of Repentance. What can Hahkel teach us about this process and how can we use it to achieve more complete repentance?

The first day of Sukkot, when Hakhel is performed, is called "the first day of accounting for sins." This is based on a parable brought by the Midrash;

There was a province that owed tax to the king and the king went to collect it. When he was within ten miles, the leaders of the province went out to praise him, so he canceled a third of the tax; within five miles, the middle class of people from the province went out to laud him, so he canceled another third; when he entered the province, all of the residents of the province - men, women and children - came out and celebrated him, and he canceled it all. He said, 'What is past is past. From now, let us start a new reckoning.'

So too on the eve of Rosh Hashanah, the greats of the generations fast, and the Holy One, blessed be He, cancels a third of their iniquities; and from Rosh Hashanah to Yom Kippur, the select individuals fast, and the Holy One, blessed be He, cancels another third of their iniquities; and on Yom Kippur, everyone fasts - men, women and children. And from Yom Kippur to Sukkot, all of Israel is involved in commandments. This one is involved with his sukkah, that one is involved with his lulav. And on the first day of the festival, all of Israel stand in front of the Holy One, blessed be He, with their lulavs and etrogim for the sake of the Holy One, blessed be He. And He says to them, "What is past is past. From now, let us start a [new] reckoning."

The Kli Yakar further explains, "It is well known that Hakhel is necessary for Teshuva, as the Nation of Israel, when they are gathered together become a single unit and can turn toward God in communal repentance. Only between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur is an individual's repentance accepted by God, the rest of the year only communal requests will be accepted."

From these observations alone we can learn a lot more about how the Teshuva process is supposed to work and where our efforts should be focused. We are currently in a rare time of personal introspection and improvement. This is the week that we renew and return to ourselves, so when the time comes we can congregate as a renewed community. What does that work look like? The Sefer HaChinuch's description is perfect, "To hear the words of the Torah—our essence, glory and pride! To praise the Torah and speak of its glorious worth, and implant within their hearts a desire and motivation to study and know G‑d." This is our time to personally work on recommitting ourselves to the Torah and its values. To remember that living a righteous life is the ultimate pride and to acknowledge this righteousness is in us, it is our essence. We can find new motivation in our religious pursuits and rekindle our desire to be close.

Once we have done this work individually we will be able to enter the period after Yom Kippur when we are too busy with preparations for Sukkot to have time to sin. This gives us a chance to experience a taste of the righteousness we have worked to develop a deep yearning for. Then altogether we have a chance to go into the streets as one, men, women, and children to praise the king. If we pour our hearts into this teshuva process he will respond, "What is past is past. From now, let us start a [new] reckoning."

Shabbat Shalom
