This week is Shabbos Teshuva, the Shabbos between Rosh Hashana and Yom Kippur during Eseres Yemi Teshuva. It is a holy and awesome time with tremendous potential for repentance. With the strong themes of Teshuva that surround us, we should review exactly what it is.

The Rambam explains that there are four essential components to this process:

• The transgressor stops doing the transgression and removes it from his thoughts.

• He resolves that he will not do it again in the future …

• He regrets [that which he did] in the past …

• He verbally expresses his wrongdoings to God and makes a verbal resolution for the future.

• Regarding wrongdoings against one’s fellow man: one must reimburse whatever he owes him, and ask the other person’s forgiveness.

The Rambam explains that:

“He who confesses by speech but has not his heart's consent to abandon his erstwhile conduct, behold him, he is like one taking an immersion of purification and in his grasp is an impure creeping thing, when he knows the immersion to be of no value till he cast away the impure creeping thing.”

This parable to a immersion with a bug has become a common idiom within the Torah world but the Rambam does not use it in the vernacular, he is understood to mean it quite literally. If this is the case, we must investigate the laws regarding one who immerses with a creepy crawly to better understand the role of confession in the Teshuva process.

There are two ways to understand the spiritual mechanics that prevent this immersion for having its purifying effect. The first, presented by Rabenu Yona and affirmed by R’ Chaim Soloveitchik based on a Gemara Yerushalmi, is that this immersion never works at all and is unable to interrupt the original impurity bestowed by the creepy crawly. According to this understanding, the immersion has no purifying effect whatsoever. The Rokeach however, disputes this understanding. He holds that in the same manner that the offerings of the wicked maintain their status as offerings, this immersion is still an immersion. The external problem of there being a creepy crawly does not affect the purifying power of immersion at all, it just immediately returns the subject to a state of impurity.

To return to our original case of confession without sincere intent to abandon the sin, we can see two different understandings emerge. According to Rabbenu Yona and R’ Chaim, the confession in such a case is not even considered a confession, while the Rokeach’s understanding validates this as confession albeit one that doesn’t result in full Teshuva.

What are we to do? Leaving our old ways behind, turning a new page, starting fresh, is so hard. Deep down we long to return to our destructive habits, to our impulses and desires. When we stand in reverence on Yom Kippur do we need to be concerned that because we have not yet learned to silence our Yetzer Hara our Teshuva is at stake? How can we ensure that our confession is sincere while acknowledging that truly acquiring our “heart’s consent” can be easier said than done?

To explain the value of verbal confession even before the heart’s tacit consent is acquired, the Yad HaKetana points to another Halacha in the Rambam. Regarding men who refuse to issue divorces to their wives, the Rambam writes that the courts, “beat him until he says he desires to give the divorce.” There is a concept that unless the divorce is given out of free will it is invalid. How does this restriction fit with the Rambam's advice to coerce this bad actor? The Rambam explains that deep down every Jew has the burning will to do what is right. In this case, the man is just being overcome by his evil inclination, something external to his own will. When the courts are beating him into a better mindsight they are not changing his will, rather they are helping him reveal a deeper more sincere will that he has lost connection to.

This same concept applies to our verbal confessions and our heart’s consent. Sure we may still face an inclination to return our sin, but this is not a product of our true will. This is an externality that is merely preventing us from realizing our higher convictions and commitments. From this perspective, even if our hearts are still attached on some level to our past misdeeds this is not a product of our will. Therefore, our verbal confessions hold even more value. The confession transforms our soul's silent yearning to improve into a real physical expression of regret and longing. The confession becomes a tool to expose our deeper pure will and help draw it to the surface.

This Yom Kippur, we are going to hear the same negative voices in our heads who question our ability to change. They ask us if we are really ready to abandon the indulgences we have grown to depend on. They will tell us that our efforts to return are insincere as we still desire our old lives. These voices will convince us that they are us, that they represent our will. At these moments we need to remember the Rambam and Rabenu Yona as they offer the best retorts. Rabenu Yona reminds us to tell this voice that even if we do still long for our old ways, this does not invalidate our confession. And the Rambam reminds us to tell the evil inclination that you are not me! I have a soul that longs for closeness and eternal life, I may get distracted but this spark is truly my defining characteristic. With this in mind, may we all merit to silence the prosecutors on this coming Yom Kippur and have a Gmar Chasima Tova.

Shabbat Shalom, Noah
