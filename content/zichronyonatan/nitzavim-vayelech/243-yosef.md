Rosh Hashanah, besides for being the Day of Judgment, is also a day of Kingship. In fact, the machzor is almost devoid of any mention of our personal actions, and is rather focused on declaring and recognizing the Kingship of Hashem. The message here is that the way to earn a favorable judgment is not so much by examining our past deeds as by aligning ourselves and our futures with the Kingdom of Hashem. To the degree that we can, today, lift ourselves up and and join the ranks of the servants army of the King, to that degree will we merit a year of life and prosperity. Of course, past deeds will not be forgotten and it is imperative that we do repent and fix ourselves, but that is not the focal point of Rosh Hashanah but of Yom Kippur.

On that note, I'd like to share a perspective of what the Kingdom of Hashem is, and how it differs from the mortal kingdoms of yore.

While we tend to think of monarchy as a system catering to the king's benefit, with him tending to the people only for the sake of his own betterment, this cannot possibly be the system of divine kingship. Firstly, as the Omnipotent Creator of the universe, Hashem lacks nothing and does not at all need us to be His servants. Secondly, we find that the relationship of the Jewish people with Hashem is additionally described as 'אני לדודי ודודי לי', 'I am to my beloved and my beloved is to me.' This adds a dimension of marriage to the relationship we have with Hashem, as dodi refers to a spouse.

Marriage is unlike any other partnership known to man. Business partners, employers and employees, investors and the like are also partners, but they partner on a much more superficial level. Let's take the business partners for example. Each cares to earn his own living and to lead his own life irrespective of his partner. Only that this one is business minded and this one is artistically gifted, so they join forces to run their business. They are unified only in what they do, in the execution, but in their deeper wills and aspirations, they are as separate as strangers.

The partnership of marriage, however, is on a whole different plane. A husband and wife are partners in their very essence. They seek not their personal good, but the good of the couple. When one is in pain, it pains their spouse, and when one meets success it is the success of both. Their bond is not one of action and execution, but one of self and soul.

And so is the union of the Jew with Hashem. We don't just serve Hashem in action, and He does not just provide us what we need in order to serve him. Our very essence and our very lives are rooted in and centered around the same thing. That purpose which we are mutually centered around is the revelation of Hashem's glory in the world. It is for this purpose alone that we exist, making it our personal destiny and deepest desire (though we may sometimes allow distractions to stand in the way of that desire).

This perspective has a profound impact on how we relate to the world and the circumstances which come our way. Even someone who is a loyal Jew, focused on the service of his Creator, could make the following mistake. When a circumstance comes his way, especially a hardship, he is liable to understand that the circumstance came for it's own purpose. Only now that it is here, he understands the need to do his best to serve Hashem even in the circumstance. In light of the above, however, this perspective is flawed. It would be true of a business type relationship where the action, in this case the revelation of Hashem's glory, is the extent of the relationship. But because our union with Hashem is like that of marriage, it means that the circumstance is part of man's essence as well. The circumstance came for the express purpose of allowing him the opportunity to reveal G-d's glory in this moment, and thereby achieve his own fulfillment.

If we would see the world through such a lens it would rid us of a lot of the frustration which comes with life's ups and downs. If we would view the roller-coaster as our personal destiny designed specifically for us to affect our exact purpose in the world, then we would find every twist and turn playing to our own hand. And ultimately, when we feel like we are exactly where we want and need to be at all times, we are much better equipped to achieve success than when we feel like we are merely fighting off the misfortunes of life.

This Rosh Hashana, let us perceive how our King is part and parcel of ourselves, and thereby align ourselves fully with His kingdom. And as we continually strive to make ourselves one with the Kingdom of Hashem, may we all merit a year of life and prosperity.

Good Shabbos and Shana Tova,
Yosef
