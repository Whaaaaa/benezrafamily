"And it will be, when all of these things befall you, the blessing and the curse which I have placed before you… and you will return to Hashem, your G-d…"

As we near the end of the month of Elul, the time allotted us to do teshuva and return to Hashem, it is high time that we all make a serious reckoning of how we are fulfilling our mandate. The Torah tells us two of the ways in which our Loving Father aids us in reaching this lofty goal.

The second is one that is easily understood. When a person finds himself on the receiving end of ‘the curse which I have placed before you’, it can be a powerful impetus to change his ways. After a large degree of suffering, there usually comes a point when we tire of pointing fingers, blaming happenstance, and feeling sorry for ourselves. There comes a point when we decide to accept responsibility for our misfortunes and focus inwards towards fixing our faults towards Heaven and towards our fellowman.

The first, however, is less understood and less commonly taken advantage of, but no less effective. That is when we look around us and see an abundance of ‘the blessing which I have placed before you’. When we realize the overwhelming blessings which we enjoy in every facet of life, there is also usually a point when the thinking individual is forced to acknowledge that it is not his intellect and talents which have got him where he is, but rather the hand of G-d. When this reality comes into focus, it is also meant to bring us to teshuva. Whether it comes from a feeling of gratitude, or whether from a feeling of embarrassment; when we stop to think about the tremendous debt which we owe to Hashem it moves us to reach higher.

On Rosh Hashana, we stand at a crossroads of unspeakable trepidation and of equally thrilling opportunity. The entire year which lies before us will be decided on this greatest of days. Our future and the future of the whole world hangs in the balance. There is simply too much at stake to be taken lightly.

This, my friends, is true every year. But this year the message has never rang more clearly. Who can ignore the chilling outcome of last Rosh Hashana? Are we blind to the implications of ‘the curse which I have placed before you’? How many must perish, how many must suffer, and how much must fall into disarray for us to finally get the message? Looking back at the awesome power of last Rosh Hashana’s decision should be plenty to inspire us to teshuva.

And at the same time, I think all of us who are privy to be sitting in our homes - safe, healthy, and comfortable, must be struck by the great ‘blessing which I have placed before you.’ Across the globe, millions have passed away before their time, tens of millions have lost their livelihoods, and still, more are living in a constant state of turmoil, loneliness, and fear. If we can look at our lives and find that we have escaped, to whatever degree, any of these problems, shouldn’t we be overcome with the debt we owe to Hashem? Shouldn’t a feeling of gratitude propels us to cross fire and water in the service of our Great Benefactor? Shouldn’t we be terribly ashamed of our shortcomings in the face of His benevolence?

This Rosh Hashana, we have been equipped to respond to the call of Teshuva unlike we have ever been before. Let us seize the opportunity, tap into the ideas mentioned in our Parshah and highlighted by current events, and return to Hashem with our entire being.

Good Shabbos and K’tiva Vachatima Tovah,

Yosef
