During the month of Elul, our spiritual focus sharpens as we prepare for the High Holy Days. As Rosh Hashanah, and Aseret Yemei Teshuva quickly approach, our attention is directed toward repentance. The Torah portion Nitzavim - Vayelech speaks directly to this theme of returning to God and the accessibility of teshuva.

In this parsha, Hashem issues a stern warning, cautioning us about the consequences of our actions. However, there is an underlying message of hope. Hashem assures us that, despite our wayward paths, "You, however, will again heed Hashem and obey all the divine commandments." This promise is a beacon of encouragement, reminding us that no matter how far we have strayed, sincere repentance is always an option, and Hashem welcomes us back with open arms.

The Torah reinforces this by stating, "Surely, this Instruction which I enjoin upon you this day is not too baffling for you, nor is it beyond reach. It is not in the heavens, that you should say, “Who among us can go up to the heavens and get it for us and impart it to us, that we may observe it?” Neither is it beyond the sea, that you should say, “Who among us can cross to the other side of the sea and get it for us and impart it to us, that we may observe it?" Here, the Torah emphasizes that the path to righteousness and connection with the Divine is not a complex or unattainable one. It is not concealed in the heavens, requiring extraordinary efforts to reach it. Instead, it is right before us, waiting for us to embrace it.

In Eruvin, our sages expand upon this message using the examples from the verse above. Rava teaches that “It is not in heaven” means that Torah is not to be found in someone who thinks his mind is above the Torah and he does not need a teacher and similarly it is not to be found in someone who expands his mind over it, like the sea, i.e., he thinks he knows everything there is to know about the topic he has learned. Rabbi Yochanan says “It is not in the heaven” is meant to teach us that Torah is not attainable by the haughty and “Nor is it beyond the sea” means that it is not to be found among merchants or traders who are constantly traveling and do not have the time to study Torah properly.

This teaching sets the bar for our teshuva back to the Torah. While it is an approachable path there are some basic prerequisites. We must humble ourselves intellectually, relinquish our pride, and commit time and effort to the process. The Torah is most accessible to those who humbly seek knowledge, set aside consistent time for growth, and recognize that learning is a lifelong journey.

As we navigate the profound season of Elul, we must internalize these teachings. We should approach our preparations for Rosh Hashanah with humility and a commitment to learning and growth. Teshuva, as highlighted by our Torah portion, is within our reach, and the wisdom of the Torah is accessible to those who seek it with sincerity. In the coming year, may we draw closer to Hashem through our genuine repentance, and may our Torah study be infused with humility and a deep desire for spiritual understanding.
