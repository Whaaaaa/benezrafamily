Parsha Miketz tells the story of Yosef's rise from imprisonment to kingship. As a young man Yosef dreamt of himself as a celestial body with his siblings bowing to him; jealousy led his brothers to sell him into slavery. In Egypt, his skill interpreting dreams -- first for fellow prisoners, then for Pharaoh himself -- lands him second to the Egyptian throne.

The Rambam's Eight Chapters notes that in dreams the imagination allows for images impossible in waking life -- a boat floating in the sky, an elephant through the eye of a needle. Yet in English we also use "dreams" to speak of our hopes for the future. How could there be a greater contradiction? We do not simply stumble into our life's path; it takes real interpretation to refine our dreams and direct them toward something tangible.

A famine strikes Canaan, and Yaakov sends his sons to Egypt for grain, not recognizing that the powerful Egyptian official before them is Yosef. He accuses them of spying, takes Shimon hostage, and later plants a silver goblet in Binyamin's sack to test his brothers' loyalty.

In this trying time, many of us carry contradictions in our own dreams -- we want peace but at what cost; we love Israel and we hate war. I pray that someone will make the right interpretations and plans to steer us in the right direction, that those in power will seek counsel from those beneath them, and that we remember all of Israel is responsible for one another. May we only have good dreams.
