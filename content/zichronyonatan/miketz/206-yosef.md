The Pharaoh had been up for nights, tossing and turning with dreams of wheat and cows. He knew these dreams must have a meaning, but he just couldn't find an interpreter that would assuage his worries. Finally, Yosef was recommended, taken out of jail, and brought before the king. He provided a clear explanation, addressing every point and even was divinely informed of the pieces Pharaoh had changed or left out. He didn't even make do with a dry bones explanation of the dreams themselves, which foretold of seven years of abundance followed by seven years of terrible famine, but even outlined a plan of action. He advised that someone be appointed to take a store for the hard years while the land was still plentiful, thus guaranteeing an economic stimulus package to last the people of Egypt through the famine. Pharaoh, understandably amazed at the young man's wisdom, immediately appointed Yosef himself to carry out the plan, explaining the promotion by virtue of the fact that 'there is surely no one as wise and understanding as you.'

The question that begs to be asked is that this position doesn't seem to be one that requires the most wise and understanding person in the land. Nowhere in the job description was there any expectation to advise, set laws, or otherwise make sensitive decisions. It was a simple, albeit critical, management position which required command and execution, but seemingly not so much wisdom. Why was Yosef's great wisdom so critical to his role?

The answer is that to properly fulfill the task at hand, the appointed party would need to perceive in vivid clarity throughout the seemingly endless years of plenty that there would come a time when not a grain would be found. He would have to skimp and scrape not to waste a morsel even when food was abundant beyond anyone's wildest dreams. This ability to see seven years down the road as if it were right in front of him, to act in a way incongruent with the present because of knowledge of the future, and not to be lulled by today's affluence into forgetting about tomorrow's poverty, are things possessed only of the wise.

In our lives as well, there are figurative times of plenty and times of famine; times of tremendous opportunity for growth and accomplishment followed by times when opportunity is scarce.

For example, youth is the years of plenty, when one is full of vigor and has little responsibility on his shoulders. In one's youth, he is free to pursue all of his personal goals and he has the strength and vitality to attain them. Later, hopefully, a person is laden with the responsibilities of a family and the need to make a living; things which, although certainly a worthy obligation to carry, make personal achievement something much more elusive.

But don't worry if youth has passed you by, there are many more times of plenty if one opens his eyes, some big and some small. Retirement is certainly an opportunity, when one is freed of the time constraints of work but is still of able body and mind. Even old age can be a time of plenty, when one is unencumbered by many physical pursuits and able to turn inward and achieve greatness of heart and soul. And there are many others, too many for this author to think of or to enumerate, but each person can surely find them in there own lives.

In truth, though, with all the ups and downs in our lives, the overarching time of plenty encompasses our entire sojourn in this world – as long as we are still outside of the grave. This world is our only chance to accomplish, to perfect ourselves, to perform mitzvos and study the Torah, and to earn the immense and eternal reward of the World to Come. When this life ends, there will be a 'famine'; a time when we will enjoy fully every mitzvah which we have amassed but in which we will be completely and utterly unable to accomplish anything more.

In fact, when we enter a graveyard, we hide our tzitzis in order not to cause pain to the deceased. If they would see the simple strings hanging from our garment, with which we can so easily earn untold reward, they would be eaten up with jealousy for the opportunities which we are afforded every day that we are counted among the living. Even the great Vilna Gaon, while lying on his death bed after a life of tremendous accomplishment, held his tzitzis longingly and lamented the fact that he would soon pass from the world in which the opportunity for eternal reward could be had for just a few dollars.

So let us not be so short-sighted and obtuse as to be fooled by our times of plenty into ignoring the inevitable famine which is sure to follow. Let us, like Yosef, see in vivid clarity how the achievements and accomplishments which are today so readily available, will one day be beyond our grasp. And let us be spurred on by this perception not to squander a single day or a single chance, but to spend every moment of our oh-so-short lives attaining greatness, amassing true wealth, and ultimately meriting eternity.

Good Shabbos,
Yosef
