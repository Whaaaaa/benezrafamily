*Each blade of grass has an angel that tells it to grow. - Zohar*

This week's parsha begins with Yaakov sending angels to his brother. On this opening verse, Rashi comments that the angels were, "literally angels." This is a strange observation for Rashi to make. What is he pointing out to us that isn't immediately understood from the verse itself?

To answer this question we first have to understand what angels are from a Jewish perspective. Everything, from plants to animals to humans has a spiritual force that causes it to grow and express its full potential. A tree does not grow independent of the spiritual reality that exists around it, rather it grows because there exists within the spiritual reality a force that commands it to sprout, develop and eventually bear fruit. Additionally, angels are the guardians of the created world that we exist in. They represent the forces that we see, in very practical ways, around us.

Our forefathers throughout Bereshit facilitate conversation with God almost exclusively through angels, while Moshe does not use angels as his primary method of interaction with God. The difference in these communication styles can be attributed to the distinct roles that these characters played in our development as a people. Our forefathers were the seeds of spirituality struggling to germinate in a physical world. They strove to put out strong eternal roots that could eventually sustain a Holy People in a mundane world. Our forefathers were altering nature itself through their actions in order to carve out a place for the future Jewish People. They were responsible for building a physical vessel that would eventually be able to receive the Torah, our direct link to God, from Moshe.

Moshe's purpose was very different. He had the impossible task of bringing the Torah to the Jewish people. The Midrash famously relates the angel's concern with God's decision to "defile" the Torah by putting it in the hand of flesh and blood humans capable of error. Moshe responded in classic Jewish fashion by asking them a question, "The Torah says, 'Do not worship idols,' do you live among people who worship idols?" It is clear from this exchange that Moshe is showing that only a human being can elevate creation by infusing it with spirituality. Moshe would have never arrived at the point to argue with angels if his ancestors had not worked with them to make room in creation for spirituality to take root. While Avraham Yitzchak and Yaakov modified the physical world to accommodate spirituality through angels, Moshe could actualize this potential as a human.

With this understanding, Rashi makes a bit more sense. Yaakov didn't send out couriers, messengers or emissaries that were anything short of angels. These angels were responsible for showing Esau that things had changed, the physical world was fundamentally different now as the seed of spirituality had taken root and a holy nation had room to grow.

If this was the case and Yaakov's message to his brother was clear, we still must ask why Yaakov himself had to follow. Why wasn't sending the entourage of angels enough to diffuse the situation?

The answer to this lies in the nature of the relationship between Yaakov and Esau. Yaakov prays to God for deliverance "from the hand of my brother, from the hand of Esau." The battle that was being waged here was on two levels. Yaakov had to confront Esau both as an adversary who hated him but also as a brother who was close and dear. While the angels could demonstrate that the room for spirituality had been carved out, they could not demonstrate that this void had been filled. Esau was not ignorant of the existence of a spiritual realm. Our tradition maintains that he was spiritually powerful and knowledgeable. By being informed that a space between these two realms had been made for a special nation to inhabit he was not deterred. He still thought that he and his children could be that people. In the capacity of a physical adversary Esau was stopped by Yaakov's angel, but as a brother, the eldest brother with incredible potential, he remained committed to stopping Yaakov from filling the space of a chosen nation with his progeny. This conflict with Esau as a brother could only be resolved once Yaakov, a human endowed with the ability to bring holiness into the physical creation, showed that he had already created the family that would be the chosen people.

In my life I often blur the boundaries between what I can do and what requires angelic assistance. While the entities on high make room for development and even demand it, only a human can actually fill this potential. Our rich tradition and heritage provide us ample opportunity for spiritual fulfillment and growth but too often we expect divine intervention to not just provide the opportunity, but to also take full advantage of it. I am blessed with a family, a community and with Shabbos, and I pray that these opportunities for development only grow and deepen. But this week I am not praying that I will be a good father, husband, son, community member, and Jew. I am confronting Esau myself and showing him that I am here to fill the potential that was so graciously given to me.
