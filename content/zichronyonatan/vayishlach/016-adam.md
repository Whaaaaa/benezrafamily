Each blade of grass has an angel that tells it to grow, says the Zohar. This week's parsha begins with Yaakov sending angels to his brother. Rashi comments the angels were "literally angels" -- a strange thing to point out. To understand why, we must understand what angels are: forces that command the created world to grow and express its potential.

Our forefathers throughout Bereishit communicate with G-d largely through angels, while Moshe does not. Our forefathers were the seeds of spirituality struggling to germinate in a physical world, altering nature itself to carve out a place for the future Jewish People -- building the vessel that would eventually receive the Torah. Moshe's task was to actually deliver it. The Midrash relates the angels' concern that G-d would "defile" the Torah by giving it to error-prone humans; Moshe's response -- "do you live among idol worshippers?" -- shows that only a human being, embedded in the physical, imperfect world, can actually elevate creation by infusing it with spirituality.

With this, Rashi's comment makes sense: Yaakov didn't send mere couriers, but literal angels, to show Esav that the physical world was now fundamentally different -- that the seed of spirituality had taken root.

Still, why did Yaakov himself have to follow? Because Yaakov had to confront Esav not only as an adversary but as a brother -- and while angels could demonstrate that room had been carved out for holiness, only Yaakov himself, by having already built the family that would become the chosen nation, could show that the space had actually been filled.

In my own life I often blur the line between what I can do myself and what requires angelic assistance. Our tradition provides ample opportunity for growth, but too often we wait for divine intervention to do what only we can do ourselves.
