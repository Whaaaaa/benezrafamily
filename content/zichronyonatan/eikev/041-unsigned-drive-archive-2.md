While in the desert, the Children of Israel were sustained by the miraculous "manna", a bread that fell from the heavens to the door of each individual's tent. In fact, this manna possessed whichever taste the one eating it wished for, and it sustained all of their needs for the entire forty-year period in the desert.

But what does this have to do with us? How is it relevant to the world as we know it, when bread doesn't fall from the sky, but is earned through hard work and effort? The Torah itself addresses this question and tells us that more important than that which it sustained our fathers is the message which it imparts to us for all time. "In order to let you know that not on bread alone is man sustained, but rather on whatever is decreed by the mouth of G-d shall man be sustained." The lesson for us is that G-d has many ways to sustain us. Yes, the natural order ofs things is that bread is earned through hard work; it doesn't fall from the sky. And yes, the natural order is that food sustains, while other things do not. But in truth, even that is really just a decree from on high. Nature is just what we call the heavenly decrees which we observe on a reliable basis. It serves as a mask to allow one free will and the choice to disbelieve.

This reminds me of a story from the Talmud which Uncle Yonatan loved to tell. Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa was extremely poor, and one week his family had no oil with which to light Shabbos candles. His daughter told him of the predicament, and he calmly told her to light the wicks with vinegar. When she questioned him that vinegar does not burn, he responded, "He who said that oil should light will say that vinegar shall light." And that's how it was. Rabbi Chanina, who truly believed with all his heart that the entire world and its "laws" are just a projection of the will of the Almighty, was thereby not bound by those laws.

For those of us who aren't on Rabbi Chanina's level to totally discount nature, we must play by the rules and relate to the world as it is masked by nature. But it isn't all or nothing. Even as we work within the framework of nature, G-d will bend the rules to the extent by which we see His hand behind it and grant us extra divine assistance.

This week has been incredibly difficult for all of us. Uncle Yonatan's passing leaves us with a gaping hole in our lives and our hearts -- a hole which cannot possibly be filled. Let us realize, though, that death is not an accident, and the cause of death, be it what it may be, is not a blind "law of nature", but rather an expression of the will of G-d. There is a Master Plan and everything is being done by the hand of G-d. I know Uncle Yonatan could be in no better place than in the hand of G-d.

*The learning should be a merit for Yonatan ben Avraham Avinu a"h*
