This week is Shabbat leading into the month of Elul, the month immediately preceding Rosh Hashanah and the High Holidays!

This week's parsha, Eikev, begins with a very important verse: “And it will be, Eikev Tishm'un, as a consequence of your heeding these laws, and your guarding and fulfilling them, Hashem your God will guard for you the covenant and the kindness which he swore to your forefathers.”

These two Hebrew words, Eikev Tishm'un, are translated by Rashi, the foremost commentator of the Torah, to encompass two things. Eikev literally means heel. But figuratively in this verse, it means as a consequence of, or as a result of. Tishm’un means your listening or your adhering to. Thus the translation utilized above.

But in his first comment to this week's Parsha, Rashi says that Eikev also encompasses its literal meaning. Every word in the Torah is intentional and important. So we must delve into the usage that God felt necessary to convey with the specific words He did.

Rashi says, “If the light commandments which a person (has a tendency to) tread on with his Eikev, with his heel, you will heed, Hashem will guard for you…”

The use of the word heel is there to adjure us to pay specific heed to the small things, the small mitzvos which we may deem less important.

So many times in life, we look to be grand, to be a hero. We try to think of the big ways with which we can make an impact and we try to jump high and fast in our relationship with God. But Rashi tells us an imperative lesson to take account and look to the small things.

With this period leading into the High Holidays, when we all look to improve ourselves and make resolutions, we read an imperative lesson in doing so.

Uncle Yonatan, I believe, was a king of this. We know that with the intensity and brilliance he had, he was well aware of flaws, both in character traits and Torah observance. But he had internalized the message that growth happens slow. And every one of us who gained from his reconnection to Hashem knows that it took years. And that he knew where he wanted to be every step along the way. For most of us, cognitive dissonance makes true, lasting growth very difficult in these moments because we are so far from who we wish to be. But as Yonatan's example showed us, we can reach it.

You may have heard of the Chinese Bamboo. Like any plant, growth of the Chinese Bamboo Tree requires nurturing – water, fertile soil, sunshine. In its first year, we see no visible signs of activity. In the second year, again, no growth above the soil. The third, the fourth, still nothing. Our patience is tested and we begin to wonder if the daily watering and effort will ever be rewarded.

And finally, only in the fifth year, is there visible growth. And what growth it is! The Chinese Bamboo Tree can grow 80 feet in just six weeks.

But in all honesty, does the Chinese Bamboo Tree really grow 80 feet in six weeks? Did the tree lie dormant for four years only to grow exponentially in the fifth? Or, was the little tree growing underground, developing a root system strong enough to support its potential for outward growth in the fifth year and beyond?

Very often the big leap we seek may be beyond our current abilities but there is a small step, which if we build small steps upon small steps, we can use our heels to push us to new horizons that we may not even currently consider possible. And we can build from our heels up, the foundation upon which we can become the Giants we are capable of being.

As we enter this auspicious and meaningful time of year, let's choose small, reasonable baby steps that we can stick to each day and we will merit that “Hashem will guard the covenant and the kindness,” both parts of our unbreakable relationship with Him until we can merit the arrival of Mashiach, together!
