This week's Parsha is called Eikev, which has a dual translation. In the simple form it is rendered 'because' and thus the first verse reads that because of our keeping of the mitzvos we will merit a lengthy list of blessings. But Rashi tells us the hidden meaning of the word here, which can also be translated as the heel. If we keep the mitzvos that one normally tramples on with the heel, that is how we will merit those blessings.

Which mitzvos is Rashi referring to here? Rabbi Moshe Feinstein (quoted in Kol Ram) explains that the intention is those mitzvos which we grow up from a young age to pay little attention to. Those mitzvos which we have trained ourselves to disregard, are precisely the ones which we need to pay extra attention to, and those are the ones which we can take to the bank with just a little effort.

One of Rabbi Feinstein's prime examples is the mitzvah of praying with concentration. Most of us pray on a regular basis, even three times a day and, for the men, even with a minyan. But how many of those prayers are said with the proper level of concentration and how many are said by rote – barely even knowing which words our mouth is spouting. In fact, the Talmud states that prayer is one of the things which stands at the pinnacle of the universe and which people treat cheaply. Is there a better example of a mitzvah which is trodden upon? And what can we do to rectify the situation?

In response to this problem, I'd like to share a very powerful explanation of the first three brachot of the amidah, which I believe have the potential to uplift and empower the rest of our prayers.

The Rashba (one of the great early talmudic commentators) explains that the first three brachot are designed to underscore the difference between requesting from mortal man and beseeching from the Almighty. Each of the three brachot is pointed towards a specific facet of the uniqueness of prayer.

First, when one asks his fellowman for something, there is usually some element of reciprocity, wherein either the person you are asking from already owes you something, or at least that he stands to benefit from you in the future. The average request is not made for a complete freebie. Not so when we pray to Hashem. Hashem owes us nothing and He lacks nothing that He could hope to gain from us in the future. Thus, when we ask from Hashem we must realize that we are asking without any merit whatsoever – it is a request for pure and complete divine kindness. This is brought out in the first bracha, where we state that Hashem helps us 'in memory of our fathers', He is the 'One who bestows pure and good kindness', and 'He who does for the sake of His name, out of love'. With these praises, we recognize that there is no reciprocity here – just a humble servant beseeching his Master for undeserved goodness.

Secondly, when requesting from a mortal, one understands that it may very well not be in his power to fulfill the request. This is not necessarily the address where he can hope to have his needs fulfilled. However, when beseeching Hashem we recognize that there is absolutely nothing which is not within His power to grant. This is brought out by the second blessing, which declares Hashem the One who revives the dead. In this blessing we state that Hashem is completely unbound by the shackles of nature – He can make a total reverse of nature like the revival of the dead, and certainly any other need is simple in comparison to that. Hashem is most definitely the address where we can come to for any and every need.

The third fact about a mortal benefactor is that whoever this person may be, he is not the only hope. If the first person turns down the request it's not the end of the line; there are others on the list who may still be able to help. With Hashem, though, it is not this way. If one asks Hashem for something and is turned down, there is no backup plan and nobody else who is able to help. One absolutely must receive the blessing of G-d for him to be able to carry on. If Hashem will not help him, then rest assured that nobody else can. This is the third blessing, where we name Hashem 'the Holy G-d' or הא-ל הקדוש. *Kedushah*, holiness, in a Torah sense means something or someone who is separate and removed from the rest of the world. In this context, it is a description of Hashem's quality of Oneness, referring to how He stands far above any other power in creation; alone in His ability to run the world.

If we would say these three blessings with such an understanding, I believe it would change the entire form of our prayers. By using the beginning of the amida to realize and understand the power and importance of what we are about to do, it would give another whole edge to our prayers. So next time we begin to daven, let's utilize the first three blessings to revitalize our entire prayer. Let's remember how much we need to beg, seeing as we are asking without any merit of our own, and how important such requests are, seeing as He certainly can help whereas nobody else can without Him.
