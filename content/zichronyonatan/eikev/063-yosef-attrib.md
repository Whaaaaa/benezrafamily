While in the desert, the Children of Israel were sustained by the manna, bread that fell from heaven with whatever taste the eater wished for. But what does this mean for us, in a world where bread doesn't fall from the sky? The Torah tells us plainly: "not on bread alone is man sustained, but rather on whatever is decreed by the mouth of G-d." The lesson is that G-d has many ways to sustain us; even the "natural order" -- that bread comes from labor, that food alone sustains -- is itself just a heavenly decree we've come to call "nature." Nature is a mask that allows for free will and the choice to disbelieve.

This reminds me of a story from the Talmud Uncle Yonatan loved to tell: Rabbi Chanina ben Dosa was so poor his family had no oil for Shabbat candles. He told his daughter to light the wicks with vinegar. When she protested that vinegar does not burn, he answered, "He who said that oil should light will say that vinegar shall light." And so it was.

For those of us not on Rabbi Chanina's level, we must play by the rules of nature as we find them -- but even so, G-d bends those rules to the extent that we see His hand behind them, and grants us extra assistance along the way.

This week has been incredibly difficult for all of us. Uncle Yonatan's passing leaves a gaping hole that cannot possibly be filled. Let us realize that death is not an accident, not a blind "law of nature," but an expression of the will of G-d -- there is a Master Plan, and everything is done by His hand. I know Uncle Yonatan could be in no better place than in the hand of G-d.
