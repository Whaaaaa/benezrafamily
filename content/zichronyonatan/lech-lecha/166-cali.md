In this week's Parasha we being to follow Avram and Sarai on their journey, from leaving Avraham's birthplace to the trials they faced building a family of their own. One part of this Parsha which always sparked my curiosity is the relationship between Avram, Sarai, and Hagar. How did Sarai go from encouraging Avram to have a baby with Hagar to afflicting Hagar once she became pregnant?

In the Parsha we read:

וַתֹּ֨אמֶר שָׂרַ֜י אֶל־אַבְרָ֗ם הִנֵּה־נָ֞א עֲצָרַ֤נִי יְהוָה֙ מִלֶּ֔דֶת בֹּא־נָא֙ אֶל־שִׁפְחָתִ֔י אוּלַ֥י אִבָּנֶ֖ה מִמֶּ֑נָּה וַיִּשְׁמַ֥ע אַבְרָ֖ם לְק֥וֹל שָׂרָֽי׃ וַתִּקַּ֞ח שָׂרַ֣י אֵֽשֶׁת־אַבְרָ֗ם אֶת־הָגָ֤ר הַמִּצְרִית֙ שִׁפְחָתָ֔הּ מִקֵּץ֙ עֶ֣שֶׂר שָׁנִ֔ים לְשֶׁ֥בֶת אַבְרָ֖ם בְּאֶ֣רֶץ כְּנָ֑עַן וַתִּתֵּ֥ן אֹתָ֛הּ לְאַבְרָ֥ם אִישָׁ֖הּ ל֥וֹ לְאִשָּֽׁה׃. וַיָּבֹ֥א אֶל־הָגָ֖ר וַתַּ֑הַר וַתֵּ֙רֶא֙ כִּ֣י הָרָ֔תָה וַתֵּקַ֥ל גְּבִרְתָּ֖הּ בְּעֵינֶֽיהָ׃: וַתֹּ֨אמֶר שָׂרַ֣י אֶל־אַבְרָם֮ חֲמָסִ֣י עָלֶיךָ֒ אָנֹכִ֗י נָתַ֤תִּי
שִׁפְחָתִי֙ בְּחֵיקֶ֔ךָ וַתֵּ֙רֶא֙ כִּ֣י הָרָ֔תָה וָאֵקַ֖ל בְּעֵינֶ֑יהָ יִשְׁפֹּ֥ט יְהוָ֖ה בֵּינִ֥י וּבֵינֶֽיׄךָ׃: וַיֹּ֨אמֶר אַבְרָ֜ם אֶל־שָׂרַ֗י הִנֵּ֤ה שִׁפְחָתֵךְ֙ בְּיָדֵ֔ךְ עֲשִׂי־לָ֖הּ הַטּ֣וֹב בְּעֵינָ֑יִךְ וַתְּעַנֶּ֣הָ שָׂרַ֔י וַתִּבְרַ֖ח מִפָּנֶֽיהָ.׃.

As the Parsha goes, Sarai approaches Avram in distress and tells him that he should have a child with Hagar. When Hager becomes pregnant it says that she "lost esteem from her mistress" (Sarai) and felt entitled to be viewed as the mother of Avram's child and not as Sarai's maidservant.

Upset by the change in dynamic, Sarai asked Avram, how could you go to my maidservant on behalf of yourself when we are trying to build a family together? Avram agrees with Sarai and says, "Your maid is in your hands. Deal with her as you think right." From that point, Sarai began to treat Hagar harshly, afflicting her with hard labor.

In the midst of affliction, Hagar decided to leave the tent of Avram and Sarai but on her way out is approached by an angel who tells her to, "Go back to your mistress and submit to her harsh treatment. I will greatly increase your offspring, And they shall be too many to count…. For God has paid heed to your suffering."

Ramban strongly criticizes Sarai's treatment Hagar. "Our matriarch sinned through this oppression, as did Avraham by allowing this to transpire. So God heard her [Hagar's] affliction and gave her a son who would be a wild ass of a man to afflict the seed of Abraham and Sarah with all kinds of affliction." Ramban who was living in a time of conflict between Jews and Muslims, clearly saw the episode of Sarai and Hager reflected in his own historical reality.

Living in our neighborhood enables constant interaction with our cousins. It means going to the store and calling out "Shaku Maku?" in Arabic, and receiving a "Baruch Hashem" in return. It means sitting in my house listening to the call to prayer 5 times a day. And it means grieving for lives that are taken away too early in the name of God.

I don't think this conflict is easy to explain but I can't help but wonder: How would our current situation be different if the relationships among Avram, Hagar, and Sarai would have been simple? If only Sarai would have understood the implications of Hagar's pregnancy on her relationship with Avram, if only Hagar would have been more sensitive to Sarai's marital insecurity in this trying time, if only Avram would have facilitated better communication within his household, could generations of tension and hostility have been averted?

When I think of Avram and Sarai, or any of our patriarchs, they seem untouchable. But I think it is important to remember that these holy characters were also human, with human emotions and reactions. Complicated family dynamics and miscommunication are fundamental parts of the human condition. Even without overt hostility, our free will enables us to become entangled in complicated emotional webs where there is no perfect solution, where it is inevitable that someone's feelings will get hurt. It is silly for us to judge Sarai or Avram, the progenitors of monotheism, but it is no coincidence that the uniquely human relationship to God was forged in the uniquely human dysfunctional family.

No family is perfect but when we focus on clearer communication, when we emphasize empathy, and when we honestly analyze the implications of our actions, we strengthen our familial bond and get closer to peace.
