Our forefather Avraham was given ten great tests in his lifetime. These were the defining moments in his life and served as the catalysts for his becoming the father of the entire Jewish nation. In the beginning of Parshat Lech Lecha we find one of these tests; the test to leave his family and his homeland in order to travel to the land of Israel.

However, it is curious to note that when he was commanded to set out, he was guaranteed by Hashem that it would be to his benefit. He was told that at the other end of his journey he would be blessed with children, wealth, and honor. So what exactly was the big test? Doesn't everyone leave their home in search of these much-coveted blessings?

Rabbi Moshe Feinstein explains that the test here was not about the actual journey, it was about the mindset. Avraham knew very clearly that G-d could do anything at all. He had already experienced numerous miracles which defied all laws of nature. This awareness could have led Avraham to a very simple question -- why must he travel so far in order to receive G-d's blessings; couldn't G-d give them to him right there and then? The test Avraham was faced with was to accept the decree of the Almighty without asking questions. To realize that G-d has a master plan, and that we are not meant to understand everything.

This was the greatness of Avraham, and it behooves all of us to learn from our great forefather and incorporate this attribute into our daily lives.

We often want a particular thing, be it a job, an item, or a relationship, and we convince ourselves that the only way we can be happy is if we have that thing. But, if you're anything like me, then you know that more often than not we don't actually receive our wishes. At that point the natural reaction is to feel a great deal of frustration and sadness, or even anger and depression. We are absolutely certain that we would have benefited from that thing and we can't see any good reason for it not going the way we'd like.

It is here that we are charged with the task of "*tamim tihyeh im Hashem Elokeichem"*, "you shall be wholehearted with G-d". This means to trust Him and accept that He knows what is for our ultimate good much much better than we ever could. We need to realize that in our human frailty our vision is grossly jaded and short-sighted. We see such a small part of the picture that it is ludicrous to think that we know best. Who then can we rely on? On He who knows and controls all of creation, He who can see the ultimate outcome of any given scenario, and He who loves us unconditionally and wants only our very best.

I heard a beautiful story last week that really drives this point home. First, let me introduce Rabbi Yehoshua Hoffman to those who don't know him. Yehoshua was once a regular guy, like you and me. But when he was twenty-two years old, everything changed, as he broke his neck in a car accident and suffered paralysis in most of his body. Suddenly he found himself severely handicapped, unable to perform the simplest of tasks by himself. Yehoshua could have very easily chosen to wallow in self-pity for the rest of his life, but instead he has decided to constantly count his blessings and let G-d run the show. Indeed, he is one of the happiest people I know, and he has inspired countless individuals with his positive outlook.

Now I'll share the story. As you read, try to imagine yourself in Yehoshua's shoes. One morning, Yehoshua woke up early as usual with a packed schedule ahead of him. He requires an aide to help him out of bed and the aid regularly arrives at an appointed every morning. But this morning, the aide was nowhere to be seen and the minutes passed with agonizing slowness. Five minutes turned to ten, and then to fifteen. Yehoshua was totally stuck; no one was around to help him and without assistance he couldn't so much as sit up in bed. He felt the irritation rise within him -- he had so much to accomplish that day and because of something beyond his control he was unable to do a thing. But then he realized the futility of those thoughts and decided to replace them with a proactive and positive approach. It dawned on him that if the situation was totally beyond his control then G-d must want him to be right here right now -- exactly where he was, lying in bed and not out doing all the things he had planned. He was able to loosen his grasp on the reins and let Hashem choose the terms. This brought him an inner peace and a joy rooted in the knowledge that he was doing exactly what G-d wanted from him. This attitude enabled him to make it through the entire two hours that it took for someone to finally help him out of bed.

Can you imagine yourself stuck in that bed? Can you picture the deep feelings of frustration and self-pity threatening to overrun you? And now can you come to Yehoshua's powerful realization and feel the comfort and peace of mind wash over you? Finally, can you take that powerful picture back into your own life and recognize that wherever you are, whatever the circumstances, Hashem is leading you by the hand to exactly where you need to be, whether you understand it or not.

This was the test which Avraham withstood as he followed Hashem's directive away from his home and family. Did he understand why he needed to leave in order to receive G-d's blessings? No. But he understood that he was in good hands and surely Hashem would only act in his best interests.

If we can emulate Avraham's ways, then we are sure to live a life of endless happiness, peace of mind, and security -- with G-d's help.
