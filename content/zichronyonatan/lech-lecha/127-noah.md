This week’s parsha introduces us to the progenitor of our nation. Avraham is presented to us as a Jew in his natural habitat, amidst a divine test. He has been commanded to “Lech Lecha” or “go for himself.” He is instructed to leave his land, birthplace and father’s house. Unlike the command given to Noach last week, Avraham was not called to service to save the physical earth. He did not need to amass an unparalleled menagerie in a grand gesture to repopulate a barren world. Avraham’s test was not about generalities, it was specific and requested an individual sacrifice. Managing a zoo is no easy feat but abandoning everything you know because of a private monotheistic revelation is an accomplishment deserving of its own religion. Or three.

We see from Avraham that our relationship with God is most frequently developed through intimate and personal tests of moral character. This is the realm where we have opportunities to grow closer to the best version of ourselves and to God. These tests are not generic or standardized and certainly not multiple choice. Frequently they are steeped in nuance and force us to question our innermost views of morality. Rabbi Nachum learns Lech Lecha as meaning, “Go to yourself.” He teaches that we are being commanded to reconnect with our inner self, our personal morality. Only through reestablishing a dialog with our easily ignored consciousness can we truly discern if we are moving closer to God or farther away. And as the world is a reflection of the Torah it is clear is why the archetypal characters throughout Bereshit are so complex. The Ramban criticizes Avraham for leaving Canaan and holds him partially responsible for the Egyptian exile. Likewise, he laments Sarah’s oppression of Ishmael
and links our ongoing tensions with our cousins to her harshness. But these critiques hardly detract from the greatness of our forefamily. To the contrary, they show us that the tests they faced were of the same nature as ours, personal, messy and complicated.

Rabbi Sacks explains that we too can rise to the test of Avraham even in an age where idol worship is decidedly passe. He tells us to look with the clarity of Avraham at what aspects of our lives are directed toward service of God and which are directed toward service of self. The latter, he explains, are the idols of our time and we must emulate our foremost patriarch in annihilating them. It’s far from simple but we must remind ourselves constantly that, “We are not what we own but what we share, not what we buy but what we give.” Any deviation from this philosophy leads to the worship of material possessions for their own sake, independent of their higher purpose to be elevated in Godly service.

While we are given the gift of an Avraham Avinu within us, hearing his loving advice and accepting his guidance is no easy task. We have buried this voice deep within us, covered it with years of ego-driven self-satisfaction and trained ourselves to ignore it at all costs. Our society at large has primed and trained us through an obsession with “rugged individualism” and personal rights to value the individual above all. Avraham teaches us that the value of the individual lies within its ability to experience God in a close and personal way. The value of an individual is the ability to work on one's self, constantly striving to become better. Without a focus on spiritual introspection and the needs of the community, individualism is idolatry.

Shabbat Shalom, Noah
