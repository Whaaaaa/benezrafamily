Lech Lecha is replete with events applicable to this horrific situation we find ourselves in. Avraham is instructed to leave his birthplace, one of the ten major trials he will face in his life, he is given the land of Israel for his offspring to inhabit, and Hagar is expelled from her home.

While the scale and magnitude of this conflict make it easy to try and draw direct historical parallels to understand what lies in store, this approach is left best to prophets. The deeper themes, however, can give us a more nuanced and empathetic understanding of the people living through these situations. While the geopolitical situation has changed greatly over the last couple thousand years, the basic psychology of people has remained remarkably constant and our parsha can offer perspectives from people who endured many of the challenges we are faced with today.

By most counts, Avraham's first test was when he was instructed to leave his home. Despite being promised that this departure would be for the best, his ability to comply and trust in his future is considered one of his fundamental achievements. As a people familiar with displacement and exile we have no trouble imagining the incredible difficulty and sacrifice involved. Across the country hundreds of thousands are now displaced, hopefully so that they can have a better future, but nonetheless they are enduring a life-altering test of their faith. For those of us still sleeping in the comfort of our beds, how can we demonstrate our solidarity and empathize properly?

The Gemara in Rosh Hashana quotes our parsha:
And some say: Also, a change of one’s place of residence cancels an evil judgment, as it is written: “And the Lord said to Abram: Go you out of your county” (Genesis 12:1), and afterward it is written: “And I will make of you a great nation”

The Meiri comments:
Even though someone's judgment can seem as though it is already sealed against him, He should not give up on forgiveness, prayer, and repentance. He should change his actions and make plans, such as changing a name or a place, that will awaken a change in his conduct.

For us to ensure that the overwhelming feelings of displacement being experienced now are not in vain we must direct that feeling inward and use it to rouse ourselves to be better.

When Avraham is promised his inheritance in Israel he is told, “Get up, walk about the land, through its length and its breadth, for I give it to you.”

The Gemara debates whether this can be used as proof that simply walking through the property can be a means of acquiring it but concludes it cannot. If so what was the reason that Avraham was instructed to walk through it? The Gemara answers somewhat cryptically, “So that it would be easy for his descendants to acquire.”

The Rashbam fleshes out this answer writing, “It will make them seem like inheritors and not thieves and will prevent the accuser from prosecuting us.”

In a time when it seems that we are constantly being misunderstood as thieves and prosecuted for staking our claim to Israel, the Rashbam’s advice is incredibly practical. We must demonstrate that we are of this land, not mere inhabitants of it. We establish that claim by following in the footsteps of Avraham and getting up to experience the actual land.

Finally, we have the expulsion of Hagar at the hands of Sarai. The parsha says:
“She afflicted [Hagar], and [Hagar] fled from her.” Regarding this episode, the Ramban writes, “Our matriarch sinned in this affliction, as did Avraham in permitting it. God heard [Hagar’s] affliction and gave her a son who would be a wild man, afflicting the offspring of Avraham and Sarah with all manner of afflictions.”

This sentiment can easily be misconstrued to lend credence to the false narrative that our current conflict is biblical and intractable. This view misses the much larger moral message that we can learn. Affliction breeds instability and wildness. Hurting others never brings about resolution, it just contributes to cycles of conflict. We constantly have opportunities to rectify this misdeed both by refraining from unduly injuring others and by never standing by when it occurs in our midst.

At a time that is so rife with pure emotion, it is easy to be overcome. We can fall into despair and hopelessness. Avraham teaches us that even when faced with displacement and tribulations we need to cling to our belief that is a necessary step toward building a brighter future. We can feel that the criticism from the prosecutors who deny our right to live in this land is irreparable. Yet through Avraham being instructed to go out and encounter the land, we learn that through our engagement with Eretz Yisrael, we solidify our role as inheritors and silence those who disagree. Finally, we can easily allow this emotion to lead us to cruelty and the natural urge to afflict those we perceive as having afflicted us. We learn from Hagar that this inclination must be suppressed and that it only perpetuates unrest.

Hopefully, the coming weeks and months will be full of hope, confidence, and compassion for all of us, and am yisroel.

Shabbat Shalom.
