Avraham achieved a large part of his greatness when he followed the command of Hashem to leave his homeland and venture to the land of Canaan, which later became known as the land of Israel. The land of Canaan was not a random place on the map which G-d just happened to choose as the holy land, rather the land was (and is) intrinsically special. In fact, we are told that the waters of the great flood which covered the entire globe did not enter the land of Canaan. It was Hashem's chosen land from the beginning of time, and it has many qualities which can aid a person in spiritual matters.

What is fascinating to note is that traveling to Canaan was not a new idea which Hashem gave Avraham in this week's Parsha. Actually, it was thought up before by someone we don't consider particularly virtuous. That person was none other than Avraham's father, the idolatrous Terach, who had his own son thrown into a furnace for breaking Terach's idols. It seems that after Avraham emerged unscathed from the furnace his father had a change of heart and decided to follow Avraham's lead and pursue the service of Hashem. To that end, the Torah states that Terach took his son Avraham and the rest of his family, left their hometown of Ohr Kasdim, and began traveling towards Canaan. Why Canaan? For no less lofty a purpose than to reap all the spiritual benefits of the land. This means that, before Avraham ever set out on his own journey to Canaan, his wicked father was inspired to make the trek first.

Terach's aspirations and convictions at that time were every bit on par with the aspirations of Avraham – and yet he fell short. The very next verse tells that, while en route to Canaan, Terach stopped in a city called Charan, where he settled and eventually died. He dreamed, he yearned, but ultimately he died the same as he had always lived, never succeeding to elevate himself.

Not so our father Avraham. In this week's Parsha, the Torah states, “Avraham set out to go to Canaan, and he arrived at Canaan.” When Avraham set his eyes on the goal he couldn't be deterred by anything, in such a way that his plans were inextricably linked to their fulfillment.

This is what set Avraham apart from Terach, this is what sets the righteous apart from the wicked, and this is what will decide the fate of each and every one of us.

We all have flashes of inspiration, we all have dreams of achievement, and we all have moments when we stare at ourselves in the mirror and resolve that we must change. But all of our good intentions and promises to become better are far from enough. We must take immediate and aggressive action to fulfill our dreams. If we get distracted, then we are at risk of ending up just like Terach, who died the same as he had always lived, without ever reaching the levels for which he aspired. But if we harness the power of our dreams by acting on them without delay, there's no telling how far we can go.
