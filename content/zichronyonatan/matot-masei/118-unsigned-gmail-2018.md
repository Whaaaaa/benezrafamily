This week’s parsha introduces the concept of the cities of refuge. These 6 cities are designated as safe havens for those guilty of manslaughter that are fleeing from the relatives of the deceased. Law allows for immediate relatives of the victim of an accidental killing to pursue the killer until he reaches these cities, at which point he is once again protected by law. The only way for the manslaughter ensure that he is not murdered in retaliation is for him to remain in the city of refuge until the death of the Kohen Gadol.

Many facets of these laws are not easily understood. First, by establishing these cities the courts are effectively sanctioning extrajudicial manhunts by grieving families. Second, why does the Kohen Gadol’s death so directly impact the fate of manslaughters? The relationship between the Kohen Gadol and the manslaughters became so intimate due to this connection, that the Talmud explains how the mother of the Kohen Gadol would deliver food to the cities of refuge in hopes of preventing the enclave from praying for the death of her son!

In response to the second question, we see two main answers. The first is brought in the Talmud. “A venerable old scholar said, I heard an explanation at one of the sessional lectures of Rava, that the High Priest should have implored divine grace for the generation, which he failed to do.” Here, we are taught that the Kohen Gadol is responsible not only for the explainable preventable mistakes of his community, rather he is responsible for divine grace as well which would have prevented such awful accidents from happening.

The Rambam offers another explanation entirely. “A person who killed another person unknowingly must go into exile because the anger of “the avenger of the blood” cools down while the cause of the mischief is out of sight. The chance of returning from the exile depends on the death of the High Priest, the most honoured of men, and the friend of all Israel. By his death the relative of the slain person becomes reconciled; for it is a natural phenomenon that we find consolation in our misfortune when the same misfortune or a greater one has befallen another person. Amongst us no death causes more grief than that of the High Priest.” This explanation entirely neglects the Kohen Gadol’s responsibility for the spiritual circumstances that allowed for a calamity to occur. Instead, the Rambam offers a natural explanation of consolation and safety. As we understand too well from the national tragedies we have lived through, people come together and reconcile in an unparalleled way
during times of grief. The Rambam seems to indicate that this force is so strong it can pave the way for forgiveness even between a killer and the surviving family.

These two explanations seem to have little to do with one another. While the Talmud focuses on a spiritual reality that impacts physical events, the Rambam seems to offer a brief lesson in psychology. To understand how these relate, we simply have to consider each from a different perspective of grief. When any death touches a family, it feels preordained and supernatural. For relatives whose grieve for accidental death, it can feel as though their loved one was taken for no reason. It can feel like they were robbed of their rightful conclusion to their spiritual journey and it was instead cut short. With these people, the Talmud empathizes. It validates their feelings and passes the blame away from the deceased.

Meanwhile, the killer himself had no intention of taking life away from this innocent man. The death undoubtedly weighs heavily on his conscience and is source of pain and grief to him as well. With the killer, the Rambam empathizes. His explanation acknowledges the very real hurt that was caused by the killer while contextualizing it and allowing room for time and history to mend.

Through these explanations we can answer our first question as well. The laws about Cities of Refuge aren’t designed to allow or prohibit behavior. We are not trying to reprogram the human being that feels loss, anger and pain. Our laws are designed to make room for the full human experience. When an accidental loss occurs our legal system empathizes with all parties, the result of which is a City of Refuge connected to the High Priest.
