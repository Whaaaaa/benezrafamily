When the Israeli Defense Force regained power of the Old City of Jerusalem and the Western Wall, it was an incredibly emotional moment. Many of the soldiers who had fought in the actual liberation laid their heads on the wall and began to weep. Standing there was one soldier, Dani, who was very far-removed from Judaism. For a minute he watched his comrades in silence, unmoved by the significance of this religious landmark. But suddenly he too broke down, sobbing bitterly, until his cries rose above those of his religious companions. Dani's friend knew that he lacked any connection to Judaism and asked with surprise, 'Why are you crying?' To which came Dani's profoundly tragic response, 'I am crying because I don't know what there is to cry for.'

These are the 'three weeks' leading up to the fast of Tisha B'av, the saddest day of the year. During these days, we mourn the destruction of the Beit Hamikdash, the exile of our people from the Holy Land, and the distance which stands between us and the Divine Presence. On Tisha B'av, in shuls and homes throughout the world, we will sit on the floor, remove our shoes, and recite some of the most haunting and sorrowful liturgy ever written.

In times not long gone by, the mourning of the three weeks and Tisha B'av was felt deeply by every member of our people. Tears flowed freely from every eye, and our bereavement pierced every heart even more sharply than the death of a loved one.

And yet today, our hearts of stone are all but impervious to the calamity which has befallen our people. We fast, but we count down the hours until we can grab that glass of orange juice. We sit on the floor, but we moan and groan from discomfort and celebrate when we finally are able to sit back up on our couch. We recite the Kinot, but the words seem far-away and empty, rarely eliciting a lone teardrop from a weary eye.

One of the reasons that we are so estranged from the proper spirit of the thee weeks and Tisha B'av is the fact that we never knew any other existence. We never experienced the ecstasy of such an intimate connection to G-d. We never felt the purity of one who had brought an offering and cleansed his past misdeeds. And we never witnessed the open miracles which abounded in the Beit Hamikdash.

Bottom line, we have no idea what it is that we are missing.

We are akin to a child who is born without any sense of sight. If we would try to explain to him what a colorful, vibrant, and brilliant world there is, he would never understand. He simply lacks any frame of reference for such things. So are we, born into a world of spiritual darkness. We can talk all day about the times of old, but a true and accurate perception of such loftiness is as unimaginable to us as color is to the blind man.

So, of course, we must try mightily to glean whatever sliver of understanding we can from all the wealth of information on the subject. But when, inevitably, we fail to comprehend, let us realize the desperate situation we are in. And let us cry because we don't know what there is to cry for.

Good Shabbos and may we merit celebrating this coming Tisha B'av with the rebuilding of the Beit Hamikdash,

Yosef
