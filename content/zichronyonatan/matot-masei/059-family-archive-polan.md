This week's Parsha, Matot-Masei, discusses how the land was divided among the twelve tribes. Gad and Reuven, owning large herds, ask for land outside Israel proper, on the eastern bank of the Jordan -- a request that angers Moshe, who discourages the whole nation from settling outside the Land. As a compromise, the men of Gad and Reuven agree to fight alongside the other tribes for the conquest of Israel before returning to their families across the Jordan.

Why was Moshe not punished for his sharp anger here, as he was for striking the rock? Rav Kook explains that Moshe's anger was justified -- discouraging aliyah, even with a reasonable argument, follows in the footsteps of the infamous spies.

A Tosafot in Menachot cites a midrash about a two-headed child who, before King Shlomo, demands a double inheritance; Shlomo, testing whether the child is truly two people or one, pours boiling water on one head, and when the other cries out in pain, the truth is revealed. The metaphor suggests that the Jewish people, despite our many different "heads" -- our varied ways of thinking that seem to detach us from one another -- are still, fundamentally, one body: pour boiling water on any part of the Jewish world, and the rest of us feel it.

Spending a week in Poland and six weeks in Israel, I have seen how true this is. In Poland, visiting Auschwitz, Birkenau, and Majdanek, I felt the "boiling water" and the pain of the six million murdered. A week later, arriving in Israel, I felt the joy and love of a country full of Jews, connected by religion, culture, and language -- a melting pot with a place for every Jew. Every city in Israel carries its own culture, much as the tribes were divided, and yet somehow we remain connected and linked. Being a Jew is a brotherhood and sisterhood, and it is felt nowhere more than in the Holy Land.
