The Ibn Ezra, about the mitzvah not to covet your friend's possessions, famously explains the tactic to avoid jealousy with a parable to a peasant and a princess. The peasant, in his overalls and boots, covered in mud and grime, living in a straw hut, would never even dream of marrying the King's daughter. As her entourage passes through his city, replete with a magnificent marching band, beautiful stallions, and royal chariots, he feels no twinge of longing to have her hand in marriage. Why? Because the princess is so far removed from the peasant and such a shidduch is so far from the realm of reality that his mind doesn't even travel down that route – it is simply unthinkable.

So too, says the Ibn Ezra, when we see something that our friend has, we have to realize that it has absolutely nothing to do with us. If we are decreed to have that thing we will have it and if not we will not, and our friends' possessions are completely and totally out of reach. With such an understanding, we, just as the peasant and the princess, will be rid of any and all feelings of jealousy.

This is a well-known concept and something which logically makes perfect sense. Jealousy is truly not something based on rational thinking, and we all know that it gets us nothing but heartache. Yet still, if we are driving a 20-year-old clunker that has been at the mechanic every other week and we suddenly see our neighbor driving a sparklingly new and beautiful car, it is tremendously difficult to put the above parable to work and avoid any tinge of jealousy.

However, for some reason, when it comes to spiritual matters, where we are supposed to covet the virtues that we see in others, we are all very quick to dismiss our friends' achievements as beyond us. If our friend is incredibly kind, we immediately assert that he is a tzadik by nature. If he is more learned then he is simply a genius. If he is more diligent then he is a machine. The bottom line is, that we are very content to define our friends as the princess and ourselves as the pauper, without the slightest twinge of envy, in flawless execution of the Ibn Ezra's teaching.

Rav Aharon Kotler zt"l explains that this complacency and lack of ambition in matters of spirituality is one of the primary reasons that we have such a hard time mourning the destruction of the Beit Hamikdash.

When the Beit Hamikdash stood, every Jew lived with a pristinely clean slate. If one stumbled into some sin, he would immediately bring a korban (sacrifice) and be cleansed. Our relationship with Hashem was tangibly real, as we witnessed constant miracles and Hashem spoke to us through his prophets. Our Torah learning was with a level of understanding and retention that we cannot begin to fathom. And the list goes on and on. [As an aside, for one who wishes to gain a more complete picture of the differences between then and now, I would highly suggest going through the section of the Chazan's Repetition for Yom Kippur immediately following 'Mar'eh Kohen'.]

That was in the times of the Mikdash. But today, we are sullied beyond belief, buried under years and even generations of sin. When we try to daven and connect with Hashem, we often feel so distant that we have trouble even concentrating on the words. We flounder without knowing exactly what we are meant to do in the world, never hearing the clarion call of the prophets. We struggle mightily to make headway in our learning, and after hours of exertion we cannot reach the toes of even our recent predecessors.

We are so hampered and limited in every facet of spiritual achievement precisely because we are in exile, so how could we not mourn our loss? The answer is complacency. We have grown so accustomed to our challenges and we are so ridiculously content with our predicament, that we don't even wish for more.

To change this outlook will certainly not be easy, but in truth this is a lifeline for all of us who struggle to feel a sense of mourning.

Allow me to explain. Another reason that we have such a hard time time mourning is because we never experienced the Beit Hamikdash. We are like a child who's father passed away before he was ever born. He doesn't have any knowledge of who his father was, so he naturally cannot connect to the pain of his loss. We are likewise born into the exile, and not just us but our parents and grandparents for so many generations. So, naturally, we have a terribly difficult time relating to our loss.

Our spiritual struggles, however, are no strangers to us. If we involve ourselves even minimally in self-improvement and spiritual pursuits, then we are keenly aware of our shortcomings. We can certainly understand and relate to these hardships.

As such, it should be much easier for us to work on our spiritual ambitions, and to wish and pine for the ability to achieve those ambitions. We can then use this as a springboard to mourn the entirety of our loss, and ultimately, through our tears, to merit the rebuilding of the Beit Hamikdash and our final redemption.
