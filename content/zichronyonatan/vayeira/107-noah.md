In this week's Parsha we are treated to a masterclass in overcoming anger, taught to us by Avraham and God.

Then the LORD said, "The outrage of Sodom and Gomorrah is so great, and their sin so grave! I will go down to see whether they have acted altogether according to the outcry that has reached Me; if not, I will take note."

Hashem has heard a wretched scream, either from a certain female victim or from the country itself, emanating from Sodom and Gomorrah and is enraged. The first thing God does when teaching us how to deal with our anger is, "I will go down to see…" Or HaChaim and others question this language. What does it mean that God has to descend to see his creations? Surely in the context of the infinite creator, up and down have little meaning. Rather, he explains, this is God informing us that he does not judge a person in comparison to his infallible self but instead takes into account the inadequacies of the sinner. "God does not literally descend to earth to gain knowledge He did not have, but God lowers His profile in order to give the defendant an additional chance to downgrade his sin."

The next part of God's response is also telling. "I will go to see whether…" does not sound like it is coming from an omniscient God. Rather we are being taught another lesson about dealing with the impulses of anger. We must always give others the benefit of the doubt. Furthermore, Rashi explains that this is God giving them an opportunity for Teshuva. While God knows what has already transpired, our free will allows us to make different decisions going forward and to rectify our ways. These points are essential in dealing with anger, extending the benefit of the doubt and always remembering that people have the capacity to improve.

Now Avraham gets a turn at the blackboard:

Abraham came forward and said, "Will You sweep away the innocent along with the guilty?"

Rashi, based on Targum Onkelos, explains that the words "Will You" (הַאַ֣ף) can be translated as "Anger" rendering the meaning of the verse, "will Your anger urge you to destroy righteous with wicked?"

This understanding reveals an important tool we have when we are angry or helping someone who is. Our anger is something separate from us. It is not a fundamental part of who we are but an external force that encourages us to overreact. Furthermore, Avraham shows us the main exploit anger uses to get us to react, throwing the baby out with the bathwater! When we are blinded by anger, we quickly restrict our thinking to extremes; everything is awful, this person is worthless, and it's all your fault! This juvenile thinking leaves no room for us to appreciate our much more nuanced existence. We must instead heed Avraham's advice and adopt his mindset, realizing that even in the Sodom and Gemmora's of our lives, there may be hidden goodness that we are responsible to find and redeem.

Next, we have an appeal to justice.

Far be it from You to do such a thing, to bring death upon the innocent as well as the guilty, so that innocent and guilty fare alike. Far be it from You! Shall not the Judge of all the earth deal justly?"

Once we have calmed our anger and changed our attitude, we can rely on the concept of justice to placate our anger. We live in a society of laws and norms that are meant to protect us and protect others from us. To avoid the Hobbesian state of nature, we have agreed to a social contract of rules and guidelines that preserve our individual freedom. Part of this contract is the mediation of disputes through a court system or system of justice. We cannot extrajudicially react in anger (or overreact as is commonly the case) to a perceived slight or there would be no end to conflict.

"In such condition, there is no place for industry; because the fruit thereof is uncertain: and consequently no culture of the earth... no knowledge of the face of the earth; no account of time; no arts; no letters; no society; and which is worst of all, continual fear, and danger of violent death; and the life of man, solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." - Leviathan

We have been given step by step instructions on how to avoid the above mentioned hell and to deal with our anger constructively in this week's parsha. Upon receiving news that inspires ire:
* Get off your pedestal, we all make mistakes, if an infinite and infallible God can take our faults into account we certainly can amongst each other!
* Offer the offender the benefit of the doubt
* Remember that this one episode doesn't define them, they are capable of change
* Acknowledge that our anger is an external force that pushes us to overreact
* Consider the complexity in every situation and remember rarely are things all bad
* Have faith that a system of justice exists to help you achieve recompense

With these pointers from the Parsha I hope we are better equipped to deal with anger wherever it appears!
