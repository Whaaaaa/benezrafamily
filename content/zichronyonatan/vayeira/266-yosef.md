When Avraham prays for the salvation of Sedom, he excuses himself to Hashem for coming with so many requests. In his apology, he humbly notes that really, he isn’t worthy of speaking to Hashem in the first place, and he eloquently expresses this sentiment with a statement of incredible humility.

Before we go into what Avraham said, I think it is worthwhile to mention just how great Avraham was, although my depiction will not even be a drop in the bucket of his full stature. Avraham lived in a world devoid – completely and totally devoid – of any knowledge of Hashem. The entire world served idolatry and to even think otherwise was something punishable by death. And yet Avraham, based on his own thoughts and musings, was able to discover the truth of God and was bold enough to act on those convictions. Possessed of incredible oratorial and inspirational power, Avraham began to teach the masses about The Omnipotent God. He traveled from town to town and tens of thousands were inspired to change their lives and follow him. Additionally, he was tremendously wealthy and was famed and beloved for his incredible generosity. And to top things off, he had just won a magnificent victory against four mighty kings, defying all odds. To put it shortly, Avraham was the most respected, wealthy,
and powerful person alive; he was quite simply on top of the world.

But Avraham didn’t see it that way. He spoke to Hashem, He who discerns even the slightest insincerity, and declared, “I am but dirt and ashes.”

The Beis Halevi explains the analogy. Dirt is something that has no past, it has never been formed into anything useful and it itself is almost valueless. However, dirt does have a future, since bricks and pottery are formed of dirt. Ashes, on the other hand, have no future. You can’t build anything out of ashes, but they do have a past as they once stood in the form of a proud tree.

Avraham said to Hashem that he is not just like dirt or just like ashes, but rather the hybrid of both – one with no past and no future – no redeeming qualities whatsoever. Now, Avraham certainly knew of his greatness; he knew of his impact on the world, recognized the advantages of his wealth, and acknowledged his prowess in war. Without knowing one’s qualities, one cannot accomplish anything in the world. So, what did he mean by such a statement? He recognized with utter clarity that all that he had was nothing but handouts from Hashem. His achievements, his wealth, his wisdom, his influence, his victories – everything was just the greatness of Hashem bestowed upon him. But Avraham, by himself, without the help of Hashem, was but dirt and ashes. Avraham had no personal past and no personal future; Hashem alone was the source of his every success.

In the merit of this statement, the Jewish people were given two mitzvos. They received the mitzvah of the red heifer, by which the ashes of the calf can cleanse the impure. And they received the mitzvah of sotah, where a woman suspected of immorality can be proven innocent by drinking a mixture of water and dirt combined with the name of Hashem.

The Beis Halevi beautifully explains the precision of Avraham’s reward. Because Avraham declared himself to be like dirt, without a past, he received the dirt of sotah which ‘has a past’ as it reveals what previously happened. Because he claimed to be like ash, without a future, he merited the ash of the heifer which ‘has a future’ as it purifies going forward.

The terrifying situation in Israel, with the lives of our people in grave danger, urge us to realize, as the progeny of Avraham, that we too are but dirt and ashes. We must pursue success and victory in every way possible, but we must realize that at the end of the day it is not in our hands. Personally, we have no past and no future, and that’s a very scary predicament to be in. But thankfully, we are not supposed to do it alone, but to graciously receive the blessings of God by acknowledging his ultimate control of it all.

Good Shabbos and may we hear only good news,

Yosef
