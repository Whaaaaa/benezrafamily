This week's parsha introduces us to the first Jewish Family Unit. Before the birth of Yitzchak there was another heir apparent to Avraham's household -- his nephew Lot. Lot and Avraham share a relationship that is somewhere between Father-Son and Brothers. But whereas Yitzchak becomes the face of our nation, Lot is relegated to a far lesser role. What separated them?

The Torah tells us Avraham was "madbik" -- forcibly, strenuously attempting to keep Lot attached to him -- while Lot constantly wandered, never attaching himself with an open heart. A relationship capable of sustaining an eternal nation cannot be forced, from either side.

Avraham's hospitality, welcoming all guests regardless of background, is contrasted with Lot's: when angels visited Avraham after his Brit Milah, Avraham washed his guests' feet before offering rest, careful that the dust of idol-worshippers not enter his domain; Lot reversed the order, showing he had learned the form of hospitality without its high spiritual standard.

When Sedom is destroyed, Lot flees to isolation on a mountain, and this isolation ultimately leads to tragedy within his family. Avraham, by contrast, responds to catastrophe by rededicating himself to helping others -- moving toward where he can find guests rather than collapsing into self-pity. The Jewish Home cannot be preoccupied with despair; when facing adversity, it doubles down and looks for ways to elevate the world.

As a descendant of Avraham, I must strive to internalize these lessons -- to approach mitzvot with open arms rather than force, and to answer adversity with rededication rather than withdrawal.
