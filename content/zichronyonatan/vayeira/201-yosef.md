Our mother Sarah saw Yishmael laughing, so she told Avraham to banish him from the house. Just like that.

How are we supposed to understand such a thing? What's wrong with laughing? And whatever it is, could it really deserve such a swift and harsh punishment?

Rashi explains with the famous Midrash that the word 'metzachek', although literally translated as laughter, here refers to the three capital prohibitions – murder, idolatry, and immorality.

If we are to take Rashi's comment literally it would certainly answer our question, but a literal understanding seems unlikely. Had Yishmael really sunk to such depravity, Avraham would not have needed Sarah's plea to banish him; he would have done it himself long before. Also, later in the Parshah, when Yishmael is about to die of thirst in the desert, the Midrash records a discussion between Hashem and the angels whether or not to save him miraculously. The angels said that a miracle could not be performed for one who's progeny would later kill the Jews with thirst, to which Hashem responded that since Yishmael was still innocent at the time he would be saved. If Yishmael had already committed such a heinous list of sins bearing the death penalty, he could hardly be called innocent.

Rabbi Avigdor Miller therefore concludes that Yishmael must not have committed the sins in actuality, but Sarah saw from his laughter that he already had the potential for such things – it was only a matter of time.

But what did Sarah see in this laugh?

Rabbi Miller explains that the house of Avraham and Sarah was the Beit Hamikdash of their days, so to speak. To the naked eye, it was a regular house in which they ate, slept, and lived. They performed all the daily functions of life in their house, but in everything they did they concentrated their thoughts on a relationship with Hashem. When they ate, they focused on the greatness and kindness of He who provided them with food. When they slept, they did so only so that they would have strength to serve Him. When they interacted with people, they strove to emulate G-d's love and benevolence and to teach of His ways. In this way, they elevated the mundane nature of a house to the loftiest heights, and thereby their house became the house of Hashem.

So holy was the tent of Avraham and Sarah, that when, generations later, the Jews set forth to build Hashem's house, they found no better model than the house of Avraham and Sarah. And so we find the midrashim linking every facet of our forefather's tent to the tent of the Almighty Himself.

Laughter is no crime, but there are some places where you don't laugh. In the Beit Hamikdash, laughter is understandably inappropriate. The holiness and sublimity of the place demands a gravity which leaves no room for laughter. The greatest of happiness, yes, but not the levity of laughter.

Such was the state of things in Avraham's tent on a daily basis. It was the holiest place on earth, the dwelling place of the Shechina itself, and laughter was therefore highly improper.

Now we can imagine that when Yishmael did laugh, everyone stopped in their tracks, unsure of what to do. One could have heard a pin drop. Those gathered were aghast at the lack of respect for everything which Avraham's tent stood for. Laughing here showed a grievous dishonor to the presence of G-d which permeated the place.

But Sarah alone saw where this was headed. If Yishmael could shake off the fear of Hashem here of all places, there was nothing stopping him from the worst of sins. It is for this reason that Yishmael was banished.

There are many lessons here, but I'd like to focus on one that is incredibly empowering. We think that holiness and greatness come from lofty spiritual pursuits and magnificent acts. We therefore tend to think that true greatness is just not for us, and we resign ourselves to mediocrity. Here we find that Avraham and Sarah achieved their exalted relationship with Hashem not through big things, but by channeling the small day-to-day grind towards Hashem. They achieved the highest spiritual hights in their tent – at home, with normal everyday functions.

This gives me a tremendous sense of power and possibility. I need not travel to the heavens to find Hashem. No. I can find him right here. In the seed of an apple and the wind rustling through the trees. As I talk to my friend and drive down the street. When I play with my child, and while cleaning the house. When I eat. When I sleep. At every turn – there I can connect. I can turn the mundane into the ultimate holiness. All I need to do is think, focus, and channel my actions towards the service of my King.
