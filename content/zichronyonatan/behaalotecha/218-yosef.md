When Aharon saw that all of the princes of Israel brought lavish offerings to Hashem in the end of the last parsha, he felt bad that he had not also done the same. To comfort him, Hashem told Aharon at the beginning of this week's parsha about his personal mitzvah of lighting the menorah in the tabernacle – a great mitzvah reserved for the high priest alone.

At face value, this story seems puzzling. If Aharon indeed felt bad that he hadn't brought the same offerings as the others, why was he just sitting around feeling down about it? There was no reason he couldn't get up right now and do the same, so why the feelings of dejection?

I heard a beautiful explanation from Rabbi Dovid Kviat zt”l. He explained that all of the princes were moved of their own accord to bring their offerings to Hashem; it was something that came from deep within and was a genuine expression of their inner love of Hashem and their excitement for His service. Aharon, on the other hand, was not personally inspired to bring such an offering. However, when he saw this great outpouring from his brethren, it dawned on him that this would really have been a very nice thing to do, and he longed that he too should be able to do such an act for Hashem. Why then didn't he follow suit? Because service of Hashem, and indeed everything we do in life must be genuine. If we do something just because others are doing it, then however praiseworthy the act may be, it is empty and largely devoid of meaning. This doesn't mean we shouldn't learn from the virtue of others, but before we act in kind we must be sure that we have already internalized the lesson and that we are acting according to our newfound *personal* inspiration – not just copying their external act.

I believe Uncle Yonatan was fond of relating a story in the Talmud, in which one sage said that he was like vinegar the son of wine in regards to his father. Meaning that his father was far greater than him. How so? Because his father waited a full twenty-four hours between meat and milk, whereas he only waited from one meal to the next [parenthetically this is the basis for the six hours prevalent in our days]. The question again is, that if he perceived this as a shortcoming, why didn't he do the same? It is certainly a safe assumption that this great sage was easily able to overcome the urge for that slice of pizza, so why not just wait a little longer to achieve the greater level. The answer is that the father did so out of a genuine fear of sin which drove him to separate so far from a chance of mixing meat and milk. The son, however, did not possess this level of fear, and although he could have easily replicated the behavior, it would have been devoid of meaning if not fueled by internal inspiration.

Let's aspire to achieve ingenuity in all that we do. Let us learn from others, but not copy them. Let us be virtuous not only in action, but in our hearts and minds. Let's drop our society's obsession with the external and make sure that our inner world duly reflects all the virtue that we display on the outside.

Good Shabbos,

Yosef
