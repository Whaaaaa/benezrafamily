Parsha Behaalotecha means "when you step up." This week's Parsha focuses on themes of leadership and social order, and teaches us a great deal about living in community. The Jewish people have been camped at Mount Sinai for nearly a year, and G-d instructs Moshe on the procedures for their departure.

Pesach Sheni, a second Passover, is instituted after a group of ritually impure Jews petition Moshe, asking, "why should we be deprived?" On the journey, the people grow dissatisfied with the manna and complain for meat; quail arrive, but Moshe, overwhelmed by his duties, delegates smaller matters to seventy elders. Miriam speaks ill of Moshe's leadership and develops leprosy; the entire camp waits for her recovery while Moshe prays on her behalf.

Our leaders can never make everyone happy all the time. Moshe demonstrates resilience, fighting for his people even when their requests are unreasonable, while also knowing when to delegate. Pesach Sheni shows that Judaism has an open-door policy, seeking to give everyone the opportunity to participate. These are the traits that keep a Jewish community strong and united -- gratitude for what we have, and recognition of the divine intervention we benefit from daily. Shabbat Shalom!
