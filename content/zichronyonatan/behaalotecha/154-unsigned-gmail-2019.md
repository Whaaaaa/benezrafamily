This week's Parsha is Behalotecha, Israel is leaving their encampment at Sinai where they have been for almost a year. Before setting out Aaron is commanded to light the menorahs and the tribe of levi are assigned to temple duties in the Mishkan. We are also given the commandment for Pesach, G‑d spoke to Moses in the Sinai desert ... saying: "The children of Israel should prepare the Passover [offering] at its appointed time. On the fourteenth of this month, in the afternoon ... in accordance with all its decrees and laws...."

After conveying the mitzvah of Pesach to Bnei Yisroel Moshe was approached by a few ritually impure people that would be unable to give a sacrifice on that day. "...Why should we be deprived, and not be able to present G‑ds offering in its time, amongst the children of Israel?" And Moses said to them: "Wait here, and I will hear what G‑d will command concerning you." God gave over the specifications for a second Pesach a month later for those who had been "in contact with the dead or out on a distant road" to rectify their failures.

Also in this weeks Parsha Bnei Yisroel complain to Moshe about not getting meat, forcing Moshe to setup a more elaborate system of hierarchy to function smoothly, thus losing the approval of his sister Miriam. After speaking negatively about him, Miriam contracts leprosy.

The idea behind Pesach Sheini - the second pesach is Teshuvah the practice of transforming negative experiences like "contact with the dead" into uplifting interactions with the living and Returning after wandering "out on distant roads" makes us homesick. Hashem initially commanded just the one Pesach, it took a small group of men to ask for an exception. Returning often seems automatic, less active than seeking something out, but the Torah teaches us the opposite is true, it takes 613 mitzvot to define God's will to add a channel for instant return detracts from the initial obligations. While the first Pesach is seven days long and leavened bread is forbidden, Pesach Sheni is observed one day and though the sacrifice offered is very similar, the distinctions between the times are clear. In the words of the midrash, They asked Wisdom, "What is the punishment for the sinner?" Wisdom replied: "Evil pursues sinners."

They asked Prophecy, and Prophecy replied: "The soul who sins shall die."

They asked Torah, and Torah replied: "He shall bring a guilt-offering and he shall be forgiven."

They asked G‑d, and G‑d replied: "Let him do teshuvah and he shall be forgiven." (Yalkut Shimoni, Tehillim 702)

In the realms of our wisdom and prophecy teshuvah cannot be a consideration. However through torah study and a relationship to the divine, we can carve the path for second chances to be granted, before transgressions are made.
