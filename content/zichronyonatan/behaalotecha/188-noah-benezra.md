This week's parsha concludes with the paradigm of lashon hara, Aaron and Miriam speaking about Moshe. This saga begins,

וַתְּדַבֵּ֨ר מִרְיָ֤ם וְאַהֲרֹן֙ בְּמֹשֶׁ֔ה עַל־אֹד֛וֹת הָאִשָּׁ֥ה הַכֻּשִׁ֖ית אֲשֶׁ֣ר לָקָ֑ח כִּֽי־אִשָּׁ֥ה כֻשִׁ֖ית לָקָֽח׃
Miriam and Aaron spoke against Moses because of the Cushite woman he had married: "He married a Cushite woman!"
What is the relevance of Tziporah's Cushite heritage in Aaron and Miriam's gossip about Moshe?
Rashi citing Sifri fleshes out the story a little more, giving us a clearer picture of the interpersonal dynamics at play. Following the appointment of the 70 elders, there was a period of celebration. One evening, Miriam said regarding the new appointees, "Fortunate are these men, and fortunate are their wives!" to which Tzipporah replied, "Do not say 'Fortunate are their wives,' say 'Woe unto their wives!' For from the day that God spoke to Moses your brother, he has not had relations with me." This is where our Parsha continues the story, "Miriam and Aaron spoke against Moses about the woman"— about him separating himself from the woman. They accused Moshe of being prideful in his separation from Tziporrah, unaware that God had instructed Moshe to, "stay with me" following the revelation at Sinai, necessitating his celibacy.

With the story presented in its fuller form, it is still hard to understand why Tziporrah being a Cushite is the impetus for their Lashon Hara.

The Abarbanel offers an explanation,

הם אמרו שמשה בהפרדו מאשתו לא היה אפשר שיהיה אלא לאחת מג' סבות או לשלשתן יחד. הא' להיותם כושית כעורב כי צפורה היתה ממדין והיתה שחורה כי מדינים הם ישמעאלים שחורים מכח השמש וחומו ואולי משה נמנע ממנה לפי שאינה ישרה בעיניו. והב' מפני התבודדותו לנבואה והיותו מוכן אליה בכל עת. והג' להיות משה בטבעו ענו ומסתפק ומואס בפחיתות המשגל כי חרפה הוא לו.
They (Aaron and Miriam) said that Moshe's separation from his wife could only be for one or all of these three reasons. First, Tziporra was black as a crow. She was a Midianite, and was black, as Midianites are Yishmaelites that are black from the power and heat of the sun. Perhaps Moshe wouldn't be with her because she was not proper in his eyes. Second, maybe he needs to be ready for prophecy at all times. Third, maybe his nature caused him to regard relations as a disgraceful act.
The Abarbanel goes on to explain that these reasons were eventually rejected by Aaron and Miriam as well, but it was this line of reasoning that resulted in Miriam contracting leprosy.

In both Rashi's and the Abarbanel's explanations of these events, Aaron and Miriam making judgments based on extraneous evidence is the crux of the incident. In Rashi's retelling, the evidence is an offhand comment in a social setting, in the Abarbanel, it's Tziporrah's complexion. But it is clear why the Torah emphasized Tziporrah's ethnicity at the commencement of this episode. What could be more emblematic of the problems with making an unfounded judgment based on superficial reasoning than a racial justification for Moshe's celibacy?

In a similar vein, Pninei Halacha sites our Parsha not in dealing with classic Lashon Hara, but rather the obligation to judge each other favorably.
פניני הלכה, ליקוטים ב א׳:י׳:ה׳
וככל שהאדם יותר צדיק, כך צריך יותר להתאמץ לדון אותו לכף זכות. ועל כך נענשה מרים הנביאה ע"ה, שלא דנה את משה רבנו ע"ה לכף זכות (במדבר יב, א-טז).
And the more righteous a person is, the more he needs to strengthen his favorable judgment of others. As we saw with Miriam, she did not judge Moshe Rabbenu favorably.
In keeping with the above teaching, the Talmud offers a homiletical interpretation of Miriam and Aaron's reasoning that applies the favorable judgment to them that they lacked for their brother.
"על אודות האשה הכושית אשר לקח" וכי כושית שמה והלא ציפורה שמה אלא מה כושית משונה בעורה אף ציפורה משונה במעשיה
"Miriam and Aaron spoke against Moses because of the Cushite woman he had married." Was Cushite her name? Certainly not! Tziporrah was her name! Rather she was called a Cushite because just as a Cushite has exceptional skin, Tziporrah had exceptional actions.

Especially now, we can take from the Talmud's perspective and learn to look past the cosmetic and make judgments based on character.
