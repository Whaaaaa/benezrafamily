This week's parsha, Vayeshev, introduces us to Yosef. We are told that he is Yaakov's progeny and quickly are acquainted with the precarious family politics he finds himself amidst. The Torah tells us, "וַיִּרְא֣וּ אֶחָ֗יו כִּֽי־אֹת֞וֹ אָהַ֤ב אֲבִיהֶם֙ מִכָּל־אֶחָ֔יו וַיִּשְׂנְא֖וּ אֹת֑וֹ וְלֹ֥א יָֽכְל֖וּ דַּבְּר֥וֹ לְשָׁלֹֽם" or, "And his brothers saw that their father loved him more than all his brothers, so they hated him, and they could not speak with him peacefully." Rashi is bothered by this verse. He says, "From what is stated to their discredit, we may learn something to their credit, that they did not say one thing with their mouth and think differently in their heart." Rashi seems to be extolling the virtue of authenticity even when this leads to hatred and resentment.

On the other hand, the Kli Yakar learns a similar lesson from the exact opposite perspective. Bothered by the redundancy of the bolded word in the posuk, "וְיִשְׂרָאֵ֗ל אָהַ֤ב אֶת־יוֹסֵף֙ מִכָּל־בָּנָ֔יו כִּֽי־בֶן־זְקֻנִ֥ים ה֖וּא ל֑וֹ וְעָ֥שָׂה ל֖וֹ כְּתֹ֥נֶת פַּסִּֽים" or, "And Israel loved Joseph more than all his sons, because he was a son of his old age to him, and he made him a fine woolen coat." The extra, "to him" begs to be explained. The Kli Yakar first explains what behavior is characteristic of a "son of old age." He describes a maturity and thirst for the previous generations wisdom. Separate from the other children, this gem cleaves to his parents and behaves like a man among children. The Kli Yakar explains that Yosef did behave in this manner but only to Yaakov. This is what the extra word is coming to teach us. Yosef's behavior was not always as refined and sophisticated as it was when Yaakov was watching. This duplicity is regarded as a critique of Yosef's character, notwithstanding the grim sequence of events that would be triggered by his sibling's outsized reaction.

The commentators seem to be establishing legitimacy for the abusive brothers through claims of authenticity while heaping criticism onto the victim. What can we learn from this emphasis on the authentic even when it runs counter to our moral code?

To answer this question, we must understand how deeply our convictions must run. Judaism is not a religion about the cosmetic. Our rituals and practices are designed to not just elevate what we do, but who we are. When we are insincere and assert sets of ideas and beliefs because they are easy or politically expedient, we undermine the core of our religion. This is true to such an extent that even when mistakes are made through sincere intentions we are willing to extend praise. And, when chicanery leads to a pit, we are willing to offer critique. How fortunate we are to have a system of beliefs that encourages us to be true to ourselves in the pursuit of god and self-development. This coming week we should all merit to continue on our paths of sincere authentic connection, even if it is a struggle.

Shabbat Shalom

Noah

The posuk says, "וַיִּ֨קָּחֻ֔הוּ וַיַּשְׁלִ֥כוּ אֹת֖וֹ הַבֹּ֑רָה וְהַבּ֣וֹר רֵ֔ק אֵ֥ין בּ֖וֹ מָֽיִם" meaning, "And they took him and cast him into the pit; now the pit was empty there was no water in it." Rashi explains, "Since it says: "now the pit was empty," do I not know that there was no water in it? For what purpose did the Torah write, "there was no water in it"? [To inform us that] there was no water in it, but there were snakes and scorpions in it."

How did Rashi know that there were snakes in the pit? I heard in the name of the Vilna Gaon that it is hinted to by the very next word and its cantillation mark. The next verse begins with the word, וַיֵּֽשְׁבוּ֘ with a squiggly line over the last syllable. While this word means, "and they sat down" it can be broken down into two separate words, וַיֵּֽשְׁ בוּ֘ meaning "There was in it." If you are wondering what was in the pit simply look at the squiggly cantillation mark and there is your snake.
