There's a widespread misconception that you cannot tell someone how to feel. You can tell them not to kill, but you can't tell them not to hate. You can tell them to act kindly, but you can't tell them to love. You can tell them not to steal, but you can't tell them not to covet. This school of thought claims that our feelings are automatic impulses which are out of our control. Yet the Torah teaches us otherwise. We are commanded on many occasions to control and direct our inner feelings themselves. It is not enough to refrain from hurting another; we must remove the hate from our hearts. It is not enough to act kindly; we must love our brother as ourselves. And it is not enough to refrain from stealing; we may not even desire that which belongs to our fellowman.

How can this be expected from a person? The answer is that feelings come from our thought processes and our actions. By thinking positive thoughts about another we can indeed direct our feelings toward them. Likewise, by actions of kindness to another, we can plant love in our hearts.

What about desiring another's possessions? One of the Ten Commandments states, “You shall not desire your fellow's wife, nor his slave, his maidservant, his ox, his donkey, nor anything that belongs to your fellow.” Jealousy is one of the most deep-seated feelings in the human psyche. A one-year-old infant will already cry when his mother holds another child. For children, one of the most frequent complaints is that a sibling or friend has more than themselves. And isn't the idea of “keeping up with the Joneses” the single greatest cause of unhappiness in the adult world? How are we expected to rid ourselves of this most natural of feelings?

The Ibn Ezra offers a parable. If a simple peasant sees a beautiful princess, he will not even desire to marry her because he knows that she is totally and completely outside of his reach. He knows that never in a million years can he achieve such a notion and thereby his heart does not even desire it. We must realize, says the Ibn Ezra, that one can only have that which is granted to him by heaven. Anything which was given to your friend and not to you is completely and totally out of your realm. If we will realize this, then we will completely cease to desire our friends' possessions. We will fulfill the commandment of the Torah and simultaneously enable ourselves to live happier, more fulfilled
lives.
