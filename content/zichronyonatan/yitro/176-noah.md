The events of this week's parsha cement Moshe as the archetype for human achievement. While the revelation at Har Sinai was supposed to be for all of Bnei Yisrael to experience together, after the first commandment it was clear that only Moshe had achieved the spiritual perfection necessary to comprehend and deliver the message without dying. His relationship with God was fundamentally different from that of anyone and his level of prophecy exceeded that of all prophets that came before or after. Moshe is closest we get to a perfect person and yet at the outset of this episode we see a lackluster family reunion.

וַיָּבֹ֞א יִתְר֨וֹ חֹתֵ֥ן מֹשֶׁ֛ה וּבָנָ֥יו וְאִשְׁתּ֖וֹ אֶל־מֹשֶׁ֑ה אֶל־הַמִּדְבָּ֗ר אֲשֶׁר־ה֛וּא חֹנֶ֥ה שָׁ֖ם הַ֥ר הָאֱלֹהִֽים׃

Jethro, Moses' father-in-law, brought Moses' sons and wife to him in the wilderness, where he was encamped at the mountain of God.

וַיֹּ֙אמֶר֙ אֶל־מֹשֶׁ֔ה אֲנִ֛י חֹתֶנְךָ֥ יִתְר֖וֹ בָּ֣א אֵלֶ֑יךָ וְאִ֨שְׁתְּךָ֔ וּשְׁנֵ֥י בָנֶ֖יהָ עִמָּֽהּ׃

He sent word to Moses, "I, your father-in-law Jethro, am coming to you, with your wife and her two sons."

In these two posukim we see a stark contrast in the presentation of Moshe's relationship with his children. In the first posuk, the Torah tells us that Eliezer and Gershom are Moshe's sons. In the second, Yitro pointedly refers to them as only the sons of Tziporah, Moshe's wife.

The Sefer Chibba Yezteria picks up on this inconsistency and resolves in it a way that seems to challenge the notion of Moshe as a perfect person:

ואשתך ושני בניה עמה. בניה ולא בניו, כיון שלא סייע לגדלם לכן נקראו על שמה.

They are referred to as her children and not his because he didn't help raise them.

This explanation can be combined with the Ibn Ezra and other Rishonim's position that Yitro's arrival chronologically occurred after the Torah was given. According to these commentators, while the entire nation watched Moshe ascend to the greatest spiritual height, his immediate family was nowhere to be found.

In light of their absence, the following passage from Bamidbar Rabba is not very surprising.

"Moses said, "See it is the time for me to claim my needs. If daughters inherit, it is [also] proper for my sons to inherit my glory." The Holy One, blessed be He, said to him (in Prov. 27:18), "He who tends a fig tree will enjoy its fruit.' Your sons sat with their own concerns and were not involved with Torah study. Whereas Joshua served you much and showed you much honor. And he got up early to your house of meeting and left late. He arranged the benches and spread out the mats. Since he served you with all of his ability, he is worthy to serve Israel, so as not to lose his compensation." (Numb. 27:18) "Take Joshua bin Nun," in order to fulfill what is stated (in Prov. 27:18), "He who tends a fig tree will enjoy its fruit."

Moshe wanted his honor to be passed to his progeny. He wanted Gershom and Eliezer to become central leaders of the Jewish people, following in his footsteps. But they couldn't. They weren't raised by Moshe, they didn't have the opportunity to arrange the benches in Moshe's beis medrash or accord him honor. They missed the central revelation of Judaism and their father's greatest achievement. And still, Moshe is the example of the full realization of human potential.

To resolve this we have to accept that there is no such thing as human perfection. Being human requires making decisions and every decision we make has unforeseen consequences and tradeoffs. Every moment of our time invested somewhere comes with the opportunity-cost that it could have been spent somewhere else. Moshe's single focus on spiritual development enabled him to reach the highest heights but came at the expense of family life.

I find this perspective inspiring. Through humanizing Moshe we can see the tremendous personal sacrifice he was willing to make on behalf of his people. He sacrificed his role as a father and as the first king in a birthright monarchy. He endured years of separation from his wife all while shouldering the burden of a stiff-necked people. And all of this was done while being a person. While experiencing desires and emotions and frustration and longing Moshe still managed to commit himself fully to his goal of actualizing his spiritual potential. And although there are tradeoffs and compromises Moshe proved the sky's the limit.
