Yisro was a powerful and wealthy priest in Midyan, living in the lap of luxury. And yet, when he heard about the miracles of the Exodus, he was moved to leave behind everything and venture into the desert to join with Hashem's people and become a Jew. Our sages were amazed by the dedication and sacrifice displayed by this heretofore unaffiliated idolater, so they set out to discover just what Yisro had heard which had such a profound impact on the course of his life.

Rashi, in one explanation, explains that it was the war with Amalek which really got Yisro going. At face value, this is a reference to the miraculous salvation which Hashem had performed for the Jewish people, giving over the mighty army of Amalek to their hands. Such an understanding would simply render that it was Hashem's special protection of the Jewish people which Yisro also sought to enjoy.

But under further speculation, it seems unlikely that this is Rashi's intention. Firstly, if the victory was the defining point here, Rashi should have called it the 'victory over Amalek', whereas the wording 'the war of Amalek' would seem to point to something about the war itself as opposed to the ultimate result of the war. Second, if the much more stunning and miraculous display of wonders which had brought Egypt to its knees had not yet inspired Yisro, it would be rather strange that the much smaller-scale victory over Amalek would suddenly light the spark.

Rabbi Yaakov Neiman, answers these questions with a striking change of perspective. It was not excitement for the Jewish victory which drove Yisro, but disgust for the actions of Amalek which made him long for something greater.

To understand, let's try to picture the times in which this battle took place. Egypt was the greatest nation of antiquity; the wealthiest, the most powerful, and the most advanced. You can be sure that the goings-on in Egypt were very well circulated across the globe. Suddenly, the front pages of every worldwide newspaper were filled with the unbelievable destruction taking place right in the home of this world power. They told of the water supply turning to blood, of the frogs and lice which gave them no rest, of wild beasts and pestilence. Next came news of boils and fire-filled hail, of locusts the likes of which no one could dream, and the week of absolute darkness. Finally, they told of the death of every firstborn and the civil war which it brought upon the already shattered nation. There were even some pictures of Pharaoh, pajama-clad, begging Moshe to leave in the middle of the night, which immediately went viral.

And in case somebody didn't read the news or had something against social media, they couldn't possibly have missed the splitting of the sea when ALL bodies of water in the entire world miraculously split. With your bathwater and drinking water splitting before your eyes, you couldn't help but ask around and try to figure out what on earth was going on over here. And every person one bumped into was talking about it, at bars and at bus stops, at social gatherings and when passing a neighbor in the street, there was just one topic of conversation -- the miracles and retribution taking place in Egypt. All around the world, people spoke about the greatness of Hashem, about His special care and love for the Jews, and about the gift of the Torah which He was about to give his people. The Jews were universally revered, celebrated, and feared.

It was at this incredible juncture in history, when Amalek reared its ugly head, refused to be moved by the miracles of which they knew, and did the unthinkable act of waging war against the fledgling Jewish nation.

This apathy and refusal to learn the lessons staring Amalek in the face is what brought Yisro to the decision that he could no longer remain a member of the nations of the world. As he witnessed just how blind man can be without the Torah to guide his life, he knew that he must join the nation of G-d and have the Torah for himself -- no matter what it would cost him. He realized that man, when left to his own self-concocted value system, can see the undeniable truth before his eyes and remain unchanged and unyielding. He understood that, when driven by our own distorted view of right and wrong, one can literally march to his grave in the name of the greatest perversions, while convinced of his own righteousness. And he knew that the only way perceives the world through a lens of truth and clarity, is by subscribing to the unchanging morals and ideals of Hashem himself, as communicated through the Torah.

We have lived through, and continue (please G-d) to live through, unprecedented times. Since the Exodus, there have not been many events in history that have caused such a disruption in our way of life, or demanded such introspection and thought as the novel Coronavirus. Like the Exodus, it has captivated and terrified the world in a way no one would have thought possible. No one has been spared of its devastation, and no one can possibly have missed the clarion call imploring us not to turn a blind eye, but to change.

And yet, we see the world around us striving mightily to put this whole story behind us, forget about it, and continue onward to the same abyss toward which it has been hurdling for decades. The world wants nothing of teshuva and self-improvement; it can view life as we know it halted and destroyed, but remain unmoved and unchanged.

The hopelessness of such a worldview and culture, which will follow its desires to the ends of the world unwilling to heed the call to change, must shake us to our core. It must light within us deep unrest with the society around us, and ignite a burning drive to rededicate ourselves to the Torah and its path of truth and self-perfection.
