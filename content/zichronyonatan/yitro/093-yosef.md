Yeedle was a peddler who lived with his family in a tiny shtetl in Poland. Once a year, Yeedle would travel to the other end of the country by horse and buggy to attend the grand trade show for two weeks. It was a long and arduous journey both ways and the stay at the show was no picnic, but Yeedle knew that his investment for those two weeks was the way that he could provide for his family for the entire year to come. During the show, as you can imagine, Yeedle barely ate or slept. He was singularly focused on the work at hand, and couldn't afford to waste a minute of this precious opportunity.

One year however, Yeedle ran into an old childhood friend whom he hadn't seen for years. For old times' sake, he gave his old friend a few minutes of his time, but after a short while he excused himself to return to his business ventures. His friend, however, wasn't so keen on cutting their conversation short, nudged Yeedle to come with him to the local tavern for some well-deserved rest and good times.

Do you think Yeedle took the bait? Of course not.

Yeedle understood full well that the entire year ahead was dependent on what he could produce in these two weeks. Sure some relaxation sounded nice, but it was a pleasure he simply could not afford. Instead of being lured by his friend's invitation, Yeedle was incensed -- feeling like his friend was trying to ruin his livelihood. Without a second thought, Yeedle turned and marched out the door, bidding his friend a terse farewell.

Moshe had two sons, Gershom and Eliezer, who were both named after events in Moshe's life. "The first was named Gershom, because he said 'I was a stranger (ger) in a strange land', and the name of the other was Eliezer, 'because the G-d of my father was my helper (ezri) and he saved me from the sword of Pharaoh.'"

The Chofetz Chaim asked two questions on these verses. First of all, Moshe named them out of order: chronologically his salvation from Pharaoh preceded his sojourn in Midyan? And second, why is the fact that he was a stranger in a strange land an event worth naming his child after?

The Chofetz Chaim explains that Moshe was concerned that his stay in the idolatrous land of Midyan, far away from any traces of Judaism would have an effect on him, and he might come to learn from their ways with the passing of time. To this end, he immediately named his firstborn son Gershom, as a personal reminder that this was not his place. Every time Moshe spoke the name of his son, he reiterated to himself that his stay in this place was only temporal and he would soon return to his people. Moreover, it was a message to himself about this world at large, which is also only a short journey until we will return our soul to its maker. In this way, Moshe was able to keep a constant vigil and never lose track of his ideals and his goals. Since this name was a safeguard for Moshe's very identity, it had to proceed the name commemorating his salvation from Pharaoh, even though it was out of order.

The Chofetz Chaim now draws our attention to the story with which we started. He explains that it is a parable to our life in this world. The true home of our soul is in the highest heavens, where it was hewn from beneath the heavenly throne itself. But in the heavens, the soul cannot achieve and it cannot earn the mitzvos which are the provisions necessary to sustain it for eternity. To this end, each soul is sent on a short business trip, as it were, to this world. It is a hard and strenuous journey, very far from its spiritual comfort zone, but the soul makes the trek because it knows that this is its only opportunity to provide for itself for all time. The soul arrives in this world with a singular drive to fulfill its mission before the time comes to return home, but along the way it encounters many a 'friend' who try to distract it with the cheap pleasures of this world. If the soul listens to these urges and allows itself to be deterred from its mission, the results can be catastrophic -- it only has one chance to earn a living for eternity. Just like our friend Yeedle, we need to keep the goal clearly before our eyes in order to withstand the temptations which are constantly pulling at us. Like Moshe, we need to find ways to constantly reiterate to ourselves the fact that we do not belong here, but are just passing through for a short time.

Practically speaking, though, not everyone has the opportunity to name their son Gershom, so what can we do to keep the message before our eyes? I think everyone has to find their own reminders, but the main thing is to find every excuse we can to think about the World to Come. If we hear of a death r"l, we should take a moment to remind ourselves that we won't live forever. When we see over-the-top physical indulgence, we should quietly condemn to ourselves the lack of priorities we are witnessing. When we do a mitzvah, we should consider the fact that these will be the only things which will be ours for eternity. If we are tempted by a misdeed, we should realize the everlasting damage of such actions. In this way, we can hope to properly utilize the immense opportunity of life and be able to return 'home' heavily laden with mitzvos to last an eternity.
